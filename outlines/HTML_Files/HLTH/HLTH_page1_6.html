Course Description

GERON 310 / HLTH 310 / KIN 310:

The physiology of human growth, development, and aging is examined, with special reference to the influence of diet, environment, exercise, and disease on the normal processes.

HLTH 310: View requirements for HLTH 310

KIN 310: View requirements for KIN 310

GERON 310: View requirements for GERON 310

Additional Course Description:  

  • There are three commonly studied areas of development â physical, cognitive, and psychosocial. The present course will focus upon the physical domain and the influence on development of genetics, hormones, and environmental variables such as stress, nutrition, and chemical teratogens.  We will examine the normal physiology of prenatal development, postnatal development, growth, and aging as well as disease processes or health issues associated with development across the lifespan.
  • The current course is not focused solely upon embryology, endocrinology, neurobiology, or pathobiology, so these areas will not be addressed as fully as might occur in other classes; rather, the course will address the health-related aspects of these fields (and others) in relation to growth, development, and aging.

Course Learning Objectives: 

The objective of this course will be to provide students with the ability to embark upon a continuing investigation into the biological determinants of health promotion and disease prevention from a developmental and lifespan perspective. Given that the field of development is expansive and multi-disciplinary, this course will serve as a broad introduction meant to build the three main foundations of any expertise: 

Knowledge: The factual, theoretical, and conceptual basis of a field of inquiry. 

Skills: Increasing familiarity with the critical thinking skills required to read and analyze scientific literature pertinent to a field of inquiry. 

Attitude: The confidence that one can bring some measure of order to a field of inquiry through the reasoned application of the scientific method.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe gametogenesis and the major stages of embryonic and fetal development using accurate medical terminology.
Explain physiological processes associated with normal post-natal growth through puberty, including how these processes are influenced by environmental, genetic, epigenetic, and hormonal factors.
Describe health issues associated with/or factors that can disrupt the major stages of pre- and post-natal development and growth.
Explain critical periods of development and give examples of possible outcomes associated with environmental exposures.
Describe different theories on the biology of aging and assess evidence that either supports or refutes each theory.
Differentiate between primary and secondary aging in physiological systems, with a focus on the endocrine, nervous, cardiovascular, and musculoskeletal systems.
Describe risk factors for the major chronic diseases associated with aging.
Explain the theory of developmental origins of disease and the roles of genetics, evolution, environmental factors, and epigenetics.
Critically assess and apply findings from scientific literature on human reproduction, development, and aging to support arguments in written assignments and in-class activities.

Participation Mark (10% total): There are two main avenues for your active participation in this class, answering questions using iClickers (ie an app on your phone/laptop/tablet), and group activities such as filling in worksheets based on one of the weekly readings or answering questions based on a case study.  We will also have short follow-up discussions based on your group activities.

            iClicker mark (5%): Your iClicker mark is based on participation only, not on whether you answered the questions correctly.  In order to earn the full 5% iClicker mark, you must achieve 75% of the total possible iClicker points for the whole term.  Below 75%, scores will be pro-rated downwards. iClicker questions will be asked during most lectures. This system of iClicker grading means that you can miss a few classes and you may still be able to earn the full 5% iClicker mark.  Information on how to register for iCIicker will be provided in LEARN.

Assignments (3 x 10% each, 30% total):  There will be three homework assignments, one for each of the 3 different modules of the course.  The assignments are meant to help you synthesize the material you are learning in the lectures and your readings.  This course is NOT about simply memorizing facts.  One of the main objectives of the course is for you to learn to think at a deeper level about the health implications of physiological processes at different developmental stages and explain how these processes are influenced by various factors (eg genetic factors, nutritional status, levels of physical activity, environment) to either increase or decrease risks for diseases over the life course.  The assignments require students to critically assess and apply findings from the scientific literature on human reproduction, development, and aging to support arguments.

Each assignment will be posted to the LEARN course website at least two weeks before the due date.  The assignments must be uploaded as a Word document to the LEARN Dropbox by 11:59 pm on the due date.  All assignments have very clear requirements and rubrics so it is up to students to make sure they understand the expectations and to ask their TA or the course instructor for clarification before the assignment is due.

           Late Assignments: Everyone in the course will be allowed Three 24-hour slip days, no questions asked, which can be used for any of the 3 assignments.  You decide if you want to use them, and for which assignments. You could use 1 slip day for each of the three assignments, 2 slip days for 1 assignment and 1 slip day for another assignment, etc. You cannot split up the 24 hours of a slip day amongst assignments.  Please indicate when you upload your late Assignment to the Dropbox (in the comment window of the Dropbox) whether you wish to use one or more slip days and you will not be penalized.  You do NOT have to email your TA or instructor ahead of time to request permission to use slip days. After you use up your slip days, a penalty of 5% will be applied for every 24 hours that an assignment is late, up until 7 days after the due date.  After 7 days, late assignments will NOT be accepted and the Dropbox will be closed.  If you have extenuating circumstances you may be granted an extension on an Assignment (see below under Policy Regarding Missed Tests and Assignments Due to Illness or other Extenuating Circumstances).

            Assignment Grades: If you have any questions or concerns regarding your marks or feedback on an assignment, you MUST communicate with the teaching assistant who marked your Assignment within 7 days of the posted mark.  If you still have a concern after corresponding with your TA, then you may request a re-grade from the instructor, but be advised that the assignment grade could also be adjusted lower.  10 days after assignment grades have been posted, it is too late to request a re-grade by the instructor.            

Term Tests (3 x 20% each, 60% total):  Your mastery of the material discussed in the lectures, assignments, and the required readings will be evaluated based on 3 in-class, non-cumulative Term Tests, each focusing on one of 3 Modules in the course.  The format of the tests will be 45 multiple-choice questions and you will have one hour to complete the test during the normal class time. Although the class times are one hour and 20 minutes, the first 10 min will be used to distribute the tests and get students settled in their locations.  The last 10 minutes after the tests end will be used to collect the test papers and bubble sheets.  Thus you will only be allowed exactly one hour to write the test (you will be allowed a few minutes to fill in your name and ID, etc on the bubble sheet and test before officially starting the test). Although each term test will evaluate you on material you learned in the current module (i.e. since the previous test), some of the themes are common to all three, such as critical periods in development, epigenetics, and developmental programming.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as researching health information, citing scholarly sources, paraphrasing, and other written communication skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the authorâs original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Policy Regarding Missed Tests and Assignments Due to Illness or other Extenuating Circumstances

From time to time, students will encounter extenuating circumstances such as significant illnesses or mental health challenges, ongoing medical conditions, unforeseen emergencies, a death of a loved one, or religious observances that prevent them from meeting academic obligations. The University is committed to assisting students who experience these events.  If you miss a course element (e.g., exam, due date of an assignment, in-class participation activity, etc) due to extenuating circumstances, please notify your course instructor by email as soon as you can, preferably within 24 hours of the missed course element. In addition, please follow the appropriate step below to document your absence.  

Students have the following options for declaring an absence in Quest during the Winter 2025 term:

  1. Short-term Absence (once/term) - an absence of 2 calendar days or less during the formal lecture period. The absence can be for any reason that prevents a student from meeting their academic obligations. No accompanying documentation is requested or required. Only one short-term absence can be self-declared per academic term.  Students must notify their instructor(s) no later than 24 hours after the missed assessment(s). If possible, students should contact the instructor prior to the expected missed assessment(s). 
  2. COVID-19-related Absence (once/term) - an absence of 10 days or less due to COVID-19 related illness (including vaccination side-effects) or a requirement to self-isolate. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in online course elements may be reasonable. Students can self-declare one Covid-19-related absence in an academic term.  It is the studentâs responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence to discuss arrangements for missed course components.
  3. Absence due to a religious/creed-related observance - please use the Student Absence Declaration link in Quest. Students are still required to consult with their instructor(s) within 2 weeks of the announced due date or scheduled exam. Documentation is not required.
  4.   5. If the studentâs absence is due to extenuating circumstances other than illness (e.g., funeral, varsity sports, etc. ), they should use the  Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form.

Land Acknowledgement:

I acknowledge that I live and work on the traditional territory of the Neutral, Anishinaabeg, and Haudenosaunee peoples. The University of Waterloo is situated on the Haldimand Tract, the land granted to the Six Nations that includes six miles on each side of the Grand River.

University Policy

Academic integrity: In order to maintain a culture of academic integrity, members of the University of Waterloo community are expected to promote honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.]

Grievance: A student who believes that a decision affecting some aspect of their university life has been unfair or unreasonable may have grounds for initiating a grievance. Read Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances, Section 4. When in doubt, please be certain to contact the departmentâs administrative assistant who will provide further assistance.

Discipline: A student is expected to know what constitutes academic integrity to avoid committing an academic offence, and to take responsibility for their actions. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.] A student who is unsure whether an action constitutes an offence, or who needs help in learning how to avoid offences (e.g., plagiarism, cheating) or about ârulesâ for group work/collaboration should seek guidance from the course instructor, academic advisor, or the undergraduate associate dean. For information on categories of offences and types of penalties, students should refer to Policy 71, Student Discipline. For typical penalties, check Guidelines for the Assessment of Penalties.

Appeals: A decision made or penalty imposed under Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances (other than a petition) or Policy 71, Student Discipline may be appealed if there is a ground. A student who believes they have a ground for an appeal should refer to Policy 72, Student Appeals.

Note for students with disabilities: AccessAbility Services, located in Needles Hall, Room 1401, collaborates with all academic departments to arrange appropriate accommodations for students with disabilities without compromising the academic integrity of the curriculum. If you require academic accommodations to lessen the impact of your disability, please register with AccessAbility Services at the beginning of each academic term.

Course Description

AMATH 361:

Stress and strain tensors; analysis of stress and strain. Lagrangian and Eulerian methods for describing flow. Equations of continuity, motion and energy, constitutive equations. Navier-Stokes equation. Basic equations of elasticity. Various applications.

View requirements for AMATH 361

Continuum mechanics is the study of motion and forces in solids and fluids. These materials are treated as continua and described in the language of vector calculus. We will derive the basic partial differential equations for solid and fluid mechanics and apply them to a variety of physical problems. This course provides a strong basis for further study in a number of different areas of application, including meteorology, oceanography, astrophysics, building ventilation, aerodynamics, seismology, structural engineering, etc.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
develop proficiency in using Cartesian tensors
model fluids at rest: pressure and hydrostatic balance
model fluids in motion: ideal, incompressible and compressible flows
model Newtonian fluids subject to viscosity
model rotating fluids
model solids at rest: stress and strain
model a magnetohydrodynamic fluid

Lectures:  Lectures will be in person.  The lectures are a great learning opportunity and I encourage all of you to attend lectures and ask questions to allow you to learn the course material most easily.

Discussions: Group discussions should be posted on piazza at this link here.  But you are also welcome to email the instructor for whatever question you might have about the course. [ francis.poulin@uwaterloo.ca ]

Assignments: There will be 5 assignments (see schedule above) and the lowest mark will be dropped. Assignments are a crucial element of this course – they will help you to work through, practice, and apply the material discussed in the lectures. They are an essential part of learning the material and preparing for the tests and exam. Assignments are due on Thursdays on Crowdmark by 5:00 pm. You can submit assignments up to 72 hours late without penality.

Collaboration: I encourage you to discuss the assignment problems with your classmates. However, since these are individually marked assignments, I expect you to write up your solutions independently. Please include an acknowledgements section in your assignment that lists the names of everyone you discussed the assignment with. If you consult any references other than the textbook and lecture notes, please cite them in your assignments. Copying solutions from any source, including your classmates, and/or presenting someone else’s work as your own is a serious academic offence and all suspected cases will be reported to the Associate Dean for Undergraduate Studies.

Policy on late/missed assignments: You are expected to complete the assignments on time but if you need an extension please ask me and I will consider each situation on a case by case basis. 

Midterm test: There will be one hour midterm test to be held in class time on February 24th. 

Final examination: To be scheduled during the official exam period. Remember that travel plans are not considered acceptable grounds for granting an alternative examination time. Check the final exam schedule (usually available by mid-February) before making travel plans!

Practice problems: There are lots of practice problems at the end of each chapter in the course notes. Some of these problems will be included in the assignments. You should attempt as many of the remaining problems as possible - they are an excellent way to learn and practice the course material.

Pronouns: We will gladly refer to you by your chosen name and correct pronouns. Please consider indicating your pronouns in Quest and your chosen name in WatIAM.

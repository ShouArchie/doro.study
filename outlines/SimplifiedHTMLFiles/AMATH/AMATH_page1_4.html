Course Description

AMATH 242 / CS 371:

A rigorous introduction to the field of computational mathematics. The focus is on the interplay between continuous models and their solution via discrete processes. Topics include pitfalls in computation, solution of linear systems, interpolation, discrete Fourier transforms, and numerical integration. Applications are used as motivation.

AMATH 242: View requirements for AMATH 242

CS 371: View requirements for CS 371

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Explain the shortcomings of finite precision arithmetic
Analyze problems in terms of conditioning, and algorithms in terms of stability
Know some common and basic algorithms in computational mathematics, e.g. Numerical solution of linear systems, Numerical Integration, etc.
Apply these algorithms to solve problems from various domains

Student Assessment

Component Value
Assignments 40%
Midterm 30%
Final exam 30%

The course grade will be based on 4 assignments,  a midterm examination and a final examination. 

Assignments have two components: theoretical and computational. These components will have more or less the same weight, but not exactly equal. Theoretical questions will measure the learning outcomes related to the analytical aspects of the topics while computational questions are more focused on application and programming. The preferred programming language for this course is MATLAB (or its open source alternatives, Octave, SciLab, ...). While Python and Julia are also acceptable, they are not suggested. If you decide to use Python or Julia, we may not be able to give proper feedback -as some tools available in MATLAB are not available/accessible in these languages- and it is your responsibility to translate any template provided to whichever language you are using. Other programming languages will not be accepted. Students are expected to provide well commented code with the expectation that what a code is doing can be well understood (there will be a small number of marks for each coding programming question set aside for this). 

Assignments will be posted on LEARN and Crowdmark approximately 7 days before the due dates. All assignments must be submitted via Crowdmark before the due date. Any MATLAB codes (.m files) should also be included in your Crowdmark submission (you can take picture of your code). 

Most of the marks for the computational problems will be given for the description of the algorithm and the explanation of results and/or outputs. Submitting "raw code" will earn very few marks. Figures and graphs should be carefully created to present the data and conclusions in an effective and clear manner. Poor presentation of your work will result in an equivalently poor mark. In all cases, we expect you to explain your algorithm, and articulate your observations in detail. You are expected to submit your own code with documentation. MATLAB has good plotting features, make use of them. Create figures, when necessary, to include in your assignments.

Late submissions will be accepted up to 1 hour after the due date. There will be %100 lateness penalty for late submissions (more than 1 hour). If you submit an assignment late, e-mail the instructor so that you don't get an automatic zero.

The midterm is tentatively scheduled for Feb 13 6-8pm.

The final exam will be in the same format as the midterm exam. Time and date will be announced later.

All assessments are strictly individual work. Submitting anything that is not your own work will be processed according to the academic integrity rules (see below).

Requests related to the missed materials are handled by the instructor on a case-by-case basis, except when they fall under the Faculty of Mathematics guidelines (see https://uwaterloo.ca/math/accommodations). Missing the final exam will require documentation and may result in an INC grade at the discretion of the instructor. To be considered for INC, you must have passed the midterm and the assignments. In addition, you must both contact the instructor and submit a VIF within 48 hours of the examination. Otherwise, a grade of DNW, with an associated mark of 32, will be assigned.

Student responsibilities are as follows:

  • The student is expected to be familiar with the University policies, the course rules and any assessment specific guidelines. 
  • If there are circumstances which may prevent successful completion of coursework, the student is expected to inform the instructor in a timely manner, which generally means ahead of time or during the assessment. In some cases, contacting the instructor a short time after the deadline is acceptable.

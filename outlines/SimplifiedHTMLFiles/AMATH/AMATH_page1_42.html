Course Description

AMATH 475 / PHYS 476:

Tensor analysis. Curved space-time and the Einstein field equations. The Schwarzschild solution and applications. The Friedmann-Robertson-Walker cosmological models.

AMATH 475: View requirements for AMATH 475

PHYS 476: View requirements for PHYS 476

AMATH 675:

Tensor analysis. Curved space-time and the Einstein field equations. The Schwarzschild solution and applications. The Friedmann-Robertson-Walker cosmological models. (Held with AMATH 475. Students in AMATH 675 will be expected to meet some additional learning objectives.)

View requirements for AMATH 675

The goal of the course is to give an introduction to Einstein's theory of general relativity, starting form the symmetries of spacetime and culminating in a discussion of several simple predictions of the theory (Black holes, Comsology, etc). The differential geometry and tensor analysis that is needed to formulate the theory will be developed in the first part of the course.

Tentative Course Schedule

Block 0: Foundations of Tensor Analysis

• Introduction to index notation.

• Covariance and contravariance.

• Scalars, vectors, one-forms, and tensors.

Block 1: Review of Special Relativity

• Postulates of Special Relativity.

• Covariant formulation of Special Relativity.

• The Lorentz group and its properties.

• Applications.

Block 2: Introduction to Differential Geometry

• Differentiable manifolds: functions, curves, vectors, n-forms, and tensors.

• Tensor operations.

• Lie derivative, pull-backs, and push-forwards.

• Diffeomorphisms and their properties.

• Parallel transport and covariant derivatives.

• Curvature: geometric interpretation and formalism.

• Metric manifolds and pseudo-Riemannian manifolds.

• Isometries and Killing vector fields.

Block 3: General Relativity

• Postulates of General Relativity.

• The stress-energy tensor and energy conditions.

• The Schwarzschild metric and its properties.

• Relativistic orbital mechanics: precession of periapsis (e.g., Mercury).

• Gravitational deflection of light.

• Black holes: basic theory and applications.

• The cosmological principle and its implications.

• Derivation of the Friedmann equations.

Self-Declared Absence and VIF policy

The weight of any missed assignment deadline due to a justified cause (e.g., self-declared absence or Verified Illness Form [VIF]) will automatically be transferred to the next in-person assessment. Specifically:

• If you miss the deadlines for Assignments 1, 2, or 3 due to a justified cause, their weight will be added to the midterm.

• If you miss the deadlines for Assignments 4, 5, or 6 due to a justified cause, their weight will be added to the final exam.

In the event of a missed midterm due to a justified cause, its weight will also be transferred to the final exam. If the final exam is missed due to a justified cause, an INC (Incomplete) grade will be assigned.

Please be proactive in contacting the TAs if you find yourself in any of these situations so they can properly document your absence and ensure the weight adjustment is applied.

Acknoledgements and collaboration policy

For assignments, you are allowed to consult any materials and collaborate with others. However, you must be able to independently reproduce all the content of your submission without assistance and should be able to defend your solutions when requested by the instructor or the TAs. If you receive help in solving an assignment, you are required to include an Acknowledgements Section, detailing:

• Who assisted you and in what capacity.

• Any textbooks, sources, or materials you consulted, including links where applicable.

Failure to acknowledge the use of external materials, previous year solutions, or assistance from anyone currently enrolled in or who has previously taken the course will be flagged and reported for an academic integrity evaluation, in accordance with Policy 71.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

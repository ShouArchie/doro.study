Course Description

HLTH 443:

This course introduces the basic methods for communicable disease epidemiology and discusses important infectious diseases, including their history, ecology and current public health significance. The course focuses on epidemiological methods in infectious disease research, prevention, and control, such as outbreak investigations, disease surveillance, case-control and cohort studies, laboratory tools, molecular epidemiology, dynamics of transmission, and assessment of vaccine field effectiveness. A range of communicable diseases will be discussed including respiratory infections, diarrheal diseases, sexually transmitted diseases, bloodborne diseases, and vector-borne diseases.

View requirements for HLTH 443

Territorial Acknowledgement

This course – about how communicable diseases spread and impact populations – is happening on the land traditionally cared for Indigenous Peoples, in a country whose historical and ongoing colonization, oppressive laws, and broken treaties have directly determined who is more protected from, and who is more vulnerable to, communicable diseases. For example, who is well protected from waterborne diseases because they have clean, potable water available for drinking and hand washing, and who is not? In this course, we recognize that colonialism is a determinant of health, and acknowledge the ways it has shaped, and is embedded in, the discipline of epidemiology.  

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, you should be able to:
1. Describe the epidemiology of important infectious diseases, as well as their history, ecology, and public health significance
2. Identify important factors (current and future, proximal and distal) that impact disease risk and transmission
3. Apply infectious disease models and their concepts, to select options for disease prevention and control
4. Design surveillance programs and epidemiology studies, to investigate various infectious diseases, including to identify risk/protective factors, and evaluate effectiveness of public health interventions
5. (Co-)Investigate outbreaks of infectious diseases

Tentative Course Schedule

Course Structure

This course uses a hypothetical ongoing outbreak, to mimic how infectious disease epidemiologists operate in practice and to scaffold the concepts and activities of the course. We will tackle the outbreak in ~10 minute segments each week. You will be invited to use the outbreak to explore important infectious disease concepts, which you will then work through and apply, using a combination of self-study and class discussion/exercises. 

Please see the Detailed Schedule and Outbreak Workbook in LEARN, for the concepts and outbreak segment we will tackle each week.  Please note that we will use class time to explore concepts that are more complex and for which you have questions, and to integrate concepts and apply them to various scenarios.  This means there will be many concepts you will need to study on your own outside of class.  

Connection Between HLTH 443 and HLTH 445

HLTH 445 ("Pandemic Preparedness") is also happening this term with Dr. Zahid Butt. Dr. Butt and I see great value in connecting the two classes:

  • In HLTH 443, you and your peers have the opportunity to become class experts on a large range of pathogens, some of which have pandemic potential. Collectively, your Wanted Posters will showcase this information, and you will have the chance to share your posters with HLTH 445 students, so they can appreciate a fuller range of pathogens than HLTH 445 has time to cover. 
  • In HLTH 445, students will create things like pandemic plans and case studies for real pandemics. Although HLTH 443 uses a fictional pandemic to scaffold course concepts, we don't delve into pandemics in detail. Thus, towards the end of March, you will have the opportunity to explore items created by HLTH 445 students, and you will be asked to consider them in your Outbreak Reflection. Specific details of what to explore, and how, will be announced during the term.

If you are taking both classes, and have ideas about how to connect them, feel free to email me. Dr. Butt and I are always interested in fun or novel ways to make connections between the two courses.

Rules for Group Work

For the 'Pathogen Project Part 1 - Wanted Poster', you can choose to work individually or in self-selected pairs. If you work as a pair, both of you will receive the same grade.  

For the 'Pathogen Project Part 2 - Transmission Synthesis', you will work with your classmates on some preparatory work, but the assessment itself must be done individually.   

For the 'Outbreak Reflection', you will work with your classmates to complete aspects of the Outbreak Workbook in class, but the assessment itself must be done individually.

For the mid-term and final examinations, you can work with your classmates when mocking up answers to the provided questions, but any materials you bring to the open book examinations must be completely and 100% your own creation, and be all your own thoughts and content. 

Any other collaboration or group work on assessments is not allowed.  If you have questions about specific activities or situations, please email me before submitting the assessment.

Handing in and Return of Assessments

Please submit the Pathogen Project (Parts 1 and 2) and the Outbreak Reflection electronically via the LEARN Dropboxes.  The Mid-Term and Final Examinations will be written in-person during scheduled time slots, outside of class time. Please see LEARN for due dates and details.  

Once assessments are graded, grades and feedback will be available in LEARN. The goal is to return assessments within two weeks after their due date, although it may be longer depending on the nature of the submissions, to ensure you are given sufficient feedback to apply to remaining course assessments.

Caring for ourselves

This is a course about infectious diseases and their impacts, as we come out of a pandemic, at a time of year when people often get sick. This affords us great opportunities to learn but can also generate great stress. Your health and wellbeing are top priority.  Please take care of your physical and mental health, and bring the utmost respect to your interactions with your peers and with me. Assume everyone is doing their best. Be kind.  Please see LEARN for more details about where to access University supports. 

Mutual respect and the ability to listen to others

The ability to engage with and value diverse perspectives is an important skill for epidemiologists; for example, it allows us to collect valid data and accurately interpret results. 

In this class, we will debate concepts and how they relate to communicable diseases in populations, including concepts related to human behaviours that can be deeply rooted in culture, ethnicity, religion, gender, and other factors. We won't debate someone's humanity. Please approach our discussions with respect and curiosity. Sexist, racist, homophobic, transphobic or any other discriminatory remarks are not acceptable. We are all learning and have different lived experiences, so if I ever miss the mark please let me know. I want this class to be an open and welcoming place for us all to learn and grow.

Each of us has our own intersections and spaces of privilege, that shape how we experience the world, including this class. I will talk about my own positionally further in class. Late and Missed Assessments, and Accommodations 

Please see LEARN for details about accommodations, short-term, COVID-19-related, and other types of absences, and the processes to follow in these situations.

For course assessments that are not exams, in the absence of approved accommodations or considerations, late submissions will be penalized by 5% of the assessment value per 24-hour period, to a maximum of 5 calendar days, including weekends and holidays. The late penalty begins after the start of the class in which the assessment is due. After the 5 day maximum, submissions will not be accepted and a grade of ‘zero’ will be given for that assessment. In most circumstances, approved accommodations or considerations will be an extension to the deadline by a number of days proportional to the absence, for example, by two days for a short-term absence, or the next day for a religious observance.

In the event of a missed mid-term examination, and in the absence of approved accommodations or considerations, the value of your mid-term (30%) will be assigned to your final exam (which will become worth 60%). For the mid-term, in most circumstances approved accommodations or considerations will either be: writing a make-up mid-term examination during the week of March 10 to 14, at a time set by the instructor following University regulations (e.g., the evening of Friday the 14th); or having the value of your mid-term (30%) assigned to your final exam (which will become worth 60%).

In the event of a missed final examination, in most situations you will need to write a make-up final exam during the registrar-scheduled make-up exam days.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement for some, but not all, assessments. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and are outlined on assessment instructions and grading rubrics. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

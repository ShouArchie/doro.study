Course Description

HLTH 102:

This course expands upon the general concepts introduced in HLTH101 while extending the discussion to include issues such as: additional barriers to health; disease prevention; and methods used to plan, monitor, and improve individual and population health. Discussion will include when various interventions are justified and why, and where and when combinations of policies, treatments, education, and other approaches are necessary. The health of Canadians relative to that of people in other countries, as well as the inequalities that exist in health status within Canada and around the globe will be examined. Case examples will be used to illustrate points.

View requirements for HLTH 102

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Discuss the definitions of health, wellness, health behaviour and population health, and indicate how they are inter-related;
Understand and define fundamental concepts underlying public health practice;
Analyze the relationships between determinants of health and health status, using core public health principles;
Examine and apply theories, concepts and models of health promotion in addressing determinants of health;
Understand the major modifiable determinants of chronic disease in Canada and the impact that those chronic diseases have on individual and population health;
Describe how the environments and group characteristics can affect the health of individuals and populations; and,
Understand the breadth of health interventions and the difference between clinical interventions, individual behaviour change interventions, and population-level interventions.

Required Reading

On average, there will be ~1-2 readings per week. Readings will be primarily from scholarly journals and technical and community reports (there is no mandatory textbook for this class). The course reading list on Learn has direct links to online readings or will list the reference that is searchable through UWaterloo library. Readings are considered preparatory, and hence required. Exams

There will be a mid-term and final exam in this course that will include multiple choice and/or true/false questions. These exams are designed to assess comprehension and understanding of the core concepts covered for each course topic, which include both lecture and reading materials. 

Tutorial Assignments

Students are expected to participate in tutorial discussions and complete tutorial assignments. The assignments are explained in tutorial sessions and their respective due dates are listed in the tutorial schedule section above. All tutorial assignments are individual work. If assignments handed in for credit are deemed to be the result of excessive collaboration, a mark of zero will be awarded.

There are 4 tutorial assignments for this course, however only the top three assignment grades will count towards a student’s final grade. This allows for flexibility and/or extenuating circumstances that may prevent a student from submitting an assignment during a particular week. For example, students who complete all four assignments will have their lowest assignment grade dropped. For students who miss an assignment or choose not to complete 1 out of the 4 assignments, the missed assignment will automatically be dropped. 

Late Tutorial Assignments without Accommodations, Self-Declaration of Illness or Notice

All tutorial assignments are due at 9:30am on the day of your scheduled tutorial session on the dates indicated in the course outline. Assignments are to be submitted to the dropbox via Waterloo Learn.

  1. Late assignments will be penalized by 10% of the assignment total mark per 24-hour period to a maximum of 5 days. The late penalty begins immediately after 9:31am on the day of your scheduled tutorial session when the assignment is due.
  2. After the 5-day maximum assignments will not be accepted and a grade of ‘zero’ will be recorded for the assignment.

Elective arrangements (e.g. travel plans) or heavy workloads are not considered acceptable grounds for variance.

Tutorial Assignment Grading  

Students are required to check Learn regularly for their marks. If there is a concern over a grade, or how it was obtained, students are to seek out their tutorial TA first, within 7 days of receiving feedback. After that time, you may not request a re-grade. Students who believe they provided valid answers that have not been addressed by the TA are invited to make a case to the Lead TA (hlth102@uwaterloo.ca) after an initial discussion with their own TA. Please note that upon appeal, the Lead TA reserves the right to re-grade any portion of the submitted material. Hence, it is possible that an appeal may result in a lower grade. 

Missed Exams

Mid-term examination: In most circumstances, approved accommodations or considerations will either be: writing a make-up mid-term examination during the week of March 3 to 7 (at a time set by the instructor following University regulations); or having the value of your mid-term (25%) assigned to your final exam (which will become worth 60%). In the absence of approved accommodations or considerations, the value of your mid-term (25%) will be assigned to your final exam (which will become worth 60%). 

Final examination: In most situations, you will need to write a make-up final exam during the registrar-scheduled make-up exam days.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Intellectual Property

Be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of the instructor, teaching assistant, and/or the University of Waterloo. Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof).
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g.,PowerPoint slides).
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or teaching assistant or used by the instructor or teaching assistant with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, teaching assistant and/or University of Waterloo permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g. to an online repository).  

Permission from an instructor, teaching assistant or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years. In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights and academic integrity.

Accommodations for Late Assignments, Missed Examinations or Extenuating Circumstances

Absences declared through Quest 

Religious or creed-related absence

Students can submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration in Quest. This allows students to register the dates of potential conflict with coursework. As the dates of important religious observances are generally known well in advance, please consult with the lead TA (hlth102@uwaterloo.ca) within two weeks of the announcement of the due date or scheduled examination date for which academic accommodation is being sought. 

Short-term absence (once/term) 

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason (no documentation required). A short-term absence covers up to two calendar days during the formal lecture period. It is the student’s responsibility to contact the lead TA (hlth102@uwaterloo.ca) within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

COVID-19-related absence (once/term) 

Students can self-declare an absence of up to 10 days due to COVID-19-related illness. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify the lead TA of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact the lead TA (hlth102@uwaterloo.ca) within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Absences Requiring Documentation 

Absence due to illness  

When students are unable to meet academic obligations due to illness, they are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their Verification of Illness Form (VIF) to the site. The University of Waterloo Verification of Illness Form (VIF) is normally the only medical documentation accepted to support requests for academic consideration. Once approved, the student and the instructor will receive a confirmation email of the student’s absence. Course instructors will determine how missed course components are accommodated. 

Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation directly to their instructors, teaching assistants, or advisors. To request and arrange accommodation, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the lead TA (hlth102@uwaterloo.ca) within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their health condition. 

Absence due to extenuating circumstances

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (bereavement, family illness, varsity sports, etc.), students are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their official documentation (e.g., plane tickets, court documents, signed letter from varsity team coach, etc.) to the site. To request and arrange accommodations it is the student’s responsibility to contact the lead TA (hlth102@uwaterloo.ca) within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their circumstances.

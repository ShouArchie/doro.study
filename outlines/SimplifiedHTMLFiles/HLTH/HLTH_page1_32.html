Course Description

HLTH 609:

Approaches to strategic planning and organizational design, key concepts of human resource management in achieving the strategic objectives of public health organizations, and the fundamentals of operation planning, budgeting, financial management and project management. Prerequisite PHS 601.

View requirements for HLTH 609

HLTH609, "Management and Administration of Public Health Services," is a course designed to equip Master of Public Health students with the knowledge and practical skills necessary to lead and manage public health organizations effectively. Through case-based learning and experiential methods, students will explore critical concepts in strategy development, people management, financial decision-making, governance, and logistics. The course emphasizes identifying root cause problems and crafting actionable solutions tailored to public health settings. With a focus on collaborative teamwork and real-world scenarios, students will develop leadership capabilities, decision-making strategies, and an understanding of the complex interplay between management practices and public health outcomes. This course is aligned with CEPH competencies to ensure graduates are prepared for impactful careers in public health leadership.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Identify root cause of management and leadership challenges within public health organizations.
Design actionable and evidence-based strategy to address complex problems in public health settings.
Evaluate and interpret financial data to inform decision-making in public health programs.
Demonstrate effective use of management controls to enhance organizational performance and accountability.
Apply leadership principles to foster collaboration, motivate teams, and drive organizational change.
Differentiate between governance and operational responsibilities within public health organizations.
Develop operational strategies to optimize resource allocation and service delivery in public health settings.
Organize and present information effectively to support decision-making and stakeholder engagement.
Analyze case studies to develop practical solutions using contemporary management theories and tools.
Reflect on personal learning experiences to identify areas for professional growth and application.

The Course Rhythm: modules 2-5

There is a repetitive rhythm to most of the modules (modules 2-5) in the course:

On the first Monday of a module:

  • The case becomes available.
  • Reflections on the previous module are due.

On the first Tuesday of a module:

  • A short live class (~30-60 minutes) is held in the evening. The class is recorded for those who are unable to attend so that they can watch later. 

On the first Saturday of a module:

  • Your root cause problem statement is due. (Completed individually.)

On the second Friday of a module:

  • Your team’s case solution is due. The module ends.

This pattern repeats starting on the next Monday. The result is you usually get a short Sat/Sun “break” between modules.  Please note, Modules 1 and 6 are only one week in length and do not contain the same set of deliverables as Modules 2 through 5.  While Module 7 is two weeks, it only requires a team case solution. 

Deliverables are always due at midnight EST. 

LEARN is stern with cutoff times and therefore give yourself some slack. If you try to submit at 23h59 EST you can easily find LEARN denying the submission should the clock strike midnight while the upload is happening. Don’t wait until the very last minute and submit well before the deadline to avoid any issues.

Student Assessment

Root Cause Problem Statements (25%) 

  • 5 individual submissions. 

Modules 2 through 6 begin with reading a case that you download from HBR. The case presents a scenario that usually concludes with a critical inci­dent or the need to make a decision.  Your task is to identify the underlying root cause problem that has placed the organization in the predi­cament it faces and to explain why you believe this is the root cause problem using evidence from the case to support your reasoning. 

A handout titled Crafting a Root Cause Problem Statement can be found under CONTENT >> BACKGROUND MATER­IALS.  Be sure to review this before writing your first root cause problem statement. When completed, upload your root cause problem statements to your TA’s dropbox. 

Please provide a Microsoft Word file (No PDF please) in the drop box and follow the file naming con­vention described in the Crafting a Root Cause Problem Statement handout. 

Case Solutions (30%)

  • 5 group submissions

Your group spends the second week of most modules addressing the root cause problem in the case[1] with a recommended course of action that would solve the problem.

Your solution should be prescriptive and action oriented. What does the organization need to do?  When, why and how do they need to take these steps? It is important to support your recom­mended solution with reasoning (evidence) drawn from the case or con­temporary manage­ment think­ing/lit­erature. 

A handout named Crafting a Case Solution can be found under CONTENT >> BACKGROUND MATER­­IALS.  Read this handout before tackling your first group case solution. 

When completed, upload your case solution to your group’s dropbox.  Please provide a Microsoft Word file (No PDF please) and follow the file naming con­v­ention described on the Crafting a Case Solution handout. 

The MyCase Activity (25%)

  • 1 individual submission

NOTE: There is a MyCase deliverable that occurs during Module 4.  Read the Module 7 page in LEARN at the start of the term so you know what to do.

For the MyCase activity you have a chance to apply the theories and concepts you’ve been reviewing in class to a management challenge you are exper­iencing, have experienced, or hope never to experience, by writing your own case study. 

Details to guide you in writing your MyCase can be found in LEARN under Module 7. Wait until you have read and solved a few cases before starting to create your MyCase. This is because there is a style to cases that you will want to incorporate into your MyCase. However, it is never too early to start thinking about a sticky management or leadership problem that could form the nucleus of your MyCase and it is never too early to go and read the directions under Module 7, so you know what is expected.  Myself and the TA’s will select one case from all those submitted and, with the permission of the selected MyCase, it will then be distributed to the class for Module 7. 

If your MyCase is selected as the case for Module 7, you automatically get 100% for this deliverable. 

Please note that there is no individual root cause problem state­ment submission required in Module 7. However, your group will need to decide on what the root cause problem is before tackling preparing your solution.

There are some special rules that will apply so that your group doesn’t have an unfair advantage when crafting their recommended solution if you happen to have the student who wrote the winning My­Case in your group.  More to follow on this once the MyCase selection is made for that group.

Submit your MyCase in Microsoft Word in order so that I can work with you to edit your case if it is selected as the MyCase for the class.

Module Reflections (20%)

  • 5 individual submissions

After each module you are asked to share what you thought were the key learnings from the module and reflect on practical applications you might see for those learnings in your career.

The Module Reflec­tions task appear under QUIZZES in LEARN.  ‘Reflections’ are not a ‘quiz.’ The Quiz function is simply an easy way to track who has completed the activity and provides an efficient way to provide feedback. 

Each reflection will be read and assessed. There are no “right” or “wrong” answers for the Module Reflection activity.  However, feedback will be provided if reflections seem superficial or aren’t related to the issues being explored. 

Reflections for a module must be submitted by midnight on the Monday of the following module. Your Reflection should be succinct. Just a few sentences in response to the questions I ask you to reflect upon is usually sufficient. 

A comment on how assessment works in HLTH609

The objectives of the written submissions are to help you: 

a)  perfect the technique of finding root cause problems.

b)  identify effective solutions to perplexing management problems; and 

c)  present information effectively for con­sumption by man­agers. 

Some in the class might discover in their first submission that they identified a symptom of the problem instead of the root cause problem. Or maybe the position taken wasn’t supported by evidence (e.g., there was no explicit linking between the root cause problem chosen and the facts in the case.) These factors would impact the score awarded.   Specific coaching will be provided to help with your next submission. Incorporate the ideas provided. In previous years, with incorporation of feedback provided, increased scores are often observed and thus, if your scores are consistently low, or are trending downwards, please reach out so we can review what is happening and can provide some addi­tional coaching.

Writing Style, Page Layout, Submission Length and References

There is no “academic writing” required in this course, such as is required for a journal article, essay, or thesis that you likely have had to accomplish in other MPH courses. Instead, your written sub­missions should employ a business writing style. Grammatically correct point form is fine when appropriate. Short, concise sentences are welcomed. 

You will also note that I don’t specify particular fonts or sizes, page margins, line spacing, page layouts, etc. for submissions. I will leave it to you (just as your employer will do) to make decisions regarding how you wish to present your recom­men­­dations in a professional manner. Presen­tation style is assessed and thus, if you choose a small font that is hard to read on a piece of paper, then this will be addressed as a part of the presentation part of the papers review. As such, please respect the page limits.

You can use any convention you wish when citing sources. (APA, Chicago, or even something you’ve created yourself, etc.) The only criteria is  that you provide sufficient information to be able to locate the source should we wish to do so. When referencing a fact from the case in a submission, you can simply say something like [Case, pg3].  (Title, author, etc. are not needed.)

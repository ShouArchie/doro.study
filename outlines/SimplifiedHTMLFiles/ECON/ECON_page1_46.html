Course Description

ECON 322:

This course covers the core topics needed to estimate linear models using ordinary least squares and to interpret estimates for cross-sectional data. Students will learn to interpret the coefficients of linear models for continuous and discrete regressors, to conduct reliable inference for different specifications of the error term, and to determine which model is the most suitable among the class of linear models. Students will be introduced to a statistical software package and will be required to complete regular computer-based assignments throughout the course using economic data.

View requirements for ECON 322

Students will be introduced to a statistical software package and will be required to complete regular computer-based assignments throughout the course that either: (i)  simulate the statistical distribution of the least squares estimator under an assumed model; or (ii) estimate the parameters of an assumed model using a sample of data from the real world.

The statistical software package used in this course is R. It runs on Windows, Mac or Linux and can be downloaded for free at https://www.r-project.org/. It is also available in any Arts computer labs. You will be trained to use it in class and in tutorial sessions. If you have a computer, you should go to the above link and install R before the first tutorial session. It is also recommended, but not required, to install R-Studio, which is also available for free at https://www.rstudio.com/. It is an IDE (Integrated Development Environment) for R. It greatly facilitates the use of R.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Estimate linear models with R
Use R to produce different graphs to visualise the results and run some diagnostics.
Use markdown to create PDF documents that include text, R code and output.
Interpret the coefficients of a linear regression
Run hypothesis tests on the coefficients
Test if the model is correctly specified
Identify the limitations of regression models

Administrative Policy

Policy Regarding Assignments

All assignments must be produced individually. It is encouraged to work in groups, but the final documents uploaded to Learn must be your own with unique coding and/or interpretation. Any similarity in the coding or interpretation will be reported as a violation of academic integrity. The following is considered academic misconduct and will be reported to the Associate Dean of Arts:

  • Copying codes or interpretation from another student.
  • Give access to your answers to other students: If two copies are similar, both students are penalised even if only one of them copied.
  • Use codes from an online AI. This is very easy to verify, because it also recommend the same code. Anything that deviates from the codes provided in my note will raise suspicion. Because of this, I only accept codes from my notes.
  • Use  codes from any outside sources.

Note that you are allowed to copy and paste the codes from my notes. 

The marking scheme for assignments are

  • 0/10 if not submitted or have completed less than 50% of the questions.
  • 5/10 if  completed between 50% and 75% of the questions.
  • 7/10 if completed all questions, but I notice little effort answering them. This includes no effort in the interpretation (a few words or one or two short sentences when more is required), reproducing errors that were explained in previous assignments or not presenting the results in a well formatted document. We will explain what we mean by "well formatted" in class and tutorial sessions
  • 10/10 If completed all questions in a satisfactory way.

Nowhere I mention that the answers to the questions must be right. As long as I see effort trying to answer the questions, you get full mark. It is therefore useless to try to get answers from your classmates.  There is no benefit in terms of points, you learn nothing by doing it and it puts you at risk of violating academic integrity. 

If you submit your assignment late, a penalty per day will be applied to your mark. The penalty starts after being 1 hour late according to the following rule: -3/10 if 1 hour to 24 hours late, -6/10 if 24.001 to 48 hours late -10/10 if more than 48 hours late.

Accommodation for Missed Assessments

If you miss a midterm and have an approved accommodation, the weight of the midterm will be shifted to the final exam. 

All assignments must be submitted. Learning how to create reports using R is a mandatory component of the course. Therefore, you get 0 for any assignment you do not submit. If you cannot submit it on time due to illness or other extenuating circumstances and you have an approved accommodation, the deadline will be extended. 

If the solution is posted before your new approved deadline, you may be asked to do a different assignment. 

If the access to the drop box is conditional on obtaining a minimum grade in quizzes, you must complete this requirement before submitting the assignment. To avoid having to complete the quiz and assignment in a short time, I recommend you start working on them as soon as they are made available.

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. 

Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights.  For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.  In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online. The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as coding and interpreting results. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

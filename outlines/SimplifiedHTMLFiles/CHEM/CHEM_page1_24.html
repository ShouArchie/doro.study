Course Description

CHEM 265:

Nucleophilic addition and substitution at CO carbon. Aromaticity and simple MO theory of conjugated systems. Electrophilic and nucleophilic aromatic substitution reactions. Applications of spectroscopic techniques in organic chemistry.

View requirements for CHEM 265

Chemistry 265: Organic Chemistry II is a continuation of Chem264. The emphasis of this course is on the development of problem-solving skills in the context of structure elucidation, synthesis and mechanism of reaction of organic compounds. This course aims to reinforce the fundamental aspects of organic chemistry and show how these can be used to explain physical properties and reactivity patterns of organic molecules.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Learn to employ mass spectrometry (MS), infrared spectroscopy (IR) and nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR), for correctly identifying unknown organic compounds
To understand the mechanisms of common reactions of carbonyl containing compounds.
To understand the mechanisms of common reactions of aromatic compounds.
To understand the mechanisms of common reactions of amines.

Problem Set Policies:

Problem sets are submitted every other week according to the course schedule using Crowdmark. They are to be completed individually and are an important learning tool in this course. Students who put time and effort into the problem sets will see that effort pay off on the term tests and the final exam. Students are encouraged to seek out help from the instructor and TAs to maximize their understanding of the concepts covered on the problem sets. The problem sets are a good representation of the types of questions on the term tests and the final exam.

Students will be assessed on their top 5 problem sets, dropping the lowest problem set. If a student’s final exam grade is higher than their top 5 problem set grade, then the weight of the problem sets will be shifted to the final exam, increasing the students overall grade. This is done so that students have nothing to lose by making valid attempts at the problem sets. 

Students cannot use their problem set grade to pass this course. Students must pass at least one term test or the final exam to pass this course. Students that fail both term tests and the final exam will not pass this course.

Policy 71 is in affect for all graded components in this course, including problem sets. This does not mean students cannot discuss problem set questions with their peers, but it does mean students must write out their answers using their own words and own structures, drawn themselves. Here are some tips to ensuring you do not violate this policy:

  • Avoid writing out your answers, including drawing diagrams, during discussions with your peers; make it a habit of going to your own space to write/draw things out. This will help keep your language and drawings original, so they are flagged as a potential violation.
  • Never give your answers to another student “to look at”; giving your answers to someone violates Policy 71 as much as the person who copies your answers.
  • Do not “snip” drawings from online sources, either draw them yourself using a chemical drawing program or by hand.
  • Keep in mind that posting solutions online. 

Term Test Policies:

Students missing a term test must provide accepted documentation (i.e., VIF, self-declaration, etc.). Students who miss a term test and do not provide documentation will receive a zero on that term test and the weight will not be shifted to the final exam. Students who miss a term test with documentation will automatically be assessed using grading scheme 2. Students missing more than one term test for a valid reason will have the weight of the missed term tests automatically shifted to the final exam – increasing the weight of the final exam accordingly. 

There will be no make-up term tests or term tests offered outside of the scheduled date/time for any reason.

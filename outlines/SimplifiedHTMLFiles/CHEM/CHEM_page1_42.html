Course Description

CHEM 360:

Enolate alkylation, condensation, conjugate addition, and pericyclic reactions. FMO theory, organometallics, and transition metal-catalyzed C-C bond formation.

View requirements for CHEM 360

Chemistry 360 builds on the concepts and chemistry taught in CHEM 264 and CHEM 265. It is expected that CHEM 360 will help tie together and bring relevance to previous course material. By the end of this course, you should be familiar with basic aspects of organic reactivity, understand the mechanistic basis for synthetically important reactions, and be able to predict the outcome of many organic reactions. If you want to do well in CHEM 360, you should re-familiarize yourself with some of the material in your introductory organic chemistry courses.

More specifically, CHEM 360 will familiarize students with some of the modern synthetic methods for the formation of carbon-carbon sigma bonds in organic compounds. An array of anionic, cationic, pericyclic, and organometallic methods will be introduced as part of three general sections, and the factors governing the mechanistic, stereochemical and regiochemical course of these processes will be presented and discussed as appropriate. 

Section 1 will cover enols and enolates, condensations and a-substitutions of carbonyl compounds (acid- and base-catalyzed), and conjugate addition to a,b-unsaturated carbonyl compounds (acid- and base-catalyzed). 

Section 2  will cover pericyclic reactions (cycloadditions under thermal and photochemical conditions), with an emphasis on how an appreciation of frontier molecular orbitals allows for prediction of the reactivity of the systems (thermal and photochemical conditions), as well as relative reaction rates, regiochemistry and stereochemistry.  

Section 3  will present transition-metal catalysis and organometallic reactions, with a particular emphasis on Pd-catalyzed cross-coupling reactions.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1) To learn to use curve arrow formalism to “push” pairs of electrons in writing resonance forms and in sketching electron flow in chemical reactions.
2) To master the mechanism of formation of enols, enolates, silyl enol ethers, enamines and aza-enolates, to show how they act as nucleophiles with an array of electrophiles, namely protons, halogens, alkyl halides, acyl halides, aldehydes, ketones, esters, and a,b-unsaturated carbonyls, and to be able to predict the products of a-substitution reactions.
3) To master the fundamental condensation reactions, namely aldol, Claisen, Knoevenagel condensations, and Michael reactions (inter- and intramolecular). To be able to use the understanding of reactions mechanisms to predict the outcome of these chemical reactions (mechanistic reasoning to interpret pattern of reactivity).
4) To be able to identify intermediates versus transition states in an energy diagram for all fundamental condensation reactions and be able to determine the difference between kinetically and thermodynamically controlled reactions, identify equilibrium and reaction rates, and relate to the energy of the transitions state, starting material, and final products.
5) To be able to recognize the products of variations of the standard condensation reactions, such as crossed Claisen condensations, Knoevenagel condensations, malonic ester syntheses, acetoacetic ester syntheses, Robinson annulations, varying the ketones, esters, and acid derivatives.
6) To be able to use the understanding of reactions mechanisms to predict the outcome of chemical reactions (mechanistic reasoning to interpret pattern of reactivity).
7) To be able to identify neutral cyclic transitions states, involving bond-forming and bond-breaking events, in cycloaddition reactions, under thermal and photochemical conditions.
) To be able to use frontier molecular orbitals (FMO’s) to predict the reactivity of cycloadditions under thermal and photochemical conditions, as well as relative reaction rates, regiochemistry and stereochemistry (Endo rule).
9) To be able to identify the structure of s and p complexes, to understand and apply the 18-electron rule, and to be able to determine the oxidation state of transition metal complexes.
10) To master the organometallic mechanisms, namely oxidative addition, reductive elimination, ligand (alkene, alkyne, and CO) migration (migratory insertion), from metal to carbon, ligand exchange including transmetallation, and alkene activation.
11) To be able to generate catalytic cycles for common cross-coupling reactions, namely Suzuki, Stille, Heck, Sonogashira, and Buchwald-Hartwig (Buchwald-Hartwig amination), as well as variations involving carbon monoxide.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

Students are encouraged to reach out to campus supports if they need help with their coursework including: 

  • Student Success Office for help with skills like notetaking and time management
  • Writing and Communication Centre for assignments with writing or presentations
  • AccessAbility Services for documented accommodations
  • Library for research-based assignments

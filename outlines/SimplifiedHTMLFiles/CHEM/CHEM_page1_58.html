Course Description

CHEM 740:

Discussion of specialized topics related to the research interests of the members of the Centre. Special topics could include for example: theory of intermolecular forces; density matrices; configuration interaction; correlation energies of open and closed shell systems; kinetic theory and gas transport properties; theory of the chemical bond.

View requirements for CHEM 740

"Quantum Mechanics in Chemistry" is the title for this section of Chem400

In the Winter 2025 term the focus of the course will be electronic structure theory and a smaller segment on coupled electron-nuclear motion, developing theory beyond the Born Oppenheimer approximation. The course will cover a description of the basic electronic structure problem introducing an atomic orbital basis set, Slater determinants and the Slater-Condon rules. Then we will cover Hartree-Fock theory in fair detail and discuss density functional theory. We will move on to the technique of second quantization and discuss techniques to include electron correlation: Configuration Interaction, Coupled Cluster, methods for excited states. Second quantized methods and Wick’s theorem will be discussed. In another segment we will discuss vibronic coupling, introducing diabatic electronic states and simple model problems and introduce techniques to solve them using simple Python programs. It is my intention that students will write their own (simple) programs to do actual calculations. One assignment will be to write your own Hartree-Fock program. For students who have very little programming experience they can alternatively do a computational project using Gaussian or ORCA, or a literature review project.

 

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Have a good understanding of the electronic structure problem in Chemistry and how it connects to thermochemistry.
Have an in depth understanding of the Hartree-Fock method and the basic Hartree-Fock algorithm.
Have an understanding of the basic elements of Density Functional Theory.
Understand the concept of Full Configuration Interaction.
Be able to construct Hamiltonian matrix for small problems using Slater-Condon rules
Understand the use of the second quantization technique and occupation number representation.
Have introductory knowledge of Coupled Cluster methods for ground and excited states, and the concept of size-consistency.
Have introductory understanding of non-adiabatic nuclear dynamics going beyond the Born-Oppenheimer approximation.

Tentative Course Schedule

Week 1, 2: Chapter 1, Szabo and Ostlund plus exercises. Reading Material/Assignment. Q&A session only.

Week 1,2: General introduction to quantum mechanics and the main uses in chemistry / thermochemistry. Class notes 1. S&O,Chapter 2, section 2.1 

Week 3,4: Finite basis quantum chemistry (electronic structure). Symmetry, permutational symmetry and antisymmetry principle. Full Configuration Interaction. Derivation of Slater-Condon rules. Application of CI to atomic energy levels. Brief review of angular momentum theory. Class notes 2, 3, 4. Chapter 2 S&O, sections 2.2, 2.3

Week 5,6,7: Discussion and Derivation of Hartree-Fock method. Discussion of Hartree-Fock algorithm. Discussion of fundamental principles of Density Functional Theory. Class notes 5,6, S&O Chapter 3

Feb 17-21: Reading week

Weeks 8,9: Vibrational problems and ladder operators. DVR technique. Extension to multiple electronic surfaces. Vibronic models beyond Born-Oppenheimer. Class notes on Vibronic coupling.

Weeks 10,11,12: Second quantization and occupation number representation. Discussion of truncated Configuration interaction. Class notes 7. Selected topics: Coupled Cluster Theory, Wick's Theorem, (many-body) similarity transformations. CC for excited states. Class notes 8. Nooijen Research.

Student Assessment

Component Value
Problem sets 80%
Project, to be specified 20%

We will just use hand written assignments in this course, to be handed in, in class.

There will not be a midterm or final exams. The grading is bases on extensive assignments and a project. You are allowed to collaborate on assignments. Just make sure to write the final solutions out by yourself.

The project can vary for different students.

A. For graduate students it involves a programming assignment of a Hartree-Fock program in Python.

B. For students unfamiliar with the Gaussian program it will require the study of the thermochemistry of a simple reaction and a small research report, similar to a lab report. It is also possible to do a computational project using ORCA. Explore something new.

C. For others it can be a literature study of a computational or theoretical paper, with a written report (summary).

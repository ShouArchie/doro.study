Course Description

CHEM 267:

A continuation of the concepts of CHEM266, including material on amines, aromatics, enols and enolates, and nucleophilic addition and substitution at the carbonyl group. Introduction to nuclear magnetic resonance and infrared spectroscopy.

View requirements for CHEM 267

CHEM 267 is a continuation of the study of fundamental aspects of organic chemistry begun in CHEM 266. The emphasis of the course is on the development of problem solving skills in the context of reaction mechanisms, structure-reactivity relationships and structure elucidation. The course will require you to understand why some molecular features lead to certain reactivity patterns and use that knowledge to make sound predictions in different but related systems. Topics include a description of chemical and physical properties of carbonyl, aromatic, and nitrogen compounds and an introduction to spectroscopic methods for the identification of organic molecules.

 

 

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Predict the chemical stability and reactivity of reagents, transition states, intermediates and products
Describe and discriminate between different reaction mechanisms to predict reaction pathways.
Identify reagents and reaction conditions for the preparation of simple organic molecules.
Explain synthetic pathways and devise simple synthetic strategies for the preparation of organic compounds
Analyze IR and NMR spectra to identify simple organic compounds

Learning Methods

Active participation in the classroom. Approximately 60% of the class time is spent completing questions in groups. To prepare for the lecture, you must read sections of your course notes, watch the pre-lecture videos on Learn and make notes in your course notes. Actively engage in the videos. The lectures are taught in an "active-learning" way, emphasizing problem-solving and not the regurgitation of information. There is memorization, but you must memorize with understanding to do well in the Course. You must be able to see the differences and similarities between reactions.

Practice–practice–practice.

Solving problems–making connections–and recognizing patterns are key steps to succeeding in this Course. This Course requires constant effort; you cannot learn a course of this nature right before an exam. While the initial effort may seem overwhelming, you will better understand the course material in the long run and not have to "cram" before a term test or exam (and do much better in the Course!!)

Listen to the podcast: How to be great at just about anything: http://freakonomics.com/podcast/peak/

And the other podcasts on LEARN. They talk about how deliberate practice is used to improve performance. Just putting in the hours does not make you an expert. Also, people need to be guided through the hours of study. To bring talent to fruition, you need to absorb what has been done before to make the next step possible. Not all ideas and comments in the podcast can be used to learn organic chemistry, but I truly believe in deliberate practice or, in the case of learning a university course, active studying. Instead of just passively reading, you must actively participate in learning to teach yourself a subject with the guidance of an Instructor.

Administrative Policy

Absence on the day of a test or exam: If you miss any examination, either a term test or final, then you shall be given a grade of zero on that examination unless you missed the examination for valid reasons (e.g. illness). If you miss an examination because of illness, you must provide a completed Verification of Illness Form, which can be obtained from the Health Services web-site (http://uwaterloo.ca/health-services/student-medical-clinic/services). The completed form must be registered with the Science Undergraduate Office. The Undergraduate Office will keep the original form for their records and contact the course instructor. The completed form must specify the precise period of absence and include the date of the test/exam. If you become ill while away from the University and miss a test or an exam as a result of that illness, then you may supply a Doctor’s certificate covering the precise period of absence, provided the certificate includes a classification of the illness as slight, moderate, or severe. In all cases, the letter or form must include contact information of the person who signed the letter or form, and it must be registered with the Science Undergraduate Office.

 A Verification of Illness Form (VIF) or a doctor’s note does not necessarily excuse a student from missing a test or exam! The instructors and course coordinator will consider the information provided on the VIF or doctor’s note when deciding whether a student should be excused.Being excused from a test or exam is NOT automatic upon the presentation of suitable medical verification. Students should carefully consider the wisdom of missing a test or exam. If it is decided that a student has a legitimate excuse for missing a term test or an exam, the following rules apply.

  • Missing a test: The weight of the test will be transferred to the final exam. (There are no make-up term tests!) 
  • Missing the final exam: A makeup exam will be written in April, scheduled by the Science Undergraduate office. Missing the make-up exam will result in an INC which eventually converts to an FTC for CHEM 266 which is equivalent to 32% in the course.

Course Description

MTHEL 131:

Individual life insurance products. Introduction to property and casualty insurance. Introductory risk management, insurance pricing and valuation. Pension plan design.

View requirements for MTHEL 131

Situations involving risk are everywhere, including natural events such as storms and earthquakes, data breaches, corporate strategic initiatives, and everyday decision-making, where outcomes may be positive or negative. It is crucial to understand and effectively manage risk, rather than attempt to eliminate it. This course covers principles of risk management and insurance, including the identification of risk management problems, evaluation and implementation of various risk management tools such as insurance, emerging risks, and the various ways in which risk professionals, including actuaries, contribute. 

This course aims to help students (i) develop an appreciation for the role of insurance and risk management in society, and the variety of opportunities for risk professionals to contribute, (ii) contextualize the quantitative tools in their future coursework, and (iii) practice critical thinking and communication skills in a domain knowledge setting.

You need at least 60% in MTHEL 131 to declare Actuarial Science as a major.

You can find the Actuarial Science program and plan requirements in the Undergraduate Academic Calendar. To connect with an academic advisor, visit the department’s advising webpage.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
recognize the general types and characteristics of situations involving risk, how to identify them, and why they are important;
possess the skills and knowledge to measure and assess risk;
know the basics of how risk management tools work, including insurance, and their benefits/costs in any given situation;
apply the risk management process in a simple situation involving risk;
explain the value that risk management and insurance provides individuals, organizations, and society.

Active learning and lecture participation

Active participation is crucial for developing a strong understanding of course material and for applying concepts effectively.

This course aims to help you build the skills needed to apply what you learn. Lectures are designed to support this by providing:

  1. Opportunities to practice applying the material through individual and group polling questions, exercises, quizzes, and homework.
  2. Time to ask questions and clarify concepts.

While participation through in-class activities each week comprises 12% of the final grade, the main intent of these activities is to practice applying the concepts as a class (i.e., they are meant to be more formative than summative). Activities will be graded and returned within one week using Crowdmark, and the solutions will be discussed in class.

Exam coverage will be announced one week before each exam.

The material covered in each exam will be announced on LEARN one week before each exam. The final exam covers all topics introduced in the course.

Your exam seat will be assigned.

Your seat location will be assigned a few days before each exam and can be found on Odyssey.

You have one week to request an exam regrade.

All exams will be graded within one week, and the solutions will be discussed in class. You will have one week to file a regrading request. Instructions on how to file a request will be posted on LEARN with the return of each exam. The instructor/TA reserves the right to review the grading of your entire exam, and after the review, your resulting grade can be higher, lower, or stay the same.

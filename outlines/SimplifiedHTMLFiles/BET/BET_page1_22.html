Course Description

BET 460:

We negotiate every day with potential employers, coworkers, merchants, service providers, customers, and suppliers. Negotiation is the art and science of securing agreements between two or more independent parties. It is a craft that must hold co-operation and competition in creative tension, and even the most experienced negotiators often fall prey to common biases and errors in judgment. This course is highly experiential; therefore, students will practise, reflect, analyze, and practice again.

View requirements for BET 460

Knowing how to negotiate business deals effectively is an essential skill for entrepreneurs. They must initiate deals with various parties—from potential co-founders or employees to clients, investors, suppliers, corporate partners, and other external stakeholders.  Yet, they often find themselves at a disadvantage and unprepared to close these deals. But whether you are an existing or aspiring founder, learning how to negotiate effectively can tip the balance in your favour. Negotiation is the art and science of securing agreements between two or more parties. A key challenge is creating and claiming as much value as possible without making the other party unacceptably worse off in actual or perceived terms. This requires managing the conflict-prone tensions between value creation and value claiming or cooperation and competition in such deals.  Even experienced negotiators sometimes struggle to get this right because biases, errors in judgment, and ethical dilemmas get in the way. This course integrates theory and practice by intensively using realistic negotiation exercises that can improve your negotiation skills. As a result, it offers an enriched learning experience that can help you excel at business negotiations. 

Teaching Methods: This course will be delivered in person, with the physical location to be announced in advance. In compliance with its objectives, various teaching techniques will be used. These may include lectures, seminars, case studies, individual or group presentations, individual or group exercises, self-assessment surveys, and audiovisual materials. In addition, guest lecturers may be invited to augment the learning process. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Understand the nature of negotiation, particularly from the perspective of business leaders (e.g., entrepreneurs) in less established companies (e.g., startups and small businesses).
2. Understand the strategies, tactics and styles that can be deployed in business negotiations.
3. Develop the capacity to change your negotiation strategies, tactics or styles to meet new situational demands.
4. Understand essential strategies for creating value.
5. Understand essential strategies for claiming value.
6. Manage the conflict-prone tensions between value creation (cooperation) and value claiming (competition).
7. Become more aware of common biases, errors in judgment and ethical dilemmas that can undermine business negotiations.
8. Understand how to analyze and evaluate business deals, the deal-making context, and the negotiation behaviour of the individuals, groups or organizations involved.

Negotiation Planning and Role Preparation [15% of grade]

To get the most out of the scheduled negotiation exercises, you must carefully read the exercise materials and the instructions for your assigned role in advance. Based on the negotiation planning document provided in iDG, you will confirm that you have thoughtfully completed and submitted this document before the negotiation exercise. You are also expected to use your completed document when negotiating. Each completed document should be a maximum of 600 words. 

Your grade for negotiation planning and role preparation will depend on these two factors: (a) the number of adequately completed planning documents and (b) the efficacy of your negotiation planning and role preparation (i.e., z-scores achieved on scorable exercises as described below). Specifically, I will use this step-by-step approach to determine your grade: 

Step 1/3: Confirm the adequacy of your negotiation preparation

Determine whether each completed negotiation planning document is adequate (i.e., “adequate, 1” versus “inadequate, 0”) for your assigned role and negotiation situation. You must engage in the related negotiation exercise for this planning document to count toward your grade.

Step 2/3: Identify and normalize your gain (i.e., raw gain or score) from each exercise by calculating your z-score

where the gain is the surplus above what you would get if there were no negotiation agreement (i.e., the quantitative result for your Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement (BATNA)). To make the gains comparable across negotiators (i.e., control for differences in bargaining power across negotiation partners), I will separate the gains by role (e.g., gains for buyers only versus sellers). The more similar (or different) the gains are for the same-role negotiators, the more each negotiator represents (or departs from) the “average negotiator,” which implies a lower (or higher) standard deviation of the gains for the same role.

Z-score: Your calculated z-score tells us how many standard deviations away from (below or above) the average same-role gain your gain lies.  Consequently, the higher (or lower) your z-score, the better (or worse) your negotiation performance on a scorable exercise—which could reflect your negotiation skills, random events (i.e., “good luck” or “bad luck”), and negotiation-specific events (i.e., incompatible negotiation styles or personality traits between you and your negotiation partners). I will ensure that your negotiation skills predominantly determine your negotiation performance by using iDG to assign you to negotiation roles and groups randomly.

Journal-Report Assignments [40% of grade]

In your scheduled negotiation exercises, I would like you to reflect on what you and your classmates do and the associated outcomes. This will require you to distance yourself from the immediate negotiation situation, pinpoint critical events and processes, and integrate course and external content to produce deep insights. Going one step further, you will need to share your analyses in a structured way by completing well-written journal report assignments for 2 of 4 negotiations. 

Each written journal report should be a maximum of 600 words. Specifically, you are expected to prepare a report that covers these three core areas in a clear, precise, compelling, and coherent way:

 

  • Brief description of what happened (up to 150 words): Share relevant background information upfront: what the negotiation was about; your role versus your counterparty’s role; your negotiation strategy and tactics; whether your negotiation ended with or without an agreement; and whether you were satisfied with your agreement outcomes in substantive (i.e., economic interests) and relationship (i.e., interested in a positive, long-term relationship) terms, or the state of your disagreement or impasse.
  • Perspective (up to 350 words): You must comprehensively explain and substantiate why and how your specific negotiation strategy and tactics worked, or were ineffective, in your situation, taking your counterparty’s strategy/tactics, among other situational factors, into account. More generally, there should be a clear and logical chain of reasoning, arguments, or storytelling that precisely connects the strategy and tactics deployed to the negotiation outcomes that materialize. The perspective section is the most critical component of your journal report.
  • Application to future negotiation situations (up to 100 words): Drawing on insights from the perspective section, share concrete insights on what you would do the same way or differently when faced with specific conditions in a future negotiation situation. In other words, how differently would you engage in negotiation planning? When and how would you adjust your assumptions, strategies, and tactics to optimize the chance of reaching a satisfactory agreement?

You could make your work (especially the perspective section) even more outstanding by integrating relevant concepts and perspectives from course material and external (cited) research. In doing so, you could add profound, engaging, and novel insights beyond what we covered in class. In other words, intentionally search for external academic or practitioner articles, integrate relevant insights with what you already know from course content, and ultimately, try to discover and share new and relevant insights that deepen our understanding of why and how to negotiate more effectively and efficiently. After all, I want to learn something new from you too!

You may choose 2 of these four negotiations: Zephyr, Atalanta, Aerospace Investment and D-Loyal. You are expected to submit your journal report on Learn (Dropbox folder) one week after your chosen negotiation exercise is debriefed. In addition, you are expected to upload your journal reports on iDG.

 

Group Projects: Final Paper and Presentation on Negotiation [40% of grade]

During a specified period, you can self-enrol in groups for group projects featuring a written paper and presentation on negotiation-related topics. Each group must select a distinct, exciting negotiation topic along these two lines: (a) Use a real-world business deal to address a specific negotiation topic and question or (b) Review and integrate relevant academic and practitioner research on a general negotiation topic and question. 

Additional information and clarifications on the group paper you are expected to write

Your written paper should be a maximum of 1500 words. It will be graded out of 100 and converted to a share of 25% of the course grade, the weight assigned to this paper component of your teamwork. 

Instructor-Group Meetings: As a first step, please arrange to meet with me to discuss and confirm your research topic and question before proceeding. But note that I will primarily consider these questions when evaluating your topic and question: What exactly will I learn from you that could help me gain a deeper understanding of how business negotiations work (i.e., how to formulate and execute negotiation strategies and tactics) and how to get better results? How will your work build on and add to what we already know about business negotiations? After you confirm a topic and question with me, you are required to post your topic and question in the “Group Papers-Presentations: Topics and Questions” section of the discussion forum on LEARN. 

Whether you plan to focus on a topic connected to a business negotiation case or a general topic, recognize that you could develop an outstanding paper by addressing a specific negotiation topic and related question in a focused, clear, deep, compelling, and coherent way. This includes sharing novel and profound insights from course material and external research. In other words, do well-written work that challenges what we already know about business negotiation or helps us better understand how to perform essential strategic and tactical tasks better when negotiating. This means working diligently to ensure that your work does not elicit this reaction from the informed reader: “So what?”. 

If you are considering a real-world negotiation case or a general topic, stay focused and carefully manage the scope of your work—because covering too many issues or themes could result in unfocused work without novel or profound insights. One way to do so is to establish the core negotiation question you want to address for your selected topic. After deciding on the critical question, consider how you will structure your work to help readers quickly navigate it and eventually learn something new and exciting about formulating and executing business negotiation strategies. 

For a business case, it is essential to initially develop a brief upfront description of the negotiation situation. You can then progressively draw on relevant case information to create focused, nuanced, deep insights into your core issue or question. For a general topic, it is similarly critical to start with a solid opening paragraph that frames the relevant subject and sets the stage for your discussion—which should be carefully developed with appropriate arguments, evidence, and cited work over several well-connected paragraphs. 

Your conclusion section should include justified recommendations or takeaway messages (i.e., based on the insights or findings discussed). It can result in more efficient and effective negotiation strategies and tactics. 

To emphasize the expected focused and careful approach, note the following:

  • When considering a specific negotiation deal, do not write a predominantly descriptive paper that mainly covers one or more of these things: (a) background information. (b) the specifics of a negotiation deal, (c) the general application of distributive, integrative or mixed negotiation strategies. Instead, frame and position your crucial topic and question upfront and selectively use relevant background and deal information to develop and share compelling and insightful ideas that build on what we already know—by appropriately combining insights from case analysis (i.e., only relevant information about the deal in question), course content and external research. Regarding negotiation strategies, I strongly recommend focusing on a specific aspect of a distributive, integrative or mixed strategy or tactics.

 

  • Avoid making unsubstantiated assertions when developing arguments or perspectives. Specifically, instead of saying that something is essential for the described negotiation results (i.e., mutually satisfactory agreement or disagreement/impasse), provide concrete support by using relevant arguments (logic or theory), case study findings, and statistical evidence. This is especially critical when explaining why and how specific strategic and tactical factors account for the negotiation outcomes addressed. Based on the rubric below, this component of your work falls under “Perspective and Applications,” which accounts for a relatively large proportion of your marks.

Group paper rubric--I will use the following criteria to mark your group paper:

 

  • Foundational Elements (30%)
    • Start by introducing the topic and conveying why it is relevant to the formulation and execution of a strategic negotiation plan in a business context. There is a strong hook (i.e., statistic, question, quote, or anecdote) that grabs the reader’s attention.
    • Appropriately frame and position the topic in relevant background work. Specifically, briefly describe relevant insights or findings from coursework, practitioner articles, or academic research. Next, make a case for your paper by clarifying what is missing from this background work or inadequately understood, why it is vital to address this gap and state the question you will answer to do so.
    • The relevant aspects of the negotiation business context are clearly and precisely described.
    • Relevant concepts are communicated and correctly interpreted.
    • Appropriate examples or cases are used to illustrate the key concepts meaningfully.
    • All main and supporting points are backed by logical arguments, reliable evidence and valid academic or practitioner sources.
  • Perspective and Applications (50%)
    • Core perspective (i.e., idea, thesis, explanation, theory) is developed, substantiated, and articulated clearly, precisely, coherently, and compellingly. In other words, your presentation should primarily convey a convincing perspective that provides logical and substantiated explanations for the negotiation outcomes: why and how specific negotiation factors, strategies, and tactics influenced your negotiation experiences or outcomes. Furthermore, you should provide an informed and convincing answer to the core question.
  • Provide novel and profound insights that build on and add to relevant course content, integrating relevant course content and external research (i.e., reputable practitioners or academic studies).
  • Conclude with coherent and justified recommendations or takeaway messages based on the insights or findings discussed, which could result in more efficient and effective negotiation strategies and tactics.
  • Organization (10%)
    • Outstanding paper structure: a clear introduction that appropriately frames and positions the topic in the relevant academic or practitioner work or discourse; the logical flow of ideas; smooth transitions between well-developed ideas or points that deepen or move the discussion forward; a thoughtful conclusion that appropriately wraps up the paper.
  • Writing mechanics (10%)
    • Extremely well-written papers are expected: sentences are well-formed and appropriately varied in length and style; there are few spelling or grammatical errors.

Honour Code

A strict honour code applies to this class as follows:

You may not make up facts that materially change the power distribution or a negotiation exercise (e.g., your family just bought the company you are currently negotiating, or state that you can’t grant a request because it is illegal when this isn’t the case). If you are asked a question that calls for information not in your confidential instructions, you may say, “I don’t know.”

You may tell the other side what you wish, but you may not, under any circumstances, show them your confidential role instructions.

  • You may not borrow notes, discuss cases, or share quizzes and assignments with people not taking the class.
  • To get the most out of this class, everybody must feel free to share openly. To help make our class a “safe space,” class discussion is expected to stay in this class. Students must never look at another student’s journal entry without the writer’s explicit permission.
  • Material used in this class, including but not limited to handouts, exercises, cases, discussion questions, charts, and graphs, are copyrighted and, hence, may not be used for purposes other than the education experience of this class without the written consent of the instructor.

Course Description

BET 340:

Entrepreneurial success starts with finding a solution to a problem and then creating the right strategy to bring it to market. The final step in the process is explored in this course, the disciplined execution of strategy. A broad range of skills are required to do this including understanding founder and team dynamics, hiring and firing, cash flow management, project management, developing suitable equity structures and compensation, pitching, fundraising, internal and external communications, and people skills. This course explores the business skills and knowledge needed to take a new venture, whether it is a start-up or part of an existing organization, from strategy to market.

View requirements for BET 340

Entrepreneurial success involves creating an innovative solution to a “big problem.” But in addition to validating their business model (i.e., how they intend to create, deliver, and capture value), entrepreneurs must develop a strategy to bring this solution to market. Eventually, they must execute this strategy. This course is primarily concerned with this execution process. It recognizes that successful execution calls for exceptional judgments and decision-making involving challenging tasks—such as building a founding team; hiring, motivating, and firing employees; pitching to investors; managing cash flow; communicating with internal and external stakeholders; acquiring and onboarding customers; negotiating with industry partners; and managing multiple projects. Thus, there is an emphasis on actionable insights that can foster entrepreneurial judgment and decision-making. Ultimately, these insights can enable enterprising individuals to successfully take on a new venture—either as a start-up or part of an existing organization—from strategy to market.

Teaching Methods: This course will be delivered in person, with the physical location to be announced in advance. In compliance with its objectives, various teaching techniques will be used. These may include lectures, seminars, case studies, individual or group presentations, individual or group exercises, self-assessment surveys, and audiovisual materials. In addition, guest lecturers may be invited to augment the learning process. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Understand how to craft a compelling story targeted towards a specific audience (e.g., investors, customers, employees).
2. Pinpoint equity distribution's short-term and long-term implications while maintaining a productive working relationship with your co-founders.
3. Customize selling strategies to the target audience to effectively negotiate with clients and other industry partners in mutually satisfactory ways.
4. Build an effective personal brand by communicating and networking in ways that align with an emerging or established venture brand.
5. Identify the primary sources, strengths, and limitations of cash or funding available to new ventures.
6. Critically evaluate the role and importance of investors to new ventures and create and deliver a compelling pitch to raise external capital.
7. Recognize when a pivot is needed and the type that might be most useful for any given circumstance.
8. Manage multiple projects using the appropriate methodology to support the venture’s dynamic strategy.
9. Lead and motivate employees to work towards a shared goal with the support of appropriate strategies for managing people.

Assignment 2: Business Negotiations (Lesson 4b: Sales &Negotiations)

Use the planning document on Learn to plan for and engage in an actual business negotiation within the next two weeks. This should involve cases in which there is at least one real substantive interest (i.e., you want something for an economic or financial reason) alongside potential relationship interests, combined with a desire to reach an agreement through bargaining or adjusting relevant terms. Some prominent examples include negotiating a job offer, purchasing or selling an item, renting or subletting a property, and negotiating with a co-founder or investor. Whether you reach an agreement or not, you are expected to submit a 600-word (maximum) journal report that covers these three areas in a clear, precise, compelling, and coherent way:

 

  • Brief description of what happened (up to 150 words): Share relevant background information upfront: what the negotiation was about; your role versus your counterparty’s role; your negotiation strategy and tactics; whether your negotiation ended with or without an agreement; and whether you were satisfied with your agreement outcomes in substantive (i.e., economic interests) and relationship (i.e., interest in a positive, long-term relationship) terms, or the state of your disagreement or impasse.
  • Perspective (up to 350 words): You must comprehensively explain and substantiate why your specific negotiation strategy and tactics worked, or were ineffective, in your situation, taking your counterparty’s strategies/tactics, among other situational factors, into account. More generally, there should be a clear and logical chain of reasoning, arguments or storytelling that precisely connects the strategy and tactics deployed to the anticipated negotiation outcomes. The perspective section is the most critical component of your journal report.
  • Application to future negotiation situations (up to 100 words): Share concrete insights on what you would do the same way or differently when faced with specific conditions in a future negotiation situation. In other words, how differently would you engage in negotiation planning? When and how would you adjust your assumptions, strategies, and tactics to optimize the chance of reaching a satisfactory agreement?

High-quality reports will address all these issues while integrating course content and external(cited) research to add deep, engaging, and novel insights.

Marketplace Simulation [Group work: 16% of Course Grade—Simulation performance and report 8% each]

In the past, there has been minimal opportunity to experiment with starting a real new business as part of your business education. It has been too risky, time-consuming, and expensive for a single-term course. But Marketplace makes up for this in essential ways. 

Through computer simulation, you will start and run a virtual company with your classmates for one-and-a-half years of compressed business life cycle (i.e., from the startup stage to development, growth and eventually, near maturity)—which amounts to six weeks of decision-making rounds in class. In addition to evaluating the market opportunity, you are expected to execute your venture’s strategy across multiple functions—from marketing to distribution, manufacturing, human resources, finance, accounting, and team management. Repeatedly, you will analyze your business situation in a domestic or international market, plan a strategy to improve it and then execute that strategy. You will face great uncertainty from the outside environment and your business situation. Incrementally, you will learn to skillfully adjust your strategy as you discover the nature of real-life decisions, including the available options, linkages to other parts of the business, conflicts, tradeoffs, and potential outcomes.

Your Marketplace grades will come from these two sources:

End-of-Simulation cumulative performance grade [8% of course grade]: The simulation will generate a graded cumulative balanced scorecard at the end, which will be converted to a share of 10% of your course grade.

Marketplace Journal Report [8 % of course grade]: Throughout operating your virtual venture in Marketplace, I expect you to reflect on what you and your classmates do and the associated performance results. This will require you to distance yourself from the immediate business situations or outcomes, pinpoint critical events and processes, and integrate course and external content to produce insightful analyses. After doing so, you are expected to write a journal report with a maximum of 1000 words. This form of report must cover these core areas in a clear, compelling, and coherent way:

  • Brief description of what happened (up to 200 words): Share relevant background information upfront: what is your business about; what competitive strategy and tactics you relied on; how your company performed over the last three quarters; and whether you are satisfied with your performance.
  • Perspective (up to 650 words): You must comprehensively explain and substantiate why and how your specific competitive strategy and tactics were effective or ineffective in your changing business situations, taking your competitors’ strategies and tactics, among other situational factors (i.e., changing operational and financial performance and customer needs), into account. This is the most critical component of your journal report.
  • Application to future business situations (up to 150 words): Share concrete insights on what you would do the same way or differently when faced with specific conditions in a future business situation. In other words, how differently would you engage in venture planning and execution? When and how would you adjust your assumptions and strategies to adapt to changing situations and improve business performance?

You could make your work even more outstanding by integrating relevant concepts and perspectives from course material and external (cited) research. In doing so, you could add profound, engaging, and novel insights beyond what we covered in class. In other words, intentionally search for external academic or practitioner articles, integrate relevant insights with what you already know from course content, and ultimately, try to discover and share new and relevant insights that deepen our understanding of why and how to execute a venture strategy more effectively and efficiently. After all, I want to learn something new from you too!

Required: Your Marketplace journal report must include the names of contributing group members, their functional responsibilities in the simulation, and precisely how they contribute to the written report.

Additional information and clarifications on the group paper you are expected to write. 

Your written paper should be a maximum of 1500 words. It will be graded out of 100 and converted to a share of 14% of the course grade, the weight assigned to this report component of your teamwork. 

Instructor-Group Meetings: As a first step, please arrange to meet with me to discuss and confirm your research topic and question before proceeding. But note that I will primarily consider these questions when evaluating your topic and question: What exactly will I learn from you that could help me gain a deeper understanding of venture planning and execution (i.e., how to formulate and execute venture strategies and operations) and how to get better performance outcomes? How does this work build on and add to what we already know about venture planning and execution in this course? After you confirm a topic and question with me, you are required to post them on the “Group Papers-Presentations: Topics and Questions” section of the discussion forum on LEARN. 

Whether you plan to focus on a topic connected to a founder or a general topic, recognize that you can develop an outstanding paper by addressing a specific venture planning and execution topic and related question in a focused, clear, deep, compelling, and coherent way. This includes sharing novel and profound insights from course material and external research. In other words, please undertake well-written work that challenges what we already know about venture planning and execution or helps us understand how to perform essential venture planning and execution tasks. This means working diligently to ensure that your work does not elicit this reaction from the informed reader: “So what?”. 

If you are considering a topic linked to a founder or a general topic, stay focused and carefully manage the scope of your work—because covering too many issues or themes could result in unfocused work without novel or profound insights. One way to do so is to establish the core question you want to address for your selected topic. After deciding on the critical question, consider how you will structure your work to help readers quickly navigate it. Ultimately, you should help them learn something new and exciting about venture planning and execution. 

For a topic inspired by a founder, it is essential to first frame and position your topic and question in the relevant course, practitioner, or academic work. Afterwards, you can clarify why and how you will use relevant aspects of the founder’s life and business venture to address them. Do not provide a separate biographic or business overview upfront. Instead, progressively draw on relevant founder- and business-related information, combined with appropriate course content and external research, to develop focused, nuanced, and deep insights on your core topic and question.

For a general topic, it is similarly critical to start with a solid opening paragraph that frames and positions your topic and question in the relevant course, practitioner, or academic work and, ultimately, sets the stage for your follow-on discussion—which should be carefully developed with appropriate arguments, evidence and cited work over several well-connected paragraphs. I will return to this line of advice below.

In general, your conclusion section should include justified recommendations or takeaway messages (i.e., based on the insights or findings discussed) and can result in more efficient and effective strategy execution. 

To emphasize the expected focused and careful approach to the selected topic and question, note the following:

  • Do not write a predominantly descriptive paper covering one or more of these things: (a) founder’s life or business story or general background information. (b) overviews on the founder’s business experiences and outcomes, (c) broad coverage of external factors (i.e., crisis) that impact broadly covered aspects of startup or new venture operations, (d) broad coverage of multiple aspects of venture planning and execution for founders, new ventures, or established companies, based on what we already know from course content. Instead, frame and position your crucial topic and question upfront and selectively use relevant background and information to develop and share compelling and insightful ideas that build on what we already know--by appropriately combining insights from case analysis (i.e., specific information about the deal in question), course content, and external research.
  • Avoid making unsubstantiated assertions when trying to justify your arguments or perspectives. Specifically, instead of saying that something is vital for the success of a founder or business, provide concrete support by using relevant arguments (logic or theory), case study findings, and statistical evidence. This is especially critical when explaining why and how specific founder, business or external factors account for the founder- and business-related outcomes (i.e., success or failure) addressed. Based on the rubric below, this component of your work falls under “Analysis and Applications,” which accounts for a relatively large proportion of your marks.

Administrative Policy

Fair Contingencies for Emergency/Remote Teaching

The course outline presents the instructor’s intentions for course assessments, their weights, and due dates. We will keep to the specified assessments, weights, and dates as best as possible. To provide a contingency for unforeseen circumstances, the instructor reserves the right to modify course topics, assessments, weight, and deadlines with due and fair notice to students. In such challenges, the instructor will work with the Department/Faculty to find reasonable and fair solutions that respect the rights and workloads of students, staff, and faculty.

Attendance Policy

This course has an attendance policy that is strictly enforced. If you miss a Marketplace simulation exercise, you must contact the instructor well before the scheduled class, which will be impacted. You may miss one exercise without penalty if you give the required notice in advance. If you fail, you will receive a Marketplace simulation grade, one letter grade lower than your group simulation grade. Further, failure to participate in more than one exercise (regardless of notice) will result in a grade corresponding to the proportion of the six exercises you attended and contributed to. 

Late Assignments

Only on-time assignments will be accepted.

University Policy

Academic integrity: In order to maintain a culture of academic integrity, members of the University of Waterloo community are expected to promote honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.]

Grievance: A student who believes that a decision affecting some aspect of their university life has been unfair or unreasonable may have grounds for initiating a grievance. Read Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances, Section 4. When in doubt, please be certain to contact the department’s administrative assistant who will provide further assistance.

Discipline: A student is expected to know what constitutes academic integrity to avoid committing an academic offence, and to take responsibility for their actions. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.] A student who is unsure whether an action constitutes an offence, or who needs help in learning how to avoid offences (e.g., plagiarism, cheating) or about “rules” for group work/collaboration should seek guidance from the course instructor, academic advisor, or the undergraduate associate dean. For information on categories of offences and types of penalties, students should refer to Policy 71, Student Discipline. For typical penalties, check Guidelines for the Assessment of Penalties.

Appeals: A decision made or penalty imposed under Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances (other than a petition) or Policy 71, Student Discipline may be appealed if there is a ground. A student who believes they have a ground for an appeal should refer to Policy 72, Student Appeals.

Note for students with disabilities: AccessAbility Services, located in Needles Hall, Room 1401, collaborates with all academic departments to arrange appropriate accommodations for students with disabilities without compromising the academic integrity of the curriculum. If you require academic accommodations to lessen the impact of your disability, please register with AccessAbility Services at the beginning of each academic term.

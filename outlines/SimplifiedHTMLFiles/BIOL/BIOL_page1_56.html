Course Description

BIOL 484:

The role of classical genetic analysis in the context of today's genomic era. Topics discussed in lectures and labs include meiotic recombination, meiosis, complementation analysis, chromosome aberrations, and genetic interactions. Methods used in model eukaryotic genetic organisms such as forward and reverse genetic screens, genetic mosaics, conditional mutants, and genetic mapping will be discussed. Examples will illustrate how genes continue to be identified using classical-based approaches in a variety of biological processes, including cell cycle progression, cancer and metastasis, learning and memory, as well as pattern formation and embryonic development. Alternatives to traditional mutagenesis-based screens and the links between classical and molecular genetics will also be discussed.

View requirements for BIOL 484

Advanced Eukaryotic Genetics is designed to introduce students to the world of genetic research, with particular emphasis on the attributes and use of model genetic organisms (the yeasts, Neurospora, Drosophila, C. elegans, Zebrafish, Arabidopsis) in basic research.  This course also strives to introduce students to some advanced concepts in classical genetics and to relate how classical genetics is relevant in today’s era of genome sequencing, genomics, proteomics, and genome editing.

The course is delivered using PowerPoint lecture presentations that will be available before lectures. Tutorial sessions are used to go through example problems, to review material before midterm exams, and to allow for demonstrations of literature and database searches. Tutorials sessions may also be used as lectures if we fall behind schedule wrt the lecture material.

 

For every hour of lecture time you can expect to spend one hour reviewing and reading supplementary information (most will be provided on the LEARN site). 

The problem assignments (there will be two) are generally considered tricky.  Each problem set will probably take about six hours to complete as you may be required to do some additional background reading to figure out how to approach the problems.  Many genetic problems are like puzzles and they will take some time to solve.  Do not leave them until the night before they are due – you will have over one week to work out each problem set.

 

LECTURE MATERIAL:

1) Introduction & Review of 239 Material:

Prokaryotes vs. eukaryotes, Mendel’s Rules (two main laws), the dihybrib cross, the concept of genetic linkage, the stages of meiosis and the main events of meiosis, the chromosome theory of inheritance and its proof, recombination mapping (genetic distances), genetic maps vs. physical maps, polytene chromosomes.

2) Concepts for Genetic Analysis:

Model genetic organisms (general attributes and characteristics), balancer chromosomes and “balanced” genetic stocks, pericentric and paracentric inversions, methods of inducing genetic mutations, heterochromatin, RNAi, the genetic engineering and use of transposons as genetic tools 

3) Details of Drosophila as a model genetic organism (MGO)

4) Details of C. elegans as an MGO.

5) Details of yeast as an MGO.

6) Details of Arabidopsis as an MGO.

7) Advanced Genetic Mapping:

Tetrad Analysis in Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Perkin’s Equation, Centromere Linkage in Ordered and Unordered Tetrads, Half Tetrad Analysis, crossover interference, sister chromatid exchange.

8) non-Mendelian Inheritance

9) Genetic Complementation

Knowing how the complementation test can fool you, genetic modifiers, examples of genetic screens using MGOs, epistasis 

10) Genetic Mosaics

11) CRISPR/Cas9 –– implications for model genetic systems and efficacy for gene therapy, and “genome editing” 

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Regain familiarity with the concepts introduced in BIOL 239, such as Mendel’s laws and classical based genetic analysis
Be in a good position to consider and evaluate possible graduate school projects in the areas of basic biomedical research, developmental or molecular biology
Learn to appreciate the laws of inheritance and exactly why organisms such as flies, worms, and yeasts have become major tools of basic research in genetics, cell biology, and developmental biology

Course Description

BIOL 441:

A survey of the recent advances in the vertebrate immune response; the cells and tissues of the lymphoid system; humoral and cell-mediated immunity; initiation and regulation of the immune response; the immune system and disease, emerging techniques used in immunology.

View requirements for BIOL 441

BIOL 441 is scheduled as an in-person and on-campus course. There are no online or hybrid options available. Classes and discussions are NOT recorded. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Synthesize and articulate recent advances in the field of immunology.
Evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of research techniques and animal models used in immunology.
Identify the appropriate technique and corresponding controls to generate high-quality, meaningful data.
Critique primary literature papers in immunology by critically interpreting data and forming evidence-based conclusions.
Design experiments to address research hypothesis and questions

Assignments: 

There are three (3) equally weighted seminar assignments in (approximately) the first half of the term due on the date specified in the course schedule. These are to be completed individually (i.e not group work). Assignments should be handed in at the start of class, before the class begins. There are no make-up assignments available for missed deadlines and weighting cannot be transferred to other items. 

Midterms/Examinations: 

***Note there is no final examination for this course. Instead, there are two “midterm” examinations. 

Students must earn an average passing grade of 50% or greater across the midterms to pass the course, as these assess your individual knowledge. Students who do not achieve a grade of 50% or higher averaged across their two midterms will receive a grade of 46% or the average grade across their two midterms, whichever is lower. 

Exam format will be written response and will test your knowledge on material from lectures/papers, techniques, ability to analyze data and design experiments to test hypothesis, and ability to synthesize and critically analyze a paper.

  • Midterm #1 will be based on all material covered before the exam including lectures, paper discussions, group discussions, worksheets, assignments.
  • While ~50% of Midterm #2 will mainly focus on material covered after Midterm #1, Midterm #2 is cumulative and students should still have an excellent mastery of the material covered in the first half of the course as the immune system is interconnected and many of the same techniques are used in immunology research. Thus, students are still expected to know these immunology concepts and techniques. The other ~50% of Midterm 2 will be questions on a new research paper (I.e. has not been covered in class) that students will be required to critically read and interpret. This paper will be posted to D2L ~2 weeks before Midterm #2. This is to test your ability to synthesize and critically analyze a paper.
  • Examinations are “open book” and are to be completed individually. Students are permitted to use a hard copy of lecture notes and the papers discussed (class materials) on the exam (no worksheets or assignments permitted). However, students should should have a mastery of the material such that they could complete the midterm without course materials as there is not enough time to learn material during exams – lecture notes and papers are for quick reference in case you have forgotten a detail. Use of electronic devices (computers, tablets, phones, smart watches, etc.) or any other materials is NOT permitted.
  • If we have in-person exams, all usual University of Waterloo policies surrounding examinations will apply (I.e. you must bring your WatCard, no cell phones on your person, no wearing of hats, only clear water bottles are allowed, no food is permitted, etc).
  • Examinations will be viewable by  appointment with Dr. Katzenback for two (2) weeks following the release of grades. If a student wishes a question to be re-graded, the student must submit a written explanation/justification as to why they believe the question was graded incorrectly within 2 weeks following the release of grades, and the entire item is subject to re-grading.

Group Oral Presentations and Group Written Paper Critiques: 

  • Each student will work in  groups of 3-4 (group size may vary based on class size) to prepare an oral presentation and a written paper critique.  The presentation and the written critique  review will cover different topics.
  • Dates for group oral presentations and group written paper critiques will be determined in class after the add/drop period (usually sometime during the 3rd or 4th week of class). A list of topics will be posted in advance of the class and sign-up for topics will occur in class. Students should be aware that they will stay in the same group for both the group oral presentation and the group written paper critique.
  • During the second half of the course, each week will cover one topic. There will be two primary literature papers per topic. These papers will be different but related. At the start of the topic, we will have a short intro to the topic, followed by group presentations (two papers per topic, one per class), and then a worksheet to aid in students synthesizing the information from and across the papers. Links to the primary literature papers that will form the basis for each respective student presentation or paper critique will be posted a minimum of two (2) weeks prior to the due date to ensure that all students have ample time to prepare their deliverables.
  • Group Oral Presentations: Presentations will be a critique of the paper and will be approximately 25 minutes in length followed by ~5 minutes for questions. Presentations should be submitted (as pptx) at least 24 hours prior to the start of the class in which the presentation will be made to the corresponding Dropbox folder on Learn. Late presentations (that is, submitted less than 24 h before the start of class in which the presentation is due) will result in a 10% loss in the overall grade assigned to this deliverable for all group members. Students should bring a copy of their presentation (in pdf or pptx) on a USB key (or have it on a laptop they will be using) and arrive a few minutes early so that the presentation can be loaded onto the podium computer. 
  • Group Written Paper Critiques: Written paper critiques should be a maximum of 4 pages, double spaced, in 12 pt Times New Roman font with no less than 2.54 cm margins on all sides, not including figures or references. Paper critiques should be submitted 24 h prior to the start of class in which the paper is to be discussed through the  Dropbox folder on the BIOL441 course page on D2L. Late paper critiques will result in a grade of “0”.
  • For all group deliverables: groups must include a credit statement indicating the contribution of each member to the group. This does not need to be shown during the oral presentation (but a slide needs to be included with this information in the version submitted to Learn) and does not count towards page limits for the Group Written Paper Critique (this information can be placed on the title page).
  • Peer evaluation: Of the grade assigned to each item (presentation or written critique), 3% will be based on how your other group members evaluate your contribution to the final product. More details on how peer evaluation for each deliverable will be conducted will be provided at a later date.
  • Worksheets: After both student presentations, students will work in groups (students should mix groups for this part) to work collaboratively to discuss and formulate answers to questions posed in the worksheet. These worksheets are not graded but will greatly help you improve your understanding of the material and begin forming linkages between the papers. Each student is responsible for their own mastery of the paper being presented, regardless of whether they presented the paper or not or how well the paper was presented, as it is expected everyone will have learned the skills of how to do this by the second half of the course.
  • If you need help with writing your critique or tips on preparing presentations, please consider engaging with the Writing Centre on campus – they are here as a resource to all students and are always willing to help (https://uwaterloo.ca/writing-and-communication-centre/ )

Academic Offences

Any attempt on the part of a student to gain an unfair advantage may be construed as academic misconduct, irrespective of whether any benefit was gained by the student(s) concerned. (UW academic regulations)

 

Unauthorized co-operation or collaboration (excessive collaboration)

This is a form of plagiarism that occurs when someone writes sentences, or constructs paragraphs for you.  This also applies to art, graphs, diagrams or figures, data, and calculations. Unintentional plagiarism (e.g., I was just getting help; I ran out of time; we wrote the sentences together) is still plagiarism. It is not the instructor’s job (and it is not possible), to uncover a student’s motives for plagiarizing. It is what it is.

Students should treat their academic work as their own property. It is the responsibility of students to protect their own work. Students should ensure that electronic copies of their work are stored securely and cannot be copied or stolen by another person; for example, in computer laboratories. Allowing someone to represent your work as their own is also academic misconduct.

 

Examples of plagiarism and excessive collaboration

  • working with another person on an individual assignment/test
  • having someone else complete all or part of your assignment for you
  • having someone rewrite your assignment for you
  • obtaining, or attempting to obtain, assistance with an individual assignment, for example, by using an essay-writing service or any artificial intelligence (AI) system, platform, or service
  • obtaining, or attempting to obtain, access to assignment/test materials (e.g., examination/quiz/ i-clicker questions) including questions/answers/marking schemes during, prior to, or after the assignment/test without explicit permission from an instructor or TA. This includes obtaining access to screen shots, cell phone/i-pad pictures etc., of above materials.
  • copying, communicating, or attempting to copy or communicate, assignment/test materials (e.g., examination/quiz i-clicker questions) including questions/answers/marking schemes during, prior to, or after the assignment/test without explicit permission from an instructor or TA. This includes taking and passing along screen shots, cell phone/i-pad pictures etc., of above materials.
  • copying, or attempting to copy, the work of another student or instructor and submitting it as your own.
  • submitting work produced in collusion with another person(s) for an assignment that is to be assessed on individual work.
  • inclusion of unauthorized members in student teams when conducting group work assignments.
  • lending work that has been submitted for assessment to another student, now or in the future.

It is the student’s responsibility to ensure they have understood the definition of academic offences. See Office of Academic Integrity (https://www.lib.uwaterloo.ca/ait/misconduct.html) for more information and seek clarification from your course instructor if you are still not clear on what constitutes an academic offence. 

Note that if the extent of the collaboration allowed for an assignment or lab is not stated explicitly in the syllabus, students must assume that absolutely no collaboration is allowed.

 

Plagiarism is unethical because:

It diminishes the value of another person’s original work.

It gives an unfair advantage over the student who does their own work. 

It undermines student learning, respect for the learning process, and the value of academic achievement. 

Unchecked plagiarism devalues the worth of the degree, the reputation and credibility of students, faculty, and the institute itself.

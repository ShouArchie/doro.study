Course Description

BIOL 361:

An introduction to hypothesis testing and experimental design in biology. Topics will include exploratory data analysis, analysis of variance, categorical data analysis, correlation, linear regression, multiple linear regression, power analysis, and visualization tools. Statistical software will be used in computer laboratories to explore examples and applications.

View requirements for BIOL 361

Biology 361 is a practical course in statistics and experimental design for students of the biological sciences. 

The objectives of the course are to:

  • Provide you with a set of tools and techniques so you can perform your own data analysis and experimental design. The tools include statistical-analysis tools and graphical approaches to data analysis and display.
  • Teach you enough to understand and critically assess the statistical portions of most articles in the biological sciences.
  • Equip you with the experience and vocabulary to make an exchange with a professional statistician productive.
  • Foster ability to make adept decisions about data analysis and form conclusions on your own.

This course offers training in data handling & analysis, hypothesis testing and experimental design in Biology. Topics will include: exploratory data analysis, two-sample tests, analysis of variance, categorical data analysis, correlation, linear regression, multiple linear regression, power analysis, experimental design, and data visualization tools and statistical software.

The Course Schedule provides the list of topics covered during each week of the course. The course is delivered as two hours of lectures per week (remote, asynchronous) where you will learn the background information, and 10 Lab sessions (each ~2 hours) where you’ll put statistics and data analysis into practice. Students are registered in one of 6 different Lab Sections (LAB 101-106) which are at scheduled times (i.e., in-person, synchronous) and use IBM-SPSS statistics software. The lab sessions provide important skill development and practice for activities that students will use in the written assignments and tests of the course – so, the Lab sessions are the most important components of the course! Make sure to attend them!!!

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Effectively use a sophisticated statistical software package (e.g., IBM SPSS Statistics).
Summarize and display data so you can learn what they show, and to communicate the key features to others.
Choose which statistical test is most appropriate to use for your data.
Form accurate, well-supported conclusions and report the results obtained from statistical tests.
Gain confidence in solving problems and answering scientific questions with data (= evidence).
Begin to design your own experiments and avoid common pitfalls when conducting experiments.

This is a practical course that provides students with experiential learning opportunities to master skills in handling data (graphical and numerical summaries), statistical analysis and experimental design. Students have opportunities to learn how to:

  • Effectively use a sophisticated statistical software package (e.g., IBM SPSS Statistics).
  • Summarize and display data so you can learn what they show, and to communicate the key features to others.
  • Choose which statistical test is most appropriate to use for your data.
  • Form accurate, well-supported conclusions and report the results obtained from statistical tests.
  • Gain confidence in solving problems and answering scientific questions with data (= evidence).
  • Begin to design your own experiments and avoid common pitfalls when conducting experiments.

A. Course Software:

IBM SPSS Statistics (SPSS) is a sophisticated statistical software package that is widely used in research and business. The course labs and assignments require the use of this software (or other equivalent statistical and graphical software). Proficiency in use of SPSS will become a useful skill to add on your CV. You may find SPSS useful for work in other courses, and in subsequent employment. SPSS is menu-driven and thus easier to use than software that uses code. This allows you to maximize mastery of use of statistics and minimize time spent learning to use the tool. The TAs will also provide files that allow students to complete the labs in R.

B. Reference Books (no cost): Below is a list of books I use for the lectures.  I have placed them on 1-day reserve in the UW Davis Library so students can access them. 

There is no required textbook that students must buy. Instead, there’s an excellent (Free!) online ‘textbook’ that you can use to supplement your learning of the course material – called HyperStat. It is an excellent resource with many links to other statistical resources – try it out when you have a statistical question.  http://davidmlane.com/hyperstat/index.html

For those interested in buying a statistical textbook, I recommend the one by Motulsky (#1 in the list below). Before you design your own experiments, you’ll benefit from reading the short book by Ruxton & Colgrave (#8 below). The textbook you used in your first Statistics course at UW is likely also an excellent resource for you (hopefully you kept it!).

  1. Motulsky, H. 1995. Intuitive Biostatistics. Oxford University Press. ISBN 0-19-508606-6. Library Call # R853.S7 M68 1995. Excellent introductory biostatistics text that focuses on explaining the proper scientific interpretation of statistical tests rather on the mathematical logic of the tests. Some of the lecture material will be based on material covered in this text.
  2. Dytham, C. 1999. Choosing and Using Statistics: A Biologist’s Guide. Blackwell Science Ltd. ISBN 0-86542-653-8. Library Call # QH323.5.D98 2003.
  3. Sokal, R.F. and F.J. Rohlf. 1995. Biometry: The Principles and Practice of Statistics in Biological Research (3rd Edition). W.H. Freeman & Co. ISBN 0-7167-2411-1. Library Call # QH323.5.S63. An excellent introductory and advanced biostatistics text. You will find everything that is covered in this course and much, much more.
  4. Rohlf, F.J. and R.F. Sokal. 1995. Statistical Tables. Library call # QH323.5.R63. Statistical tables to accompany the Biometry text (above).
  5. Zar, J.H. 1999. Biostatistical Analysis (4th Edition). Library call # QH323.5.Z37. Excellent introductory to advanced biostatistics text.  Includes a full complement of statistical tables.
  6. Krebs, C. J. 1998. Ecological Methodology (2nd Edition). Library call # QH541.15.S72 K74. Excellent resource for experimental methodology in the ecological sciences.  This textbook is available on Dr. Krebs's website. Please find it there at bottom of the page! (http://www.zoology.ubc.ca/~krebs/books.html)
  7. Triola, M.F., W.M. Goodman & R. Law. 1999. Elementary Statistics (1st Canadian Ed.). Addison Wesley Longman Ltd. ISBN 0-201-47811-0. Library Call # QA276.12. T76x 1999. A good, clear introductory text – a good resource to use if you are struggling to understand the basic introductory material in this course.
  8. Ruxton, G.D. & N. Colegrave. 2003. Experimental Design for the Life Sciences. Oxford University Press. ISBN 0-19-925232-7. Library Call # QH307.2.R88 2003. A short, efficient, highly useful book for anyone wanting to know about principles of good experimental design. A must-read before launching any experiment, whether you are experienced or a novice!

The Labs and Assignments in Biology 361 are the most important part of the course!!  Statistics is like a sport in that you cannot become good at it just by reading about it – you must participate! It is for this reason that 60% of the grade in the course is based on your assignments (which build upon the Lab sessions). So, approach the Labs and Assignments with care, dedication, and timeliness (i.e., don’t skip the labs!).

Each student must complete all 3 Assignments offered in this course. Assignments are posted on the course LEARN site. Your report must be submitted to a Dropbox in LEARN before the start of your assigned computer lab on the due date (see Schedule above). Assignments are generally marked and returned within 2 weeks. The marking is time-consuming, but provides important feedback (so, read the feedback!).

Administrative Policy

This course includes independent development and practice of specific skills by students, such as organizing data in spreadsheets for analysis by statistical software, making choices about the most effective graph and table style to display information (data), choosing the appropriate statistical test for a stated hypothesis and data set, drawing your own interpretations and conclusions from the data, and communicating your own understanding. Therefore, the use of GenAI trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class because it does not help you develop these skills. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility, and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

BIOL 373L:

Laboratory exercises to study the principles of human physiology.

View requirements for BIOL 373L

The laboratory exercises in this course are designed to illustrate the physiological principles described in Biology 273 and Biology 373 lecture courses. In this course, you will have the opportunity to gain first-hand experience conducting and creating experiments in human physiology. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Lab 1: A) To investigate some properties of the special senses and the somatic senses B) To elicit some reflexes C) To illustrate fine motor control
Lab 2: A) To record a human nerve reflex impulse, and to determine its speed
Lab 3: A) To measure and investigate the activity of the medial and lateral rectus muscles during eye movement (saccades, pursuit, vestibular ocular reflex and vergence)
Lab 4: A) To observe and measure muscle contraction, and to understand the effects of fatigue on muscle strength
Lab 5: A) The investigation of heart sounds and their correlation to cardiac cycle events B) The determination of blood pressure under resting and exercising conditions C) The observation of an electrocardiogram (ECG) under resting and exercising conditions D) The investigation of the induction of bradycardia by facial immersion E) The study of some aspects of circulation
Lab 6: A) To measure chest dimensions during the respiratory cycle B) To study the effects of changes in Pco2, Po2, and pH on the breathing movements C) To perform Valsalva's maneuver D) To examine neural input to respiration E) To determine a subject's respiratory volumes and capacities (iWorx)
Lab 7: A) To begin the development of a physiology lab exercise with lab partners
Lab 8: A) To continue work on the development of a physiology lab exercise with lab partners
Lab 9: A) To conduct the previously created physiology lab exercise with lab partners

Laboratory Reports 

During the term you will submit laboratory reports on three of the exercises. The standard format for laboratory reports in Biology courses is found at the end of this manual (see page 103). Lab reports are due in the Dropbox BEFORE the start of your lab period. Any reports handed in after the lab has begun will be considered late = 5% per day (max 3 days). The marking scheme for reports can be found at the end of the lab manual (see page 107).

No student can receive credit for a report on a laboratory exercise that they did not perform. That means that students MUST attend a lab exercise if they expect to write a lab report for that exercise. If you were not present at an exercise assigned by your TA for a lab report, you will be required to write a report on an exercise you DID attend instead. 

For a description of your responsibilities in preparing written reports and the rules concerning academic misconduct, please see p. 108: Regulations for Academic Discipline. 

Text matching software (Turnitin®) will be used to screen assignments in this course. This is being done to verify that use of all materials and sources in assignments is documented. Students will be given an option if they do not want to have their assignment screened by Turnitin®. In the first week of the term, details will be provided on the UW LEARN Website about arrangements and alternatives for the use of Turnitin® in this course. 

There will be TWO Turnitin® Dropboxes: a DRAFT Dropbox and a FINAL SUBMISSION Dropbox for each of the lab reports required in this course. Lab reports can be submitted for a draft review for Turnitin® in the DRAFT Dropbox. Students can submit a single draft for review that will NOT be marked, are not examined by TAs or the Instructor for assessment, and do not become part of the review database. Any issues highlighted are for the student to correct BEFORE submission. 

Students will need to submit their final draft SEPARATELY. Submitting to the DRAFT Dropbox DOES NOT mean you have handed in the assignment – ONLY reports in the Final Submission Dropbox will be considered as “handed in” to meet report deadlines. Students are NOT required to hand in a printed copy of their reports (see MARK DISTRIBUTION above for due dates). 

Students can find a separate document on the UW LEARN site in the Course Information folder with detailed instructions for using the DRAFT and FINAL SUBMISSION Dropboxes, and how to interpret the matching report generated by the Turnitin® software. 

Attendance At Your Laboratory Session 

Only medical certificates of incapacitation (requiring a Verification of Illness – VIF) or dire emergencies are accepted excuses for absence from your laboratory period. If you foresee circumstances ahead of time that will make it impossible for you to attend your normal laboratory period (such as job interviews or participation in athletic events), please make arrangements with Mike Allen, B1-381 (mike.allen@uwaterloo.ca) as soon as possible to attend another lab section that week. Because of the severe demands of time, space and equipment, switching to another section may not always be possible. Please note that laboratory exercises CANNOT be “reset” to any other day or time other than those originally scheduled. 

Students who miss THREE or more labs (with or without documentation) have missed more than 30% of the Lab sessions will receive an INC and will not be allowed to write the Lab Final Exam. 

Also, students who do not hand in at least TWO of the three required lab reports (with or without documentation) have failed to complete the minimum 65% of the graded material (written assignments) and will receive an INC and will not be allowed to write the Lab Final Exam 

If EITHER situation above occurs, students will be assigned a grade of INC, and be required to complete any missed lab sessions (and/or lab reports) the next time the course is offered and will also write the Lab Final Exam in that same subsequent term. Currently this lab course is offered in the Winter term every year, and in the Spring term as needed. 

For more information on missing lab sessions, report deadlines, or exams, and how to obtain a VIF, see p. 110: Missed Labs, Assignments or Examinations.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as working in a lab and writing lab reports. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

KIN 611:

An assessment of research in the biomechanics of human motion including electromyography, muscle modelling, link segment modelling and analysis, energy and power analysis. The above concepts will be utilized in the assessment of athletes and both the normal and atypical population in sport and rehabilitation contexts.

View requirements for KIN 611

This student-driven seminar course is designed to provide an historical overview coupled with current research trends in biomechanics. Biomechanical concepts will be utilized in the assessment of normal, athletic and atypical populations encountered in sport and rehabilitation contexts. The major topics
covered will vary from year to year depending on recent research activity and student interest.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Articulate their knowledge of biomechanical contributions from historical and state-of-the-art perspectives
Critically appraise, analyze, discuss and debate the merits and short-comings of original biomechanical research design, methodology, analysis and interpretation within an appropriate historical context
Evaluate a body of biomechanical literature to identify future research goals
Develop and articulate a compelling, evidence-based and balanced scientific argument

Student Assessment

Component Value
Seminar & Discussion 30
Research Pitch Outline 20
Grant Proposal 30
Engagement & Professionalism 20

Your course grade will be derived from four components:

Seminar & Discussion (30%)

The majority of classes will be led by students. Topic selections will be completed in the first week of class, and guidelines will be presented during the term. 

Biomechanics Research Pitch (20%)

The purposes of the research pitch are to identify an important research gap in biomechanics and apply knowledge in biomechanics, research design and methods to develop a state-of-the-art experiment that addresses the research gap. This assignment can be viewed as an initial pitch for an academic grant application, an application for an industrially funded contract, or a pitch to your employer for support. Students will submit their outline on LEARN by Monday, February 24th at 11:55pm. Further details regarding this assignment will be released throughout the term.

Grant Proposal (30%)

Students will expand on this research pitch into a full grant proposal. Depending on the nature of the research, students will select an appropriate funding agency and complete the requisite application. This submission will be due on LEARN by Monday, April 7th at 11:55pm. Further details will be released throughout the term.

Engagement & Professionalism (20%)

A great class relies on discussion. Please engage as an audience member, with a focus on quality, not quantity:

  • Examples of high level engagement include being prepared with pre-readings, conveying knowledge that is relevant to the discussion, listening to the opinions of others, and asking questions that encourage deeper understanding of biomechanics.
  • Examples of low level engagement include being unprepared for class, conveying limited knowledge or knowledge that is not relevant to the discussion, repeating material, difficulty listening to others (e.g., overtalking others), and not asking questions.

Professional behaviours include attending class, punctuality, respectful interactions, ability to give and receive constructive behaviours and encourages the participation of others. Non-judgemental interactions are essential to professional behaviours.

Generative AI

Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

KIN 415:

This course explores current clinical neurophysiological concepts important for the control of human movement as well as current techniques used in clinical neurophysiology. Emphasis is placed on the origin of bioelectrical activity underlying human movement and the application to the diagnosis of movement pathology following nervous system injury.

View requirements for KIN 415

Emphasis is also placed on the neurophysiology underlying human movement pathologies and the application of this understanding to rehabilitation following injury to the central nervous system. Specific topics of discussion include cellular neurophysiology, neuroplasticity, spinal cord injury, stroke, Parkinson’s disease and multiple sclerosis.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Describe neurophysiological principles of the central nervous system in healthy and pathological states.
2. Understand and explain techniques used in human clinical neurophysiology.
3. Critically read and evaluate original neurophysiological research papers including the methodology used and the interpretation of the results.
4. Explain the pathophysiology of various nervous systems diseases/disorders and how methods can be applied to the diagnosis/study of pathophysiology.

Research Paper Discussions & Group Seminar Presentation (15%):    

  1. Across the term selected lectures (4) will be used as seminars where there will be group discussions of a research paper provided by the instructor on LEARN. There will be time allotted during class for group discussion followed by an overall summary of the paper.  These papers will use techniques discussed during the term in the context of neurorehabilitation of the related system.  Content from these papers / discussions will be evaluated on the midterm and/or final exams.
  2. For these discussions students will work in groups of ~4-5.  In Seminars 3 and 4, groups will be required to give an oral presentation of a research paper selected from those provided by the instructor on LEARN.  These papers will use techniques discussed in class in the context of neurorehabilitation.  Presentations should be no longer than 15 minutes and will be followed by 5 minutes of discussion.  Further details will be provided in lecture / on Learn (15%)

 

INSTRUCTIONS FOR PAPER ASSIGNMENT (25%)

  • The deadline for handing in the term paper is Thursday, April 3, 2024 at midnight in a dropbox on LEARN
  • Papers handed in late will be penalized 10% per day.

Select one of the research papers listed on the course website as the basis for the assignment (these will be listed under Course Materials on LEARN).

The assignment is to write a report based on the paper you select that incorporates the following criteria: 

  1. Summary of the main objectives, hypotheses and findings of the paper. (approx. 1 page) – 5 marks
  2. Discuss the neurophysiological principles investigated in the research paper. (approx. 2-3 pages) – 15 marks
  3. Critical review: Specifically, summarize and comment on the dependent measures used to address the objectives / hypothesis. Discuss the strengths and weaknesses of the study? (approx. 2-3 pages) – 15 marks
  4. Future directions &/or Clinical Application: Discuss potential future research directions to follow up from this study &/or the clinical implications of this work. For suggested research, include what you think would be the best measures to use. (approx. 2 pages) – 10 marks

There will be 5 additional marks for writing style, including spelling and grammar. Proofread carefully!

The report should be a maximum of 9 pages double-spaced (1-inch margin, 12-point font). References may be included on a separate page.

Administrative Policy

Online Resources

The instructor will use LEARN for all communications, please check your @uwaterloo.ca email address regularly or link your UW account to a 3rd party email service to stay up-to-date. MS Teams: There is a KIN 415 MS Team to support this course. The resource is intended as a contingency in case of a switch to remote learning. Students will be notified via email and LEARN if a switch to remote learning is necessary. MS Teams will also be the preferred format for 1-on-1 or small group meetings with the instructors if meeting outside office hours.

Self-isolation

If you test positive for COVID-19 or are told to self-isolate it is important that you communicate with your instructor(s) and complete the self-declaration of illness form on Quest. It is your responsibility to connect with your peers to get caught up on lecture notes. If your self-isolation period interferes with a due date for a course element (e.g., iClicker/attendance, midterm, presentation), then you must let your instructor(s) know so that they can have a discussion with you regarding a possible accommodation. 

Contingency Planning

Ideally, KIN 415 will run in-person, as scheduled. In the event of a disruption to our ability to meet due to COVID-19 restrictions, the instructor will communicate a course continuation strategy via email and LEARN postings. The following contingency plans have been set to mitigate disruptions due to COVID-19.

Long-Term Disruptions (whole group)

If it is not possible to continue with in-person lectures for an extended period of time (i.e., more than 3 lectures) due to University-wide changes in public health guidelines or if the instructor must self-isolate, then KIN 415 will transition to pre-recorded online lectures. Lecture recordings will be posted to LEARN. 

Short-Term Disruptions (whole group)

In the event of a short-term disruption to the meet pattern (i.e. 1-3 lectures), KIN 415 will transition to synchronous online lectures via MS Teams. Lectures will occur during regularly-scheduled meet times. Online synchronous lectures will be recorded and posted to LEARN. 

Individual Disruptions

It will not be possible to record in-person lectures in anticipation of unexpected absences. You should reach out to peers and/or use posted lecture slides to catch-up on missed material. Please inform the instructor if you must miss multiple lectures due to self-isolation. In rare occurrences, it may be possible to include isolating students remotely, pending availability of necessary infrastructure.

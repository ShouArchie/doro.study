Course Description

KIN 446:

Selected examples of nutrient metabolism will be examined in detail in regard to dietary intake, metabolism, and mechanistic effects on human health. Comprehensive coverage of all nutrients is not intended, but rather the examples will be examined in detail with a focus on research techniques and approaches, and various factors that interact with and influence nutrition.

View requirements for KIN 446

KIN 646:

Selected nutrients will be examined in detail in regards to dietary intake, metabolism, metabolism and mechanistic effects on human health. Comprehensive coverage of nutrients is not intended, but the specific nutrients examined will include at least one example each from lipids, proteins, carbohydrates, water soluble vitamins, lipid soluble vitamins and minerals.

View requirements for KIN 646

An example of a nutrition-disease interaction will be examined in detail for each major macronutrient category (i.e., lipids, proteins and amino acids, and carbohydrates). Folate, B12 and human health will be discussed in the context of recent data surrounding mandatory fortification. An overview of emerging molecular fields within the nutritional sciences will also be examined. This course will focus on developing editing skills.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Understand detailed examples of biochemical and physiological aspects of nutrition.
2. Understand how rational dietary therapy based on understanding underlying biochemical pathways can be used to treat specific diseases.
3. Use AI to generate a starting document and identify critical weaknesses.
4. Have an enhanced ability to understand the scientific methods used to study nutrition.

  1. Lectures and Tests (95% of final grade)

This class will be conducted using a “Hybrid” model. Lectures will not be delivered ‘in-person’, but rather will be made available online as pre-recorded segments that follow the Lecture Slide Decks. We will use the classroom for an optional "Meet and Greet" in the first week of classes, and to provide an option to watch Lorenzo’s Oil together. A copy of Lorenzo's Oil and notes for a 'self-guided watching' of the movie will also be provided online. 

There are 9 lecture decks, and 5 associated tests worth 19% each of the final grade, for a total of 95% of the final grade. 

The lectures have been subdivided into short recordings, to make it easier to download and watch them. All of the lecture content will be available online. There are detailed notes below the slides that accompany the recordings, to help you understand the course materials. In order to access all of the required content that you are responsible for in this course, you must review the slides, and also read the notes below each slide, in addition to watching the videos. Tests will be based on content in the slides, in the notes, and in the videos. 

In the first 2 weeks, the lecture material covers how nutrition is studied, including limitations and unique aspects of the field, scientific writing and how scientific review papers are written, as well as gene-diet interactions, which are becoming common in studies on nutrition in humans. 

Test #1 covers material from Slide Decks #1 and #3 only. The material in Slide Deck #2 will not be tested, but will be useful as you start to think about your AI Critical Evaluation Assignment. It is also a useful resource to help guide you in accessing and contributing to the scientific literature, which is an excellent way to enhance your understanding of topics you are curious about during your undergraduate studies, and in the years beyond!

In subsequent weeks, the lecture material has been structured to cover each of the macronutrients, as well as B12 & folate as sample micronutrients, with an emphasis on understanding how each dietary factor can interact with metabolic physiology to create and treat disease. The lectures include historical information on how diagnostic mysteries were solved to uncover the causes of these diseases, and find rationale dietary therapies.

We will watch the movie Lorenzo’s Oil, which provides both a historical overview of the elucidation of the etiology of X-linked Adrenoleukodystrophy, which led to an important therapy, and a medically accurate portrayal of the clinical development and course of the disease. A copy of the movie and notes for viewing will be available in D2L. 

Test #2 will cover material from Slide Deck #4 on Protein and Phenylketonuria.

Test #3 will cover material from Slide Deck #5 on Fructose and Hepatosteatosis.

Test #4 will cover material from Slide Decks #6, 7 and 8 on Lipid Biochemistry and Essential Fatty Acids, X-linked Adrenoleukodystrophy, and on the Movie: Lorenzo’s Oil. There will be a small number of questions from the movie on this test.

Test #5 will cover material from Slide Deck #9 on the masking of a B12 deficiency by folate, as well as other aspects of the metabolism and disease interactions of these vitamins. 

All tests will be available online in the Quizzes section of Learn for a period of one (1) academic week. Tests will open on the indicated date at 12:01am, and will close on the indicated date at 11:59pm.

Tests are “open-book”, which means that you can use your notes from class while writing the test. However, you must complete the tests alone. You are NOT allowed to communicate or collaborate with others regarding the tests before, during, or after the tests. You are NOT allowed to make copies of the questions or otherwise record the test material, share the test material, or discuss the test material with others inside the class, or outside the class. 

After a test, you are welcome to make an appointment with the instructor or TA to review you test questions and answers.  

DROPPING THE LOWEST TEST MARK: I know that sometimes we all have a bad (test) day! Based on helpful student feedback and requests, a change has been made to the test grading scheme. If you complete all tests, your lowest test mark will be dropped from the overall test weighting. Thus, although each test is initially worth 19% in our grading scheme, giving the tests in this class a total assessment weighting of 95% of the final grade, your lowest test will be dropped from the grading scheme at the end of the term, and your other 4 better tests will be raised in proportionate value to have a weighted value of 23.75% each of the final mark. Thus, tests will still account for 95% of the final grade, but will be based on your best 4 marks.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper citation and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will be discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Absences in Quest

Religious or creed-related absence

Undergraduate students can now submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration in Quest. This allows students to register the dates of potential conflict with coursework. As the dates of important religious observances are generally known well in advance, students must consult with their instructors within two weeks of the announcement of the due date or scheduled examination date for which academic accommodation is being sought. 

Short-term absence (once/term) – Undergraduate students only

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason (no documentation required). A short-term absence covers up to two calendar days during the formal lecture period. Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), and Studio (STU) courses/class components do not qualify for accommodation as a result of a self-declared short-term absence. There is no expectation that a self-declared short-term absence be total; that is, students may elect to participate in any course and complete any course element(s) despite not being required to. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

COVID-19-related absence (once/term) 

Students can self-declare an absence of up to 10 days due to COVID-19-related illness. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in online components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

 

Absences Requiring Documentation - Absence Database – Undergraduate students only

Absence due to illness  

When students are unable to meet academic obligations due to illness, they are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their Verification of Illness Form (VIF) to the site. The University of Waterloo Verification of Illness Form (VIF) is normally the only medical documentation accepted to support requests for academic consideration. Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation to their instructors, teaching assistants, or advisors. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their health condition. Once approved, the student and their instructors will receive a confirmation email of the student’s absence. Course instructors will determine how missed course components are accommodated. 

Absence due to extenuating circumstances

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (bereavement, family illness, varsity sports, etc.), students are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their official documentation (e.g., plane tickets, court documents, police reports, etc.) to the site. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact instructors within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their circumstances. 

Important note: Travel that results in arrival to campus after the first day of class, absence before or after a reading break or departure from campus during the final exam period DO NOT warrant accommodation and any missed academic obligations will receive a grade of zero.

Accommodations

False claims of illness or false claims of the circumstances outlined above and/or the submission of false supporting documentation of extenuating circumstances constitute an academic offence that will result in disciplinary action under Policy 71. 

Adjustment of due dates or deferrals of term tests or final examinations are not automatic upon the presentation of suitable documentation. Instructors will use this documentation along with all information available to them when determining whether accommodation is warranted. When instructors elect to provide an accommodation, the options available to students vary based on the nature of the extenuating circumstances they are facing, and on the type of evaluation they were unable to complete on time. 

For in-term assignments, instructors may use their discretion and allow an extension.

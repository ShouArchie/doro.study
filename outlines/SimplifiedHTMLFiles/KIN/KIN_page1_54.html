Course Description

KIN 470:

An examination of current major issues and trends in Kinesiology. Students select areas of major interest from a series of faculty introduced topics.

View requirements for KIN 470

Welcome to KIN 470: Kinesiology Seminar (Remote Measurement of Health in Neurologic Diseases)! My name is Beth Godkin and I’m looking forward to learning together. KIN 470 is a seminar course that encourages the application of knowledge and skills acquired in your 100-300 level courses and provides an opportunity to further develop skills related to knowledge acquisition and communication. In this section, students will develop skills including literature searching, appraisal and interpretation, verbal presentation and writing skills, and peer evaluation while focusing on topics related to remote health measurement in neurologic diseases.

Our learning will take place on the traditional territory of the Neutral, Anishnaabeg, and Haudenosaunee peoples. The University of Waterloo is situated on the Haldimand Tract, the land promised to the Six Nations that includes six miles on each side of the Grand River. Our active work toward reconciliation takes place across our campuses through research, learning, teaching, and community building, and is centralized within the Office of Indigenous Relations. For more information about the purpose of territorial acknowledgements, please see the CAUT Guide to Acknowledging First Peoples and Traditional Territory.

While I recognize our course syllabus is long, it is very important as it outlines our class norms and expectations—please read it thoroughly!

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Complete advanced learning in a specific topic within/related to Kinesiology.
Apply self-directed learning skills to an independent project.
Communicate effectively, as demonstrated through verbal presentations, facilitated discussions, and original writing for a scientific audience.
Provide quality peer evaluations.

Short (Non-Specialist Audience) Presentation (15%)

Each student will choose a topic related to remote measurement of health in neurologic diseases to explain to a general audience (i.e., non-specialists, someone without a scientific background); each presentation must be on a different topic area that will be chosen by the end of class on January 9th. The presenter will prepare a 5-minute presentation on their topic using appropriate supporting materials such as PowerPoint (1-5 slides recommended) so that anyone can understand the topic. There will be a 5-minute interactive open feedback and discussion period following each presentation where fellow students will ask questions, provide positive and constructive comments, and share ideas for consideration on the presentation. 

All presentation files are due on the same day: Friday January 17th @ 11:59 PM. Regardless of your presentation date, your presentation files (e.g., PowerPoint slides) must be completed and submitted by this deadline. Files will be uploaded to the LEARN Dropbox. This is to ensure equity between students in the class who may be presenting many weeks apart. 

Four students will be selected to provide feedback for each presenter. You will not be required to submit evaluations on the dates of your own presentations. Remember that your comments on the evaluation form are very useful for the professional development of the presenter. You will be expected to include a remark outlining something done well and something that could be further developed. The peer evaluation will be submitted electronically (using Microsoft Forms) no later than 11:59pm on the evening of the presentation. Feedback can be completed using a laptop during the presentation. Alternatively, feedback forms can be completed on paper, but the student must upload their responses to Microsoft Forms by the deadline that same evening. Positive and constructive comments will be evaluated by the instructor and must accurately reflect the quality of the presentation. The peer evaluations (worth 5%) will be combined with the instructor’s comments (10%) and will be provided anonymously to the presenter.

Long (Scientific) Presentation (35%)

Each student will develop and prepare a 15-minute presentation on their selected topic using appropriate support materials, such as PowerPoint. The topic of the presentation should be the same as the Short Presentation. If the student wishes to make an adjustment to their topic, they must discuss the change with the course instructor. The presentation should be based on scientific evidence, especially from primary peer reviewed research articles. Other website materials, anecdotal articles, lecture notes and opinions are allowed, but should be used to provide context for the scientific literature.

Like with the short presentation, all presentation files are due on the same day: Monday, February 24th @ 12:00PM (noon). Regardless of your presentation date, your presentation files must be completed and submitted by this deadline to ensure equity between students in the class who may be presenting many weeks apart. Files will be uploaded to the LEARN Dropbox.

The peer evaluation will be submitted electronically (using Microsoft Forms) no later than 11:59pm on the evening of the presentation. Feedback can be completed using a laptop during the presentation. Alternatively, feedback forms can be completed on paper, but the student must upload their responses to Microsoft Forms by the deadline that same evening. Positive and constructive comments will be evaluated by the instructor and must accurately reflect the quality of the seminar. The peer evaluations (10%) will be combined with the instructor’s comments (25%) and will be provided anonymously to the presenter.

Research Report (35%)

A written, scientific report is due on Friday April 11th @ 11:59PM. The report will be uploaded to the Dropbox on LEARN. Like the presentation, there should be enough background information so that the reader can understand the topic you selected and include a detailed literature review (minimum of 10 references) that supports how the problem has been identified and addressed thus far.

Overall, the paper must be at least 5 pages, but no more than 7 (double spaced; Font: Arial or Times New Roman; Margins: 1” all around; no condensed spacing). This does not include title page, figures, tables, and references. You are encouraged to use headings and sub-headings to appropriately structure and organize your report. 

This report should not simply summarize the literature but should show that you’ve thought critically about the topic and interpreted the findings of the papers you’ve read.

Students are provided the opportunity to submit a one-page draft by Friday March 21st @ 11:59PM to receive constructive feedback prior to the full paper submission. Some things to keep in mind when considering this optional submission:

  • This early submission is optional and provided as an opportunity to receive written feedback to help guide the remainder of the written assignment.
  • Submission can be crafted in a method of your choice. Some examples might include bulleted format (full topic sentences with supporting bullets), rough framework, or a single fully written page from your current draft. However, it must be only one page and adhere to the formatting guidelines (1” margins, 12-pt font size, double spaced). Anything beyond one page will not be reviewed.
  • Feedback will be provided within ten days of your submission. Feedback may include suggestions on high-level spelling/grammar/sentence structure, content/organization of arguments, clarity, and/or tone of your document.

While this is not required, it is strongly encouraged as an opportunity to improve your writing skills prior to your final graded work.

Attendance & Engagement (10%)

In-person attendance is required in KIN 470. You will also be expected to actively engage during our classes (this looks like asking questions, offering ideas, active listening, participating in activities etc.). This may also include serving as a presentation chair/moderator to introduce peers during presentations. Your class attendance and engagement will be assessed throughout the term by the instructor. 

Quality of Peer Evaluations (5%)

You will be required to submit peer evaluations for both the Short (Non-Specialist Audience) Presentation and the Long (Scientific) Presentation. For each presentation, you will be responsible for submitting feedback for ~4 different presenters. In turn, you will receive feedback from ~4 peers. An online Microsoft Form will be used to collect feedback. The peer evaluation must be submitted no later than 11:59PM on the evening of the presentations you are assigned to evaluate. Feedback can then be shared with each student anonymously. The quality of your feedback will be assessed and contribute to 5% of your overall course grade.

KIN 470 Assessment Guidelines

Individual-authentic work: All graded assignments in KIN 470 are expected to be individual, authentically created work. Therefore, no sharing, collaborating, copying, or using classmates’ work (or previous cohorts’ assignments) on your assignments will be tolerated. 

Generative AI tools: This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as communicating effectively (written and verbal) via original material. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is NOT permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

In alignment with the University of Waterloo’s academic integrity office’s website on Artificial intelligence and ChatGPT, “A student who does not comply with the instructors rules about the use of [AI] tools will be subject to Policy 71 and an investigation into academic misconduct.” You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of your work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee.

Intellectual Property Copyright Violations

Academic integrity is a cornerstone of any educational institution. Students and faculty alike benefit when academic integrity is well understood, encouraged and maintained. The University and faculty strongly discourage students from participating in/uploading course materials on online sharing platforms, such as Course Hero, OneClass and similar platforms. In many cases, the material in question is created and owned by the faculty member or instructor. Faculty are indeed presumed to be the Intellectual Property Owners of the course materials. 

Students are strongly advised to err on the side of caution and ask for permission from their instructors before sharing and/or distributing any course materials. These course materials will usually be made available only for personal use by students. Students may not distribute or reproduce the materials, whether for commercial purposes or not, without the express written consent of the faculty or instructor. Neither faculty nor the instructors waive their intellectual property rights when granting limited use of their course materials to students. 

Uploading course materials, including lectures, quizzes, and exams, onto online sharing platforms such as Course Hero, OneClass, Facebook, Reddit, and similar platforms is considered to be a copyright violation, regardless of whether an individual did so intentionally or unintentionally. Ignorance of the law is not an excuse.

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. Intellectual property includes items such as: 

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository). Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years. In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed written consent is considered a violation of intellectual property rights. Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online. The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

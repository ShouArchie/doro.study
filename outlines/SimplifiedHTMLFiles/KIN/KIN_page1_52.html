Course Description

KIN 470:

An examination of current major issues and trends in Kinesiology. Students select areas of major interest from a series of faculty introduced topics.

View requirements for KIN 470

Welcome to KIN 470!

This section of the Kinesiology seminars is focused on concepts surrounding development, design, testing, application, and assessment of tools used to improve human activity. KIN 470 is a seminar course that encourages the application of knowledge and skills acquired in your 100-300 level courses and provides an opportunity to further develop skills related to knowledge acquisition and communication. In this section we will develop skills including literature searching, appraisal and interpretation, oral presentation and writing skills, and peer evaluation while focusing on topics surrounding concepts of assistive design. 

What kind of considerations would you need to make a better joint replacement? What about an exoskeleton to help people at work? Perhaps a better hockey helmet? How would you see if these things are better or worse than current design? What kind of materials should you consider? Our goal will be to examine how we implement things to better our life through the use of the virtuous circle - if we can see something, then we can measure it, and ultimately implement a solution before starting the cycle again.

Our learning will take place on the traditional territory of the Neutral, Anishnaabeg, and Haudenosaunee peoples. The University of Waterloo is situated on the Haldimand Tract, the land promised to the Six Nations that includes six miles on each side of the Grand River. As part of the UW community, I encourage you to engage with the resources and allyship information provided by the Office of Indigenous Relations as a starting point.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Complete advanced learning in a specific topic within/related to Kinesiology
Expand skillsets in self-directed learning
Develop skills in scientific literacy
Develop communication skills through oral presentations, facilitated discussions and through original writing for a scientific audience
Enhance assessment skills through peer evaluation

SHORT (NON-SPECIALIST AUDIENCE) PRESENTATION (15%)

Each student will choose a topic related to physical activity behaviour change to explain to a general audience (i.e., non-specialists, someone without a scientific background); each presentation must be on a different topic area that will be chosen by the end of class on January 10th. The presenter will prepare a 5-minute presentation on their topic using appropriate supporting materials such as PowerPoint (1-5 slides recommended) so that anyone can understand the topic.

All presentation files are due on the same day: Friday, January 17th @ 11:55 PM. Regardless of your presentation date, your presentation files (e.g., PowerPoint slides) must be completed and submitted by this deadline. Files will be uploaded to the LEARN Dropbox. This is to ensure equity between students in the class who may be presenting many weeks apart. 

There will be a 5-minute interactive open feedback and discussion period following each presentation where fellow students will ask questions, provide positive and constructive comments, and share ideas for consideration on the presentation.

Four students will be selected to provide feedback for each presenter. You will not be required to submit evaluations on the dates of your own presentations. Remember that your comments on the evaluation form are very useful for the professional development of the presenter. You will be expected to include a remark outlining something done well and something that could be further developed. The peer evaluation will be submitted electronically (using Microsoft Forms) no later than 11:59pm on the evening of the presentation. Feedback can be completed using a laptop during the presentation. Alternatively, feedback forms can be completed on paper, but the student must upload their responses to Forms by the deadline that same evening. Positive and constructive comments will be evaluated by the instructor and must accurately reflect the quality of the presentation. The peer evaluations (worth 5%) will be combined with the instructor’s comments (10%) and will be provided anonymously to the presenter.

LONG (SCIENTIFIC) PRESENTATION (35%)

Each student will develop and prepare a 15-minute presentation on their selected topic using appropriate support materials, such as PowerPoint. The topic of the presentation should be the same as the Short Presentation. If the student wishes to make an adjustment to their topic, they must discuss the change with the course instructor. The presentation should be based on scientific evidence especially from primary peer reviewed research articles. Other website materials, anecdotal articles, lecture notes and opinions are allowed, but should be used provide context for the scientific literature.

Like with the short presentation, all presentation files are due on the same day: Monday, February 24th @ 10am. Regardless of your presentation date, your presentation files must be completed and submitted by this deadline to ensure equity between students in the class who may be presenting many weeks apart. Files will be uploaded to the LEARN Dropbox.

The peer evaluation will be submitted electronically (using Microsoft Forms) no later than 11:59pm on the evening of the presentation. Feedback can be completed using a laptop during the presentation. Alternatively, feedback forms can be completed on paper, but the student must upload their responses to Forms by the deadline that same evening. Positive and constructive comments will be evaluated by the instructor and must accurately reflect the quality of the seminar. The peer evaluations (10%) will be combined with the instructor’s comments (25%) and will be provided anonymously to the presenter.

SCIENTIFIC REPORT (35%)

A written, scientific report is due on Friday, April 7th at 11:55pm. The report will be uploaded to the Dropbox on LEARN. Like the presentation, there should be enough background information so that the reader can understand the topic you selected and include a detailed literature review (minimum of 10 references) that supports how the problem has been identified and addressed thus far.

Overall, the paper must be at least 5 pages, but no more than 7 (double spaced; Font: Arial or Times New Roman; Margins: 1” all around; no condensed spacing). This does not include title page, figures, tables, and references.

This report should not simply summarize the literature but should show that you’ve thought critically about the topic and interpreted the findings of the papers you’ve read.

Students are provided the opportunity to submit a one-page draft by March 24nd @ 10:00AM to receive constructive feedback prior to the full paper submission. Some things to keep in mind when considering this optional submission:

  • This early submission is optional and provided as an opportunity to receive written feedback to help guide the remainder of the written assignment.
  • Submission can be crafted in a method of your choice. Some examples might include bulleted format (full topic sentences with supporting bullets), rough framework, or a single fully written page from your current draft. However, it must be only one page and adhere to the formatting guidelines (1” margins, 12-pt font size, double spaced). Anything beyond one page will not be reviewed.
  • Feedback will be provided within ten days of your submission. Feedback may include suggestions on high-level spelling/grammar/sentence structure, content/organization of arguments, clarity, and/or tone of your document.

While this is not required, it is strongly encouraged as an opportunity to improve your writing skills prior to your final graded work.

ATTENDANCE & ENGAGEMENT (10%)

In-person attendance is required in KIN 470. You will also be expected to actively engage during our classes (this looks like asking questions, offering ideas, active listening, participating in activities etc.). This may also include serving as a presentation chair/moderator to introduce peers during presentations. Your class attendance and engagement will be assessed throughout the term by the instructor. 

QUALITY OF PEER EVALUATIONS & SELF-REFLECTION (5%)

You will be required to submit peer evaluations for both the Short (Non-Specialist Audience) Presentation and the Long (Scientific) Presentation. For each presentation, you will be responsible for submitting feedback for 4 different presenters. In turn, you will receive feedback from 4 peers. An online Microsoft Form will be used to collect feedback. The peer evaluation must be submitted no later than 11:59pm on the evening of the presentations you are assigned to evaluate. Feedback can then be shared with each student anonymously. The quality of your feedback will be assessed and contribute to 5% of your overall course grade.

OTHER COURSE INFORMATION

Individual-authentic work: All graded assignments in KIN 470 are expected to be individual, authentically created work. Therefore, no sharing, collaborating, copying, or using classmates’ work (or previous cohorts’ assignments) on your assignments will be tolerated. 

Submitting files: For written work, please submit assignments as PDF documents. For presentations, you are welcome to use and submit PowerPoint files. If you plan to use another technology, it is your responsibility to connect with me in advance to ensure I can open the file type on my computer. Your last submission to the LEARN Dropbox prior to the deadline will be graded. APA 7 will be used; proper referencing is expected for all graded submissions.

Late penalty: On all assessments, including peer-reviews, a penalty of 5% per day will be applied to all late submissions without documentation or prior discussions with the instructor. If you have a concern about a deadline, please reach out to discuss. 

Reviewing feedback: I am happy to go over your graded work and provide clarification when needed. However, students must wait 24 hours after feedback has been returned before contacting me; during this time, students are encouraged to review and reflect on the feedback received. After 24 hours, students have up to 1 week (7 days) to contact me to ask for further feedback or clarification.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as scientific literacy, literature search and review, and academic interpretation of research. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

Students are encouraged to reach out to campus supports if they need help with their coursework including: 

  • Student Success Office for help with skills like notetaking and time management
  • Writing and Communication Centre for assignments with writing or presentations
  • AccessAbility Services for documented accommodations
  • Library for research-based assignments

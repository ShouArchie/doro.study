Course Description

KIN 404:

This course will examine the environmental and physiological aspects of obesity and Type 2 Diabetes, emphasizing the molecular pathways involved in energy balance regulation. Topics to be covered include the epidemiology of obesity and Type 2 Diabetes, regulation of energy intake (appetite), energy storage and energy expenditure, and the role of physical inactivity and activity in the causes and prevention of obesity and Type 2 Diabetes.

Welcome to KIN 404: Physiological Basis of Obesity and Type 2 Diabetes! My name is Dr. Russ Tupling and I’m looking forward to learning together. KIN 404 is a lecture- and readings-based course that encourages the application of knowledge acquired in your 100-200 level courses and provides an opportunity to further develop knowledge related to human physiology, metabolism, exercise and nutrition. KIN 404 is a required course for the Medical Physiology Minor and a restricted elective course for the Human Nutrition Minor.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Explain the clinical definitions and assessments used for the diagnosis of obesity, insulin resistance, pre-diabetes, and diabetes mellitus
Explain the neuroendocrine regulation of appetite and energy balance
Explain leptin resistance in obesity
Understand the contribution of major organs, metabolic pathways, and cellular processes to tissue and whole body energy expenditure
Explain the role of brown adipose tissue and skeletal muscle in adaptive thermogenesis
Explain insulin signalling in skeletal muscle, liver, and adipose tissue
Explain the link between obesity, insulin resistance and diabetes
Explain the mechanism of action of the major anti-diabetes drugs
Explain the hormonal and metabolic adaptions to weight-loss that contribute to weight regain
Explain the benefits of exercise and calorie restriction for individuals with obesity and type 2 diabetes
Explain the environmental factors that contribute to the obesity epidemic
Explain the mechanisms of exercise-induced hypoglycemia in people with type 1 diabetes

Term Tests – Four term tests worth 24% each on lecture content and assigned readings.

Term Tests are closed book, no aids are allowed. More detail will be provided in class and on Learn.

i>Clicker quizzes – 1% per section during Group Competition lectures (see below)

i>Clicker quizzes during the Group Competition lectures are closed book, no aids are allowed. However, you may work with other members in your group to discuss the questions and seek out the correct answers.  

Bonus marks: It is possible to earn one bonus mark per section up to a maximum of 4% (i.e. it is possible to achieve 104% in this course) by performing moderate-vigorous physical activity (MVPA, 3-6 METs) at least 150 min/week (CSEP guidelines), which must be tracked using a wearable device (e.g. fitbit, apple watch, garmin) and/or physical activity app (Strava is highly recommended), and shared with the TA or instructor's PhD student (Maryam Khan mm9khan@uwaterloo.ca). Physical activity intensity will be determined using the Adult Compendium of Physical Activities. Each section is 3 weeks so students must perform 150 min of MVPA for all 3 weeks of a section to earn 1 bonus mark for that section. The schedule is:

Section One (Regulation of Energy Intake): Week One - Jan. 6-12; Week Two - Jan. 13-19; Week Three - Jan. 20-26

Section Two (Regulation of Energy Expenditure): Week One - Jan. 27 - Feb. 2; Week Two - Feb. 3-9; Week Three - Feb. 10-16

GROUP COMPETITION BASED ON ASSIGNED READINGS                        

There are 2 research articles and 1 review article that will be assigned reading for each section. It is recommended that students read the review article prior to the first lecture for that section. Students should read all the articles prior to the Group Competition class for each section.  I have divided the class into 8 groups as follows: Group 1A, BIOMEDSCI students (1st half of alphabet); Group 1B, BIOMEDSCI students (2nd half of alphabet); Group 2A, BIOL students; Group 2B, SCI students; Group 3A, HLTH students; Group 3B, CS, ERS, LIBST, PSYS, PD, and Exchange students; Group 4A, KIN Regular students; and Group 4B, KIN Co-op students. The groups are posted on D2L/Learn so you can verify which group you belong to. All Group One members (1A and 1B) will be responsible for generating 3 multiple choice questions (see how to format your questions below) based on the assigned readings and lectures (i.e. 1 question for each research article and 1 question based either on the review article or the lectures) for Section One which they will post on D2L/Learn at least 24 hours prior to the Group Competition Class (worth 1%) and the other groups (all members) will attempt to answer selected questions during the Group Competition Class using i>clickers (worth 1% for a passing grade, i.e. >50% correct). All Group Two members (2A and 2B) will generate MC questions based on the assigned readings and lectures for Section Two and so on, which means each group will be tested for 3 out of the 4 sections and the group with the highest total (%) of correct answers at the end of the term will win a pizza party with me. It is possible that some of the student-generated questions could be used on the Term Test. This format is intended to generate discussion and review content related to the assigned readings and the lectures and hopefully to help prepare students for the Term Tests. The assigned readings have been selected to present current research from some of the best obesity and diabetes researchers in the world. Some of the readings cover topics that are not covered in detail in lectures.

STUDENT ABSENCES – ACADEMIC CONSIDERATIONS AND ACCOMMODATIONS

Students have the following options for declaring an absence during the Winter 2025 term:

Absences in Quest

Religious or creed-related absence

Undergraduate students can now submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration in Quest. This allows students to register the dates of potential conflict with coursework. As the dates of important religious observances are generally known well in advance, students must consult with their instructors within two weeks of the announcement of the due date or scheduled examination date for which academic accommodation is being sought. 

Short-term absence (once/term) – Undergraduate students only

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason (no documentation required). A short-term absence covers up to two calendar days during the formal lecture period. Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), and Studio (STU) courses/class components do not qualify for accommodation as a result of a self-declared short-term absence. There is no expectation that a self-declared short-term absence be total; that is, students may elect to participate in any course and complete any course element(s) despite not being required to. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

COVID-19-related absence (once/term) 

Students can self-declare an absence of up to 10 days due to COVID-19-related illness. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in online components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Absences Requiring Documentation - Absence Database – Undergraduate students only

Absence due to illness  

When students are unable to meet academic obligations due to illness, they are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their Verification of Illness Form (VIF) to the site. The University of Waterloo Verification of Illness Form (VIF) is normally the only medical documentation accepted to support requests for academic consideration. Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation to their instructors, teaching assistants, or advisors. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their health condition. Once approved, the student and their instructors will receive a confirmation email of the student’s absence. Course instructors will determine how missed course components are accommodated. 

Absence due to extenuating circumstances

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (bereavement, family illness, varsity sports, etc.), students are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their official documentation (e.g., plane tickets, court documents, police reports, etc.) to the site. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact instructors within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their circumstances. 

Important note: Travel that results in arrival to campus after the first day of class, absence before or after a reading break or departure from campus during the final exam period DO NOT warrant accommodation and any missed academic obligations will receive a grade of zero.

Students' submissions are stored on a U.S. server, therefore students must be given an alternative (e.g., scaffolded assignment or annotated bibliography), if they are concerned about their privacy and/or security. Students will be given due notice, in the first week of the term and/or at the time assignment details are provided, about arrangements and alternatives for the use of Turnitin in this course.

It is the responsibility of the student to notify the instructor if they, in the first week of term or at the time assignment details are provided, wish to submit alternate assignment.

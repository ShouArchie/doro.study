Welcome to KIN 471b: Physical Activity Behaviour Change! My name is Dr. Sheereen Harris and I’m looking forward to learning together and exploring the theories and principles of exercise psychology and behaviour change. 

Our learning will take place on the traditional territory of the Neutral, Anishnaabeg, and Haudenosaunee peoples. The University of Waterloo is situated on the Haldimand Tract, the land promised to the Six Nations that includes six miles on each side of the Grand River. As part of the UW community, I encourage you to engage with the resources and allyship information provided by the Office of Indigenous Relations, as a starting point.

While I recognize our course syllabus is long, it is very important as it outlines our class norms and expectations—please read it thoroughly!

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe theory-based processes of physical activity behaviour change.​
Evaluate the suitability of specific behaviour change techniques for targeted use in physical activity interventions.
Develop targeted mass-media intervention materials promoting physical activity for a special population.
Deliver a tailored physical activity behaviour change intervention.

Student Assessment

Component Value
Physical Activity Measurement Assignment 15%
Behaviour Change Technique Presentation 20%
Target Population Intervention Project 25%
Individual Counselling Intervention Project 30%
Self-Evaluation 10%

Below are the due dates and submission details:

Physical Activity Measurement Assignment (15%)

Students will conduct a physical activity assessment on yourself using one self-report measure and one device-based measure. You will then complete a reflection comparing the two assessment tools including their advantages and disadvantages as well as reliability and validity in the context of behaviour change.

Behaviour Change Technique Presentation (20%)

In pairs, students will prepare and deliver a presentation critically reviewing commercially available smartphone applications commonly used to monitor and change physical activity behaviour. Groups will identify the behaviour change techniques used in the apps, rank the apps from best to worst, and provide evidence-based suggestions.

Target Population Intervention Project (25%) 

Students will develop skills required to create a print media intervention promoting physical activity in a target population. You will create a 1-page (single sided) colour brochure using a combination of text and images using specific behaviour change techniques and messaging techniques. You will also complete a written justification highlighting the content of your brochure and explaining your rationale guiding your decisions.

Individual Counselling Intervention Project (30%)

The assignment will consist of the following components: 

1. Case Report (20%)

You will complete a written case report including a literature review and background information, procedures and measures used throughout the intervention, a summary of key results, and a brief discussion and reflection of the intervention.

2. Case notes (5%)

You will maintain a set of case/session notes chronicling your reflections on each of your counselling sessions. Each reflection should include the objectives outlined in the lesson plan and any modifications, a description of what your client has done since your last meeting, the content of your discussions during the session, and your thoughts on future modifications for future sessions with your client.

3. Presentation (5%)

You will prepare and deliver a presentation summarizing key components from your intervention including a description of your client, baseline information related to your client, intervention objectives, post-intervention results, and a brief discussion on successes and challenges of the intervention.

Self-Evaluation (10%)

Your Self-Evaluation Grade will consist of regular class attendance, participation in lecture discussions and activities, asking questions, and contributing to the learning of your peers and instructor during lecture. You will complete and submit the Self-Evaluation Form at the end of term. 

Please Note: The Course Instructor will have final say and ability to modify your Self-Evaluation mark if your Self-Evaluation mark does not match your justification and is grotesquely inflated. So, please be honest – the purpose of including a Self-Evaluation mark is to encourage some dialogue and participation during lecture, as well as provide an opportunity to have a little control over one aspect of your final grade. If the self-evaluation is not submitted during this time, a student will receive a zero for this portion of the final grade.

Other Assessment Guidelines:

Authentic work: All graded assignments in KIN 453 are expected to be authentically created work by the individual (Measurement Assignment, Target Population Intervention Project, Individual Counselling Project) or group (Behaviour Change Technique Presentation). Therefore, no sharing, collaborating, copying, or using other classmates’ work (or previous cohorts’ assignments) on your assignments will be tolerated. 

Submitting Files: For written work, please submit assignments as Microsoft Word or PDF documents. For presentations, please submit PowerPoint files. If you plan to use another technology, it is your responsibility to connect with me in advance to ensure I can open the file type on my computer. Your last submission to the LEARN Dropbox prior to the deadline will be graded. APA 7 will be used; proper referencing is expected for all graded submissions.

Late Penalty: On all assessments, a penalty of 5% per day will be applied to all late submissions without documentation or prior discussions with the instructor. If you have a concern about a deadline, please reach out to discuss. 

Reviewing Feedback: I am happy to go over your graded work and provide clarification when needed. However, students must wait 24 hours after feedback has been returned before contacting me; during this time, students are encouraged to review and reflect on the feedback received. After 24 hours, students have up to 1 week (7 days) to contact me to ask for further feedback or clarification.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as communicating effectively (written and verbal) via original material. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including outlines, research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Academic regulations, student absences, and accommodations 

As a reminder, in Quest, students can declare these types of absences during the Fall 2024 term:

  1. Short-term absence (once/term) - an absence of up to 2 calendar days during the formal lecture period self-declared for any reason that prevents the student from meeting their academic obligations. Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), and Studio (STU) courses/class components do not qualify for accommodation as a result of a self-declared short-term absence. Students must notify their instructor(s) no later than 24 hours after the missed assessment(s). If possible, students should contact the instructor prior to the expected missed assessment(s). 
  2. COVID-19-related absence (once/term) - an absence of up to 10 days due to COVID-19-related illness.
  3. Verification of Illness - unable to meet academic obligations due to illness. Students complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their Verification of Illness Form (VIF) and/or other supporting documentation to the site. The webform includes a list of courses the student is currently enrolled in, and the student is required to indicate any missed elements. Students should not be submitting their Verification of Illness Form (or other documentation) to instructors or advisors. The UG Associate Dean’s Office will review each submission, including documentation, and determine whether to approve the absence request. Once approved, students and instructors will receive a confirmation email of the absence. Advisors will be notified if there are questions or concerns about a student’s well-being, such as submitting multiple VIFs in one academic term or documentation for a lengthy absence.
  4. Extenuating Circumstances – unable to meet academic obligations due to extenuating circumstances (bereavement, family illness, varsity sports, etc.). Students are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their official documentation (e.g., plane tickets, court documents, police reports, etc.) to the site. 

For all types of absence, students should submit documentation no later than 48 hours following the due date of the missed assessment. 

In addition to illness, there may be events that preclude students from meeting academic obligations. You can read more about academic regulations and accommodations here. The following are examples of valid circumstances and required documentation that may warrant academic accommodation:

  • A member of a varsity team travelling for competition: a letter/email from the Head Coach verifying the departure and return dates and times.
  • Death of a family member: a copy of the death certificate, a copy of the obituary or a link to the online obituary.
  • Travel: from time-to-time students are required to travel during an academic term for reasons NOT related to pleasure/vacation. If you are travelling and require accommodation, you must inform the instructor at least 2 weeks in advance of your travel, indicate the reason for travel and provide a boarding pass upon return to campus.

Important note: Travel that results in arrival to campus after the first day of class, absence before or after a reading break or departure from campus during the final exam period DO NOT warrant accommodation and any missed academic obligations will receive a grade of zero.

University Policy

Academic integrity: In order to maintain a culture of academic integrity, members of the University of Waterloo community are expected to promote honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.]

Grievance: A student who believes that a decision affecting some aspect of their university life has been unfair or unreasonable may have grounds for initiating a grievance. Read Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances, Section 4. When in doubt, please be certain to contact the department’s administrative assistant who will provide further assistance.

Discipline: A student is expected to know what constitutes academic integrity to avoid committing an academic offence, and to take responsibility for their actions. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.] A student who is unsure whether an action constitutes an offence, or who needs help in learning how to avoid offences (e.g., plagiarism, cheating) or about “rules” for group work/collaboration should seek guidance from the course instructor, academic advisor, or the undergraduate associate dean. Appeals: A decision made or penalty imposed under Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances (other than a petition) or Policy 71, Student Discipline may be appealed if there is a ground. A student who believes they have a ground for an appeal should refer to Policy 72, Student Appeals.

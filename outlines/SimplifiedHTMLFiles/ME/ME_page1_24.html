Course Description

ME 362:

Basic equations of two-dimensional flow, potential flow, exact viscous solutions. Introduction to lubrication, boundary layers, turbulence, and compressible flow. Turbomachinery fundamentals and applications. Selected advanced topics.

View requirements for ME 362

The material in ME 362 is an extension of ME 351 – Fluids I covering topics in theoretical and applied fluid mechanics.  Though we will review the main topics from Fluids I at the beginning of the course, familiarity and basic competence with the prerequisite material will be essential for success. The course relies on a solid background in: (1) calculus, including differentiation, integration, partial differentiation, and differential equations; (2) physics, including Newton’s laws and concepts of work and energy; and (3) thermodynamics, including the ideal gas law and the first law of thermodynamics. Since we will not be able to allocate time to review all of these concepts during class time, please brush up on these topics as required.

ME 362 introduces you to areas of fluid mechanics, both theoretical and applied, which were not covered in the Fluids 1 course. The first part of the course will focus on the theoretical side, and involves the derivation and solution of the coupled, nonlinear partial differential equations that govern multi-dimensional fluid motion. The second part includes applied topics such as compressible flow using approaches and tools similar to those presented in the Fluids 1 course. The aim is to give you an appreciation of the full scope of fluid mechanics, and how the different parts of the subject fit together.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Classify fluid flows and select appropriate quantitative methods for evaluation.
Develop quantitative models for common fluid flows in engineering applications.
Solve practical fluids engineering problems by applying appropriate assumptions and fluid mechanics principles.

Ungraded homework:    Each week problems requiring in depth application of the course concepts will be assigned. Complete solutions to these problems will be posted approximately one week after the problems are assigned. It is strongly recommended to do the problems before referring to the solution manuals. Remember that solution manuals will not be available during the mid-term or final exams. Keep up with lecture material and with weekly problem assignments.  Solve problems independently, before looking at solution sets. Exams require problem solving skills which can only develop by regularly doing the assigned problems.

Project: There is one project in the course. The project will be performed in groups of four students (groups of three or five students may be accepted depending on total number of groups, five students are maximum allowed in one group). Group sign up will be done through Learn. A list of suitable sports will be provided at the beginning of the term. Each group must choose a sport from the list and include their choice and email Akira Tiesma (TA) the selected sport. The sign-up deadline is Friday January 17, 2025.

Each group must submit a short and clear description of their project (one page maximum, including their selected sport, objectives and methodology) by email to Akira Tiesma (TA). This document will be due by Friday March 7, 2025, submitted to a dedicated Learn Dropbox, and will be graded by the TAs. The project short description will count for 2% of the total course grade. This will be used to provide feedback. No late submission is accepted for the short description. 

Report writing guidelines will be provided in a separate document early in the term. Electronic submission through the course web site dropbox will be required by Friday April 4, 2025, 11:55 pm. The project report will count for 13% of the total course grade.

The project report has a firm submission deadline. Late submission will receive a 10% mark deduction per day and will not be adjusted to hourly rate or other partial reduction. Thus, if students submit their project report 30 minutes or 1 hour after the due date and time, 10% of the mark of the project report will be deducted. If students submit their project one day and 30 minutes or one day and 1 hour after the due date and time, 20% of the mark of the assignment/project will be deducted, and so forth.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as engineering model development, data presentation, and technical report writing. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

ME 526:

Fatigue and fracture analysis of metallic components including welded joints. Review of test and design procedures. Sources of cyclic loading. Cyclic counting procedures and cumulative damage. S-N curves and effects of mean, residual and multiaxial stressing. Stress concentrations; scatter and fatigue life distributions. Transition temperature concepts. Linear elastic fracture mechanics analysis of fatigue crack propagation and fracture initiation. Crack arrest.

View requirements for ME 526

Fatigue is the accumulation of damage in a material due to repeated loading, which leads to crack formation and eventual failure. Fatigue analysis combines material properties, geometry, and stress history to predict a component's life. Fracture mechanics, on the other hand, deals with the unstable propagation of pre-existing cracks under monotonic loads, which can result in brittle or ductile failure depending on material toughness. This course explores both disciplines, providing tools for design, failure analysis, and service life prediction.

Learning Outcomes

No explicit learning outcomes defined for this course.
  • Understand the principles of fatigue and fracture mechanics.
  • Apply stress-based and strain-based fatigue analysis techniques.
  • Utilize fracture mechanics for crack propagation analysis.
  • Gain insight into practical applications through real-world examples.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course emphasizes the independent development and practice of specific skills, including fatigue analysis, fracture mechanics, material behavior modeling, and engineering failure analysis techniques. As such, the use of generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) tools, such as ChatGPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, to produce text, images, music, or code is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use of these tools, including running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete assessments, constitutes a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids).

Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and violates the fundamental values of academic integrity: honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility, and courage (ICAI, n.d.). For more information, refer to the following resources:

  • Policy 71 - Student Discipline: Policy 71 (https://uwaterloo.ca/secretariat/policies-procedures-guidelines/policy-71)
  • Fundamental Values of Academic Integrity: ICAI Values (https://academicintegrity.org/images/pdfs/20019_ICAI-Fundamental-Values_R12.pdf)


Additionally, students should be aware that the legal and copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is currently unclear. For more information, visit the Copyright Advisory Committee:

https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

ME 354:

Emphasis on applications of thermodynamics to flow processes. Real fluids, evaluation of state functions of real fluids. Non-reacting mixtures, reacting mixtures, equilibrium considerations.

View requirements for ME 354

Course Overview: Engineering thermodynamics is the study of the transfer, storage, and conversion of energy—processes that occur in every engineered system. The course will include study of key applications of engineering thermodynamics, including converting heat to work, transfer of thermal energy from lower to higher temperature, processes involving simple chemical reactions, and air-conditioning processes (psychrometrics). The analysis techniques to be studied might be applied in addressing the following questions: How might the performance of a device/system/process be improved? What are the limits on the energy efficiency of a device/system/process? Which components of a system offer the greatest potential for improvement?

Recommended Preparation: Prior completion of ME250 is the usual prerequisite. Students should have some knowledge of basic concepts in energy and entropy analysis, and evaluation of thermodynamic property changes (of simple substances) due to a process.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Apply basic energy and entropy analysis to thermodynamic processes.
2. Evaluate properties of simple compressible substances using a variety of forms of "equation of state".
3. Perform basic thermodynamic calculations for processes involving non-reacting gas mixtures.
4. Describe and analyze simple gas and vapor cycles, and some variations.
5. Apply basic exergy analysis to thermodynamic processes.
6. Perform basic thermodynamic calculations for processes involving simple chemical reactions.
7. Integrate basic environmental impact considerations into thermodynamic process analyses.

Student Workload: For undergraduate engineering courses, it is a typical expectation that students will invest around 100 hours of focused work for each course. This includes time spent reviewing course notes, participating in lectures and tutorials, completing homework, completing projects, and studying for tests. Based on a 14-week schedule, this equates to an average of about 7.5 hours/week. Please note that this information is provided only as a guideline.

Homework Problems: Recommended homework problems and solutions will be posted on LEARN throughout the term. It is recommended that you regularly work on these problems. They are intended to help you develop an in-depth understanding of the course material. If you get stuck while working on a problem, please apply reasonable persistence in your attempts before referring to the solutions.

If our regular office hours don’t work for your schedule, please contact us to arrange an alternate time!

Grading Scheme: Your course grade will be calculated using whichever scheme results in the higher grade (but potentially modified as mentioned below).

Refrigeration Lab: In late January, groups of 2-4 students will perform a lab. Students must complete safety training prior to attending their session. A portion of your individual lab grade will be based on your participation at the session. A brief lab report will be submitted by each group, due approximately two weeks after the lab session. Additional details will be provided separately.

Midterm Exam: A 2.0 hour midterm exam will be held at a date and time set by the department (tentatively Feb 24, 11:30 am). Aids permitted will include a “crib sheet” (8.5x11 paper, two sides total), calculator, ruler, and “Property Tables Booklet”. Additional details will be provided separately.

Final Exam: A 2.5 hour final exam will be held at a date and time set by the department or registrar. Aids permitted will include a “crib sheet” (8.5x11 paper, four sides total), calculator, ruler, and “Property Tables Booklet”. Additional details will be provided separately.

   

Regarding Self-Declared Absences: The University provides instructors with discretion to determine appropriate accommodations for cases of student absences in their courses. Where a student misses a test (e.g. quiz, exam) due to a self-declared absence, appropriate accommodation could include administration of a make-up test (written, oral, or hybrid).

Late Submissions: Unless otherwise specified, late submissions for graded projects will be accepted to a maximum of 72 hours late. Late submissions will receive an immediate penalty of 10% and the penalty will increase by 10% for each additional 24 hours. Extensions may be granted in certain circumstances. Requests for extensions should be made before the due date. (Projects assessed as CR/NCR will not be accepted late except where an extension has been granted.)

AI Text Generation Tools: The assessments in this course include submission of a lab report. Part of the intent of this work is to provide you with an opportunity to develop your abilities to communicate clearly and effectively. Therefore, it is recommended that you avoid using AI text generation tools in preparing your submission. Be advised that you are responsible for the content, accuracy, and academic integrity of all work you submit in this class, including any developed using AI tools.

Assignment Screening

No online plagiarism screening tools (e.g. Turnitin.com) will be used in this course. However, manual screening for indicators of plagiarism is always performed during review of written submissions.

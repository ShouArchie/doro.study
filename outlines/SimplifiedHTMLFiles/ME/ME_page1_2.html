Course Description

ME 101:

This course is a continuation of ME100 and is aimed at reinforcing the concepts related to technical communications, professionalism, and professional development, with emphasis on mechanical engineering concepts and practical examples. The fundamentals of programming are a significant component of this course, focusing on digital computing, and basic programming and algorithm writing. Programming examples are drawn from mechanical engineering.

View requirements for ME 101

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
(A) Identify and formulate a mechanical engineering problem [2a, 2b, 2c]
(B) Design a mechanical engineering system, product or process [4+, 4a, 4b, 4c, 4d]
(C) Demonstrate a working knowledge of procedural programming [1c, 1d]
(D) Implement introductory level programs in a high level programming language [5c]
(E) Evaluate and select appropriate data storage using arrays, and function layout, for a given software problem [4b, 4c]
(F) Define and use a suitable set of test cases to evaluate whether a program is working correctly [3a, 4b]
(G) Design, implement, and verify the operation of a mechanical system using motors and sensors operated by software as part of completing an open-ended project [4+, 4a, 4b, 4c, 4d]
(H) Apply project management skills to develop and follow a feasible project plan [11a]
(I) Work effectively as a member of a team [6a, 6b]
(J) Communicate accurately and effectively [7a, 7b, 7c]

Mechanical Design

Grades – The programming and the mechanical engineering design parts of the course have equal weighting, each contributing to half of the overall grade. The grade for the mechanical engineering part of the course will be based on weekly assignments, two term tests, and a final design project that includes a demonstration, written report and teamwork assessment, using the following weighting scheme. The late penalty for any submitted work (other than the Term Tests) is 20% per working day. 

Group Work – All assessments for ME101 Mechanical Engineering Design will be group-based, except for Assignment #1a and the Term Tests. One submission will be made by the project group for all other deliverables: assignments, design reviews, project demonstration and project report. All group members will receive the same mark for each graded item. 

The course instructor reserves the right to require all group members to submit work individually for any assignment or project deliverable.  This would be done in cases where the course instructor believes that work is not being evenly distributed among the group, based on information from the following. 

  • Teamwork surveys, held twice during the term
  • Concerns arising from design review meetings
  • Personal communication from individual group members

Groups are strongly encouraged to address teamwork early and often throughout the term, as follows. 

  • Have a clear understanding of the time each person is willing to invest in the course per week
  • Identify times when the group can meet to work together and agree to meeting at these times. Inperson meetings are strongly encouraged over online meetings, text messages, chat, etc.
  • Review and update expectations regularly to account for changes in workload or other personal factors.
  • Agree on the approach to take when conflicts arise or team members don’t meet deadlines, skip meetings, etc. If you are unable to resolve these issues, ask the course instructor for help.

Term Tests – Two tests will be held during the term outside of the normally scheduled midterm and final exam periods. You are expected to write the term tests as scheduled unless you have contacted the instructor beforehand. Failure to write a test without sufficient reason (e.g. severe illness, schedule conflict) will result in a grade of zero. The term tests are closed book with no reference page or calculator allowed. 

Assignments - There will be weekly assignments for the mechanical engineering design part of the course throughout the term. Each of these assignments will provide you an opportunity to work on a portion of what will become your final design project report, such as needs analysis, design concepts, project plan, etc. 

Design Reviews – Two design reviews will be held during the term, where groups will present progress on their design project. Design reviews will be graded based on preparation for the meeting, effectiveness of presentation, and response to feedback and questions. Each design review will have a weighting equivalent to a weekly assignment. 

Mechanical Engineering Design Days – During Week 2 all project groups will participate in Mechanical Engineering Design Days (Mech Days), experiencing an open-ended design challenge in a team based, collaborative environment with limited time and material resources. There will be a reflection assignment that is completed by each group following Mech Days, with a weighting equivalent to one weekly assignment. 

“Picture a Scientist” screening – On Thursday, March 27 the documentary film “Picture a Scientist” will be shown, followed by a review activity. Attendance is optional but strongly encouraged. A bonus of 0.5% will be added to the final course grade for anyone who actively participates in this activity.

Programming

Assignments

There will be weekly programming assignments given during most weeks of the term, that will involve coding, and some will involve a LEARN quiz of short answer questions. Most weeks the coding will be completed in pairs. Students will be matched in pairs at random, with a new match occurring each week. The purpose of the weekly assignments is to give you programming practice. Some assignments may also require you to demonstrate your working program to a Tutor/TA. Time to work on the assignments and get help from the teaching team is provided during the programming labs, although for later assignments you will likely need to work on the outside the lab time as well.  Assignments are due:

  • Tuesday LAB 202: due on Wednesday at 10:00pm
  • Thursday LAB 201: due on Friday at 10:00pm

The late penalty is 20% per day.

Seek Help, Get an Extension on the Assignment Due Date: For students who have sought extra help on a programming assignment from the teaching team (before or after the due date), the EISTs/Tutors/TAs/instructor will waive the late penalty and grant a 48-hour extension. They will not however, grant the extension if you have not made an effort to work with them to complete the assignment. By seeking help again, a student can earn a second extension. Extensions beyond that time may be granted by the instructor for students who have worked with them to get back on track with regard to the course material. 

Program source code for assignments should be saved and then uploaded to Crowdmark. Each source file should include:

  • A comment that acknowledges any help received from any person not on the teaching team, or from any other source including websites, the course textbook, etc.
  • Your C++ programs;
  • Any output, as described in the assignment, which should be included as a comment at the end of the program file; output must be generated by the code that was submitted

Files that contain additional information, e.g. a drawing of a flow chart, should be legible – check your upload to Crowdmark to verify this.

The EISCs/Tutors/TAs may also, at their discretion, initially assign a zero to an assignment or to an assignment question, and then request that you resubmit it for grading.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, GitHub CoPilot, or MS Copilot may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions.   Permitted uses are limited to:

  • Information gathering for conceptual and detailed design of mechanical systems for project
  • Syntax help during the programming tutorials
  • MATLAB coding
  • With permission from the programming course instructor, generation of code for the course project; any code generated using an AI tool will be considered as coming from an external source, and will not be graded
  • Assistance with formatting the project report

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI. 

Academic Integrity

By submitting something for grading, you are indicating that it is your, or your group's, original work completed during the current term, except for:

  • Assistance provided by the teaching team, which does not need to be acknowledged
  • Use of code or code fragments provided by the teaching team, which does not need to be acknowledged
  • Assistance or code provided by others, including classmates, the textbook, websites, etc., which is acknowledged using a comment at the top of the code or included in the project report

As an example of a possible consequence for failing to abide by this, under Policy 71, the penalty for plagiarism on an assignment (assuming no previous academic offences in any course) includes a letter placed in your file describing the offence, zero on the assignment, and 5% subtracted from your overall course grade.

The following are NOT ALLOWED UNDER ANY CIRCUMSTANCES

  • If not provided by the teaching team, copying written information, data, figures, or code without acknowledging the source; sources can include classmates, books, websites, etc.
  • Submitting written work or code that has only minor modifications of another's work, e.g. changing the order of words in a sentence, or changing variable names, etc.
  • Providing written work, images, data, or code to another student or group
  • Submitting output (screen captures) that was not generated by the code submitted
  • Submitting any course information, including lecture notes, assignment questions, project descriptions, exam questions, files from the Learn site, etc. to an external website
  • At the moment, all assessments are expected to be in-person.  Should it be necessary to move to online assessment, during any online quizzes, tests, or exams, communicating with a person that is not a member of the ME 101 teaching team, and/or using written information, data, figures, or code from a website that is not ME 101 Learn or ME 101 Crowdmark

If you would like to build a repository of your code, so that you can share it with people like potential employers, please discuss this with the programming course instructor, and make it clear that you must be acknowledged as the author of the code using a copyright notice or a creative commons license that indicates you must be given credit.

Intellectual Property: Be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof).
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides).
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years. In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights and academic integrity.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online.

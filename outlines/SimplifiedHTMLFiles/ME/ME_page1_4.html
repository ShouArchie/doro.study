Course Description

ME 115:

The relevance of materials to engineering practice. The microstructure of materials, crystallinity and crystal imperfections, glasses, and amorphous solids. Elastic and plastic deformation in metals, viscoelasticity of plastics. Strengthening mechanisms in metals, polymers, and ceramics. Fracture of brittle and ductile solids. Electrical and magnetic properties of materials.

View requirements for ME 115

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand primary concepts that define materials science & engineering (Ch.1) - KB
Classify materials by properties and function (Ch.1) - KB
Define four different mechanisms of bonding in materials (Ch.2) - KB
Understand how interactions between atoms or between ions influence material properties (e.g., modulus of elasticity, coefficient of thermal expansion) (Ch.2) - KB
Understand how atoms are arranged in common crystal structures (Ch.3) - KB
Identify close-packed planes and directions in crystal structures (Ch.3) - KB
Learn how to use Miller indices to described planes and directions (Ch.3) - KB
Describe point defects in crystalline materials (Ch.4) - KB
Understand dislocations and how they strengthen crystalline materials (Ch.4) - KB
Identify likely slip systems for crystal structures (Ch.4) - KB
Understand how grain boundaries strengthen polycrystalline materials (Ch.4) - KB
Understand the process of strain hardening (Ch.4) - KB
Identify critical features of a stress strain curve (Ch.6) - KB
Define the materials property of hardness (Ch.6) - KB
Understand why flaws in a material may lead to premature fracture (Ch.7) - KB
Understand the processes of fatigue and creep in materials (Ch.7) - KB
Understand how the strength of a metal/alloy is influenced by mechanical processing and heat treatments (Ch.8) - KB
Understand the basic structure and properties of polymers and composites (Ch.16,17) - KB
Calculate material properties (e.g., density) based on crystal structure (Ch.3) - PA
Construct a stress strain curve given load and displacement data & calculate sample ductility (Ch.6) - PA
Calculate engineering & true stress/strain (Ch.6) - PA
Predict changes in material mechanical properties when subjected to different strain rates and temperatures (Ch.6) - PA
Define fracture toughness; calculate it and the critical flaw size for a material (Ch.7) - PA
Define the fracture mechanics relationship between material properties, applied stress and flaw size (Ch.7) - PA
Identify the morphology of failed surfaces that indicate whether ductile or brittle fracture occurred in metals (Ch.7) - PA
Quantify fatigue and creep behavior in materials (Ch.7) - PA
Predict the effect of cold working, hot working, and annealing on the properties of metals/alloys (Ch.8) - PA
Calculate the properties of composites, based on the constituent materials (Ch.17) - PA
Develop skills to investigate the properties of different materials by performing experiments, analyzing and interpreting data, and drawing conclusions (Labs) - I
Understand the role of materials science in the design process (Ch.1) - D
Use tensile testers, hardness testers, impact testers, and measurement tools like calipers and micrometers, and recognize the limitations of these tools (Labs) - ET
Work effectively in teams to complete labs and submit lab reports (Labs) - I&TW
Write effective, engineering lab reports (Labs) - C
Recognize the role of the engineer in protecting the public and the public interest, through appropriate selection of materials for engineering components to ensure their safety and performance - P

Graduate attributes are attributes you are expected to develop through the course of your undergraduate engineering degree, in order to prepare you for a successful career in an engineering profession. The learning outcomes for the course are mapped to these attributes.

  • KB - A knowledge base for engineering
  • PA - Problem analysis
  • I - Investigation
  • D - Design
  • ET - Use of engineering tools
  • I&TW - Individual and team work
  • C - Communication skills
  • P - Professionalism

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as preparation of lab reports (technical communication). Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

ME 760:

View requirements for ME 760

Most traditional analyses predict the response of a system operating in a defined state, which is known as the direct or forward problem. In contrast, the objective of experiments is usually to infer an unknown system state from an observed response in the form of experimental data, while the goal of engineering design is to infer the system state that provides the optimal design response. These inference problems, called inverse problems, are challenging to solve because they are often mathematically ill-posed, meaning that they: may not have solutions; have multiple solutions; or have solutions that are highly sensitive to small perturbations in the observed data or problem definition.  Inverse problems are ubiquitous in all aspects of science and engineering. This course will describe the characteristics and classification of inverse problems, as well as numerical analysis techniques for solving them.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Classify inverse problems and understand the cause of ill-posedness
Diagnose ill-conditioned matrix problems and apply classical regularization schemes to stabilize the inversion
Apply the principles of Bayesian inference and define priors
Understand Bayesian design-of-experiments and Kalman filtering
Understand issues of model complexity, degrees-of-freedom, and Bayesian model selection

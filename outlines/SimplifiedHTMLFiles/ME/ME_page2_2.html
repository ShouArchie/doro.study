Course Description

AE 573 / ME 573:

Selected topics in HVAC systems design with focus on energy-efficiency and environmental-sustainability. Heating and cooling distribution using air-, water-, and refrigerant-based systems. Constant- and variable-flow systems. Heat- and energy-recovery systems. Vapour-compression refrigeration and heat-pump systems (including air- and geothermal-source heat pumps).

Course Overview: Achieving significant reductions in building energy consumption will be important in efforts to deal with the environmental and socioeconomic impacts of energy use. The energy used by heating, ventilating, and air-conditioning (HVAC) systems represents a large portion of the energy consumption of most commercial, institutional, and residential buildings. This course will introduce selected topics in HVAC systems design, with focus on energy-efficiency and environmental-sustainability: heating and cooling distribution using air-, water-, and refrigerant-based systems; constant- and variable-flow systems; heat- and energy-recovery systems; vapor-compression heat-pump systems, including air- and geothermal-source heat pumps.

Recommended Preparation: It is strongly recommended that students have engaged in prior study of introductory concepts in thermodynamics, heat transfer, and fluid mechanics, including analysis of refrigeration cycles and basic psychrometrics. Students without this background are expected to undertake additional study to acquire sufficient competency in each topic. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Perform simple thermodynamic calculations for selected HVAC components and systems.
2. Explain operational characteristics of selected energy-saving strategies for HVAC systems.
3. Assess the potential for selected energy-saving strategies to operate effectively for a variety of operating conditions and applications.

HVAC Equipment Lab: Groups of students will perform a lab that includes: i) experiments on a variable-flow pump system, and ii) measurements on a vapor-compression refrigerant system. All students must review safety information before attending their lab session. Lab sessions will occur in early February (tentative). Each group will prepare a brief report submission involving analysis of the measurements. The report will be due in mid-March (tentative). The Final Exam may include questions related to aspects of the lab analysis.

Midterm Exam: A midterm exam will be held during the lecture period on Tues Feb 25. (No lecture this day.) Aids permitted will include a crib-sheet (one double-sided 8.5x11 sheet or equivalent), calculator, ruler/straight-edge. The duration will be ~1.75 hours (all).

Final Exam: A final exam will be held during the final exam period at a date and time set by the University Registrar. Aids permitted will include a crib-sheet (two double-sided 8.5x11 sheets or equivalent), calculator, ruler/straight-edge. The duration will be ~2.0 hours for AE573/ME573 and ~2.5 hours for ME656.

Graduate Quizzes (ME656 only): ME656 students will write two quizzes (~40 mins each) based on additional directed studies to be outlined by the instructor. Quiz 1 will be held Tues Feb 25 (after the midterm exam). Quiz 2 will be held Tues Mar 18 (during the regular lecture period). Additional information will be provided separately.

    

Regarding Self-Declared Absences: The University provides instructors with discretion to determine appropriate accommodations for cases of student absences in their courses. Where a student misses a test (e.g. quiz, exam) due to a self-declared absence, appropriate accommodation could include administration of a make-up test (written, oral, or hybrid).

Late Submissions: Unless otherwise specified, late submissions for graded projects will be accepted to a maximum of 72 hours late. Late submissions will receive an immediate penalty of 10% and the penalty will increase by 10% for each additional 24 hours. Extensions may be granted in certain circumstances. Requests for extensions should be made before the due date. (Projects assessed as CR/NCR will not be accepted late except where an extension has been granted.)

AI Text Generation Tools: The assessments in this course include submission of a lab report. Part of the intent of this work is to provide you with an opportunity to develop your abilities to communicate clearly and effectively. Therefore, it is recommended that you avoid using AI text generation tools in preparing your submission. Be advised that you are responsible for the content, accuracy, and academic integrity of all work you submit in this class, including any developed using AI tools.

Writing and Communication Centre:  The Writing and Communication Centre (WCC) works with students as they develop their ideas, draft, and revise. Writing and communication specialists offer one-on-one support in planning assignments, synthesizing, and citing research, organizing papers and reports, designing presentations, and revising for clarity and coherence. For more information, visit uwaterloo.ca/wcc .

Academic Integrity: All members of the University of Waterloo community are expected to adhere to principles of academic integrity. Further, members of the engineering profession—and those seeking entry into it—are expected to uphold high standards of personal honour and integrity. Please consider that the personal traits and habits that you develop during your education are carried forward into your career. Those who find ways to justify “taking short-cuts” as they pursue their education may be unintentionally training themselves do the same in their careers. Unfortunately, engineers who have “taken short-cuts” have been responsible for many disasters and failures. Ask the instructor if you have any questions or concerns. Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.

Flexible Grading Scheme: In cases of an academic integrity violation, the grading scheme may be adjusted at the instructor’s discretion such that use of a flexible scheme does not provide a "loop-hole" that would allow a student to avoid the consequences of the violation.

Acceptable Use of Course Materials:  Materials provided to students in any format (including, but not limited to: electronic and hard-copy documents, video recordings, etc.) are provided solely for their personal use in completing the coursework. Students are not permitted to share or distribute course materials by any means (including, but not limited to: uploading to a 3rd party website, emailing, etc.), unless prior written permission is provided by the instructor. Unauthorized distribution of any material will be considered an academic offense and may constitute copyright infringement. (Note: As clarification to the above, it is acceptable for students enrolled in the course to share material with other students enrolled in the course, while they are both enrolled.)

Confidentiality of Tests: The content of all tests (including quizzes and exams) used in this course is confidential. It is a violation of academic integrity to convey and/or transmit content from any test to another person by any means. Further, it is a violation of academic integrity to record, copy, photograph, or store images or descriptions of the content of tests, for any purpose.

Public Health Considerations: If necessitated by public health conditions, some aspects of the course (including administration of assessments) may be adjusted. As much as possible, any needed changes will be implemented with intent to avoid imposing unreasonable hardship on any stakeholder (including students, teaching team, administration) based on the discretion of the instructor.

Declaring absences: Regardless of the process used to declare an absence, students are responsible for reaching out to their instructors as soon as possible. The course instructor will determine how missed course components are accommodated. 

Rescheduling Co-op Interviews: Follow the co-op process for rescheduling co-op interviews for conflicts to graded assignments (e.g. midterms, tests, and final exams). Attendance at co-operative work-term employment interviews is not considered to be a valid reason to miss a test.

University Policy

Discipline: A student is expected to know what constitutes academic integrity to avoid committing an academic offence, and to take responsibility for their actions. For information on categories of offences and types of penalties, students should refer to Policy 71, Student Discipline. For typical penalties, check Guidelines for the Assessment of Penalties.

Appeals: A decision made or penalty imposed under Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances (other than a petition) or Policy 71, Student Discipline may be appealed if there is a ground. A student who believes they have a ground for an appeal should refer to Policy 72, Student Appeals.

Note for students with disabilities: AccessAbility Services, located in Needles Hall, Room 1401, collaborates with all academic departments to arrange appropriate accommodations for students with disabilities without compromising the academic integrity of the curriculum.

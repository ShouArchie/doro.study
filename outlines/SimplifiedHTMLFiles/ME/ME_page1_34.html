Course Description

ME 533:

This course is intended to provide an advanced treatment of the structure, properties, and processing of non-metallic and composite materials based on polymers, metals, and ceramics. The structure and properties of polymers and ceramics in bulk form and as matrices and reinforcements in composites will be covered. Processing methods for non-metallics and composites (example extrusion, injection molding, etc.) will be considered. The geometrical arrangement of fibres within laminae and their influences on elastic and strength properties of composites will be described based on suitable micromechanical models. The role of the matrix and fibre/matrix interface in determining composite properties will be described.

View requirements for ME 533

This course is intended to be an extension of the materials science and engineering series of courses (i.e. ME115, ME230, ME340) in the ME program. The ultimate aim of the course is to take an in-depth look at composite materials, their properties, how to tailor their properties, and how to manufacture them. To do this, one must understand some basic ideas regarding the components that makes up the composite itself. The case of metal has been discussed thoroughly in ME115, and ME230, so this course will focus on the non-metallic materials, that is on polymers and on ceramics. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Explain the interaction of the reinforcement phase and the matrix phases in various types of structural composite and their roles within the composites.
Assess the design of different structural composites and be able to explain the reasons behind the design.
Devise simple structural composites using the fundamental understanding in order to achieve the desired properties.

Tentative Course Schedule

  1. What is a Composite and the rationale behind them?
  2. A Primer on Polymer Science 
    1. Carbon-Carbon Bond and the Carbon backbone
    2. Carbon fibers and the source of its properties
    3. Polymer structure and how it affects strength
    4. Chain Length, Molecular Weight, and Degree of Polymerization
    5. Crystallinity, Defects, Properties & Behaviour
  3. A Primer on Ceramics
    1. Crystallinity and similarity to Metals
    2. Difficulty in Processing and its relations to Properties & Behaviour
  4. The Matrix Phase and the need for Reinforcements
    1. Polymer/Metal/Ceramic Matrices and their uses
  5. Choice and Type of Reinforcements 
    1. Choice of Materials of reinforcement
    2. Choice of Geometry of reinforcements
  6. Micromechanics of Composites
    1. Micromechanics of Continuous Fiber Reinforced Composites
      1. Rule of Mixture for Density, strength, stiffness
      2. Various orientation of fibers
    2. Micromechanics of Discontinuous Fiber Reinforced Composites
      1. Modifying the rule of mixture
      2. The importance of interfaces
    3. Micromechanics of particulate reinforced composites
    4. Extending these to macro-composites
  7. Manufacturing and Processing
    1. Manufacturing of Matrices (Mostly Polymers)
    2. Manufacturing of Fibers
    3. Extending these to composites
    4. End-of-Life, recyclability, and environmental concerns
  8. Design tools for Composites

If we have time:

  1. More Advanced topics in Composites
    1. Fracture and Failure of Polymers and Ceramics
    2. Fracture, Fatigue, and Other Failure of Composites
  2. Natural and Advanced composites
    1. Carbon/carbon composites
    2. Nanocomposites and dispersion strengthened composites
    3. Nacre, spider silk, mantis shrimp, and bio-mimicry
    4. Woods and other natural composites

A Note on Assessments 

This course will consist of numerical and conceptual assessments. These assessments will be administered online and students will be given some period of time (usually a week or so) to complete the task. 

Numerical assessments are planned to be assignment-liked. These will likely be conducted on Crowdmark. 

Numerical Assessment #1 is planned to be distributed around week 8 (~Feb 24th) and will be due at the end of term. This may be split into two assignments due at different times if the class prefers.

Conceptual assessments are planned to be Bongo video assignments to emulate a verbal exam on top of a written portion on Crowdmark. It will consist of simply "explain this concept back" to me type questions. The format may be modified a little if the class size is too large to make this feasible. 

Conceptual Assessment #1 is planned to occur 1 week after completing section 3 which is typically around week 5 or 6 (~Feb 3rd or Feb 10th).

Conceptual Assessment #2 is planned to occur 1 week after section 6-3 which is typically around week 10 or 11(~Mar 10th or 17th)

During the first week of the final exam period, there will be a final assessment with one or both a numerical and a conceptual assessment. 

Submission(s) for this course:

There are various submissions for this course, mostly on a group basis. These includes the lab. Reports and the project related submission. Unless otherwise stated, the due time is at 23:59 of the provided due date.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

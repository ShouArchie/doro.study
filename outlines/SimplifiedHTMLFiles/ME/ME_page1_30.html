Course Description

ME 482:

A continuation of ME481. The final design of the major mechanical engineering project proposed in ME481 will be undertaken. The purpose of this phase of the project is to carry out a detailed technical design and proof of feasibility of the solution proposed in ME481.

View requirements for ME 482

ME482 is the second of two required courses for the Mechanical Engineering capstone design project.[1] 

This course, together with the required first course, should provide a culminating and significant design experience for students. 

Students are required to demonstrate an “ability to design [create] solutions for complex, open-ended mechanical engineering problems, and to design [create] systems, components and/or processes that meet specified needs with appropriate attention to health and safety risks, applicable standards, and economic, environmental, cultural and societal considerations”[2].

Additionally, students are required to demonstrate “an ability to appropriately incorporate economics and business practices including [design] project, risk and change management into the practice of engineering and to understand their limitations”.[3],[4] Time management and communication will be critical to project success.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Identify, represent and communicate a mechanical engineering problem or opportunity
Design a mechanical engineering system, product and/or process
Implement, verify and refine a design
Identify and address safety, regulatory, sustainability and professional ethics
Apply project management skills and techniques
Work effectively as a member of a team
Communicate accurately and effectively

Tentative Course Schedule

Organizational Meeting 

This meeting will provide an introduction and overview for the course. It will be used to set expectations and provide additional information on key aspects of the course. 

Design Reviews: 

There will be three Design Reviews for this course, Initial (IDR), Midterm (MDR) and Final (FDR). The reviews are intended for students to update the instructional team on their project status and progress and obtain feedback and guidance from the instructional team. These reviews are intended to improve the quality of student learning and the quality of the projects, as well as to monitor and evaluate progress. Each review will have a similar format: a presentation (approximately 20 minutes) followed by or intermingled with discussion. Each presentation should include the following information (also review the paragraph below the bullet list):

  • A very brief review of the project background and objectives
  • A concise description of the project requirements, constraints, criteria (specifications)
  • A concise description of the status of the project at the last review, and a summary of response to feedback received at that review (the final report in ME481 for the IDR)
  • A summary of the status of the project, including a presentation of key design and analysis work completed since the previous review
  • A concise plan for work for the remainder of the course
  • A current summary of project management data, with an emphasis on milestones and budget(s)
  • A time compilation that shows individual member contributions to date.

Students should use their judgement in deciding what to present and to what level of detail. Most of each design review should be on new material. In each case, students are encouraged to identify issues, make considered proposals as appropriate, and use this opportunity to seek advice from the review committee. Any changes since the previous review should be highlighted. 

Initial Design Review 

The IDR serves as a ‘kick-off’ meeting for the term. Its focus will be on resetting project objectives as required, updating the project plan, and outlining the verification plan. Teams should summarize their project, update the reviewers on the status (changes since their ME481 final report, including response to feedback), and provide a plan for the term (with a focus on verification). Be sure to list specific hazards related to your project, and be prepared to present their updated Hazard Disclosure form.

Safety Review Meeting with MME Technical Staff­

After the Initial Design Review, teams must book a meeting with our technical staff. A sign-up sheet will be available. During this session, your project will undergo a preliminary review of electrical, mechanical, and chemical hazards. All team members must be present. Failure to attend this meeting will result in a 2% grade deduction.

If significant hazards are identified, subsequent actions and reviews will be required. Qualified staff must review prototypes that have safety concerns prior to testing or operation. 

Midterm Design Review 

The MDR serves as an update for the project, and focus will be placed on design progress and verification status. For the MDR, teams should summarize their project, apprise the instruction team on the status (changes since the IDR, including response to feedback), and provide a plan for the term. Other students in the class will be invited to provide feedback at this meeting. An external reviewer (usually from industry) will be invited to participate and provide feedback.

Final Design Review 

The FDR serves as the final project update and the last opportunity for formal feedback from the instructional team. This is intended to provide a key milestone prior to the Symposium, and to provide feedback on expectations for the final report. For the FDR, teams should summarize their project, update us on the status (changes since their MDR, including response to feedback), and provide a plan for the Symposium and for the rest of the term. 

Mechanical Engineering Symposium

The Mechanical Engineering Symposium will take place on Monday March 24th in E5 and E7. Arrangements will be finalized in the coming weeks once group’s space requests are made. During the Symposium, students will be expected to provide a short summary of their project, with emphasis on verification of project objectives. This will be reviewed both by the instructors, their peers and external judges (both technical and non-technical). Groups will have time to respond to questions from external judges (both technical and non-technical), the instructor, and their peers.

Design Project Report

The purpose of the Design Project Report is to summarize the entire design project and to provide all data and documentation as appendices.  The report must not exceed 9000 words[1], excluding the front matter and Appendices. Students will be asked to provide the word count for their text in the final report. Note that all material in the appendices must be referenced in the text. The report structure is as follows

Front matter, including, 

  • Title page
    • Project title 
    • Team number and students’ names
    • Instructional Team (Instructor and Faculty Advisor(s) names)
    • Confidentiality provisions as appropriate
  • Signature page outlining individual contributions, with the statement “We the undersigned take responsibility for this design” 
  • A list of Appendices
  • Summary report, including:
    • A concise summary of work completed in ME481, including a brief project justification and background, and project specifications. Be sure to only summarize the ME481 proposal and report and reference them. Include the current specifications in an Appendix. Any changes from ME481 should be briefly discussed/justified. Make sure to include key figures in the main text, and more detailed information in the Appendix.
    • Summary of the design work completed this term. Briefly summarize the options considered, design decisions made, and the engineering analyses completed to support these design decisions. Include design options considered and any detailed calculations in an Appendix.
    • A summary of the final design, with key figures in the text. Include detailed drawings and manufacturing plan, as appropriate, in an Appendix. 
    • A summary of the design verification of engineering design specifications. Summarize key calculations, simulations, and test results in the text, and include detailed information in an Appendix. Include mass, power, volume, etc. budgets as appropriate.
    • Include a brief discussion of design for safety, regulatory, sustainability and professional ethics considerations in the text, as appropriate. Include the hazard identification form, and other safety data in an Appendix.
    • Summary of design project management activities, including a brief discussion of what went well and what was not as useful, and a summary of the cash budget. Include the following information in an Appendix: 
      • Project objective and deliverables
      • Work/time budgeted and planned versus work/time expended 
      • Schedule; highlight any significant changes during the term in the text
      • Budget(s), including cash, in-kind (both materials and labour hours at a ’going rate’)
      • Risk and mitigation plan; highlight any significant changes during the term in the text
    • Reflection is an important activity to close the design loop., Include a concise statement of team lessons learned in the main text. Individual Lessons Learned surveys must be completed separately. 
    • The report should finish with concise but considered conclusions and recommendations.
  • Appendices
    • Engineering Data (as separate, stand-alone, configuration-controlled documents)
    • Engineering design specification documents 
      • Current revision of the engineering design specification
    • Verification of design data
    • Hazard disclosure form
    • Optional but desired: a team summary chart of “who a team member mentored” and “what mentoring they received” by other team members
    • Other data as appropriate
    • Design Project Management Data (as separate, stand-alone, configuration-controlled documents)
      • Schedule with work breakdown structure (i.e., a Gantt chart)
      • Time tracking chart, including individual member contributions 
      • Budget (separate in-kind and cash budgets, and compare with resources)
      • Risk register
  • Exhibits
    • Video data. 
       

Deviations from this structure are allowed provided students can justify them. This should be done prior to submittal.

Evaluation Adjustments

Instructors may adjust grades to account for individual contributions within a team, based primarily on the student peer evaluations and input from the faculty advisor(s). This could result in a significant decrease (in extreme cases no marks being awarded for relevant components of the course), or in a small increase in your course mark. Failure to submit the teamwork assessments will result in a deduction of up to 5% (2.5% per missed assessment).

Teamwork Assessments

All students are required to complete a teamwork self- and peer-evaluation at the middle and end of term. These have two components: technical contribution, and collaboration and communication (as indicated by helpful and unhelpful behaviours). 

Late Submissions, Attendance and Repeat Reviews/Examinations

Late submissions of reports and/or deliverables are penalized at 10% per day. Late attendance to review meetings will be penalized at up to 50% immediately for the individuals who are late. Students who miss a review meeting without prior approval are not eligible for marks for that meeting. 

Expected Student Learning Investment

To achieve the learning outcomes defined above, students must invest a minimum average of 8-10 hours per week. Team members should (for congruence) agree upon an average weekly time investment. The hours/week legitimately invested should be recorded by each team member and included as part of each design review, and the final report.

Safety, Regulatory, Sustainability and Professional Ethics

Safety in design must be addressed adequately and appropriately throughout the course. Each team must complete a hazard disclosure form[1], which they will provide at their safety meeting, and include an updated form in an appendix to their report.

It is the responsibility of each team to identify, address and rectify hazards related to their project. Note that the disclosure form is a summary only, to highlight key hazards. More detail should be provided in the presentations, safety review meeting, and report, as appropriate.

Canadian Standards Association

Formal CSA electrical inspections may not be possible. Teams should design as to not require CSA approval, e.g., by using CSA approved power supplies, and keeping below 100 volt-amps. Reference guidelines will be available in Learn and the technical staff can also be consulted.

Sustainability 

Appropriate attention must be given to design for sustainability, impact of engineering to society and the environment, regardless of the design project, as with professional ethics considerations.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as [fill this in with your discipline-specific skills]. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Intellectual Property: Be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof).
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides).
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years. In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights and academic integrity.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online.

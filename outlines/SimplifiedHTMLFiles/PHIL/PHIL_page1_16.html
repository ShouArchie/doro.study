Course Description

PHIL 208:

Questions regarding such matters as the nature of knowledge, logic and language, mind and brain, space and time, causality, ethics, and politics are explored through classic philosophic texts and science fiction narratives.

View requirements for PHIL 208

Science fiction depicts alternate and weird possibilities. It can also uncritically reproduce biases of its time. In both cases, science fiction helps us explore philosophical questions about what it means to be human, what the world is really like, and how we might make our world better. 

This course will cover questions that emerge in metaphysics, epistemology, philosophy of language, philosophy of science, philosophy of mind, political philosophy, and moral philosophy. We will explore philosophical themes and learn how to evaluate philosophical arguments as they appear in science fiction media, especially in short stories, film, and television. 

That being said, this course is not a systematic introduction to philosophy, nor is it an introduction to science fiction studies. It will, however, give you a foundation for applying philosophical modes of reasoning, whether in future philosophy courses, other disciplines, or in everyday life. 

What to expect in class.

There will be minimal lecture in this class. We will practice critical reading, respectful discussion with peers, evaluating philosophical arguments, and presenting our own ideas. This is a new course for me as an instructor, so we may make adjustments as best suits the learning needs of the class as we go along. 

Most of our time together will focus on discussing, as a class and in small groups, the assigned texts and the issues that most interest you about them. In general, I recommend having copies of written texts with you in class, and any time stamps for clips you may want to watch in class from the film and television media. The Scenes for Consideration (SFCs) assignment (explained below) will be the main way we structure discussions. 

I will send updates to you between class meeting time using the announcements function on Learn. I recommend checking Learn and your UWaterloo email regularly (e.g, every 48 hours).

In my experience, the following strategies, expressed by the acronym PREP, support learning and success when adopted by students:

  • Prepare for class
  • Reflect (cognitively, emotionally)
  • Engage (participate)
  • Practice patience and compassion (towards yourself and others)

Labour

Learning is an act of labour and public accountability. In this course, grades will be based on labour. 

Part of the labour of learning entails reading the assigned material and communicating your thoughts and feelings in class through speaking, writing, or some other medium. You will earn an 80 in the course if you do the following:

  • Attend 80% of class meetings and complete 80% of the readings
  • Complete five SFCs
  • Regularly make substantive contributions to class
  • Complete the two projects by the respective deadlines
  • Complete the Self-Assessment by the deadline

I have high expectations for your work. Assignments are expected to be thoughtful and thorough. Work that is incomplete or does not conform to the instructions will not be accepted. However, you do not need to achieve excellence or some other unrealistic standard to do well. You do need to try and to be willing to make mistakes; your classmates and I will be there to support you. I will provide feedback on your two projects and provide you an opportunity to revise them (if you submit them by the due date). 

To earn more than 80 in the course, in addition to meeting the labour requirements outlined above, you should contribute meaningfully to the class. We all work, learn, and make decisions better when we are supported by and collaborate with others. There is no formula for how to engage meaningfully. Trust yourself and challenge yourself to enhance the experience of the class as a whole. This could range from contributions to class discussion, to sharing links, articles, references to sources outside course readings that enrich our understanding of our subject, touching base with other students in the course throughout the term, or providing feedback on their work. Maybe you form a discussion group. Whatever it is you do in this fashion will count towards your final grade. 

I have been influenced by many people to challenge colonial structures of assessment, and I am grateful to my colleague Kevin Carey, from whom I adapted the language used above. 

Scenes for Consideration

This assignment supports learning outcomes 1-4.

Over the course of the semester, you will complete five Scenes for Consideration, or SFCs for short. Each SFC will correspond to a particular reading assigned for the course. 

In addition to being a means of note-taking, these assignments are intended to help you practice explaining philosophical ideas and articulating questions that you would like to think about with the help of everyone else. SFCs are personal reflections on the reading and do not require any outside research or prior knowledge. We will use them as the basis of class discussions. 

We will use the Discussions tool through Learn to submit SFCs. They will be due 24 hours prior to the class period for which a text is assigned. Discussion topics can be accessed by clicking Connect and then Discussions on the course navigation bar. You can also access the weekly topic threads under the appropriate week's folder in the "Notes and SFCs" module by clicking Content on the course navigation bar. 

Because there are multiple opportunities to submit SFCs, these assignments will not be accepted late. 

Here is how SFCs are structured:

  1. In one to three sentences, summarize a philosophical or conceptual issue presented within the assigned text. When more than one text is assigned for a class period, choose one of them to write an SFC about.
  2. Choose a scene that you want to discuss. 
    1. If writing about a short story, copy down a brief quotation (i.e., a few sentences), with a page number citation if possible, that captures the central issue you wish to ask about or discuss.
    2. If writing about a film or television episode, briefly describe the scene you want to discuss and include an approximate time stamp. Your description of the scene should be concise yet thorough enough to motivate the connection with the issue you summarized.
  3. Ask a question, explicitly connecting it with your scene and the philosophical issue you identified. Your question should enable the class to engage critically and constructively with the assigned text. SFCs raise questions about meaning--they are the kinds of questions that must be discussed and which cannot be answered by simply doing research online or finding out more information. There are lots of kinds of questions you might ask. You might ask about how the issue you identify plays out within the text we read, or you might ask a question about the applying the issue in a different (but relevant) context. You might articulate a question about what you find confusing, enraging, or helpful about the scene. These are just examples of what kinds of questions you might pose. The goal is to generate questions and help us approach philosophical dimensions of the texts.
  4. Provide a brief (one to three sentence) tentative answer to your question. You answer is an educated guess, and you may wish to cite another aspect of the reading or a previous class discussion to support it. It is okay if you are not one hundred percent convinced that your own answer is correct! That's why we have discussion.

Notes:

  • You can insert text into the discussion thread instead of attaching a document.
  • Throughout your SFCs, include page references/approximate time stamps to any material from course readings that you cite. (It's not always possible for readings that we do on websites). If you mention another source (for example, a reading from another class period), include a page number reference for it too.
  • You can engage with other people's SFC before and after class on the discussion board.
  • If I select your question for discussion in class, you should consider that to be a meaningful contribution!

Mid-Term Project

This assignment supports learning outcomes 1-3.

The mid-term project focuses on explaining philosophical issues that arise in science fiction, or on using science fiction to raise philosophical questions. This is not intended to be a research project, although you might draw on some outside sources. 

There are three options:

  • Write an argumentative essay (600-750 words) where you defend a particular view about a philosophical question. You might expand one of your 3-2-1 discussion questions, or develop a thread of discussion that was begun in class.
  • Write a critical review of a piece of science fiction media (600-750 words), highlighting it's philosophical dimensions and evaluating how well that issue is tackled in the piece of media.
  • Create a piece of science fiction media (art, comic, meme, poem, story, storyboard outline for a project) that depicts a philosophical problem and write a brief explainer (300-400 words) introducing the problem.

There will be an in-class project workshop on Wednesday, February 12. For that, bring a detailed outline or draft that can be shared with others to class. The mid-term project will be due on Friday, February 14, 11:59 p.m. There will be a no-penalty grace period for submission until Tuesday, February 18 at 10 a.m. 

Final Project

This assignment supports learning outcomes 1-4.

The final project focuses on introducing others to philosophical dimensions of science fiction. This project can be done as an individual or in groups of up to four people.

  • Write a book or film club guide for a piece of science fiction. Your guide should include a critical introduction to the philosophical issues, discussion questions, and suggestions for other reading and viewing.
  • Create a pitch for piece of media (book, comic, game, movie, music album) based on an original science fiction concept and related to a real world issue (ethical, political, scientific, technological). Your pitch should include a story board/outline/sketches/game mock-up/teaser, an explainer guide, and suggested reading/viewing for comparison.

There will be an in-class project workshop on Monday, March 31. For that, bring a detailed outline or draft with you to class. The final project will be due on Friday, April 4, 11:59 p.m. There will be a no-penalty grace period for submission until Monday, April 7, 10 a.m.

Self-Assessment

This assignment supports learning goals 1 and 3 and has two parts. 

In the first part, you will draw on your experience with the course readings, discussions, and projects to reflect on the relationship between philosophy and science fiction. This section will contain 2-3 questions, each of which should be about a paragraph long. 

In the second part, you will reflect on the labour you put into the course and how you meaningfully contributed to our learning community. As part of this reflection, you will tell me what grade you believe you have earned in the course. This section will contain 3 questions. 

 The self-assessment will be due on Thursday, April 17 by 11:59 p.m. through the Learn drop box. There will be a no-penalty grace period for submission until Monday, April 21, 10 a.m.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will be discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions.

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. 

Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights.  For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.  In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online. The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

Declaring absences

[Applies only to students taking undergraduate courses]

Please see the Academic Regulations section of the Undergraduate Calendar for more details. Regardless of the process used to declare an absence, it is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructor so that the instructor can determine how to accommodate the missed work. 

Absences due to religious, creed, and spiritual observances

Beginning Fall 2024, students can submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration Form in Quest. This form allows the student to register dates of potential conflicts with coursework. Students are expected to record their absence within two weeks of announcement of the due date or scheduled examination date for which academic accommodation is being sought. Submission through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. 

Absences due to verified illness 

For absences due to illness that are not pandemic-related, students need to obtain a Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances Process. Submission through the Absence Verification System, once registered, will notify your instructors of your absence. Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation to your instructors, teaching assistants, or other course personnel.  To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able to given their health condition.

Absences due to extenuating circumstances (e.g., bereavement)

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (e.g., bereavement, serious family illness), students submit a request for absence due to extenuating circumstances to the Absence Verification System for processing. Students use the form to submit supporting documentation, which will be considered in a comparable manner to a VIF. Official documentation is necessary before any action can be taken. Examples are plane/train/bus tickets, court documents, police reports, death certificates/obituaries/letters from funeral directors. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able to given their circumstances.

Self-declared absences due to COVID-related illness

Students can self-declare an absence on Quest due to COVID-related illness or a requirement to self-isolate. A self-declared COVID absence covers up to 10 days and no documentation is required. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in on-line components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence.  It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Self-declared short-term absences for any reason

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason. A short-term absence covers two calendar days, and no documentation is required. The student has only one self-declared short-term absence per term and this absence can only be used during the formal lecture period. Self-declared absences cannot be applied to Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), or Studio (STU) course components.  Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

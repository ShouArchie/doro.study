Course Description

COMMST 111:

This course focuses on developing the qualities and transferable skills necessary for integration, continuous learning, and professional development. This course establishes a common baseline of leadership, communication, and collaboration capabilities for Accounting and Financial Management majors. Focus will be on the design and delivery of various types of communication and the development of basic teamwork skills.

View requirements for COMMST 111

COURSE DESCRIPTION

COMMST 111 is a course in effective public, interpersonal, and group communication, designed to develop communication awareness in four key areas: the relationship between self and others; the relationship between purpose and responsibility; the context of communication; and interpersonal power.  Each of these areas will be explored while pursuing the Communication Studies program's primary objectives.  This course will:

  •  examine agency and implication;
  •  support critical and creative competencies; 
  • encourage a language and imagination for the public good; as well as
  • recognize diverse linguistic practices and modes of expression as resources for writers and speakers.

You will develop knowledge and embody skills in these four areas through exploration of communication theory, preparing and presenting individual and team presentations, exploring team dynamics as you work towards a team presentation, and giving feedback. 

 

COURSE PURPOSE

To succeed as a professional, you need what are typically termed “human skills” or “people skills” because those determine whether your technical knowledge can be successfully applied to deliver results. Over many of your degree-required courses, you will be put into situations where you will develop and demonstrate these “people skills.”  COMMST 111 is designed to build a strong foundation of communication and collaboration capabilities which are two of the people skills important to your academic, co-op, and professional success.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
increase your awareness of interpersonal communication in yourself and others
gain a familiarity with theories and vocabulary associated with interpersonal, group, and public communication
reflect on your own communication practices and plan and practice new strategies to hone your skills
learn the organizational, vocal, and physical awareness and skill needed to give effective presentations
gain the ability and confidence to deliver individual and team presentations
develop the critical and communication skills required to give feedback on team behaviour

Team Contract (5%)

Early in the course, you will choose, or be assigned, a group of 5 people to work with on some projects through the term. I expect that you will be in contact with your group early in order to set up expectations for communication through the term, and to negotiate how you will approach your shared assignments.  In order to facilitate your expectations for communication with each other, you will create a contract as a team, outlining what you have agreed upon in your group. The contract will be due in the third week of class and is a pass/fail assignment. If you complete it according to assignment expectations you will earn 5/5; if you fail to hand it in or fail to meet expectations, you will earn 0/5.

Response Assignments: (3 at 10% each for a total of 30%)

On assigned days you will submit a roughly two-page response assignment that connects the course readings to your own experience; these may be due outside of class or be completed in class according to instructor preference.  Each response will involve at least two direct quotations from a required course reading and will involve connecting a major concept in that reading to your assignments, your teamwork, or your general experience. Students will be provided with a new topic for each assignment. The topics are mentioned in the assignment outline. Students are required to write 3 individual responses worth 10% each.

Individual Persuasive Presentation (20%)

You will individually design and present one five-minute, prepared, persuasive presentation to be presented in class. The subject matter is up to you but you are encouraged to talk to your instructor about your proposition. 

Notes on Presentations:

  • You must electronically submit one outline and one cue card for your formal presentation. 
  • You may lose up to one full letter grade on your presentation if it is under 4:00 or over 6:00. You may lose up to 10% of your final grade on a presentation if you fail to submit an outline and/or cue card. 
  • Do not collaborate with other students on your presentation design or plagiarize your topics or sources.
  • All presentation content in all rounds must be original, including humorous material and pop culture references.

Team Presentation Interpersonal (10%)

Within your assigned teams, you will design and present one prepared, ten- minute presentation teaching the class one of the key concepts from the readings assigned on the day you present, and you will plan for another ten-minute question-and-answer period. You will be expected to do research outside of the reading and your team will help to clarify the concept with this research and engage the class in an activity or discussion that helps them to understand the concept. Two teams will present in each class during this round of presentations, and the presentations will take place at the beginning of the class.

Team Case Study Presentation  (20%)

Within your assigned teams of five, you will design and present one prepared, twenty-minute presentation, offering a solution to the financial challenges presented in the case study.  The presentations will be held in class on the dates assigned and will be graded in collaboration between your COMMST 111 instructor and a TA from SAF.

Peer Feedback for Case Study (5% Individual)

You will arrange for everyone in your team to each give written feedback to one member of the team. The feedback assignments will focus on the experience of working with your team member, drawing on the interpersonal/team relationships. Each feedback assignment will include five feedback messages, three positive and three constructive. You will be required to make your feedback available to your team member and to be available for any questions your team member has about the feedback. 

Participation (10% Individual)

Because this course is a workshop course, and because you develop skills that you will employ in your professional life, 10% of your final grade depends on your in-class participation and your professional attitude and performance.  You can earn credit easily and consistently by contributing thoughtfully on a regular basis.  Consistent contribution is important, but quality of contribution is significant as well; you will not earn an outstanding participation grade by dominating discussion.

 

Attendance grades will contribute significantly to your participation grade. Please note that attendance is mandatory; see course policies for penalties regarding absence and lateness without legitimate reason.

Lateness: After your first lateness, each late arrival to class will result in a 0.5% penalty to your participation grade.

Absence: Without documentation and/or self-declaration of absence in Quest, after your first absence, every time you are absent from class, you will receive a 1% penalty to your participation grade.

Submission of Assignments: All assignments must be submitted on the date indicated on the syllabus or by your instructor, with extensions given in extenuating circumstances and after consultation with your instructor. Late submissions without contact will receive a 2% mark down for each day late, including weekends. All assignments should be word processed and, unless otherwise specified, double-spaced and free from spelling, punctuation, and grammatical errors. I will not accept emailed assignments unless previous arrangements have been made.

Grading of Shared Assignments: Group assignments are designed to build teamwork skills and to allow groups to share decision-making and workloads; shared grades reflect this.  Your instructor has the right to give a reduced grade, or to give no grade, to any student who does not participate equitably or at all in shared assignments. 

In the case group challenges to shared grades, your instructor will require you each submit a checklist of what you have contributed and your individual grades will be assessed from there.

Research and Documentation: All submissions involving research will be in accordance with MLA format. 

Course Materials: All required readings and materials for this course will be made available to you through e-reserve. 

A word on email and office hours**: Office hours will be held online or in-person at the discretion of your instructor; you may also email your instructor with questions. Please be aware that your instructor may not answer email on weekends, evenings, or holidays, so plan accordingly. 

**When sending an email, please include a subject line, your student number, name, the class you’re in, and a detailed explanation of your inquiry. 

Discussion Guidelines: Readings, themes, and discussions in this class may challenge your attitudes, your unconsciously held beliefs, or your positions on current issues. This may feel uncomfortable at times, but discomfort can be an important part of learning. In order to ensure a safe and open space for all students to converse and learn productively, it is vital that we conduct our discussions with professionalism and respect. What this looks like is: 

  • Listening without interruption
  • Paying attention and responding to what is said
  • Using inclusive language, from addressing others according to their wishes, to avoiding harmful language, including statements that attack or demean another’s race, sex, gender identity, religion, social class, disability, physical characteristics, and personal attacks in general. Changing your language if a problem is pointed out.
  • Focusing on the points/arguments being made and avoiding personal attacks (ie, the point/ argument has shortcomings, rather than the person)
  • Supporting claims with reasoning, evidence, and relevant examples
  • Respecting instructor moderation: If you are asked to change your language, stop or reconsider a line of argumentation, or otherwise modify your conduct in consideration of others, you do so. We are all learning and it’s normal to mess up. If disrespectful conduct persists after being asked to change your behavior, you may face disciplinary action.

A note on grades: The points below address how marking works in the Faculty of Arts:

  • Instructors in Communication Studies will not start with a score of 100 and deduct points from that 100. Rather, instructors will evaluate your work to see how it meets the expectations for that assignment as outlined in the syllabus.
  • C work (62 and above) is work that demonstrates moderate attention to assignment expectations
  • In Communication Studies courses, your work will be evaluated in several areas including presentation quality (writing, speaking, etc.), critical thinking, depth, organization, cohesiveness (how well your work hangs together), creativity, understanding of concepts, etc. Assignments may be strong in one of these areas, and weak in other areas. A’s are awarded when work is exceptional in every area.
  • As indicated in the UW undergraduate calendar, the following is the UW grading system: 95, A+; 89, A; 83, A-; 78, B+; 75, B; 72, B-; 68, C+; 65, C; 62, C-; 58, D+; 55, D; 52, D-; 38, F.
  • The below offers an explanation of marks in Communication Studies

o  A, assignment is exceptional in all areas noted above and in all areas specified in the syllabus

o  B, assignment competently addresses all areas noted above and all areas specified in the syllabus, and a few sections may be exceptional

o  C, assignment moderately addresses all areas noted above and all areas specified in the syllabus

o  D, assignment offers less than moderate attention to all areas noted above and all areas specified in the syllabus

  • F, assignment generally fails to offer any attention to areas noted above and areas specified in the syllabus

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as developing persuasive techniques, writing responses to the ideas of others, and reflecting on your experiences. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. 

Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights.  For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.  In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online. The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

Accommodating religious and spiritual observances 

[Applies only to students taking undergraduate courses]

The University of Waterloo has a duty to accommodate religious, spiritual, and other creed-based beliefs and practices under the Ontario Human Rights Commission (2015) Policy on preventing discrimination based on creed. Students may seek accommodations for missed course components on religious, spiritual, or other creed grounds. In such cases the students should please consult the instructor within two weeks of the announcement of the due date for which the accommodation is being sought.  Students also may request accommodations for temporary absences from classes or other course-related activities to engage in prayer or other daily spiritual practices. Instructors are expected to make reasonable arrangements to respectfully accommodate such requests. The University of Waterloo has a number of multi-faith spaces that students can use for faith-based practices.

Declaring absences

[Applies only to students taking undergraduate courses]

Please see the Academic Regulations section of the Undergraduate Calendar for more details. Regardless of the process used to declare an absence, it is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructor so that the instructor can determine how to accommodate the missed work. 

Absences due to religious, creed, and spiritual observances

Beginning Fall 2024, students can submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration Form in Quest. This form allows the student to register dates of potential conflicts with coursework. Students are expected to record their absence within two weeks of announcement of the due date or scheduled examination date for which academic accommodation is being sought. Submission through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. 

Absences due to verified illness 

For absences due to illness that are not pandemic-related, students need to obtain a Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances Process. Submission through the Absence Verification System, once registered, will notify your instructors of your absence. Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation to your instructors, teaching assistants, or other course personnel.  To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able to given their health condition.

Absences due to extenuating circumstances (e.g., bereavement)

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (e.g., bereavement, serious family illness), students submit a request for absence due to extenuating circumstances to the Absence Verification System for processing. Students use the form to submit supporting documentation, which will be considered in a comparable manner to a VIF. Official documentation is necessary before any action can be taken. Examples are plane/train/bus tickets, court documents, police reports, death certificates/obituaries/letters from funeral directors. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able to given their circumstances.

Self-declared absences due to COVID-related illness

Students can self-declare an absence on Quest due to COVID-related illness or a requirement to self-isolate. A self-declared COVID absence covers up to 10 days and no documentation is required. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in on-line components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence.  It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Self-declared short-term absences for any reason

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason. A short-term absence covers two calendar days, and no documentation is required. The student has only one self-declared short-term absence per term and this absence can only be used during the formal lecture period. Self-declared absences cannot be applied to Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), or Studio (STU) course components.  Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

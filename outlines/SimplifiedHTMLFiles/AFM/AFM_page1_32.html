Course Description

AFM 321:

This course integrates financial planning and taxes for individuals. It connects elements of planning, saving, spending, and reporting to budgeting and tax implications, as they evolve over an individual's lifetime.

View requirements for AFM 321

AFM 321 helps students develop a do-it-yourself (DIY) individual financial planning and tax mindset. Students will learn how to prepare and assess a basic financial plan for themselves, centered around the budget, across various life stages. Within each area of planning, saving, spending, and reporting, students will develop appreciation of important financial and tax considerations that will set them up for financial success.

Level: At least 3A AFM, CFM, SFM, Mathematics/CPA, or Biotechnology/CPA students.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Prepare a financial plan and budget for various life stages using fore core elements (planning, saving, spending, and reporting)
2. Analyze and recommend a financial plan/budget for various life stages
3. Integrate tax implications and analysis within your plan/budget and each life stage
4. Use data and analytical techniques to evaluate comparable investments
5. Value and support contributions from others

Budget and Financial Planning Assignments (37%) 


Individually or in groups, students will complete mini-cases requiring them to prepare a budget and all related documentation, including tax implications, related to planning, saving, spending, and reporting. Assignments could provide a scenario; others could require the student(s) to prepare their own scenario. Three assignments will be used to represent the first three life stages in the course (student, new grad, and mid-life). Weights of the assessments will increase over the term, as will expectations for completion. All assignments will be completed and submitted outside of class time, by 11:59pm on the stated due date.

Final Exam (25%) 


Students will write a comprehensive final exam that builds on the previous budget and financial planning assignments and will require them to prepare or analyze a budget/financial plan, related schedules, and consider tax calculations and implications. The exam will represent the final life stage in the course (retired life) yet also require knowledge accumulated throughout the semester. Accordingly, you should expect to be tested on all material, including text, course readings, assignments, and class discussions delivered throughout the course. The final exam must be the exclusive work of the individual student. The final exam will be written on TBD (will be posted on LEARN once released), following the Winter 2025 schedule provided by the Registrar’s Office. 

Faculty of Arts policy provides that students who wish to review their final examination papers informally may do so without instituting a formal appeal procedure. Such review will take place under supervised access only and will be arranged in a way that is mutually convenient for the instructor and the student.

Group Data Assignment (15%)

Students will work in a group to complete an evaluation and comparison of various selected or randomly determined investments. The assignment will require a written report detailing the comparison and conclusions reached, as well as collection and analysis of data and a visualization of certain findings. The assignment will be completed and submitted outside of class time, by 11:59pm on the stated due date.

Group Formation and Feedback

Formation: For the Data and third Budget and Financial Planning assignments, students work in groups of 4 (or 3, depending on total enrolment), and groups can be formed across sections. Students can self-enroll starting Wednesday Jan 8 and must enrol by 11:59 pm on Friday, Jan 24; unenrolled students will be allocated to a group at random after the deadline. To enroll yourself, i) select View Available Groups in LEARN-Connect-Groups; ii) select Join Group in the Actions column of Group Assignments Team. To view a group member list (before enrolling), click the link in the Members column; after joining, click the Email Group icon to contact existing group members.

Feedback: By default, we assume each group member contributes fairly and thus all members of a team normally receive the team’s grade for each assignment. If you agree, you need not submit any feedback. However, peer evaluations will be available for students to highlight a difference from this norm (i.e., to note a particularly strong or weak team member contribution). If you submit feedback, we ask that you evaluate each member, including yourself. Your form will remain strictly confidential and cannot be seen by other group members. However, we will blind carbon copy all group members on an email, if at least one member submits, and ask all members for feedback so that we have a more complete assessment of the group. A dropbox for each group assignment will be on LEARN, with a feedback form and submission instructions. Finally, should any serious, significant teamwork issues arise that cannot be resolved among the team, communicate with your instructor as soon as your resolution attempts fail, not after the assignment has been evaluated.

Course Questions (13%)

TopHat Knowledge and Reflection Questions (5%)

Knowledge and/or reflection questions will appear throughout the course notes (in TopHat). Knowledge questions will be assessed for accuracy and reflection questions will be assessed for completeness. The due dates for these questions are outlined in the detailed class schedule and on TopHat. Late submissions will not be accepted or graded.

To be awarded a grade for these components, you must enroll in TopHat using your UW username-specific email (no alias UW email address, or non-UW email address). Students failing to do so will be given one follow-up opportunity to correct the error, after which a grade of zero will be assigned for subsequent assessments.

Module Practice Problems (8%)

Each of the four life cycle modules contains an assigned practice problem, posted to LEARN. Electronic submissions (Word, Excel, OneNote, etc.) are accepted; you may also handwrite your solution and scan to pdf for submission. Submissions must be uploaded to LEARN (in the respective dropbox) by the stated due date in the detailed class schedule/LEARN calendar. Late submissions will not be accepted or graded.

Module problems will be attempted independently and marked for completion. GenAI use is not permitted for these problems. You will receive 2 (full) marks with a legitimate attempt at each problem, even without the correct answer. You must show your work for the markers to be able to assess your assignment. If you make a weak attempt at completion, you will receive 1 mark; an un-submitted or “no effort” attempt will result in a mark of 0. In general, assessments of your assignments are not eligible for regrading, unless a section of your assignment was clearly missed, or the stated mark was recorded incorrectly.

Class Participation (10%)

In-class participation is expected from students since class interaction is key to a positive learning experience. Most of our class time is spent applying knowledge towards financial planning, budgeting, and taxes, but it is directed by you, the student. The instructor is a facilitator of the knowledge and discussion.

Students will receive a final grade out of 10 based on detailed records compiled by teaching assistants and reviewed by the instructor. Grades will exclude a student’s lowest 2 classes, and will be otherwise be determined as follows:

  • 0 if you did not attend classes throughout the term;
  • 1-3 if you attended classes throughout the term, but did not participate;
  • 4-6 if you attended classes throughout the term, and participated sporadically and/or inconsistently throughout the term;
  • 7-8 if you attended classes throughout the term, and participated meaningfully each week consistently throughout the term;
  • 9-10 if you attended classes throughout the term, and participated meaningfully in the discussion several times each week consistently throughout the term.

Students must attend the section in which they are registered to be awarded participation marks. Note also that unprofessional behaviour will reduce participation marks (e.g., arriving late and/or leaving early without explanation, talking loudly in side conversations or otherwise interrupting others that are speaking, etc.).

Requests to Regrade an Assessment

If you believe that a significant error has been made in the grading of a budget or data assignment, you may request a regrade. Regrade requests will not be available for Course Questions (TopHat and Module). Upon request, assignments will be evaluated by a different teaching assistant and marks could go up or down as part of the regrade process. The regrade instructions are as follows:

  1. Regrade requests must be submitted AFTER 24 HOURS and within one week of the assignment grade release. The 24 period is to ensure you give yourself sufficient time to effectively debrief your feedback and craft a reasonable request. Be mindful of the date and time grades are released, which correspond to a related announcement posted to LEARN. We will not respond or allow a regrade if you do not follow this requirement. Relatedly, if you do not receive a response confirming your request, it has not been considered.
  2. Our grading procedures for consistency and accuracy help to ensure that all students are treated the same, but exceptions can occur. Again, we expect that regrade requests will be submitted only for substantial errors in grading (e.g., your individual feedback says that you didn't do something, yet you can show that you did). 
  3. A regrade request must be made in writing by email to your section instructor and the lead Course Assistant, Damir Baizhiyenov (damirbaizhiyenov1@gmail.com), where the student submits a written report highlighting where the regrade is requested and why it is merited for specific parts of their submission. Your assignment will not be regraded based on comments like “I think I deserve a higher mark on my [category] score”. The comment should indicate what element of your submission that you believe did not receive deserved credit. Although rare, we will always make corrections for clear inconsistencies in your grade (i.e., specific feedback says you achieved one ranking, but assessment was checked for a lower ranking).
  4. Regrades will not be completed with the student present. Please do not ask your instructor to review your assignment with you for additional points, which is unfair to other students adhering to the written process.
  5. To ensure an overall fair grading process, your assignment will be reviewed in total to determine if there are reasons to increase, or decrease, the grade. Thus, regrades are not “one-way bets”. Your final grade could go up, down, or remain the same after the regrade is completed.

Late Submission Policy

All assignments are due at 11:59:59pm on the stated due date. An assignment submission at midnight (12:00:00am) or later is late. Penalties, up to 100%, occur for late submissions. The penalty is 10% for submissions within one hour (i.e., until 12:59:59am), 25% for submissions within 24 hours, and 100% after 24 hours. Where applicable, final grades for an assignment, after penalty, are rounded down to the nearest whole number. For example, an award of a mark of 89.5/100 before a 25% late penalty (25 points) means a revised mark after reflecting the late penalty of 64/100. 

Reminder: Late submissions for Course Questions (TopHat and Module) are not accepted and will not be graded.

Accommodations for missed assessments follow SAF policy, as referenced below. Familiarize yourself with the policy: Students are responsible for following the Arts process for documentation AND contacting their instructor to request accommodation for absences by the specified deadlines. Failure to follow the approved processes will result in a zero for that course component. By default in this course, the weight for accommodated assessments is rolled forward to the final (budget and financial planning) exam. 

If an accommodation is approved regarding a missed final exam, you need to consult with the course instructor about writing a deferred final exam. Self-declared absences due to COVID-related illness

Students can self-declare an absence on Quest due to COVID-related illness or a requirement to self-isolate. A self-declared COVID absence covers up to 10 days and no documentation is required. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in on-line components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence.  It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Self-declared short-term absences for any reason

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason. A short-term absence covers two calendar days, and no documentation is required. The student has only one self-declared short-term absence per term and this absence can only be used during the formal lecture period. Self-declared absences cannot be applied to Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), or Studio (STU) course components.  Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI. 

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. 

Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights.  For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.  In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online. The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

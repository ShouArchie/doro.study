Course Description

AFM 346:

This course introduces applications of classification/prediction methods and machine learning used in accounting and finance settings. Samples include bankruptcy prediction, fraud detection, and loan default.

View requirements for AFM 346

Models enable us to understand the world by providing insights into complex, large, or dynamic datasets. They can also predict important variables such as price, sales, and risk. "Prediction" involves forecasting values that have not yet been categorized or can only be measured in the future. For instance, is a particular transaction fraudulent? If so, what is the probability of fraud? Another example is predicting next month's sales and identifying the factors that will influence them.

This course introduces machine learning and its applications for prediction in accounting and finance. We will explore various machine learning methods, ranging from linear regression to neural networks. A key focus of this course is building machine learning pipelines, which include data transformation, model training, prediction, and model evaluation. Additionally, we will cover exploratory data analysis, feature engineering, and methods for assessing results, including model performance, underfitting vs. overfitting, bias vs. variance, and model explanation. Application areas discussed will include loan defaults, product pricing, product sales, and market trends.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Define predictive analytics and machine learning. Explain the model building process, overfitting and underfitting, bias and variance, discuss the use of independent training, testing and validation datasets, as well as cross-validation. (Fluency in the languages of business, entrepreneurship and technology)
Construct models and evaluate them using different performance metrics. Derive an optimal set of hyperparameters through experimentation. Analyze and interpret key model performance metrics. Establish relationships between predictor and predicted variables. (Problem-solving capabilities)
Produce replicable reports that combine code, results, and free form text. Communicate complex relationships using tables and graphs. Write and debug code for training and evaluating models, as well as making predictions. (Communication capabilities; problem-solving capabilities)
Analyze, discuss, and summarize critical issues related to data analytics and machine learning, including how predictive analytics impacts businesses, ethical implications of predictive analytics, and the relevance of model explainability, among others. (Attributes/qualities of a financial professional)

Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

 

Late Submission Policy

All assignments and projects are expected to be submitted on time. The LEARN platform will accept submissions during the period posted for each deliverable. Exceptions may be permitted only by arrangement with the instructor at least three days (72 hours) before a submission deadline, or in case of documented, extenuating circumstances (such as illness, bereavement, and so on). The penalty is 10% of your assignment grade for each day delayed.

Group Work Policy

All assignments are graded group work. Groups can consult with other groups, but each group must submit their own version of the working results.

Course Description

AFM 322:

Introduction to valuation and applications of financial derivatives such as options, futures, forwards, and swaps.

View requirements for AFM 322

The main purpose of this course is to provide students with a working knowledge of how derivatives can be used in managing financial risks. Corporations and financial institutions face financial risks arising from changes in market variables such as interest rates, foreign exchange rates, commodity prices and stock prices. Forwards, futures, options and other derivative products have become essential tools for hedging and controlling these financial risks. These products are usually more complicated than traditional cash instruments such as stocks and bonds and require more than just basic analysis.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe and contrast the various types of derivative markets and instruments.
Exhibit a sound understanding of the key factors that influence valuation of the various instruments
Analyze risk management and hedging opportunities.
Identify and apply the appropriate valuation techniques given the information available.
Appraise/critique/interpret investment opportunities in a professional setting.

Participation (10%)

Individual student participation is strongly encouraged. Your participation grade will largely reflect the consistency of your attendance and the quality of your contribution as judged by the professor. 

A seating chart will be used: in order to gain full credit for your contributions, you must i) sit in the same seat every class ii) display every class (visibly) a large, clear name tag containing the name you wish to be called.

Half of your participation mark is attendance. The other half is comprised of your contributions. Contributions are recorded and ranked by an in-class TA according to the following rubric:

  • Attendance (2 points): Attending class on time and without distractions (e.g. social media, headphones, texting, etc).
  • Tier-1 Contributions (1 point): Low-quality contributions. For example, either answering one "yes/no" type question, asking an administrative question, or reading an answer off your screen verbatim.
  • Tier 2 Contributions (2 points): High-quality contributions. For example, either one thoughtful and thorough answer to a student/instructor question, or one thoughtful question that led to class discussion.

Your participation mark for each class is calculated out of 4 contribution points. You may “over-score” for a particular class but only up to 5 points (i.e. the maximum per class score is 5/4 = 125%); therefore, consistent attendance and contribution throughout the semester is very important. Your contribution scores for each class are then tallied up and averaged to arrive at your final mark for the semester.

In addition to the above, student activity on Piazza (meaning, accurately and thoroughly answering other students’ questions) will be counted as a bonus when determining participation marks. 

Class discussions will focus on the posted material relating to each reading. However, the discussion is not constrained to that material – original thought and contributions are always encouraged. You are expected to arrive in class having read any assigned materials and attempted the problem sets. Be prepared to explain and expand upon your answers. Also, prepare your own questions or ideas.

Potential quality contributions include: identifying the major issues within the topic of discussion (helping to ‘set-up’ analysis), answering questions, asking constructive questions, providing constructive criticism, contributing comments, opinions or ideas. In the best scenario, your contributions will be made voluntarily; however, students will be called upon (“cold-called”) if needed to improve class discussion.

Paper-Trading Assignment (15%)
You will engage in an interactive simulation of scenarios that involve derivative trading, which will involve using trading platforms and tracking performance in a system colloquially referred to as “paper-trading”. You will explore the different types and uses for derivatives in a hands-on and practical way, and will be responsible to deliver a full report documenting your performance and experience at the end of the semester. A full description of the report can be found on Learn under the Paper-Trading Assignment tab.

Group Cases (15%)

Four finance cases worth 15% in total (i.e. 3.75% each) will be completed throughout the term (see the course calendar). These cases will be completed in-class and in teams of four or five students. Your group will start by reviewing and discussing the case in detail prior to the day of the in-class assignment. On case day, the professor will introduce the case and give guidance prior to the start of the in-class case assignment. Your group will then be given the case requirement page outlining the questions that you will submit answers to on the 2-3 page case requirement summary. Groups will have approximately 60-minutes to complete the case requirement. One case requirement will be submitted per group and each student in the group will receive the same grade. Being absent on case day will result in a grade of zero for that particular student unless a verifiable excuse acceptable to the professor is submitted.

Working in Groups
You will form your own group of four or five students for the group assignments. Your group will work together for all of the cases in the term.

Midterm/Final Examinations (60%)

Two written examinations worth (30% + 30%) = 60% in total will be completed on specific dates during the term (see the course calendar). There will be a midterm covering the first half of the course and a final exam in the final exam period covering the second half of the course. The final exam is not comprehensive. You must pass the weighted average of the midterm and the final examinations to pass the course (i.e. a weighted average score of at least 50%); if you do not pass the weighted average of the two exams, your final course grade will become the lesser of (a) the weighted average on the exams that you did achieve and (b) your course grade as otherwise calculated. Detailed announcements will be made on LEARN closer to the date of each exam providing more information. Both examinations will be written on campus so you must be available on each of these two dates and times.

Exam Preparation

You are expected to stay up to date with the course schedule and have read any assigned readings and slide decks, attempted any problems assigned, and prepared your own questions for posting on Piazza. For those not familiar with Piazza, it is a learning management system (LMS) which allows students to ask questions in a forum-type format. It is designed to connect students, TAs, and professors so that students can get help when they need it. Piazza will be the primary communication platform for the 322 community and is an essential component of any online course. The Lead TA will register the entire 322 community at the beginning of the term. 

Several practice problem sets will be distributed throughout the term (after every module covered). They will primarily be similar to questions from the lecture decks. Your success in the course will depend on your ability to do questions similar to those assigned. Note that doing the questions from these practice problem sets at your own pace while consulting your course notes may not constitute good preparation for doing similar questions under the time pressure of an exam.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

School of Accounting and Finance Academic Integrity Expectations

Students at Waterloo and in SAF are expected to act with integrity, even when faced with difficult situations. Integrity is the cornerstone of the accounting and finance professions. What you practice at the University of Waterloo and in SAF will inform and even define who you are as an A&F professional. As a student at the University of Waterloo and in SAF you are expected to know what constitutes academic integrity, avoid committing offences and take responsibility for your actions. While there are common university wide expectations, you will also be expected to know and comply with course specific expectations as well. Participating in the course introduction and reviewing the course syllabus are basic expectations and are helpful regarding course specific academic requirements.

Policy 71 governs student discipline on campus and SAF works with the Associate Deans of a student’s home faculty when reporting academic misconduct. All suspected academic misconduct, regardless of type or severity, is reported to the appropriate Associate Dean who will review the evidence and apply the appropriate discipline and penalties.

Specific considerations for SAF students:

  • Students majoring in accounting and finance programs at UWaterloo should be aware that, due to the highly structured nature of the study plans and the fact that many AFM courses are offered on a limited basis, a penalty imposed because of an academic offence could result in a significant delay of the student’s degree completion and convocation dates - particularly if the penalty involves a suspension.
  • Students placed on disciplinary probation will not be granted access to activities where they are viewed as SAF representatives. For example, students represent SAF in the AFM Fellowship Program, the Student Investment or Venture Fund, at case competitions, as TAs or Research Assistants, and as recruitment ambassadors. This is not an exhaustive list. When applying to participate in these activities, students are expected to declare if they are/are not on disciplinary probation. Normally, this access is denied for a period that spans two complete academic terms subsequent to the term in which the offence occurs.

The academic integrity expectations for each of the assessments in this course have been outlined above as have the potentially unique implications for SAF students who fail to meet integrity expectations. These expectations are provided for further clarity and accompany the expectations outlined below in the discipline section (University Policy) and do not replace or reduce the importance of these expectations. If ever you are unsure about what’s expected of you, you are responsible for seeking clarification from an appropriate source. “I did not know” is not an acceptable personal justification or formal defense to an allegation.

SAF Process for Missed Assessments

Students are expected to complete all course assessments and write examinations as scheduled; however, there may be circumstances where accommodating a missed assessment is approved. See your course syllabus for specific accommodation policies that apply to your course. It is possible the syllabus will identify a process that renders redundant any need to formally declare an absence by means discussed below (e.g., for regular quizzes perhaps the syllabus will identify that only x of y attempts will be included in the final grade).

Review “Declaring Absences” below for instructions on how to declare absences and request accommodations for missed course component(s). To be considered for an academic accommodation, SAF students must follow the Arts Accommodations for Illness or Extenuating Circumstances. 

If an accommodation for a missed assessment is approved, the student is responsible for contacting instructor to discuss accommodation arrangements. The instructor will determine the appropriate accommodation.

If an accommodation is approved regarding a missed final exam, the student needs to consult with the course instructor about writing a deferred final exam. In this case, the University of Waterloo Incomplete (INC) grade process will be followed, where all course requirements are not completed during the term. It is important to understand that, particularly with a cooperative education program, it is possible a deferred writing date will not be perfectly convenient to the student. If necessary, a CEE representative may be able to assist a student in their communications with an employer, however, it is still the student’s responsibility to initiate and manage any such process. Other than in exceptional circumstances, students are expected to prioritize writing the exam and completing the course (including making necessary arrangements for time away from work and being on campus). At the end of the current term, students will receive an INC course grade. Once the final exam has been written, a grade revision will be submitted based on the results of the final exam and other course work. Failure to write the final exam at the next SAF identified opportunity will result in a course grade based on the elements of the course that were completed. 

If a student does not utilize one of the indicated approved methods that can justify missing an assessment (and receive some sort of accommodation) or if the request through the VIF or Extenuating Circumstances System is not approved, the student will receive 0 for that course element missed. 

It is important to understand and properly utilize the appropriate process. Indicating that someone did not know of a process will not be an accepted justification.

Beginning Fall 2024, students can submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration Form in Quest. This form allows the student to register dates of potential conflicts with coursework. Absences due to verified illness

For absences due to illness that are not pandemic-related, students need to obtain a Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances Process. Submission through the Absence Verification System, once registered, will notify your instructors of your absence. Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation to your instructors, teaching assistants, or other course personnel.  To request and arrange accommodation, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able to given their health condition.

Absences due to extenuating circumstances (e.g., bereavement)

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (e.g., bereavement, serious family illness), students submit a request for absence due to extenuating circumstances to the Abse nce Verification System for processing. Students use the form to submit supporting documentation, which will be considered in a comparable manner to a VIF. Official documentation is necessary before any action can be taken. Examples are plane/train/bus tickets, court documents, police reports, death certificates/obituaries/letters from funeral directors. To request and arrange accommodation, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able to given their circumstances.

Self-declared absences due to COVID-related illness

Students can self-declare an absence on Quest due to COVID-related illness or a requirement to self-isolate. A self-declared COVID absence covers up to 10 days and no documentation is required. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in on-line components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence.  It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Self-declared short-term absences for any reason

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason. A short-term absence covers two calendar days, and no documentation is required. The student has only one self-declared short-term absence per term and this absence can only be used during the formal lecture period. Self-declared absences cannot be applied to Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), or Studio (STU) course components.  Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

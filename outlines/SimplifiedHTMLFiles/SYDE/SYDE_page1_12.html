Course Description

SYDE 283:

Introduction to the fundamental laws of electricity, magnetism, and optics; electric fields, voltage, resistance, current, properties of conductors and semiconductors, capacitance, properties of dielectrics, magnetic fields, Faraday's Law and inductance, properties of magnetic materials; electromagnetic waves and the nature of light, geometrical optics; reflection and refraction, physical optics; interference and diffraction.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Understand key concepts and fundamental laws related to electricity, magnetism, and optics (1b)
2. Apply these fundamental laws towards solving problems in electricity, magnetism, and optics (2b)
3. Articulate the interrelationship between charges, fields, magnetism, and electromagnetic waves, and how these impact systems. (1b)
4. Employ basic scripting and physical simulation software to solve electromagnetic problems (5c)
5. Research specific topics of interest to dig deeper and articulate findings through a short report or presentation (3a, 3c)

Student Assessment

Component Value
Topic Mastery Quizzes (10 quizzes 2% each) 20
Participation Activities (7 currently planned, 1.43% each) 10
Unit Tests (3 total, 20% each) 60
Mini Research Project 10

Topic Mastery Quizzes (TMX in course schedule) are administered through Mobius and, as the name implies, graded for mastery. This means that the 2% for each quiz is obtained only for a perfect score on the randomly generated two questions. For these quizzes, students have infinite attempts up to the deadline, and receive automated feedback on unsuccessful attempts. 

Participation Activities (PAX in course schedule) take place entirely in class and are graded for effort. If a student must miss one with legitimate leave, then it is expected that they will complete the activity outside of class within a timeframe agreed upon by the student and Prof. Hunter.

Unit Tests are traditional paper midterm examinations that occur within class time. Unlike the mastery quizzes, partial credit is possible on these assessments. 

The Mini Research Project is done in self-selected pairs of students. It is expected that both partners engage and contribute equally to the success of the project.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

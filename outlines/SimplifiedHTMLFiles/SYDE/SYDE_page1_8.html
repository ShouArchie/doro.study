Course Description

SYDE 211:

Vector differential calculus: divergence and curl, line and surface integrals, fundamental theorems, applications. First order ordinary differential equations: analytical techniques and applications. Second order linear ordinary differential equations: systems of equations and applications. Numerical methods for solving ordinary differential equations (ODEs).

View requirements for SYDE 211

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. classify and use appropriate techniques to solve first order differential equations (DEs) (1a, 2a)
2. understand how various applications lead to first order DEs, and give a physical interpretation of solutions of such DEs (1a, 2a, 2b)
3. use numerical methods to approximate solutions to first order DEs (1a, 2a)
4. solve second order DEs, while understanding the characteristics of the oscillator DE (1a, 2a, 2b)
5. solve linear vector DEs, and visualize solutions using phase portraits (1a, 2a)
6. understand the interpretation of and be able to evaluate line and surface integrals of both scalar and vector fields (1a, 2a)
7. understand the concepts of divergence and curl and use these to apply the major theorems of vector calculus (1a, 2a, 2b)
8. implement difference methods (1a)

(Numbers in parentheses refer to CEAB Graduate Attributes)

1. Knowledge Base
a. Demonstrate understanding of concepts in mathematics

2. Problem Analysis
a. Formulate a problem statement
b. Develop models to solve engineering problems including identifying approximations, assumptions and constraints

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

I encourage all students to become familiar with using large language models (LLM), such as ChatGPT, as learning aids in this course and beyond. They perform fairly well at lots of the material covered in this course, but not all. Remember that these models regularly produce inaccurate statements and should be treated more similarly to a peer than a teacher. 

The most productive way to learn from these tools is not simply to read their explanations and solutions, but to engage with them: ask follow-up questions, call out mistakes and inconsistencies, generate new examples, recognize common modes of failure, etc. 

Further, the course grade is based entirely upon in-person examinations, in which you will not have access to these tools. You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit on these exams.

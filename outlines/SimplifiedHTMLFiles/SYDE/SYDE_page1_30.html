Course Description

SYDE 535:

This course will introduce and apply the fundamentals of computational simulation for complex systems. Data collection and analysis, conceptual modelling, computational modelling, output analysis, and communication of results to non-expert audiences will be covered extensively. Students will develop computational simulations to explore potential solutions for complex societal problems, identifying emergent scenarios, costs, and benefits with the goal of informing decision-makers.

View requirements for SYDE 535

The course is designed around a class project. Students will choose the societal and environmental system they want to tackle in their projects. 

Experience with Python coding is not required, but assignments and class projects will require coding. In-class tutorials will help students develop their coding skills.  

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
List, explain, and apply the steps in the process for developing a simulation to model a socioenvironmental system or process.
Evaluate the range of applicability (strengths and weaknesses) of various modelling decisions for a particular system or process.
Be confident in their ability to use computers to solve scientific and engineering problems
Think critically about simulation results and assess the appropriate confidence level for a solution.
Communicate results in a way as to convince a non-expert audience of the reliability of a solution or simulation insights

The learning outcomes above are related to the following graduate attributes

  • 1.a, 1.d
  • 2a, 2b, 12a
  • 4a, 4b
  • 2c, 4c, 5c, 9a
  • 7a, 9b

Deliverables:

 Homework will be done individually or in groups of up to 2 students. Project-related deliverables will require groups of 3-4 students. Larger groups will only be considered if all project groups have at least 3 members. Evaluations (i.e., homework and project deliverables) will cover materials taught in all lectures. Students are responsible for reviewing materials from lectures they miss. 

Assignment deadlines:

Homework assignments and project deliverables deadlines are on Fridays at 11:59 p.m.

Assignment Submission Procedures:

Assignments will be posted on LEARN 2 weeks before the due date. Due dates are listed in the Tentative Course Schedule. Slight variations may occur and will be announced ahead of time.

Class project:

The Class Project will be announced and discussed in class at the start of the term. The project is split into 5 deliverables: a pitch, two milestones, a presentation, and a final report. The pitch and milestones will prepare the team for their final project application. The project pitch will allow the instructor to provide early feedback on the intended topic. Students will receive in-class instructions on how to prepare each deliverable. The presentation may include invited guests (e.g., UW faculty, students, and external members of the community).

Class projects include a presentation anticipated to happen on Week 12. The other team members are responsible for the presentation if a group member is unavailable. All team members will receive the same marks for their presentation. 

The groups will decide the topic of the class project. Some of the topics selected by students in previous years include:

  • Optimal monetary incentives for EV transition in Canada
  • Study of monetary and educational policies on earthquake retrofits in Vancouver, BC
  • Evaluation of optimal housing subsidies for UW income students
  • Sustainable farming practices in California considering climate change impacts on water availability
  • Assessment of micro-mobility alternatives for Vancouver, BC
  • Optimal location for windfarms in Newfound Land and Labrador
  • Sustainable practices for salmon fish farming in BC
  • Management of algae blooms in Toledo, Ohio

Late Submission Rules:

Homework: A grace period of 2 days is allowed per group, where students can submit the assignment late without penalty. The 2 days can be used for one or split between 2 assignments. A fraction of the day, i.e., hour(s) after the submission deadline, counts as a day. Once grace days are used up, a 10% per day penalty will be applied. If students switch groups for different homework, the minimum number of remaining grace days will be assigned to the group.

Project deliverables: groups are responsible for submitting deliverables on time, irrespective of individual circumstances. A penalty of 10% per day will be applied for late submissions.

No assignment screening will be used in this course.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

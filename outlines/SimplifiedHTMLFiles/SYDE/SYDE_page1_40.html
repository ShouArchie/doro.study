Course Description

SYDE 599:

This course deals with selected topics at the undergraduate level in environmental and societal systems, mechatronics and physical systems, or human factors and ergonomics.

View requirements for SYDE 599

Simulations and virtual environments are excellent tools to conduct controlled, experimental studies aimed at understanding safety, and which in turn can influence optimal infrastructural enhancements, redesign of devices and even impact policy decisions, either directly or indirectly. In this course, the functional relationships between the various elements of driving simulation such as the simulation computer processing, the validity and fidelity of simulators are examined and the factors influencing simulation design and measurement are covered. The design of simulator-based experiments is explored broadly, inclusive of data mining, data analyses techniques and mixed research methodologies. The course wraps up with a discussion of advances in simulation, touching upon augmented reality and mixed reality.

This syllabus is ambitious.  Given time constraints, some topics may be omitted.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
A succinct understanding of the functional relationships between the various sensory and feedback elements of driving simulation [GA1].
A broad familiarity with applications in transportation, psychology, and medicine [GA5]
Informed and well-articulated design of controlled laboratory experiments addressing driver safety [GA3, GA7]
Visualization and analysis of data obtained via simulator-based research methodologies [GA6, GA7]
Ability to articulate the pros and cons of applying driving simulation to a traffic engineering problem inclusive of the definition of independent and dependent variables complete with confounds noted for such experiments [GA3]

CASE STUDY

All students must present a case study seminar individually. 

  • The requirement is to identify a relatively underexplored real-world road safety problem.
  • Conduct a detailed literature review that clearly demonstrates the existence of the problem, and identifies the key Human Factors that mediate the problem.
  • Explore existing solutions (via literature review, expert interviews, or textual analysis or any other method) that can address the identified problem.
  • Critically analyze the shortcomings/strengths with potential solutions.
  • Clearly detail the challenges associated with the problem space.
  • Produce a one-page infographic that can be used to educate the common man (average road user) on the various ways in which they might mitigate a perilous safety issue.

TERM PROJECT

Ideation report - 10%

Experiment design document - 17.5%

Simulation demo - 12.5%

Final Report - 20%

The course will involve one term project expected to be completed in phases over the semester. The topical area is surface transportation human factors. More specifically, students will discuss with the instructor and identify a theoretical behavioral topic with an experimental basis. Students will be expected to define objectives, identify a priori hypothesis, design an experiment involving human subject testing, collect data from participants, analyze the data using appropriate techniques, interpret findings and arrive at conclusions. In its entirety, the project will use lessons learnt in class and teach the student a complete life cycle of experimental, behavioral research. The project (and its components) will comprise 60 percent of the total course grade.

There will be 2 short double-spaced interim reports expected, one at the Ideation phase (3 pager) and a second at the Experimental Design phase (5 pages). A simulation demo is scheduled for Mar 21 where groups must present their simulation scenarios and tasks.

The Term Project will culminate with a Final Report due at the end of the semester. The Final Term Report should be written up in a Scientific Paper format using appropriate Referencing (APA style). The goal (and hope) will be to obtain a scientifically publishable paper from the Term Project effort. Final report must be no more than 10 pages single-space formatted as per the guidelines of the Human Factors and Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

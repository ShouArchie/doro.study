Course Description

SYDE 462:

The second half of a two-term engineering design project continuing the systems design project sequence. Students work in small groups applying the principles of systems design engineering to a situation of concern of their own choosing. Students have individual project supervisors as well as an overall co-ordinator who provides the framework for the term assessments.

View requirements for SYDE 462

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Identify, formulate, analyze and solve complex engineering problems utilizing university-level mathematics and applied engineering fundamentals (KB & PA)
Investigate complex, open-ended engineering problems using appropriate methods and design solutions that will meet predefined needs using proper engineering standards while paying attention to health and safety risks and environmental and societal sustainability. (Inv and Des)
Select and/or create and use appropriate engineering tools while recognizing their limitations. (Tools)
Ability to work effectively as a member of a team. (Teamwork)
Effectively communicate complex engineering concepts to different stakeholders both orally and written. (Comm)
Recognize their role and responsibility towards society as professional engineers, especially their primary role to protect the public. (Prof)
Analyze the impact of the project on society and the environment at all stages of the project life cycle, along with the ethical impact. (Impacts and Ethics)
Apply economic analysis and project management to the project (Econ)
Identify and address their own educational need to maintain their competency in the fields necessary for the project's success. (LL)

The Faculty of Engineering expects students to spend 10-12 hours per week on a course (e.g., 3-hour lectures, 7-9 hours on course preparation, assignments, etc.). In SYDE 461, the primary learning vehicle is a team-based design project. Class members must make a minimum commitment of 7-10 hours per week to the design project over the 12 weeks of the course and 2 hours for occasionally scheduled lectures or TDM (the schedule will be posted on LEARN). Teams are required to meet to work on the project at least 1 hour a week to discuss their project status and use the class times to meet or independently work on the project on weeks when no in-class activities are scheduled.

Student Assessment

There is no defined grading scheme for this course.

Deliverables and Milestone Assessments

  • Note 1 Peer feedback: groups can get two feedback from peers. 

- Gallery Walk II grade is a bonus of up to 2% per group. Individual feedback is mandatory for each student. If your group opts out of the gallery walk, you need to let Prof. Roufail know the reason in writing. If no groups sign up for the gallery walk, the individual peer feedback grade (2.5%) will roll to the individual panel exam grade, i.e., the panel individual exam grade will be out of 17.5%. There are no other exceptions for moving the individual peer feedback grade. 

- A sustainability report is mandatory per group, as well as individual feedback. 

  • NOTE 2: All course deliverables will be graded by the Course Coordinator (Prof. Roufail) with input from engineering support staff, Orion Bruckman, the teaching assistant, and Project Supervisors. Grading will not be delayed due to delayed/missing marks from the Project Supervisor. If a Project Supervisor does not provide grades/input by the specified deadline, then the grade will default to a combination of the Course Coordinator’s grade, the Engineering support’s grade, and the TA’s grade.
  • NOTE 3: All team members must be present for and participate in all their team’s presentations. All team members are expected to be able to answer questions about all aspects of the project and their roles and contributions.
  • NOTE 4: Fair Contingencies for Emergency Remote Teaching. The course outline presents the instructor’s intentions for course assessments, their weights, and due dates in Winter 2025. We will keep to the specified assessments, weights, and dates as best as possible. To provide contingency for unforeseen circumstances, the instructor reserves the right to modify course topics and/or assessments and/or weight and/or deadlines with due and fair notice to students. In the event of such challenges, the instructor will work with the Department/Faculty to find reasonable and fair solutions that respect the rights and workloads of students, staff, and faculty.

General Terms and Assessment (individuals and teams):

  • Your competence in the execution of the engineering design process and in the application of engineering knowledge, skills, and tools.
  • Your professionalism - Engineering is a regulated profession.
  • Your ability to communicate at an appropriate level – Both written and oral communication.
  • Your attention to ethics, safety, and risk management – Both in terms of your project and the larger scale ethics and safety of your design.
  • Your ability to work as an individual and a team member.
  • Your ability to use and apply sound project management tools and practices.
  • Your ability to make good decisions and apply sound engineering judgement.
  • Your documented contributions as an individual to your design team’s efforts.

Course Requirements

  • Each student is expected to complete and submit an individual Engineering Log and related submissions, whether the student is enrolled in SYDE 461 or a GENE or BME equivalent. This is a department requirement for all SYDE-BME students enrolled in Capstone projects. Individual students can fail 461 and/or 462 if it is determined that the student has made insufficient meaningful and competent contributions to the project.
  • All students are expected to participate in panel exams through SYDE 462, GENE, or BME equivalent.
  • All students are expected to participate in all reviews, presentations, panel examinations, and demonstrations of work.

Marking of Team-Based Deliverables:

By default, marks given for all team-based deliverables will be applied to all group members. However, Prof. Roufail reserves the right to scale an individual’s mark for any specific team-based deliverable where insufficient evidence of that student’s contribution to the group effort is presented. The onus is on each individual student to provide evidence of their individual contribution to the team effort. This is one reason for using your Engineering Log consistently and diligently and for filing your Individual Contributions Statement and Reflections deliverable.

If a team feels that lack of effort by one or more group members is negatively impacting the team’s ability to meet the expectations and deadlines for the course, the situation MUST be identified and presented to Prof. Roufail PRIOR to any impacted deliverable to be considered for accommodation in deliverable grading. Otherwise, default grading criteria will be applied (e.g., project progress and outcomes will be assessed based on a full team effort). Team members must submit a written request for accommodation, PRIOR to the deliverable deadline, detailing the situation. Note that submission of an accommodation request does not in and of itself guarantee approval.

A Team Health Assessment/Peer Review type of milestone will be assigned towards the end of term in which team members will be given the opportunity to evaluate the contributions of themselves and their team members. These results may further inform individual grades.

It is the responsibility of ALL team members to ensure the accuracy and quality of all aspects of submitted team-based deliverables. Therefore, any academic integrity offences (e.g. plagiarism, dishonest reporting, etc.) arising from a team-based deliverable will impact ALL group members.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

GEOG 407:

This course focuses on ecological and biogeochemical processes that are linked to the terrestrial hydrological cycle and how these relate to the management of natural resources. The objectives of this interdisciplinary course are to explore topics that integrate ecosystem processes with physical hydrology and examine the impacts of human activities on ecohydrological and hydrochemical processes within terrestrial systems. This course focuses on the storage and movement of water, solutes, and nutrients within selected ecosystems (forests, agricultural, wetlands), considering the biogeochemical consequences of human activity such as climate change, wetland drainage, agriculture, and forest harvesting.

View requirements for GEOG 407

Having taken GEOG 303 prior to this course is strongly encouraged.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand linkages between hydrological and biogeochemical processes in the natural environment
Understand the implications of land use change and climate change on selected ecosystems
Analyze and visualize numerical data and write laboratory reports

Practical/Experiential Lab Reports (10%)

Each lab section will attend a total of two “practical labs” – one will be held outdoors in the field and the other will be held indoors in a laboratory setting. Please see the lab schedule for your lab section’s allocated week. If you miss your lab due to illness or another valid reason, you may be able to attend another lab section to catch up, but you must have the written permission of myself or the TAs to do so (this ensures that we can accommodate the number of students for that lab section). For the outdoor lab, students will learn field methods on gauging streamflow and collecting water samples for the determination of water quality parameters. For the indoor lab, students will learn standard techniques for the processing of samples and the analyses of key water quality parameters. Following your practical lab (on your assigned week), a short lab reflection must be submitted by end of day (11:59pm) on the Friday of that week in the Dropbox on Learn. Students will work in pairs or small groups on written lab reflections. 

This time of year can be cold and wet – students must have proper clothing to stay warm and dry for 1.5 hours in the field. If you are unable to participate in the practical labs due to an AccessAbility accommodation need, please see me to discuss alternatives.

 

Data Lab Reports (50% of your grade)

The meticulous construction of lab reports or field reports is a hallmark of any undergraduate education in geography and environmental sciences. The careful analysis of hydrological and water quality data is also critical in water quality research and is a skill necessary to succeed in jobs in this field. These labs will introduce you to the exploration and analysis of standard parameters in water quality analysis, by working with the data and constructing informative tables and graphs and describing what your data is showing you. The final lab, which is worth a larger proportion of your grade, will build on the first two data labs, and will enable you to synthesize them into a formal, written lab report that follows the Lab Report Guidelines (posted on Learn). You may work in pairs for your Data Lab Reports (i.e., two of you will submit one lab report). Data labs are due by 11:59pm on their assigned due dates.

 

Final Exam (35%)

A final exam will be held during the exam period. This will be based on the lecture content throughout the term. The exam will consist of a series of multiple choice, short answer and longer essay-style questions.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as understanding the importance of proper data collection and analysis, as well as the interpretation of these data. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

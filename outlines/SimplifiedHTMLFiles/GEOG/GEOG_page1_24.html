Course Description

GEOG 314:

This course provides an overview of the key concepts related to climate services (i.e., useful, timely, and robust information about climatic trends that can support decision-making) and the development of the United Nations' Global Framework for Climate Services. The value of climate-informed decision-making across diverse sectors of the economy are examined, both for climate variability and extremes today and in an era of accelerating climate change. The scientific and practical challenges of utilizing climate information for decision-making in government policy, community planning, business operations, and international development, as well as the technical standards and professional ethics associated with providing climate services, are examined. Students gain practical experience in the development and application of climate services through climate data assembly and interpretation.

View requirements for GEOG 314

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Articulate what climate models are, and how they are formulated and tuned.
Describe how climate models are evaluated, what they do well, and continuing challenges.
Use a hierarchy of models helps to answer critical problems in climate science.
Apply climate models and data to a diverse range of sectors and problems.
Skills learned will be applicable to those pursuing graduate school in a physical science discipline as well as other advanced climate research topics, including:
The development of the Global Framework for Climate Services
Climate variability assessment
Climate-informed decision-making across societal sectors
Professional ethics in providing climate services

Discussion posts

Students will need to respond to and discussion 5 posts through the term regarding the climate science and/or the sectors of climate services presented in class and via guest lectures. We will use Perusal to engage with discussion prompts and assess engagement with material. 

 

Generative AI Assignment

Students will be asked to used generative AI to explore how large language models are interpreting the varying opinions on climate change in the Canadian context and compare to the scientific evidence of climate change. 

 

Midterm #1

To demonstrate knowledge of the climate science and data, students will be required to work in teams of 3-5 to develop Instagram Reels/TikToks which depict the current knowledge of climate science, climate models, and climate change. This creative content should be directed toward the general public and/or peers to educate others on the current knowledge of this field. The content might be shared with the department and more broadly through the department's social media channels.

 

Midterm #2

To demonstrate knowledge of the application of climate services, students will need to create an infographic which describes the importance of climate data in a sector & region of the world. The infographic should be directed toward the general public/practitioners of the sector. 

 

Final Project Paper

Students will work in teams of 2-3 undergraduate students with one graduate student to explore the climate services used in a particular sector of the economy/society. The specific sector will need to be identified by the 4th week of the term. The paper will need to present a review of literature pointing to how climate data are currently used in the sector (or adjacent if the climate services in the sector are underdeveloped or nascent). 7-10 pages will be the expected length of the paper (excluding title page, tables/figures, and references). There will be three stages to the paper: outline, first draft, and final draft. The final draft will be due April 4.

 

Final Project Presentation

To demonstrate clear scientific communications skills in a more academic and/or business style, each team of students will present their paper in an engaging 10-12 minute presentation with 3-5 minutes for questions. This will take place during the last week of class.

Research Ethics: The University of Waterloo requires all research conducted by its students, staff, and faculty which involves humans as participants to undergo prior ethics review and clearance through the Director, Office of Human Research and Animal Care (Office). The ethics review and clearance processes are intended to ensure that projects comply with the Office’s Guidelines for Research with Human Participants (Guidelines) as well as those of provincial and federal agencies, and that the safety, rights and welfare of participants are adequately protected. The Guidelines inform researchers about ethical issues and procedures which are of concern when conducting research with humans (e.g. confidentiality, risks and benefits, informed consent process, etc.). If the development of your research proposal consists of research that involves humans as participants, the please contact the course instructor for guidance and see: https://uwaterloo.ca/research/office-research-ethics

Co-op interviews and class attendance: Co-op students are encouraged to try and choose interview time slots that result in the least amount of disruption to class schedules. Generative AI
Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions.

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

GEOG 101:

An introduction to human geography through key subfields of the discipline, examining population change, the rise of cities, our relationship to nature, social inequalities, economic forms, cultural difference and environmental change at the local and global scale.

View requirements for GEOG 101

Course Organisation: The course revolves around the concept of “Sustainability/Sustainable Development” as explored through Human Geography. The fundamentals of human geography are explored in the first part of the course, followed by an examination of spatial interaction and migration, and population and health aspects of human geography. So-called “food geography” is then explored, followed by an examination of the geographic basis of patterns of urbanization, and an exploration of global economic activity. The course concludes with a short unit on hazards geography, analysis of global development patterns and processes, and an assessment of human impacts on and sustainability in terrestrial and marine environments. For Winter 2025, course content will be delivered through a combination of in-class lectures and self-viewed readings and videos. 

The course explained in graphic form: https://i.imgur.com/E2FiOkG.png

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe the relationship between Human Geography and the broader discipline of Geography
Summarize the major themes which define Human Geography
Compare and contrast Human Geography theory with real-world expressions of Human Geography
Construct solutions which apply Human Geography concepts and skills to real-world sustainability problems
Synthesize the concept of “Sustainability/Sustainable Development” from course themes and content

Assignment #1 (20%): Understanding the SDGs

Summary: a short assignment in which you choose one of the 17 SDGs and explore how “your SDG” is being translated into real world sustainability action.  Due: February 14th

Directions: 

  1. Begin by generating a list of all of the SDGs using ChatGPT or some other online source. Either screenshot or copy the entire list to create page 1 of your assignment.
  2. Choose the one SDG that most interests you, connects with your current program of study or employment, or has some connection to your family or friends or a person you admire.
  3. Explain why you chose the SDG that you chose.
  4. Carry out online research to find an example of a real world project, program or intervention which is being guided by your SDG. For example, if you choose SDG 13, you would find a real world example of a project which is reducing greenhouse gas emissions, increasing carbon sinks, regenerating forests, reducing or eliminating methane gas releases, etc. You may wish to focus on a specific city or country that you have spent time in, grew up in, etc, to make the assignment more meaningful. You could also focus on a large institution (e.g. University of Waterloo, A federal government agency like Health Canada) which has been altered somehow through SDG actions.
  5. Describe this real world initiative, connecting your explanation of your SDG to the real world initiative.
  6. There is no need to go beyond 3-4 pages for full marks.

Assignment Components (45 total marks available)

  • Section 1: List of all SDGs (single screenshot or copied list) (5 marks)
  • Section 2: Summary of your SDG, and explanation of why you chose it. You MUST explain why this SDG is important to you personally...it connects to your personal interests, your program of study, your background, your current job, your family or friends, etc.  (150-250 words…10 marks)
  • Section 3: Description of a real world initiative (project/program/intervention) guided by your SDG (350-500 words…25 marks)
    • Using the questions "Who/what/when/where/why/how" as a rough starting point, describe to the reader in your own words who is leading your initiative, what the initiative is comprised of or focuses on, when the initiative was first launched, where the initiative is intended to operate, why the initiative was started, and how the initiative connects to your SDG.  [NOTE: the "Who/what/when/where/why/how" questions are just idea prompts…you do NOT have to use those exact words in your description, so please feel free to use your own vocabulary and writing style!].
    • Note that you will have to spend some time to find an appropriate initiative (e.g. something that has enough written about it, or is described clearly enough). So if you find yourself making a few false starts at the beginning, don’t worry, this is normal! Please feel free to reach out to a TA to help you with this stage, or to drop in to my office hours.
    • As much as possible, try to find clear evidence that your real world initiative was actually motivated by the SDGs/your SDG (i.e. the initiative is not just vaguely connected. Try to find a quote like "...project __ was created by the __ government in order to make progress on SDG # __")
  • Section 4: Reference List/Sources of Information: at the end of your assignment list all sources you have used to complete the assignment. Like most University assignments, you must properly cite ALL information you have obtained (using Chicago style in text citation format, otherwise known as "the author/date (Smith 2021)" style), and you MUST put all information in your own words unless you use quotation marks. (5 marks)

Expected time for completion: approximately 3-4 hours

GENERATIVE ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE (GenAi)

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used to assist to help generate ideas for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. In general, generative AI should ONLY be used to brainstorm ideas and themes, check your own work to make sure you haven't missed something, etc - NOT to do your writing for you! Please also be aware that "getting away with it" now does not mean your work will not be checked again sometime in the future when AI detection technology becomes better (i.e. students have had their degrees revoked retroactively due to plagiarism discovered YEARS after graduation).  

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence  You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

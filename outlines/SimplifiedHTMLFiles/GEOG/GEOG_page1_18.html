Course Description

GEOG 306:

This course will investigate the human dimensions of the global experience with natural hazards and associated disasters. The physical nature of a wide range of geophysical and biophysical hazards will be explored, paying particular attention to the ways in which hazards become dangerous to humans, and the pathways by which humans can either increase or decrease their vulnerability in the face of natural hazards.

View requirements for GEOG 306

Natural hazards and associated disasters are, in part, a product of inappropriate human modifications or management of the natural landscape. In a time where the human dimensions of natural hazards are becoming increasingly recognized, (e.g. Both the 2023 Turkey-Syria earthquake and Nepal’s 2015 earthquake saw heavy casualties due in large part to poor building standards, and damages from major hurricane and typhoon disasters such as 2013 Typhoon Haiyan in the Philippines and 2022 Hurricane Ian in the United States have been linked to inadequate coastal zone planning), it is appropriate that this course focus on the ways in which humans increase or decrease the risks posed by natural hazards. 

This course investigates the human dimensions of the global experience with natural hazards and associated disasters. A wide range of geophysical, meteorological and hydrological hazards are explored, paying particular attention to 1) the ways in which hazards become dangerous to humans, and 2) the pathways by which humans can either increase or decrease their vulnerability in the face of natural hazards. This is achieved by examining disaster risk reduction as it occurs throughout the disaster management cycle: mitigation, preparedness, response, and recovery. Throughout the course, case studies and examples drawn from countries and regions around the world will be used to clarify conceptual and methodological issues.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand the difference between hazard, disaster, risk, and disaster risk reduction (DRR).
Articulate key perspectives on the various ‘types’ of vulnerability and critically analyze the processes that generate vulnerability to natural hazards.
Appreciate the physical processes associated with various natural hazards, with a focus on geophysical, meteorological, and hydrological hazards.
Define and conceptualize the four pillars of disaster management, including mitigation, preparedness, response, and recovery.
Communicate the benefits and drawbacks of various mitigation approaches.
Critically analyze the portrayal of disasters in the media.
Appreciate the key components of preparing for disasters.
Explain best practices associated with post-disaster recovery, with a focus on housing reconstruction, livelihoods, and psychological recovery.
Apply knowledge to compare hazards theory with real-world case studies.
Understand key methods for reducing disaster risk and fostering resilience through all phases of the disaster management cycle.

In-Class Participation Activities (10 x 1% = 10% of final grade)

  1. Details about in-class ‘participation’ activities are provided exclusively during the class. These activities will vary in format and may include short online quizzes completed through LEARN, as well as brief written submissions completed at the end of the lecture.

  3. Responses should be submitted on LEARN or as a paper submission before leaving class.
  4. A total of 12 in-class ‘participation’ activities are planned throughout the term. Completing all 12 (or at least 11) activities provides an opportunity to improve your grade, as LEARN will automatically select your top 10 scores when calculating your final grade. Each activity contributes 1% to your final course grade, emphasizing the importance of consistent participation.
  5. Please note that these activities are designed to reinforce key concepts and promote collaboration. Active participation is strongly encouraged, as it will not only contribute to your grade but also enhance your learning experience.

Submitting Assignments:

All assignments should be submitted via LEARN. Students should make arrangements for their assignments to be submitted to the appropriate dropbox on LEARN by the time and date indicated in the weekly schedule. Assignments should not be handed to the professor in class or emailed to the professor. LEARN provides a date and time stamp for all assignments submitted to the dropbox – this will be the date used to determine any late penalties that will be applied.

Late Assignments:

Assignments should be submitted by the due dates outlined in the course schedule (also see deadlines noted for each dropbox). However, a three-day grace period will be applied for the written assignments in the course (i.e., the grace period does not apply to in-class participation activities or the final exam). The grace period means that you can submit any assignment up to three days late without explanation or penalty. To encourage students to submit on time, a bonus mark will be applied based on the following submission dates: 

  • Submitted by the original due date at 11:59pm: 3% bonus added to assignment grade
  • Submitted within 24 hours of the original due date: 2% bonus added to assignment grade
  • Submitted within 48 hours of the original due date: 1% bonus added to assignment grade
  • Submitted within 72 hours of the original due date: no bonus and no late deduction

Assignments submitted after the three-day grace has ended will have a late deduction applied at the rate of 10% per day off the assignment grade. 

NOTE: There is no need to email the instructor or TA to have this grace period applied – you can simply submit your assignment at any point within the grace period without penalty. 

Assignments will not be accepted for grading after the marking has been completed and feedback made available for students. 

In some cases, concessions may be made for exceptional circumstances. 

NOTE: If you anticipate being unable to meet a deadline for any reason, please contact the instructor in advance to discuss potential alternative arrangements.

Short-Term Absence Declarations

Short-term absence declaration will be dealt with as follows: 

  1. In-Class Participation Activities: students can either a) count an activity as one of the already included dropped grades OR b) contact the professor to have a missed in-class participation activity re-weighted towards the final exam in the course.
  2. Written Assignments: short-term absence declarations only apply to the original due dates on assignments. The absence declaration will not impact the end date of the grace period; this means that if you use the three-day grace period, a short-term absence declaration on the final day of the grace period will not result in an additional two days to submit the assignment. This essentially removes the need for a short-term absence declaration, since a longer, three-day grace period has already been applied.
  3. Final Exam: short-term absence declarations cannot be used during the final exam period.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

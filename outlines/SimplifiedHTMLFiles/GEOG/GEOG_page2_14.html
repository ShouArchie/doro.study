Course Description

GEOG 607:

Introduces the fundamentals of GIS at a graduate level, including cartographic best practices, map projections and coordinate systems, georeferencing data, quantitative mapping, web mapping, and introductory spatial analysis, including clustering, network, and multicriteria analysis. Intended for students without significant experience in GIS. *eligible for MES.

View requirements for GEOG 607

Course Details

This course provides an introduction to the fundamentals of GIS.  The goal is to provide students with a theoretical understanding of spatial data and analysis concepts.  Lectures discuss underlying theory and how it is implemented in GIS.  Lab assignments will introduce practical tools for analyzing geographic problems and how they can be applied.  Successful students will be able to learn new functions on their own and apply what they have learned to their own research.

The course is designed to offer each student the opportunity to gain a fundamental understanding of GIS through lectures, in-class discussion, and lab assignments based on a variety of GIS methods and applications.  A key component of the course is the completion and delivery of a hands-on GIS term project. 

Prerequisite

No prerequisite.  Graduate students only.  Eligible for MES.  

Students should not have taken previous GIS courses.  Antireq – GEOG 381, GEOG 387.

A familiarity with computers is assumed.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Explain the principles of GIS as a combination of geospatial data input, processing, output, and personnel components.
Design and produce effective maps for visualizing and communicating spatial data and GIS output.
Discuss current issues surrounding the application of GIS.
Apply GIS fundamental concepts and spatial analysis techniques to a variety of applications.
Demonstrate proficiency in using GIS tools for analyzing geospatial problems in the form of a term project.

This course provides an introduction to digital mapping and spatial analysis using GIS.

Course Structure

The course consists of a three hour seminar (lecture/discussion) session each week.  Introductory lectures will provide an overview of key theory for digital mapping and spatial analysis using GIS.  Students are expected to learn any concepts and functions for GIS analysis required for lab assignments or term projects independently or with supplemental readings recommended by the instructor. 

Since this course is seminar-based, no lab component is formally scheduled.  However, significant additional time outside of class will be required to complete the term assignment and to prepare for presentations and discussions.  Students are welcome to use the GIS computing labs in EV1, which are managed by Environment Technology & Instructional Support (ETIS).

Lab Assignments

Students will be required to complete four lab assignments that utilize ArcGIS Pro, which is the most popular GIS software and an industry standard in many fields.  Practical hands-on lab assignments provide an opportunity for students to learn how to use the software and to gain hands-on experience with applying concepts from lectures in the software environment.  The assignments are designed to help students make connections between the concepts discussed in the lectures and how they can be applied in the software. Practical lab assignments expose students to fundamental techniques for map design and data input, management, and analysis. 

Since no lab sections are formally scheduled, students are expected to devote significant independent time to learn software skills and to work on lab assignments.  The time required to complete lab assignments differs from person to person.  Students are expected to commit a substantial amount of time to working on software, mapping, and problem solving.  The ability to work independently is a valuable skill for all GIS users and students are encouraged to take advantage of all available resources and relevant ArcGIS Help sections.  Resources of help will be made available and students may post questions on the course discussion board or visit office hours or help sessions as posted on LEARN.

All work for lab assignments should be submitted according to the instructions provided in the exercises. All submissions will be digital, and must be submitted according to the instructions by the due date for grading.  Assignments should be properly formatted, clearly presented, and easy to read/follow. Indicated time estimates for completing lab exercises are estimates only and do not account for technical troubleshooting.  Students are advised to leave ample time to complete assignments.  Make sure to read and to follow lab assignment instructions carefully.

GIS Applications Term Project

Students will be required to complete a term project using GIS.  Evaluation of the term project will be based on, (a) the project’s methodological and organizational design, (b) application of appropriate GIS techniques, and (c) presentation of the final results in both oral and written form. The final deliverables of the term assignment include submitting a brief written summary of the proposed project topic. During the last weeks of term, final reports will be submitted and final presentations made.  At least one progress meeting should be scheduled with the instructor outside of class for project consultation, which will contribute to the student’s class participation mark.

Class Participation/Engagement/Progress & Attendance

Class participation/discussion will be monitored throughout the term and account for 5% of the total course grade.  This grade will be assigned at the instructor’s discretion and evaluated holistically, based on participation and engagement in class discussions and activities, project consultation meetings, and completion of assignments.  Note that passive attendance is not the same as active class participation (i.e., engagement), which is evaluated at the instructor’s discretion and judgment.

Students should be proactive/organized and schedule at least one progress meeting with the instructor outside of class for project consultation, advice, or update during the term, which will contribute to the class participation/progress grade.  No project consultation/update will result in at least 2% deduction.  Consideration will also be given to students who demonstrate effective time management in the course by submitting all assessments (lab assignments, term assignments, etc.) on time before stated deadlines.

Class attendance will be monitored throughout the term and account for 4% of the total course grade.  1% of the total grade will be deducted for a recorded absence up to a maximum of four.  The instructor has the discretion to evaluate the attendance grade and to deduct marks for excessive tardiness, leaving class excessively early, etc.

Generative AI: This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.).

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

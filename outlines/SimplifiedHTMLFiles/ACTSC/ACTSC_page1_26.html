Course Description

ACTSC 622:

Multiple state models; premiums and reserves for stat dependent policies, including joint life and last survivor benefits; cashflow projection methods; post retirement benefits: valuation and funding. Estimation and inference for survival models. Introduction to longevity models.

View requirements for ACTSC 622

This is primarily a course on life contingency and related topics. The course is a natural continuation of ACTSC 612. The course primarily covers topics in Chapters 8, 9, 10, 11, 13, 14 and 15 of the reference textbook Actuarial Mathematics of Life Contingent Risks.

More precisely, we will develop methodology for more complex insurance products and for a more sophisticated analysis of these products. Topics include multiple state models (for modelling insurance with modern, multiple contingency benefit design); joint life insurance; pensions; cash flow projection methods for premium and capital adequacy testing; and guarantees and options in modern insurance contracts.

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Calculate net premium, modified net premium and gross premium policy values for traditional and state-dependent insurance (including multiple-life insurance policies)
Understand and apply the principles of defined benefit pension plan valuation and funding using unit credit methods
Analyze cash flows for traditional and non-traditional insurance
Identify and evaluate embedded options in simple equity-linked products (e.g., segregated fund policies, universal life policies)

Administrative Policy

Important Remarks: 

1. Pass/Fail: Your final mark must be at least 60% to pass the course.

2. Collaboration: You may discuss assignment questions, but you should write up solutions separately, without access to any other students' solutions, unless the assignment is specifically set as a group assignment. Rules for group assignments will be explained if and when they are assigned.

3. Recommended problems: In addition to the assignment due on April 4, recommended problems will be regularly assigned (and listed on the course webpage). You are expected to work on them to learn the material and prepare for any upcoming examination.

4. Keeping your Coursework: All assignments and midterms must be returned to me or to Helen at the end of the course, for potential review by our external examiners.

5. Re-grading: Requests for re-grading must be made within 1 week after the marked papers were returned to you.

6. Missed examination: If you are ill, you must submit appropriate documentation to your instructor. 

  • If you are ill for the midterm, the midterm weight will be shifted to the final exam.
  • In almost all cases, unless there are significant extenuating circumstances, there will be no shifting of weight for the assignments, and late assignments will receive a grade of 0.

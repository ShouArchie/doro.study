Course Description

ACTSC 331:

Policy Values: Annual, 1/mthly, and continuous cases. Thiele's equation. Modified premium policy values. Multiple state models: applications in life contingencies; assumptions; Kolmogorov equations; premiums, policy values, multiple decrement models. Joint life models: valuation of insurance benefits on joint lives, dependent and independent cases.

View requirements for ACTSC 331

The objective of this course is to build upon the concepts introduced in ACTSC 232 to analyze more complex life insurance problems.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Calculate policy values in the annual, 1/m thly, and continuous cases, including using Thiele's differential equation for fully continuous policies.
Explain and calculate expense reserves and modified premium policy values.
Use multiple state models (identifying the Kolmogorov Forward Equations, calculating probabilities, and calculating premiums and policy values) in applications in life and health insurances.
Construct and use multiple decrement models.
Use models for the valuation of insurance benefits on multiple lives in both dependent and independent cases.

Course Activities

Lectures

Slides will be posted as unannotated versions prior to lectures. My rationale is that deep learning typically requires the physical act of writing things down and annotating in your own words. I strongly encourage you to print out and annotate (or annotate on a tablet, or make your own notes on plain paper) the slides during class.

Practice Problems 

Practice problem sets will be posted on LEARN every few weeks. You should attempt the questions before reviewing solutions to solidify your understanding of the concepts in lecture. Full solutions will be posted approximately 1 week after the problems are released. A list of recommended problems from the textbook will also be posted for each chapter. Practice is crucial to your success in this course and in SOA exams. You have to understand patterns in wording that the SOA uses and how to approach a variety of problems, some of which you may not completely understand until you see examples. This is perfectly natural and part of the learning process to become a great actuary.

Student Assessment

Bonus (defined in the following section) Up to an additional 5%

The Assignments for this course solely consist of the exams. I am designing it this way because a major part of the course is your preparation to take the actuarial exams corresponding to the content we cover here. Treating my lectures as your learning material and the midterms and final as your practice exams for the SOA exam is a good way to think about what we're doing here.

The Bonus consists of opportunities during lectures to contribute to the class discussion. This is an example based class where a lot of what we talk about in lecture is immediately applied. There are a multitude of "Checkpoints" throughout the lectures where I will ask you to contribute either by responding casually, giving an equation, or providing a numerical solution. When a response is given (correct or incorrect!) your name will be written in the annotated course notes for a later tally. Each contribution that you make will earn you 1 bonus point applied directly to your final grade, up to a maximum of five total possible bonus points. (e.g. if you contribute during lecture 6, 24, or 73 times you will receive 5 bonus marks on your final grade. If you contribute 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5 times you would receive that many bonus marks on your final grade. If you have a final score of 100 in the class the bonus marks do not grant you a score in excess of 100.) This is to encourage your engagement and participation to aid everyone's learning experience. The best way to learn this stuff is by practicing with concepts and seeing examples. This helps us all do that together.

If you miss any of the two midterms for any reason, the weight will automatically be shifted to the final exam. I understand that there are many reasons which a VIF may not cover that are perfectly valid for being unable to take an exam. I have had days like that myself, and so if for any reason you don't show up, understand that your weight for that exam will be transferred to the final. (e.g. If you have two events occur causing you to miss Midterm 1 and Midterm 2, your final exam will be worth 100% of your grade in our course. If you have an event causing you to miss exactly one of Midterm 1 or Midterm 2, your grade will be determined based on a 25% weight in the exam you took and a 75% weight on the final exam. I will do this regardless of VIF submission.) See the Administrative Policy section below for the policy for absence during the final exam.

Administrative Policy

Remark Requests 

You may appeal the way a test/assignment was marked by submitting a request within 1 week of it being returned. Requests must be submitted in writing by email.

Illness / Short Term Absences Policy

If you are ill, you must submit a VIF indicating severe illness. (If you are self-isolating due to flu-like or COVID-19 symptoms, you can instead complete the self-declaration form in Quest). Details can be found here: https://uwaterloo.ca/math/accommodations

  • If you are ill for a midterm, you must provide satisfactory documentation within 48 hours by emailing an electronic copy of the VIF to the instructor as well as submitting to the MUO. For satisfactory documentation, the midterm weight will be shifted to the final exam.
  • If you are ill for the final exam, you will receive an INC grade and be required to complete the exam during the next course offering, if you are achieving a passing grade going into the exam. Further, you must have written at least 1 of the 2 midterms to qualify for an INC if you miss the final exam. If not, a grade of DNW will be assigned.

The above accommodations also apply to self-declared short-term absences.

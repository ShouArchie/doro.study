How to get support

We’re here to help you succeed! There are several ways to get assistance:

1. TA-monitored Piazza discussion board

The Piazza discussion board is your online resource for course-related questions.

Who responds: A designated TA will monitor the board and respond to questions. Instructors will not monitor or log into Piazza. The TA will aim to respond within 24 hours on weekdays or by the next business day on weekends and holidays.

When to use Piazza vs. instructor office hours: Piazza is ideal for mild to moderate confusion about specific problems. If you're stuck on several problems (more than three per week) or have complex questions (e.g., "Can someone walk me through Problem 4.3?"), I suggest attending instructor office hours for more personalized support, allowing for follow-up questions and more comprehensive explanations. 

Piazza posting guidelines:

  • You can post anonymously to classmates by selecting "Show up as Anonymous," but instructors and TAs will see your name.
  • Use a clear subject line (e.g., "Problem Set 1 Q3(b)").
  • Indicate what you've tried and where you're stuck (e.g., "I thought the solution should be ... but my answer differs from the provided solution. What went wrong?").
  • If the TA’s response doesn’t fully address your question, reply to their post and explain what you’re still confused about—don’t hesitate to ask follow-up questions. If you remain unclear after the back-and-forth exchange, attend instructor office hours for face-to-face support, which can often provide more nuanced explanations.
  • We encourage you to respond to classmates' questions. Teaching others is one of the best ways to learn and helps you build written communication skills valued by employers.

2. Instructor office hours

We encourage you to join our weekly office hours. You can ask detailed questions about lecture content, seek help with specific problems, and discuss learning strategies.

If the first midterm doesn’t go as planned, don’t worry—office hours can be valuable for refining your learning strategies to improve in the rest of the course.

3. Instructor email for personal matters

For questions about personal circumstances (e.g., accommodations, scheduling conflicts) or to provide course feedback, please email your instructor directly.

4. Other resources

We highly recommend Dan Wolczuk's How to Learn Series, along with resources from the Student Success Office on time management, study skills, and stress management.

If you are managing personal or academic challenges, AccessAbility Services and academic advisors are available to help with accommodations and course planning.

Course Description

ACTSC 231:

The theory of rates of interest and discount; annuities and sinking funds with practical applications to mortgage and bond questions. Yield rates.

View requirements for ACTSC 231

This course provides foundational tools for understanding key financial concepts, such as interest rates, annuities, loans, and bonds. The principles introduced are essential for performing calculations related to everyday financial matters, like determining mortgage payments or valuing investments, and they play a key role in sectors like banking, insurance, and real estate.

By the end of the course, you will have a strong grasp of the mathematical techniques used to approach real-world financial problems, laying a foundation for further study and practical applications in finance and actuarial science.

To fulfill the ACTSC Honours requirements, you need at least 60% in ACTSC 231

You can find the Actuarial Science program and plan requirements in the Undergraduate Academic Calendar. To connect with an academic advisor, visit the department’s advising webpage.

This course meets several requirements for SOA, CAS, and CIA designations

It covers most SOA FM Exam learning objectives, except interest rate swaps and determinants of interest rates. It is accredited under the CIA accreditation program (more details can be found on the CIA actuarial education webpage and the UW actuarial accreditation and exemptions webpage).

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
[Time value of money]
Define key terms such as interest rates, present value, and future value.
Given partial information, calculate remaining quantities such as interest rate, present value, and future value, using simple or compound interest.
Solve equations of value involving variable force of interest.
Write the equation of value given a set of cash flows.
[Annuities (non-contingent)]
Define terms related to annuities, such as annuity-immediate and perpetuity.
Calculate present value, future value, and payment amounts for various annuity types.
[Loans]
Define key terms like principal, amortization, and outstanding balance.
Given partial information, calculate the remaining quantities such as interest rate, payment amount, or payment period.
[Bonds]
Define terms such as price, yield rate, and coupon.
Given partial information, calculate remaining values such as price, redemption value, or yield rate.
[General cash flows and portfolios]
Define terms such as dollar/time-weighted rate of return, spot rate, and forward rate.
Define and calculate yield rates, duration, and convexity.
Use duration to approximate changes in present value due to interest rate shifts.
[Immunization]
Define cash flow matching and immunization.
Construct investment portfolios that match liability cash flows in terms of present value and duration.
Construct an investment portfolio to immunize a set of liability cash flows.

Two-stage quiz format

Quizzes follow a two-stage format:

  1. Individual Stage: You will have 25 minutes to independently complete the quiz, which consists of two questions. This stage is closed-book.
  2. Group Stage: After submitting your individual work, you will form groups of 3–4 students. Together, you will collaborate on a shorter quiz that consists only of the second question. You will have 20 minutes for this stage, which is also closed-book.

You must complete both stages. Your overall quiz grade will be the higher of the following two options:

  1. Your individual quiz grade, or
  2. Combined grade calculated as: [Question 1 marks (individual) + 80% x Question 2 marks (individual) + 20% x Question 2 marks (group)] / [Question 1 possible marks + Question 2 possible marks].

Why this format?

  • Exam Practice: The individual stage simulates the pressure of closed-book individual exams, preparing you for midterms and the final exam.
  • Immediate Feedback: The group stage allows you to quickly identify and correct mistakes while discussing solutions with your peers.
  • Collaborative Learning: Group work enhances understanding as you learn from and explain concepts to your teammates.

Strategies for success

Attend all lectures: Regular attendance helps you grasp key concepts and stay on track with the material.

Complete practice problems: Work through the assigned textbook problems (posted on LEARN) multiple times. Independent practice is essential for developing and self-assessing your understanding. Compare your solutions critically with the provided answers to identify gaps in knowledge. I highly recommend watching Dan Wolczuk's video on effective practice.

Solve problems promptly: As new concepts are introduced, solve related textbook problems to reinforce your understanding while the material is fresh.

Ask questions early: Don’t wait if something is unclear. Ask questions during or after lectures, on Piazza, or during instructor/TA office hours to get timely clarification.

Assessments vary in difficulty based on their purpose.

Quizzes: Designed as learning tools to help you gauge your understanding of lecture material and stay current with content. 

Midterms: Serve as both learning and diagnostic tools. They assess whether you have a weak, satisfactory, or strong grasp of course content. Midterms include a few challenging questions to test your ability to apply concepts to more complex, unfamiliar scenarios, making them more difficult than quizzes. Reviewing your graded midterms offers a valuable learning opportunity.

Final Exam: Primarily diagnostic, slightly more challenging than midterms, and designed to evaluate your overall understanding of the course material.

You must use a nonprogrammable calculator. 

A financial calculator is not required, and all questions are designed so that full marks can be achieved with a standard non-financial calculator.

Assessment coverage will be announced one week before each assessment.

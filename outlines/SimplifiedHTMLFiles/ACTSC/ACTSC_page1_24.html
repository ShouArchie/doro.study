Course Description

ACTSC 621:

Mean-Variance portfolio theory; utility based portfolio theory; Capital-Asset Pricing Method, Arbitrage Pricing Theory, Efficient Markets Hypotheses; Capital structure and dividend policy; introduction to options with applications in corporate finance. Behavioural finance.

View requirements for ACTSC 621

This course provides a comprehensive foundation in financial mathematics, focusing on the risk-reward tradeoff in financial markets and practical problem-solving techniques. Students will learn to formulate and solve optimization problems, including mean-variance portfolio optimization, and explore key concepts such as the capital asset pricing model (CAPM), arbitrage pricing theory (APT), and market efficiency.

The course also covers corporate finance topics, including long-term financing, optimal capital structures, and the use of debt. Students will develop a strong understanding of expected utility theory, its axioms, utility functions, and applications in pricing insurance products. Behavioral finance topics, including cognitive biases in decision-making, are also explored.

By the end of the course, students will be prepared to analyze financial problems and apply advanced quantitative techniques in theory and practice.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand the tradeoff between risk and reward in financial markets.
Formulate optimization problems with clear objectives and constraints.
Solve optimization problems using the method of Lagrangian multipliers.
Develop and solve mean-variance portfolio optimization models.
Understand the derivation and intuition behind capital asset pricing model (CAPM).
Compare and contrast CAPM and arbitrage pricing theory (APT).
Explain the three forms of market efficiency: weak, semi-strong, and strong.
Identify and evaluate sources of long-term financing and their characteristics.
Analyze optimal capital structures and the limitations of debt financing.
Explain the axioms of cardinal utility and provide justifications as well as counter examples for each axiom.
Explain the axioms of cardinal utility and provide examples and counterexamples.
Demonstrate applications of expected utility theory in decision-making and insurance pricing.
Understand the properties of commonly used utility functions.
Identify cognitive biases and their effects on financial decisions in behavioral finance.

    • Date, time, and location to be determined.
  • Group Projects: The group project is a core component of this course, designed to reflect the collaborative and problem-solving nature of actuarial work. Many real-world projects involve building on existing research to address practical challenges. To succeed, your group must thoroughly understand the following three aspects of the research you reference:
    • The Problem: What issue is being addressed?
    • The Methodology: How was the problem approached?
    • The Results: What outcomes were achieved?
  • Group project key milestones: These milestones are designed to guide you through the project while developing critical skills in communication, analysis, and teamwork.
    • Week 1:
      • Form groups of 3–4 students.
      • Each group selects a topic provided by the instructor.
    • Week 2:
      • Deliver a 5-minute presentation outlining the problem your project addresses.
      • Note: This presentation is delivered by one group member.
    • Week 5:
      • Deliver a 5-minute presentation explaining the methodology from the research papers you reviewed and outlining your project plan (e.g., reproducing specific numerical results).
      • Note: This presentation is delivered by one group member.
    • Week 9:
      • Deliver a 10-minute presentation showcasing partial results, comparing them to the plan set in Week 5, and explaining any adjustments made to the original plan.
      • Note: This presentation can be delivered by up to 2 group members.
    • Week 12:
      • Deliver a 20-minute presentation summarizing the entire project.
      • Submit a 10-page report one week after the final presentation.
      • Note: This presentation is delivered by all group members.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will be discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

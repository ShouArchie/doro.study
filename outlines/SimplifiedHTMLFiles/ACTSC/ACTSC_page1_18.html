Course Description

ACTSC 454 / ACTSC 854:

Kaplan-Meier and Nelson-Aalen estimators for survival functions. Kernel density models. Validation of mortality tables. Estimators for Markov multiple state transition intensities. Longevity models, including deterministic and stochastic models such as Lee-Carter and Cairns-Blake-Dowd.

ACTSC 454: View requirements for ACTSC 454

ACTSC 854: View requirements for ACTSC 854

The objective of this course is to introduce statistical models for survival data. Building upon the concepts of ACTSC 232 and 331, we discuss estimation with both empirical and parametric models. Discussion of mortality improvement follows, including the introduction of stochastic longevity models.

ACTSC 854 students learn the same material as 454 students, but with extra or enhanced assessments.

Lectures are given on the board for you to copy down, and I encourage an interactive class atmosphere. Please do not hesitate to ask questions so that we can all learn together and make learning as active (and enjoyable) as possible.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Construct empirical estimates (using Kaplan-Meier, Nelson-Aalen, kernel density models) for survival data, including censored and truncated data
Apply the Cox Proportional Hazards model to survival data
Estimate mortality with Binomial and Poisson models for mortality
Construct and validate mortality tables using different methods of graduation
Discuss longevity risk and model mortality improvement with both determinstic and stochastic models

Administrative Policy

Remark Requests 

You may appeal the way an assessment was marked by submitting a request within 1 week of it being returned. Requests must be submitted in writing by email.

Illness / Short Term Absences Policy

If you are ill, you must submit a VIF indicating severe illness. (If you are self-isolating due to flu-like or COVID-19 symptoms, you can instead complete the self-declaration form in Quest). Details can be found here: https://uwaterloo.ca/math/absence-declarations 

  • If you are ill for the midterm, you must provide satisfactory documentation within 48 hours by emailing the instructor and submitting a copy of the VIF to the MUO. For satisfactory documentation, the midterm weight will be shifted to the final exam.
  • In almost all cases, unless there are significant extenuating circumstances, there will be no shifting of weight for the assignments. This is because the assignments will be available for an approximately two-week period prior to the deadline, and they are submitted online. If you have significant extenuating circumstances, please email me to discuss appropriate accommodations to the late policy.
  • If you are ill for the final exam, you will receive an INC grade and be required to complete the exam during the next course offering, if you are achieving a passing grade going into the exam. Further, you must have written the midterm to qualify for an INC if you miss the final exam. If not, a grade of DNW will be assigned.

The above accommodations also apply to self-declared short-term absences.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean.

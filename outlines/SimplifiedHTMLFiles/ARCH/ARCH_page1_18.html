Course Description

ARCH 684:

This course allows for additions in the program on a short term basis, and for the development of future permanent courses.

View requirements for ARCH 684

This seminar uses the close reading approach to study selected texts by contemporary philosophers. The selection of philosophers is based on their reference to architectural and urban themes, as illustrated by this quote by philologist Friedrich Kittler:

TECHNOLOGY. What strikes the eye of the passerby as a growth or entropy is technology, that is, information. Since cities no longer lie within the panopticon of the cathedral or castle and can no longer be enclosed by walls or fortifications, a network made up of intersecting networks dissects and connects the city–in particular its fringes, peripheries, and tangents. Regardless of whether these networks transmit information (telephone, radio, television) or energy (water supply, electricity, highway), they all represent forms of information. Friedrich Kittler, The City as a Medium 

What is meant by close reading? The seminar will use brief chapters and shorter texts to gain access to the approach. Rather than reading vast quantities of material, the class will look at fragments and discuss the content. This will give students an opportunity to pursue reading in greater depth during thesis.

Texts will be selected by students during the first two weekes in seminars. The second seminar will present several of the philosophers’ work available on UW Learn and as streamed video by UW Kanopy, Tate Modern, monoskop, ubu.com, etc. for example. The order of the seminars will be determined as a function of student selection of texts. Currently most of the selections are available as digital copies. It may be possible to add additional texts.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Learning objectives 1. be familiar with terminology, coinage of philosophical terms and their usage from formal definitions (such as ontology, etc.) to Friedrich Kittler's technological determinist slogan, 'we are subject to our gadgets'.
Learning objectives 2. construct coherent frameworks for argumentation and use reason to distinguish true from false.
Learning objectives 3. become accustomed to participation in and regular contribution to group seminars and lead seminars.
Learning objectives 4. recognize the purpose and role of philosophical concepts as defined by Gilles Deleuze and distinguish types of philosophical concepts with a variety of graphic and visualization schemes, diagramming and graphs.
Learning objectives 5. This seminar gives the students access to a broad range of discourses in philosophical texts and prepares for the concepts in philosophy that contribute theoretical foundations for research and design.

Student Assessment

Component Value
The student will present two seminars: seminar 1 (before reading week): presentation with visual documentation 20%
seminar 2: (after reading week) presentation with visual documentation 20%
class participation: attend and contribute to seminar discussions 10%
End of Term Submission: contemporary ‘media philosophy’ relies on visuals: sets of diagrams, graphs, and schemas in their final project submission.. 50%

Texts will be selected by students during the first seminar. The first seminar mst be presented before reading week, and the second seminar after, referencing several of the philosophers’ work available as text and streamed video on UW Learn and Kanopy, Tate Modern, monoskop, ubu.com, etc. for example. The order of the seminars will be determined as a function of student selection of texts. Currently most of the selections are available as digital copies. It may be possible to add additional texts. Examples of visualization, diagramming such as the Greimas Square, etc. will be presented in class. 

End of Term Submission: Because contemporary ‘media philosophy’ relies on visuals, students may choose to create a set of visualizations, including diagrams, graphs, and schemas in their final project. The final submission may include a concise selection of such images accompanying a series of short essays, preferably an edited set of 5 graphic diagrams along with approximately 1000 words of short texts with titles –a sample format might be 5 diagrams captioned with 200 word paragraphs. Usually students work individually however it may be possible to arrange to work in teams of two.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

It is not recommended that AI be used in the course due to the possibility of so-called 'hallucination' (nonsensical and unreliable outputs).

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online.

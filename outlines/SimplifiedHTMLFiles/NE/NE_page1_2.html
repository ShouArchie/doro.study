Course Description

NE 110:

Nanomaterial impacts on worker, consumer, and environmental health. Engineered nanomaterials and ultrafines. Chemical risk assessment. Nanomaterial exposure characterization. Introductory nanotoxicology. Environmental chemical impacts, transport, and bio-accumulation. Introduction to UN Sustainable Development Goals. Human nanomaterial health risks and benefits. Introductory epidemiology, including study design, strength of association, bias, confounding, and causal inference.

View requirements for NE 110

This course is designed to help students better understand the negative impacts of exposure chemicals or nanomaterials. This course covers the fundamentals of toxicology, exposure assessment, epidemiology and risk assessment. Students will develop a general understanding of how to predict how chemicals travel in the environment, and what health impacts could be expected for animals and humans. Students will better understand how to interpret results from human observational studies suggesting the association of a chemical with a particular health effect. Students will assemble these ideas and examine the role of nanotechnology in sustainable development. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
- Understand the characterization of nanomaterials needed for exposure assessment
- Understand the fundamentals of toxicology and epidemiological study design
- Understand what governs the distribution of chemicals in the environment and the risks of bioaccumulation
- Understand chemical risk assessment and the process needed to evaluate worker and public safety
- Be able to locate, cite and summarize peer-reviewed publications
- Be able to write scientific content for a specialized or general audience and provide constructive feedback
- Be able to describe the role of nanotechnology engineering in sustainable development

This course is designed to deliver content in alignment with the following CEAB accreditation graduate attributes requirements:

Impact of engineering on society and the environment: An ability to analyze social and environmental aspects of engineering activities. Such ability includes an understanding of the interactions that engineering has with the economic, social, health, safety, legal, and cultural aspects of society, the uncertainties in the prediction of such interactions; and the concepts of sustainable design and development and environmental stewardship.

Professionalism: An understanding of the roles and responsibilities of the professional engineer in society, especially the primary role of protection of the public and the public interest.

Communication skills: An ability to communicate complex engineering concepts within the profession and with society at large. Such ability includes reading, writing, speaking and listening, and the ability to comprehend and write effective reports and design documentation, and to give and effectively respond to clear instructions.

Know how to reference your sources - only use in-text citations: 

How do you place an intext citation into the text of your assignment?

NOTE: If you are paraphrasing an idea from another work, you only have to make reference to the author and year of publication in your in-text reference, and then add the full information in your References list at the end of your paper.

Example: in your text where you are discussing citations, you might state: 

According to Deakin et al. (2022), APA style is an easy citation format for first-time learners. 

Or you could write this as:

APA style is an easy citation format for first-time learners. (Deakin et al., 2022)

(et al. means there are other authors listed in the full reference)

What do you put in a References section?

In your References (in alphabetical order) you would list all of the references used in the document. 

You can consult an APA guide or follow this format:

Author’s last name, Author’s first initial. Alphabetical list of all authors. (Year of publication). Title of work. Publisher or website accessed (date of access). 

References example (you would listed all in alphabetical order):

Deakin, L.; Other, N. (2022) Learning to Cite. www.learningtocite.ca (Accessed Jan 1, 2023)

Do you include other general sources that are not specifically linked to a citation in the text?

NO.

Do you have questions? ASK before submitting!

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

MATH 235:

Orthogonal and unitary matrices and transformations. Orthogonal projections, Gram-Schmidt procedure, best approximations, least-squares. Inner products, angles and orthogonality, orthogonal diagonalization, singular value decomposition, applications.

View requirements for MATH 235

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand the definition of an abstract vector space and prove theorems about all vector spaces using the axioms.
In the context of abstract vector spaces, understand and use the concepts of linear independence, spanning sets, bases, and dimension.
Understand the definition of a linear transformation, and be able to prove whether or not a function is a linear transformation.
Prove and apply the rank-nullity theorem.
Understand and use the relationship between abstract linear transformations and matrices.
Understand the definition of an isomorphism, and prove whether or not a linear transformation is an isomorphism.
Understand and use the definitions of eigenvalues, eigenvectors and eigenspaces.
Prove whether or not a particular linear transformation is diagonalizable, and if it is, be able to diagonalize it.
Understand the axioms of an inner product, and use them to prove basic results about inner product spaces.
Understand and apply the definitions of orthogonality and orthonormal bases.
Perform the gram-schmidt orthogonalization procedure.
Use the spectral theorems to decide whether or not a particular linear operator is unitarily or orthogonally diagonalizable.
Unitarily diagonalize a normal matrix.
Compute the singular value decomposition of a matrix.

Tentative Course Schedule

See schedule on Learn for a week-by-week breakdown of topics. We will proceed through most sections of the Math 235 Course Notes as follows:

  1. Abstract Vector Spaces
    • The definition of a vector space
    • Subspaces
    • Bases and dimension
       
  2. Linear Transformations
    • Linear transformations between abstract vector spaces
    • Rank and nullity
    • Linear maps as matrices
    • Change of coordinates
    • Isomorphisms of vector spaces
       
  3. Diagonalizability
    • Eigenvectors
    • Diagonalization
    • Powers of matrices
       
  4. Inner product spaces
    • Inner products
    • Orthogonality and norm
    • Orthonormal bases
    • Projections
    • The Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization procedure
    • Projection onto a subspace and orthogonal complements
       
  5. Orthogonal and unitary diagonalization
    • Orthogonal and unitary matrices
    • Schur's Triangularization Theorem
    • Orthogonal and unitary diagonalization of matrices
    • Inner products and Gram matrices
    • Unitary diagonalization of operators
       
  6. Singular value decomposition
    • Singular values and singular vectors
    • Singular value decomposition of matrices
    • Singular value decomposition of linear maps
    • Low-rank approximation (compact SVD, rank-k truncation, and Eckart-Young Theorem)

Mobius and Written Assignments

Each week you will receive one Mobius assignment on Wednesday mornings. These will be due on Tuesdays at 5pm, beginning on January 14. So there will be 12 Mobius Assignments in total. The lowest grade from your Mobius Assignments will be dropped.

There will be 5 Written Assignments due on Tuesdays at 5pm of week 3, 5, 7, 9, and 11. 

Mobius Assignments: You may attempt each Mobius assignment as many times as you like before the deadline and your highest grade will count. These are accessible from the Mobius Assignments folder on Learn. All Mobius Assignments are available at the start of the term, and practice versions of assignments that are past due will be made available.

Written Assignments: These will be submitted on Crowdmark, and should consist of 5-6 questions per assignment. These will help you practice your ability to write proofs and check your understanding of the week's concepts. You may complete these in groups of up to 3 students. You do not need to be in a group, and you do not need to be in the same group for each assignment.  I will post a note to Piazza to help students form groups.

Remember: the assignments are here to help your learning, and are even more crucial in an asynchronous environment! You should be striving each week to understand the material on the assignments and the posted solutions. You won't be in a group setting on the final exam, and it is worth most of your grade, so you will need to understand the material yourself in order to pass the course. This is not a course you can "cram" and survive - you must build the intuition for the material gradually throughout the term.

Final Exam

The Final Exam will be scheduled by the Registrars Office. Information will be provided on LEARN regarding locations for the exam. This will be a closed book exam, with no calculators permitted. It will be a cumulative exam, covering topics from the entire course.

Passing the Course / Grade Calculation

In order to pass the course, you must receive a grade of at least 45/100 on the Final Exam. Otherwise, the highest your final grade will be is 45.

If you pass the Final Exam, your minimum possible course grade will be 50%.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as writings proofs and communicating mathematical ideas. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

Students are encouraged to reach out to campus supports if they need help with their coursework including: 

  • Student Success Office for help with skills like notetaking and time management
  • Writing and Communication Centre for assignments with writing or presentations
  • AccessAbility Services for documented accommodations
  • Library for research-based assignments

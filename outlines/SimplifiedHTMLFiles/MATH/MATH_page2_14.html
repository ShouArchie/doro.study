Course Description

MATH 648:

This course explores the foundations of integral calculus and the use of series in approximating the basic functions of mathematics. Topics include: Understanding the Riemann Integral and its flaws, the idea of Lebesque, the geometric meaning of the Riemann-Stieltjes integral, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, numerical integration, numerical series, uniform convergence of functions and the extraordinary nature of power series, Fourier Series.

View requirements for MATH 648

This course will focus on the foundations of Integral Calculus and on the fundamental properties of both numerical series and power series.

The course begins with a brief review of the basics of the Riemann Integral. We then move on to give the formal definition of the integral and show that if a function f is continuous on [a, b] or if it is monotonic on [a, b], then the integral always exists. We then state and prove the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. This is one of the most celebrated results in all of mathematics. It provides the fundamental link between differential and integral calculus, hence its name.

The second chapter introduces Improper Integrals which will will need later in our study of series. This is followed by a short chapter on what should be familiar applications of integration. 

Chapter 4 introduces the basics of differential equations. This may be new to many of you. 

As a precursor to our in-depth look at  power series, in Chapter 5 we study in detail the properties of numerical series. Here the emphasis is on positive series. In addition to various integration a tests for determining convergence, we will look at some simple but effective techniques for analyzing series numerically.

Finally, in Chapter 6, we study the rather remarkable nature of power series and the functions they represent. Along the way we introduce the concept of uniform convergence and illustrate why this is preferable to the much simpler pointwise convergence. The course culminates with Taylor series, a topic which brings us back to where we finished off in MATH 647.

Throughout the course, I encourage you to use MAPLE or any other available software both a device for exploration and as a tool for numerical calculations.

Learning Outcomes

This course will enable you to improve your knowledge and skills in four ways:
  1. Content
    • Gain an appreciation for the role of rigour in shaping modern calculus
    • Gain a new perspective on some of the fundamental aspects of differential calculus
    • Understand the role of approximation in Calculus and in mathematics in general
  2. Interpretive and analytical skills
    • Read and write mathematical proofs
    • Read mathematics critically
    • Write mathematics formally and correctly
    • Communicate clearly
  3. Professional mathematical skills
    • Gain experience with sophisticated mathematical software including MAPLE and LaTeX
  4. Professional pedagogical development
    • Formulate core and enrichment material for the classroom

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo.  Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student’s educational experience.  However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights.  For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.  In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials.  However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online.  The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

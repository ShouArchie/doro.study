Course Description

MATH 103:

An introduction to applications of algebra to business, the behavioural sciences, and the social sciences. Topics will be chosen from linear equations, systems of linear equations, linear inequalities, functions, set theory, permutations and combinations, binomial theorem, probability theory.

View requirements for MATH 103

The course begins with a review of operations with real numbers and fundamental algebra skills. The solutions of polynomial equations, both linear and quadratic, in one and two variables and their graphical representation are studied. An extension is made to linear systems with their solutions being expressed both algebraically and graphically. To solve linear systems, matrices and the associated row reduction strategies are used. The interpretation of an equation or system of equations in various applications will be studied.

The relationship between exponential functions and logarithmic functions is examined. The basic properties of logarithms are introduced and used to solve logarithmic and exponential equations. Various applications involving exponential growth and decay and logarithmic scales are presented.

Probability theory is the primary focus of the final three units. Concepts in set theory and counting theory support the study of probability theory.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Perform operations with real numbers and algebraic expressions.
Solve linear equations and inequalities in one variable, including those involving absolute value, and represent the solutions algebraically and graphically.
Solve a system of linear equations using matrix operations, introducing a free variable, or parameter as appropriate.
Solve quadratic equations in one variable.
Recognize that the solutions to a quadratic equation in two variables lie on a curve known as a parabola and analyze the properties of a parabola.
Solve problems that can be modelled by quadratic functions, including determining the function’s maximum or minimum value.
Describe the relationship between an exponential function and a logarithmic function.
Use the properties of logarithms to solve logarithmic and exponential equations, and to solve problems involving exponential growth and decay, and logarithmic scales.
Use the appropriate symbols and language in set theory to perform set operations, represent sets and subsets using Venn diagrams, and apply the counting principles for sets in real-life situations.
Calculate the theoretical probability for single or compound events involving simple experiments.
Calculate conditional probabilities, probability distributions, and expected values.
Use the Fundamental Principle of Counting, and the formulae for permutations and combinations to solve problems, and apply the skills to probability calculations.
Recognize experiments that are binomial experiments and apply the binomial probability distribution to those experiments.

Student Assessment

Component Value
Möbius Assignments (Best 7 of 8) 14%
Written Assignments (Best 8 of 9) 20%
Unit Tests (3 tests worth 7% each) 21%
Final Examination 45%

Möbius Assignments are designed to test your initial understanding of the course content. It is recommended you complete them as soon as possible after completing the associated lessons.  No late submissions of Möbius Assignments will be accepted.

Written Assignments provide a valuable opportunity for feedback on your written work. Consistent, appropriate work on assignments is highly correlated with success in the course.  You are able to ask questions of your instructor and discuss the questions with other students in the course, but the solutions that you submit must be your own work and so should be written up without any consultation with your colleagues and should include the names of any students you discussed problems with.  Written assignments received after the date and time listed in the Course Schedule in LEARN will be deducted 1% per minute that the assignment is late.  

Unit Tests are to be solved independently.  You are not permitted to discuss the questions with others.  You are welcome to use any of the course materials (lecture notes, course notes, past assignments) as needed; however, it is recommended that you treat these unit tests as preparation for the final examination, and attempt to complete them with only the aids that are allowed for the final examination.   You will be given a time-limit of 3 hours to complete and submit your work. Unit Tests received after the date and time listed in the Course Schedule in LEARN or after the 3 hour time-limit will be deducted 1% per minute that the assessment is late.

The Final Examination will be scheduled by the Registrar's Office, and the details will be provided in the published Final examination schedule when available.  The Final Examination is closed-book and will cover material from the entire course.  The only permitted aids for the final exam are: 

  • A double-sided "cheat sheet". The maximum paper size is 8.5" x 11" (A4). All content on your "cheat sheet" must be hand-written. At the end of the exam, your "cheat sheet" will be submitted to the proctors along with your final examination paper.
  • A non-programmable, non-graphing scientific calculator. Please refer to the Calculator Regulation page for recommended models available for purchase at the W Store or MathSoc (MC 3038).

Accommodations for missed assessments require proper documentation.  Please review the Accommodation for illness or extenuating circumstances page, which maintains current and clear information for students about the process.  If the assessment is a Written Assignment or Möbius Assignment and the absence is less than 48 hours, then a 48-hour extension will be granted.  For longer absences or for Unit Tests, the weight of the missed assessment will be shifted to the Final Exam.  In all cases, you must email your instructor to receive an accommodation.

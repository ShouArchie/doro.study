Instructor & TA (Teaching Assistant) Information

 

Course Description

MATH 235:

Orthogonal and unitary matrices and transformations. Orthogonal projections, Gram-Schmidt procedure, best approximations, least-squares. Inner products, angles and orthogonality, orthogonal diagonalization, singular value decomposition, applications.

View requirements for MATH 235

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand the definition of a vector space over the real or complex numbers and determine whether an object is a vector space.
Solve problems abstract vector spaces using coordinates to convert to a problem about matrices and vectors in R^n or C^n.
Check whether a function between two vector spaces is a linear transformation.
Compute the range, kernel, rank, and nullity of linear transformations between finite-dimensional vector spaces
Compute the matrix of a linear transformation given bases for the domain and codomain.
Understand the definition of an inner product and check whether a function is an inner product.
Compute an orthogonal basis of an inner product space given an arbitrary basis.
Orthogonally or unitarily diagonalize matrices and recognize when this can and cannot be done.
Compute the singular value decomposition of a matrix.

Student Assessment

Component Value
Weekly Quizzes 10%
Bi-weekly Assignments 20%
Midterm Exam 20%
Final Exam 50%

Quizzes: There will be weekly quizzes every Tuesday starting on January 14 and ending on April 1, except for February 11 and February 18. They will take place in the tutorial slot in RCH 101. The quizzes will consist of 5 True/False questions and you will have 25 minutes to complete them. The quiz itself will run from 9:35 a.m. to 10:00 a.m. with the remaining 20-or-so minutes used to discuss the answers to the questions. If all goes well, your grades will be returned to you later that morning.

Assignments: There will be 5 written assignments throughout the semester, due roughly every two weeks. All assignments are due on Fridays at 8:00 p.m. (note that EST changes to EDT the day before Assignment 4 is due). The assignments will involve both computation and proof writing. While you are allowed to discuss problems with your peers and ask for help from your instructor, submitted work must be your own.

Exams: The midterm exam is on February 11 from 4:30-6:20. The final exam will be scheduled by the Registrar's office about a month into the term. Both exams will consist of a mixture of True/False questions, computational questions, and proof questions.

Computation of your final grade: In order to pass the course, you must pass the exam portion of the course. More precisely, if 0.2M+0.5F is less than 35 (where M and F are your midterm and final percentage grades, respectively), then your final grade will be computed as (10/7)(0.2M+0.5F).

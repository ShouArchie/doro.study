Instructor & TA (Teaching Assistant) Information

 

Course Description

MATH 213:

Laplace transform methods for: solving linear ordinary differential equations, classical signals, and systems. Transfer functions, poles, and zeros; system stability. Frequency response of linear systems and its log-scale representation (Bode plot). Fourier series. Applications in areas of interest for software engineers and computer scientists. Brief introduction to Fourier transforms in the context of signals and systems.

View requirements for MATH 213

Course pre-requisites:

  1. You should have a good working knowledge of differentiation, integration, and complex numbers.  (A review of these topics will be provided.)
  2. Experience with Matlab or equivalent computational software is beneficial.

Course objective:  The main objective of the course is to develop a conceptual and mathematical framework that allows us to model, analyze, and design dynamical systems.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
(1) Explain what is meant by a signal, a system, and a differential equation (Chapter 1)
(2) Classify a given system as static or dynamical, causal or noncausal, linear or nonlinear, and time-invariant or time-varying (Chapter 1)
(3) Explain what is meant by the solution to an ordinary differential equation and an initial value problem (Chapter 2)
(4) Solve first-order ordinary differential equations using the phase-portrait approach, the separation of variables approach, and the exact differential approach (Chapter 2)
(5) Define and work proficiently with the Laplace transform (Chapter 3)
(6) Apply Laplace transform methods to solve a constant-coefficient linear ODE and explain the structure of the general solution (Chapter 3)
(7) Compute the transfer function and evaluate stability of a LTI system (Chapter 4)
(8) Estimate time-domain characteristics from a system's poles and zeros (Chapter 4)
(9) Manipulate block diagrams and explain how feedback is used to achieve tracking in a control system (Chapter 4)
(10) Define and construct the Fourier series or Fourier integral to show how a signal can be decomposed into a sum or integral of sinusoids (Chapter 5)
(11) Define, construct, and interpret the frequency response (Bode plot) of a LTI system (Chapter 5)

Tests and final exam

  • Dates:  There are three 45-minute tests, held during tutorial slots as indicated in the Course Schedule above.  The 2.5-hour final exam will be centrally scheduled by the Registrar's Office.
  • Assigned seating is used, with seating details available at https://odyssey.uwaterloo.ca/teaching/schedule about a week before each test or final exam.
  • Permitted aids:  For each of the tests, you may use a non-programmable calculator.  For the final exam, you may use a non-programmable calculator and you may prepare and use one double-sided handwritten aid sheet (on normal 8.5 x 11 inch paper).  Full test and exam rules will be provided no later than one week before the test or exam dates.
  • Rule about collaboration:  The tests and the final exam must be done individually.
  • Weightings of the tests:  For each student, assuming all three tests are written, the lowest-score test will be dropped and each of the other two is worth 15%.
  • Missed tests:  No make-up or alternatively-scheduled tests are being offered in this course.  The only reasons deemed valid for missing a test are severe illness (either self-declared covid-related or otherwise verified by a Verification of Illness form), or a self-declared short-term absence on Quest (one of the two days must overlap the test date), or other extreme circumstances approved by the course instructor.  A test that is missed without a valid reason counts as if the test was written with an earned grade of zero.  If one of the three tests is missed for a valid reason, the remaining two tests are each worth 15% of the final course grade.  If two tests are missed for valid reasons, the remaining test is worth 15% of the final course grade, and the weight of the final exam is increased to 60%. If all three tests are missed for valid reasons, the weight of the final exam is increased to 75%.

Assignments

  • General information:  There are six assignments.  Each assignment involves completing and submitting exercises selected from those included at the end of each chapter in the course notes.  The instructor may also create additional exercises per assignment.
  • Format of submissions:  Solutions should be neat, well organized, and well labelled.  They may be hand written or typed.  Only pdf files are accepted.
  • Submission location and date:  Submit in the relevant LEARN dropbox by 11:59pm (Waterloo time).  Deadlines are included in the Course Schedule table above.
  • Late submission policy:  Missing the deadline by up to 1 hour results in a grade reduction of 25% of the possible marks, while missing it by up to 24 hours results in a grade reduction of 50% of the possible marks, and beyond that the grade reduction is 100%.  Late penalties may be waived at the course instructor's discretion, but only in cases of severe illness (either self-declared covid related or otherwise verified by a Verification of Illness form), or if a student submits a self-declared short-term absence on Quest (one of the two days must overlap the deadline), or in other extreme extenuating circumstances approved by the course instructor.  In no case will an assignment submission be accepted more than three days after the due date.
  • Assignment grading:   Grading will factor in completeness, neatness, and organization.  Due to limited TA resources, only a small subset of exercises will be graded in detail for correctness.
  • Weights of assignments:  The lowest-scored assignment will be dropped for each student.  The remaining five assignments are equally weighted (i.e., each of those five is worth 5% of the final course grade).
  • Allowable collaboration/aids:
    • You may collaborate with other students in the current MATH213 class, but you must individually write up and submit your own solutions.
    • For the most part, calculations should be done manually with the help of a basic calculator.  However, you may use Matlab (or equivalent computational software) where indicated in the exercise questions or to double-check answers or to do routine calculations such as factoring polynomials.
    • You may not post assignment questions or answers on any social media or website.
    • You may not use “homework services” such as chegg.com or coursehero.com.
    • You may not use AI/ML software such as ChatGPT to help with the assignments.

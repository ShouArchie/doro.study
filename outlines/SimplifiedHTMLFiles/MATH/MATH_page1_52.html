Course Description

MATH 227:

Directional derivative and the chain rule for multivariable functions. Optimization, Lagrange multipliers. Double and triple integrals on simple domains; transformations and Jacobians; change of variable in multiple integrals. Vector fields, divergence and curl. Vector integral calculus: Line and surface integrals, Green's Theorem, Stokes' Theorem, Gauss' Theorem, conservative vector fields.

View requirements for MATH 227

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe and analyze arbitrary curves, surfaces, and volumes in three-dimensional space.
Set up and perform integrals over lines, surfaces, and volumes.
Work in and convert between Cartesian and polar coordinates in two-dimensions or Cartesian, cylindrical, and spherical coordinates in three-dimensions.
Compute and interpret the meaning of partial derivatives, directional derivatives, gradients, divergences, and curls.
Plot and describe the behaviour of a vector field.
Determine using a variety of tests if a vector field is conservative.
Understand the fundamental differences between conservative and non-conservative vector fields.
Apply Green's theorem, the Divergence theorem, and Stoke's theorem to evaluate integrals.
Determine the extreme values of a function of multiple variables.
Recognize the importance of vector calculus for physics.

Tentative Course Schedule

This course covers a selection of topics from multivariable vector calculus. In particular, we will aim to cover one item per week from the following list:

  1. A review of vectors and multivariable functions.
  2. Geometry and particle motion in three dimensions.
  3. Arc length and line integrals.
  4. Quadric surfaces and coordinate systems.
  5. Double and triple integrals.
  6. Partial derivatives and linearizations in three dimensions.
  7. Chain rule, directional derivatives, and gradients.
  8. Vectors ﬁelds, divergence, and curl.
  9. Path independence and Green’s theorem.
  10. Surface integrals.
  11. Divergence theorem and Stoke’s theorem.
  12. Extrema of multivariable functions and Lagrange multipliers.

These topics will be discussed in the context of real‐world applications in electromagnetism, gravity, and other areas of physics. A previous course in linear algebra is strongly recommended.

There is no required textbook. You will have access to the course content in Mobius as well as a PDF of the course notes posted to LEARN. I will also share short videos each week in which I work through examples and/or discuss particularly important concepts. I will also post problem sets with full solutions each week so that you have plenty of practice problems to work through. You will be expected to work through this material on your own to prepare for course assessments.

A textbook is not required but you may ﬁnd it helpful to have one on hand for extra practice. I recommend "Essential Calculus", 2nd Edition by James Stewart which was prepared specifically for our course in a previous term. However, this textbook is difficult to come by now, so if you cannot find a copy of this book, then James Stewart’s full textbook ("Calculus: Early Transcendentals") on calculus would also be a great resource since it is a superset of the recommended text.

Administrative Policy

Missed Assessments

If you miss or expect to miss an assessment, you must inform the instructor as soon as possible and no later than 24 hours after it takes place. In exceptional circumstances, the weight of a missed course component will be shifted to the final exam. In most cases, some form of documentation will be required and, for illnesses, a Verification of Illness Form must be submitted to the undergraduate office for your home faculty (e.g., the Science Undergraduate Office if you are a student in the Faculty of Science).

Requests to defer the final exam will only be considered in exceptional circumstances if appropriate documentation is provided and course progress suggests that a passing grade in the course would be expected.

Course Description

OPTOM 348B:

Students are assigned to various areas within the clinic where, under direct clinical faculty supervision, they participate in the provision of optometric services to clinic patients. In addition to primary care, they are exposed to the provision of contact lens, ocular health, and optical services.

View requirements for OPTOM 348B

Students are assigned to various areas within the clinic where, under the supervision of a registered optometrist (clinical supervisor), they participate in the provision of optometric services to patients. While the emphasis is on primary care, students also participate in the provision of clinical care in other areas including external pediatrics, diagnostic ocular imaging, and optical services.

Throughout this outline, page references are given for the relevant section(s) in the Procedures in Clinical Optometry Manual (2008).

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Systematic Assessment & Record-Keeping
o Elicit and accurately record a complete case history
o Complete a problem-specific assessment for the refractive system, the binocular system, the sensory system, and the ocular health system (pp. 1-7)
o Accurately record the outcomes of the procedures completed for each system assessment (see ‘recording’ section of Procedures manual for expectations of each procedure)
Evaluation & Management
o Begin to formulate plans for evaluation and management of each problem, including diagnostic procedures, consultations, therapeutic strategies, and patient and caregiver education.
o With the guidance of the clinical supervisor, recognize and implement appropriate strategies for routine and follow-up eye care, including preventive standards, guidance, and counselling.
Specific Competencies
o Acquire technical skill competencies* in:
• Accurately obtaining the patient identifying information, ocular and general histories, and identifying and investigating (via FOLDAR) the chief complaint
• Accurately complete assessment of the refractive system (visual acuity, retinoscopy & subjective refraction, add determination)
• Accurately complete assessment of the binocular system (differentiate strabismus vs non-strabismus, accurately identify presence/absence of muscle paresis, accurately assess amplitude of accommodation & near point of convergence)
• Accurately complete assessment of the sensory system (confrontation visual fields, stereopsis, Worth 4-dot)
• Accurately perform cover test within +/- 4pd
• Accurately complete assessment of the anterior segment (identify anterior segment abnormalities, accurate assessment of anterior segment structures)
• Accurately assess Goldmann tonometry within +/-4mmHg
o Become familiar with data acquisition of and learn how to interpret various diagnostic imaging services offered at WOVS.
o Perform frame and spectacle lens selections for common refractive errors; discuss lens options and treatments applicable to the patient’s needs; accurately measure all lens data; perform standard frame adjustments and spectacle repairs; educate patients on the proper use of their spectacle device; address patient concerns; counsel patients experiencing visual problems or adaptation difficulties with a new spectacle device.
o Present patient information concisely and clearly to others, both in oral and written form; actively participate in discussions during clinical case discussions.
Communication & Professionalism
o Effectively relate and communicate with patients, family, caregivers, colleagues, other health professionals and staff.
o Display professional attitudes to learning by showing interest and responsibility in patient care, sensitivity to patient’s needs, good interpersonal relationships and tact.
o Practice good and consistent work habits.
o Show evidence of a desire to learn and improve (by reading, study, discussions), and an ability to receive feedback and implement improvements (i.e. positively respond to feedback and corrections).

* Technical skill competencies address the precision that the student demonstrates with a technical skill, while still meeting the essential requirements. Technical skill competencies will have defined levels of precision in order to acquire the competency.

ASSESSMENT

Essential Requirements:

Essential requirements refer to the knowledge and skills that ALL students must demonstrate in the provision of clinical care. Essential requirements refer to patient safety practices that address:

  1. Accuracy – in that the results of the technique are sufficiently accurate that a correct normal/abnormal judgement can be made
  2. Patient safety practices that reduce the risk of adverse events related to a technical skill. These include, but are not limited to:
  • Obtain visual acuity before any procedures are carried out
  • Describing procedures to patients so that they can provide informed consent
  • Obtaining informed consent prior to procedures/administration of diagnostic drugs
  • Ensuring appropriate infection control and safety requirements are performed prior to conducting procedures or administering diagnostic drugs
  • Carry out pre-instillation assessment, select appropriate drug, review dosage & expiry, check for colour & precipitates
  • Positioning the patient appropriately prior performing a technique, mindful of any movement restrictions and the comfort of the patient
  • Safe manipulation of any equipment to ensure that there is no unintended contact with the patient and the equipment can be used correctly, accurately, and efficiently

The essential requirements including steps for OPTOM 348B are:

Students enrolled in OPTOM 348B must hold the ‘Meets Essential Requirements’ status in 7 of the 9 essential requirements unless they are returning to the program following an absence.  Any outstanding essential requirements to be met or revoked throughout the term must be obtained by the end of 348B in order to proceed to 4th year. Failure to meet all 9 essential requirements by the end of OPTOM 348B will result in an automatic grade of 32% for the course.

*Students returning to the program after an absence will be assumed to have the technical skills competencies that were in place when they were last in the program.  On return to the program, the student will need to demonstrate that they can meet all nine essential safety requirements on their direct care patients in clinic to earn ‘Meets Essential Requirements’ status.    

Students that have not yet achieved the ‘Meets Essential Requirements’ or has a status revoked during the term must:

(a) Ensure the supervisor is present when necessary

(b) Ensure that essential requirements that need confirmation by the supervisor are completed before proceeding with a technique

Failure to do either (a) or (b) will result in the student being assessed as failing to meet professional conduct (‘professional NO’) for each essential requirement where the supervisor was not consulted.

To obtain a ‘Meets Essential Requirements’ status, the student will have been observed on 3 consecutive patients as meeting the standard. 

Essential requirements are carried across all courses with direct patient care. If a student has the status ‘Meets Essential Requirements’ but subsequently fails to meet an essential requirement in any of the 9 activities, they must re-obtain the status. If the activity for which the status is being sought is one of 3, 6, 7, 8, & 9, the student must therefore have the supervisor in the room for observation before completing the essential requirement. 

In a case when a student feels they may be unable to carry out the essential requirement and patient safety is at risk; the student will not perform the technique and immediately seek help from their clinic supervisor. Clinic supervisors are responsible to uphold essential requirements by confirming they are performed on each patient.

Calculation of Course Grade:

Each clinical skills component is assigned a numerical grade (see below); the average of the clinical components is the calculated grade for the day. The final course grade is the average of the daily grades.

The evaluation tool is used for all clinical rotations.  Five clinical skills are assessed using continuous scales (40 to 100%) and eight clinical behaviours are assessed using categorical (yes/no) scales. 

* The Technical Ability scale includes skills in:

  • General Care, Ocular Disease, Refraction, Oculomotor-Sensory and Ocular Health
  • Optical Services: Repairs and Adjustments, Frame Selection, and Lens Design

Clinical skills are evaluated using a non-compensatory method.  That is, an extraordinarily poor or strong performance on one patient of the day will determine the grade in that skill.  For example, if on a given day the ocular health technical skills were generally acceptable for 3 patients, but a retinal detachment was missed on one patient, the grade would be poor (i.e., 40% using the descriptors).  Alternatively, if the performance was generally acceptable on 3 patients but the student found a retinal lesion using scleral indentation, the grade would be high (i.e., 100% using the descriptors). 

Evaluations are submitted electronically by the clinical supervisors. Once submitted, students have access to their grades online. The online evaluation system is accessed through www.optometry.uwaterloo.ca. In all cases, written and oral feedback is expected from the clinical supervisor, and must be given for any grade below 70%.  The clinical supervisor uses the behavioural descriptors provided to grade the student. These descriptors have been modified for each clinic. At any time, students can request a meeting with the course instructor to discuss a particular clinic evaluation. 

The clinical behaviours are not used in the initial grade calculation but repeated patterns of ‘No’ behaviours will have an academic consequence. Please see Table 1. Once, the student has received 3 “NO’s” they will meet with the course instructor.    During a meeting, the course instructor will review the inappropriate behaviours, the student’s perspective will be heard and the expectations for appropriate behaviour will be reviewed.

Frequency & Weight of Evaluation:

The written student evaluation tool is completed for each direct patient care assignment.  The clinical supervisor completes the evaluation by assigning a grade to each of the clinical skills and indicates ‘yes’ or ‘no’ to each of the clinical behaviours (a not applicable [NA] evaluation on the technical skills scale may be appropriate in some cases).  Each evaluation has the same weight in determining a course component grade.

Final Course Grade:

The final course grade will include all submitted evaluations. Successful completion of OPTOM 348B depends on meeting the following standards:

  • Overall passing grade:           70%

Although students are not required to achieve 70% in individual clinic assignments, weak performances will be identified, and students will be required to meet with the course instructor to discuss the performance. The overall grade for OPTOM 348B is determined by the clinical skills and clinic behaviour evaluations submitted throughout the term.

Repeat failure of OPTOM 348B will follow the policies as outlined by the University of Waterloo.

Administrative Policy

ABSENCE FROM COURSE ACTIVITIES 

All activities in OPTOM 348B are mandatory. 

Absences:

Intern attendance is mandatory for all clinical assignments and is a requirement even if there are no pre-booked appointments. This course follows the principles outlined in the Attendance Policy below. An intern must make-up all missed clinic time (this includes partial and complete missed days). There are no exceptions. Time will be made-up according to the following guideline:

Unplanned Absences:

Unplanned (e.g. illness including symptoms related to Covid-19, car trouble etc.) absences from clinic must be reported in accordance to the Attendance Policy. This involves sending the Unplanned Absence Notification Form to the Undergraduate Studies Administrator and informing the Clinic Operations Manager email at the earliest possible time.  In cases of illness, a Verification of Illness Form (VIF) is required for absences that are at minimum of 2 consecutive days (ie. if clinic day is Tuesday, VIF required if also absent the Monday). Unplanned lateness must be reported to the Clinic Operations Manager and course instructor immediately by email.  Failure to notify the Optometry Clinic will result in a Clinical Behaviour “No” for not following clinic policy. 

Planned Absences:

Application for planned absences (e.g., academic meetings, board examinations, wedding, etc.) must be made in accordance with the Attendance Policy to the Undergraduate Studies Administrator at least four weeks in advance of the clinical assignment or as soon as the special circumstance is known.        

In most cases, make-up time should be completed in the term in which it was accumulated. In exceptional circumstances, it may be possible to hold over make-up time from one term to another with the course instructor’s approval. If approved, interns may make-up time prior to a planned absence or during regular term time. These cases are dependent on availability of resources and will be treated on a case-by-case basis. In cases where an intern is in good standing but has not made up missed clinic time an “Incomplete” (INC) will be assigned for OPT 348B.  The intern will not be allowed to proceed into the following term until all course requirements are complete.

PROFESSIONAL CONDUCT OF INTERNS

UW Doctor of Optometry Program Mission: “to graduate competent, ethical Doctors of Optometry by providing them with a comprehensive and contemporary optometric education”

Consistent with this mission, the UW Doctor of Optometry program has identified goals for the program.  One such goal is to “provide high quality optometric education which will prepare graduates to provide independent contemporary optometric services in an ethical and professional manner”.  OPT 348B contributes greatly to the School’s ability to reach its goal of preparing graduates to meet these goals. 

Consistent with University Policies #33 Ethical Behaviour and #71 Academic Student Discipline, interns are expected to: 

  • conduct themselves with honesty and integrity at all times;
  • act professionally and respectfully toward clinic instructors, staff members, patients and colleagues;
  • put, in most cases, the needs of the patients before their own;
  • make themselves aware of, and adhere to, clinic policies;
  • be available for clinic assignment, complete all assigned responsibilities and follow the directives of their clinical teachers;
  • demonstrate an interest in self-improvement;
  • familiarize themselves with, and adhere to, the professional conduct sections as outlined by the College of Optometrists of Ontario

Interns who anticipate problems in adhering to policies as required are encouraged to speak to the course instructor. 

Complaints:

If the Clinic receives a complaint about professional misconduct by the intern, the intern will be notified of the complaint and provided with an opportunity to respond. The Clinic Director and course instructor will conduct a review. If there is sufficient evidence to indicate a breach of policy #33 or #71 the matter will be referred to the Optometry Undergraduate Committee for further investigation. If there is sufficient, evidence to indicate that the safety of patients or clinic personnel are at risk the Optometry Undergraduate Committee will consult with the Associate Dean of Undergraduate Studies and the Secretariat and Office of General Counsel. After such consultations, an interim suspension may be invoked until the matter has been resolved by the Associate Dean of Undergraduate Studies including all appeals. Interim suspension may range from suspension from clinic duties to a complete ban from campus activity. Decisions will be made on a case-by-case basis and the term, conditions and limitations will be conveyed to the intern in writing at the time the suspension is imposed.

University Policy

Course Description

OPTOM 258:

Students will observe and assist in assigned teaching eye clinics.

View requirements for OPTOM 258

Course Description: Through observations of verbal and non-verbal communication interactions in a clinical setting, literature review and group interviews, students are asked to understand, learn and appreciate cross-cultural communication in healthcare and specifically, it's application in Optometry.  Specifically, students will explore whether patient’s concerns and questions are addressed and whether patients feel their perspectives are heard and whether their unique cultural background is considered against the clinicians' own perspectives and beliefs in a clinical setting. 

Clinic Experience Session: Students will spend 2 half-day clinic sessions participating in the University of Waterloo, Optometry Clinic.  The clinical experience sessions include but is not limited to the following:

  • Observation of diagnostic procedures and use of imaging equipment
  • Observation in the treatment and management of patients
  • Observation of the administrative processes (appointment booking, payment) of the University of Waterloo, Optometry Clinic

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe, discuss and reflect upon how patients process and react to a clinical diagnosis and how this influences the type of care received
Describe, discuss, and reflect upon cross-cultural communication as reported in literature.
Develop an understanding of and adhere to professionals ethics, etiquette, communication
Articulate thoughts in a coherent and professional manner through group participation and reflective writing
In addition, the learning objectives of the course are also meant to serve as transformational skills:
To gain interpersonal communication skills: a) be able to reflect on your communication with different people and audiences; b) be able to formulate strategies to enhance communication
Term Focus:
Regardful care — health professionals deliver patient centered care, which does not diminish, demean or disempower the patient, and which incorporates the patient’s unique cultural background, and the withholding of information can contribute to feelings of patient powerlessness and consequently lead to care that is not person centered (1, 2). It is important for information to be delivered to patients in a way that they understand and in which promotes a safe-space for them to ask questions (2). In contrast, open, empathetic and authentic communication leads to beneficial patient-(health) practitioner interactions where the patient feels heard and respected (1,2). Patients have identified that when practitioners work to dismantle the power structure within health care, it opens the door for honest communication and patient understanding (1). Having students reflect on verbal and non-verbal communication, and the power dynamics within the optometry clinic setting provides the foundation to critically reflect on what it means to provide culturally safe care (1).
Engagement and discourse — health professionals undertake appropriate engagement with patients, as defined by the patient, to understand and assist in meeting the patient’s unique needs

Background:
Although patients may have the same condition, the way patients process or react to a clinical experience or diagnosis may be different. As such, it is essential for optometrists to seek their patient’s guidance and explore their patient’s concerns and questions about a symptom, diagnosis or management plan. Research has demonstrated that patients often feel disheartened that their perspectives are not considered valid in the healthcare setting, even though they are the experts on their own health.

1. Ferguson, L. M.; Ward, H.; Card, S.; Sheppard, S.; McMurtry, J. Putting the ‘patient’ back into patient-centred care: An education perspective. Nurse Education in Practice 2013, 13, 283-287.

Clinic Experience Session:
Students will be assigned to observe patient encounters at the University of Waterloo, Optometry Clinic and will be required to reflect on the deliverance of patient centered care including conveying a diagnosis and treatment. Students will specifically explore whether the patient’s concerns and questions were addressed and whether the patient felt their perspectives were heard and their unique cultural background were considered against their own perspective and beliefs.
Group Interviews:
Students will investigate and learn about cross cultural communication from the perspectives of a patient and optometrist
Group Final Reflection
Students will write a final reflection based on the course learning objectives.

Dress Code for Clinic:

  • All students must adhere to the clinic dress code when assigned to patient care.  The clinic dress code for students is as follows:
  • 2- or 3-pocket V-neck scrub top and pants in ciel/ceil blue. Shirts worn under scrubs must be white, black or the colour of the scrub uniform.
  • Footwear that is clean, safe and in good repair (running shoes are permitted)
  • WOVS Optometry clinic nametag
  • Head coverings are allowed for religious observance only.
  • Minimal fragrances, perfumes, colognes or after-shave lotions can be worn.
  • Personal hygiene and halitosis must be addressed.
  • Hair is to be neat, clean and not obstructive to the examination
  • Long hair is to be tied back to meet the clinic’s IPAC protocols

There may be extraordinary situations where total compliance with the dress code may not be required (e.g., religious observance or physical impairment).  Anyone who feels that they cannot comply with an aspect of the dress code should discuss their situation with the course instructor as soon as they are aware there is a compliance issue.

University Policy

Academic integrity: In order to maintain a culture of academic integrity, members of the University of Waterloo community are expected to promote honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.]

Grievance: A student who believes that a decision affecting some aspect of their university life has been unfair or unreasonable may have grounds for initiating a grievance. Read Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances, Section 4. When in doubt, please be certain to contact the department’s administrative assistant who will provide further assistance.

Discipline: A student is expected to know what constitutes academic integrity to avoid committing an academic offence, and to take responsibility for their actions. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.] A student who is unsure whether an action constitutes an offence, or who needs help in learning how to avoid offences (e.g., plagiarism, cheating) or about “rules” for group work/collaboration should seek guidance from the course instructor, academic advisor, or the undergraduate associate dean. For information on categories of offences and types of penalties, students should refer to Policy 71, Student Discipline. For typical penalties, check Guidelines for the Assessment of Penalties.

Appeals: A decision made or penalty imposed under Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances (other than a petition) or Policy 71, Student Discipline may be appealed if there is a ground. A student who believes they have a ground for an appeal should refer to Policy 72, Student Appeals.

Note for students with disabilities: AccessAbility Services, located in Needles Hall, Room 1401, collaborates with all academic departments to arrange appropriate accommodations for students with disabilities without compromising the academic integrity of the curriculum. If you require academic accommodations to lessen the impact of your disability, please register with AccessAbility Services at the beginning of each academic term.

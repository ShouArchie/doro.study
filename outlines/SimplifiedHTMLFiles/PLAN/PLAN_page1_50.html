Course Description

PLAN 477:

This course will focus on material flows from local to global, explored through the transportation land use interrelationships and systems lens. Students will learn the fundamentals of major freight modes, their relevant policy, regulatory and planning domains, and the complex roles, intersections, and impacts of freight in our communities. Students will develop/enhance their research, data analytics, and communication skills.

View requirements for PLAN 477

Introduction

This course is focused on the other aspect of spatial interaction and movement which is the movement of goods or freight.  It is an often overlooked aspect of transportation systems study but no less important, especially given its fundamental role within the functioning of our modern socio-economic systems.  Modern production and retailing depends on the freight system – air, rail, road and marine – like never before. Obviously, these systems are an integral part of, and impact our urban/region in diverse ways.  In this course, we will explore these impacts and influences by looking at transportation, planning and policy from a freight perspective.

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Identify and explain the components of the freight transportation system
Analyze the complex interrelations between these components and society/the city/current issues
Critically evaluate planning and policy developments in freight transportation
Demonstrate your own values concerning freight transport issues and evaluate stakeholder values
The Reference Questions associated with the Learning Outcomes
What is the freight system and where did it come from? How does it evolve?
How does freight fit in? – What roles does it play in our lives and what are its impacts?
How do we plan for freight – how is the freight related policy realm changing?
Why do you care/not care about freight transportation? – Why do people dislike/ignore freight?

Learning Modes and Expectations

A number of important propositions inform the evaluation and overall course experience

•The instructor is the guide/facilitator and the student is the one responsible for the learning – the student plays a key role in directing the course experience

Our topic exploration is supplemented very heavily by your experiences and integration of references materials and required readings.

The two individual assignments, readings engagement and a group project are designed to facilitate your exploration of freight related topics and provide an opportunity for critical assessment and reflection.  An overview of each of these components is provided below and further details will be provided as we progress through the term.

Assignments:

1.  Freight Audit (Due Feb 3.)

One of the major challenges facing the goods movement (freight) sector is that despite their essential nature, they are often an overlooked part of the broader planning and society realm. One famous quote is that planners need to move from a “regulate and restrict” mindset to an “understand and accommodate” mindset.  To that, the Province of Ontario has developed a draft set of “Freight Supportive Guidelines” (MTO, 2016) and others have advocated for communities to employ a “freight filter” in their planning, thinking about freight dimensions in all planning.   A key to these efforts is developing a clear picture of the current status of a community’s freight system/sectors (all systems and modes), their issues and prospects for the future – a freight audit – with an emphasis on a comprehensive review of current status.  In this assignment, you will be completing a freight audit for one of Canada’s major cities (Census Metropolitan Area – CMA).

2.  Freight Generators: Logistics Land Uses and Challenges (Due Mar. 10th)

The goal of this assignment is to explore and assess the industrial and logistics related land uses and developments for the city you audited in Assignment 1.  The focus here is on identifying and describing the range of logistics related spaces in your city, providing a current assessment of the industrial land market, and profiling a recent logistics development in terms of the site form and function, and relevant planning considerations and issues.  The range of spaces gives room to move from major Distribution Centers (DCs) and fulfilment centers to local delivery stations and industrial condominiums. The objective is to broaden your traditional land use planning mindset to embrace industrial and logistics land uses as well.

Readings Engagement

Part of the course evaluation will be student engagement with the readings assigned in advance of each week. The reading engagement takes place in the Perusall System, an online workspace accessed through our course Learn site. Students are expected to comment, question, and discuss the readings collaboratively in groups established at the beginning of the course.  Your level of reading engagement is graded in terms of quantity and quality. It is reading with purpose and sharing your perspectives and understanding of the materials with each other. 

The Freight (Great) Debate – Group Project (Main element Due March 28th.– interim elements due periodically through term)

The significant cumulative assessment in this course (like a final exam) is the Oxford style debate that will occur in our last class (April 3rd) .  Collectively, we will establish 4 major debate topics (statements) that will be the focus of teams throughout the term.  Pairs of teams will be assigned to each topic and will prepare to debate their topic during the final week of classes.  This debate will involve 2 team members taking on the debating role for each side with the class assessing the debate outcome.  The pairs of teams will not know which side of the debate proposition they are on until 48 hours before the assigned class debate time.  Required elements for grading will include the main element of your debate preparation materials, and of course, performance in the debate itself.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

PLAN 401:

The course will explore the concept, causes, and roles of conflict, noting potential positive as well as negative characteristics of conflict in planning practice. Various conflict resolution strategies will be examined, including alternative dispute resolution (ADR), negotiation, and mediation. The roles of formal planning tribunals will be discussed through a review of tribunal mandates and practices in Canada and abroad. The course will use simulations to develop students' skills in approaching difficult conversations and active listening.

View requirements for PLAN 401

PLAN 601:

An examination of tribunals and boards that adjudicate matters related to land use planning, environmental and heritage protection, property assessment, land valuation, and other matters. Topics include: tribunal/board history; appeal rights and procedures; the roles and responsibilities of planners, lawyers and stakeholders; and critical perspectives regarding current and alternative practices.

Introduction:

Planning takes place within complex decision-making environments. Planning issues concern many, diverse participants: community residents, local politicians, special interest groups, government agencies - in short, anybody with an interest in a planning matter. Quite often, planners find themselves in situations where there can be conflict about the type, extent and impact of changes in land use. In these situations, there is usually recourse to an arm’s length body that could adjudicate the matter. In this course, we will explore how jurisdictions in Canada and internationally manage these adjudication processes. We will also learn about alternative dispute resolution strategies and tools that can be applied in conjunction with, or instead of, the tribunal system model.

Tentative Course Schedule

Course Structure

A. Common Planning Tribunal Models

Week 1 - January 9     
Key Trends and Sources of Conflict in Planning Practice
Learning Objective: To explore common types of, and reasons for, conflict and dispute in planning.
Content: This session starts with an overview of key trends; this provides context for our discussion of planning-oriented conflict. A significant amount of urban and regional planning practice is characterized by differences of opinion and, often, conflict amongst stakeholders in the planning process. These situations can occur in many aspects of land use planning - for example, in applications to change established land uses, zoning, and planning policy, health of ecosystems, access to groundwater, and the prospect of aggregate mining. Several of the common sources of conflict, and the varying roles of stakeholders, are addressed in this module.

Week 2 - January 16       
Planning Tribunals Elsewhere in Canada
Learning Objective: To become familiar with the design and administration of planning tribunals outside Ontario.
Content: According to the Canadian Constitution (1982), land use planning and resource management is a provincial responsibility. Each Province and Territory has a version of a planning tribunal or similar appeals body. In this module, the key characteristics of these other provincial systems will be reviewed.

Week 3 - January 23   
Planning Tribunal Models: The International Experience
Learning Objective: To become familiar with the types of planning conflict adjudication models that are used in other countries.
Content: Some form of adjudication model and process is used in many other countries. In this module, we'll review how planning tribunals are designed and managed in the United Kingdom, Australia, New Zealand, and the United States. Similarities and some differences will be evident in these international approaches.

B. Working With(in) the OLT Model

Week 4 – January 30  
Ontario Land Tribunal – Mandate, Policies and Processes
Learning Objective: To become familiar with the OLT’s mandate and scope of activities.
Content: The current tribunal model – the Ontario Land Tribunal (OLT) – was established in 2021. The OLT consolidated several special-purpose tribunals into a single organizational structure. Planning is one of several areas of interest; complementary areas include expropriation, mining, heritage, and the environment, each of which is addressed through specific legislation (e.g., Planning Act). In this session, the mandate, related legislation, and key policy and process elements of the OLT will be reviewed.
Guest Speaker: Scott Tousaw, Vice-Chair, Ontario Land Tribunal

B. Working With/In the OLT Model

Week 5 - February 6    
Ontario Land Tribunal – Planning Jurisdiction and Appeals
Learning Objective: To become familiar with the appeals and decision-making processes of the OLT.
Content: The Ontario Land Tribunal (OLT)’s planning jurisdiction is considerable and includes hearing and deciding appeals regarding several related areas of concern. The OLT can hear appeals about a wide range of planning concerns such as official plans, zoning by-laws, and a host of other familiar planning tools (e.g., subdivision plans, consents, and minor variances). In this session we will have insights about the OLT from a professional planner with experience before this tribunal.
Guest Speaker: Garrett Stevenson, Director of Development and Housing Approvals, City of Kitchener 

Week 6 - February 13  
Ontario Land Tribunal – The Lawyer's Perspective
Learning Objective: To consider the OLT tribunal model from the perspective of a key participant: the lawyer.
Content: In our two previous OLT-oriented sessions, we heard from the Vice-Chair of the OLT, and from a planner with experience about this tribunal. The role(s) of the lawyer are critical in this process. In this session, we explore the lawyer's perspective on the Ontario Land Tribunal.
Guest Speaker: Eric Davis, LLB. Lawyer with SV Law (Guelph, ON)

Week 7 – February 17-23 Reading Week
No class scheduled.

Week 8 – February 27            
The Politician's Perspective On/Experience With the OLT
Learning Objective: To become aware of the roles played by local elected officials, and the implications of the OLT process on municipal decision-making.
Content: Without question, members of local or regional government Councils play a really important role in the planning system generally. They also need to make decisions that are informed by different perspectives on a planning matter. Sometimes, these matters must be referred to the OLT for resolution. What are the implications for local government autonomy and Council decision-making?
Guest Speaker: Jeff Henry, Urban Planner, Arcadis Consultants (Waterloo) and former Councillor, City of Waterloo

Week 9 - March 6
The Developer's Roles in/Views of the OLT process
Learning Objective: To become familiar with the perspectives and experiences of key actors in the planning system - developers.
Content: Developers are key actors in urban planning: they are the people who make tangible planning policies. How do developers view the roles and powers of the OLT? What are the implications for project advocates of this adjudication and review process? A representative of the development industry will share their perspectives on the OLT process.
Guest Speaker: Larry Kotseff, MCIP, RPP.  Vice-President (Real Estate Development), Solowave Investments (Waterloo)

C. Experiences and Future Directions in Dispute Resolution

Week 10 - March 13
Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)
Learning Objective: To explore alternatives to the formal planning tribunal model.
Content: In recent years, the more progressive jurisdictions have introduced dispute and conflict resolution models that are considered less expensive or adversarial than the traditional court-based model or planning tribunals. In this module, the key characteristics of alternative dispute resolution (ADR) tools and methods will be discussed.
Guest Speaker: TBA

Week 11 – March 20
Interest-Based Negotiation
Learning Objective: To learn what it means to design and manage an interest-based negotiation process.
Content: It can seem so difficult to arrive at a “win-win” conclusion to a conflict situation, whether it’s inter-personal or regarding a conflict-ridden planning matter. In this session, we’ll explore the idea of interest-based negotiation, and consider the strengths and challenges associated with this strategy. 
Guest Speaker: TBA

Week 12 – March 27
When It Doesn’t Work Out…
Learning Objective: To look for lessons learned from a failed or unsatisfactory negotiation experience.
Content: Sometimes, despite one’s best effort, negotiations fail. There can be all kinds of reasons for that outcome – i.e., the personalities involved, historical interactions, perhaps problems with the negotiation process design and management. In this session, we’ll examine common issues that can undermine negotiations, and review how to prepare for the next negotiation opportunity.
Guest Speaker: TBA

Week 13 – April 3
Case Study – City of Waterloo
Learning Objective: To acquire a good understanding of how projects evolve and change with the influence of the OLT, and through effective use of ADR, negotiation, and mediation.
Content: In this final course module, we will explore how ADR has been applied in a real-world development case. We will also consider the nature of the relationship between ADR and the Ontario Land Tribunal decision-making process.
Guest Speaker: TBA

Student Evaluation

Course Assignments: 
There will be no mid-Term or end of Term examination. There are three assignments that are required for course completion, plus a peer evaluation. Interpretive guidelines for each Assignment will be provided on the course Learn site.

Assignment #1 is an individual assignment that reflects the content provided in Section A (Context and Common Planning Tribunal Models). Each student is to create a research paper that addresses two elements: (a) the sources of conflict; and (b) describes, compares, and contrasts the types of appeals models used internationally, across Canada, and in Ontario. Value: 30% of the course grade. Due: February 14

Assignment #2 is a team assignment. This content will be drawn from the content covered in Section B (Working With/In the OLT Model). Here, the team’s paper will describe and reflect on the experiences of working with the OLT system from the perspectives of key actors. Value: 30% of the course grade. Due: March 14

Assignment #3 is a team assignment. This assignment will focus on Section C (Experiences and Future Directions in Dispute Resolution). For this assignment, teams will describe and critically review the Ontario Land Tribunal model. With that content in place, the teams will then explain what would comprise an appropriate future model for planning-oriented conflict and dispute resolution. Value: 30% of the course grade. Due: April 11

Participant Evaluation:
Each team member will receive the same grade from each of the assignments. A peer evaluation system will be used to assess team characteristics such as productivity, collegiality, and professionalism in work ethic. Peer evaluations will be completed on the university’s system (details to follow on the course Learn site). Value: 10% of the course grade. Due: April 11

Generative AI:
This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.).

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

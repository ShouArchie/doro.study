Course Description

PLAN 211:

This is a studio-based course through which students will examine contemporary urban design challenges and apply theory and technical knowledge to the development and presentation of design interventions. The course emphasizes the development of qualitative and quantitative visual analysis skills, as well as the expression of design ideas through a range of manual and digital visual communication techniques. Students work in both individual and group settings to perform research and analyze a small-scale study area and, through studio discussions and critiques, develop creative design solutions.

Prerequisite: PLAN 210 and approval from the Instructor

Introduction

This course prepares a student for attaining competence in urban design. Urban design focuses on the look and feel of our communities, and this course provides a basis for entering into that reflexively and critically. A strong studio foundation allows for a broad understanding of the scope of design practice. Studio is a place of sharing, experimentation, and critique. Design studio serves as a pivotal means of establishing positions on the built form's connections to its physical-natural setting and its socio-cultural and economic contexts. The course provides an important basis for subsequent specialization courses and studios, including PLAN 409, and elective studio courses PLAN 309, 313, and 408. This course meets as a collective body in a dual studio seminar/pin-up split, and is a one term course with credit weight 0.5. This course includes a field component. A Green New Deal Studio

The Green New Deal Superstudio is a collaborative and interdisciplinary platform that brought together over 40 design schools to translate the core goals of the Green New Deal— decarbonisation, climate resilience, social justice, and jobs—into design and planning projects for their respective region.

The Green New Deal Superstudio and Summit are strategies to continue the profound transformation set forth from the 2016 Summit on Landscape Architecture and the Future and to answer the calls to action outlined in the resulting New Landscape Declaration. The Declaration identified climate change, inequity, and mass species extinction as the defining issues of our time and asserted our agency as designers to help solve them. To increase our capacity to help solve these issues, the Declaration calls for designers to strengthen and diversify their global capacity; cultivate a bold culture of inclusive leadership, advocacy, and activism in their ranks; raise awareness of value created through design; and support research and new practices that result in design innovation and policy transformation.

For our purpose, we will focus this semester on the application of these goals to the urban design scale with a “socio-ecological resilience" mindset. In this movement, two interrelated conditions affect the viability of cities; the first is the environmental cost of modernization, and the second is the social liability primarily due to private interest. Furthermore, these forces can be intertwined, where new density to alleviate housing inequity can sometimes occupy land that might be used for carbon sequestration and stormwater management. And in another instance, the implementation of green infrastructure can result in climate gentrification, through the increase of land value and housing costs.

Therefore, this studio will ask students to reconsider design working methods to integrate and represent urban design for climate resiliency, decarbonization, social equity, infrastructure vitality, ecological adaptation, and economic sustainability. The studio will ask the students to re-think and reimagine the social and material dimensions of the urban realm for a post carbon era and as a matter of priority ask; where these projects should take place? And who will they serve?

Ideally, our design methods will aim to repurpose and reimage the “old and deteriorating” physical infrastructure of the city to meet these needs, as opposed to re-inventing the wheel and becoming a burden on public finance. The ideal design framework will empower neighborhood entities, local agencies, and individuals, and link design with policy as the governing guidelines for city building. For more information on the platform: https://www.gndsuperstudio.com/

The Site and System: The Laurel Greenway

Contemporary urban design practice is oftentimes focused on sites, not systems; and on elite desires, not public interests. The urban design studio will seek to invigorate the latter design philosophies with the purpose of achieving the Green New Deal principles in the context of The Laurel Greenway.

Currently, The City of Waterloo, along with a consultant team are conducting a feasibility study for the Laurel Greenway. The purpose of the study is to “improve the experience along the entire Greenway, focusing on the trail, wayfinding, active transportation, and connections between spaces throughout" (City of Waterloo, 2021). The greenway is a linear park space and urban corridor that starts from the Waterloo Memorial Recreation Complex on Father David Bauer Drive, weaves through uptown Waterloo, and running from Waterloo Park in the west to Weber Street in the east. The corridor also runs parallel to Laurel creek, an ecological spine for the city that has a significant nature recreation history as well as has been associated with historic flood events. Although the spine is called a “Greenway”, it is ironically littered with grey space and drosscapes (wasted and wasteful spaces) and lacks a true connected identity. In contrast, the corridor is a critical juncture within the Waterloo region context, with significant natural heritage uses, iconic built form, public open space amenities, and the ION transit line that runs in parts of the space.

This studio brief will seek to expand on the current focus of the city’s feasibility study for normative urban design and encourage students to develop a critical point of view on the Green New Deal ideals through their studio projects. Specifically, students are solicited to examine, dissect and conceptualize a 100 year transformation of the Laurel Greenway that; 1) mitigates climate impacts through GHG offset strategies (eg. alternative transportation methods, TOD, net-zero developments, sustainable flows of energy and its use) 2) adapts to a post carbon era within the urban design context (eg. remedy the corridor’s drosscapes, manage stormwater, restoration and planting vegetations where appropriate), and 3) values equitable social opportunities (eg. indigenous land recognition, integration of affordable housing, community building platforms), and 4) bolster long-term and local economic development. 

The design concept ought to reimagine the corridor as a system, rather than a singular site on design metrics including the environmental (eg. green space), infrastructural (eg. energy assets, utility lines), morphological, perceptual/ visual, temporal and social dimensions. The project encourages a deeper examination of the site history(including indigenous stories)in order to conceptualize equitable site uses. The proposed concept ought to empower sustainable built architecture guided by net-zero principles and where appropriate the restoration and enhancement of existing forms. The ultimate goal is to allow the Laurel Greenway to live up to its name, as a true “green” way in the heart of Waterloo, and one that embodies social equity and economic viability through its physical and semantic design.

Course Objectives

Analysis:

  • Develop an analytical design lens and understand how the environmental, morphological, infrastructural, perceptual, temporal, and functional planning principles shape human settlements (and specifically in the present context of the climate crisis).
  • Pursue a critical approach towards urban climate resilience and equitable planning and design.
  • Understand how and when to apply lessons from contemporary case studies.

Design:

  • Develop methods of representation and narrative that establish tactical design strategies that fit within (and alongside) human needs and uses and the natural setting.
  • Continue to enhance graphic communication skills including, but not limited to, analytical diagrams and conventional architectural projections (e.g., plan, section, elevation, perspective, and axonometric drawings).
  • Continue to enhance skills in using digital tools particularly, Adobe Photoshop, InDesign, Illustrator, SketchUp, Rhino as well as basic geospatial functions in ArcGIS and QGIS.
  • Develop a point of view on the studio itself through reviews, reporting, and critique.

Learning Modes

Students best learn the methods presented in this course by being open to the iterative nature of the design process. This requires students to have patience with their assignments and each other. Course assignments are important to student success as design studio students learn through doing. Therefore, mistakes are seen as productive, and the studio offers an open space to discuss strategies for overcoming obstacles. The assignment briefs lay out a framework for students to complete their assignments, as both individual and group deliverables.

Studio: Plan 211 is primarily a studio course. A studio is more than a physical space, it is also a method of communication and instruction between instructors and students. In essence, a studio provides opportunities for students to discuss and receive critical feedback about their ideas as they are being developed prior to an assignment deadline as well as discuss feedback after assignments have been submitted and evaluated by the instructor.

Note: Participation in Studio sessions is a required component of the course and is reflected in the course participation grades (see Course Requirements and Responsibilities).

GENERATIVE AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions.

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

GEOG 368 / PLAN 341:

This course provides students with a critical understanding of the relationship between human development and natural environments. Students will develop an understanding of core ecological principles and how these can be taken up in plans and policies to promote nature conservation in urban and rural environments. The course will include examples of how planners mitigate the environmental impacts of development of land, allotment of open space and parks, and conservation of sensitive areas and species in both urban and rural contexts.

GEOG 368: View requirements for GEOG 368

PLAN 341: View requirements for PLAN 341

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
understand biophysical and ecological processes in urban areas
plan for biophysical and ecological process when developing and managing the built environment

Ability to meet deadlines

Rather than applying late penalties, students will be marked on their ability to meet deadlines. They will receive 2.5 marks for completing for each of the Reflection Papers submitted on time and 2.5 marks for completing the Case Study Report and Video on time. No assignments or quizzes will be accepted after April 14 at 11:59 pm. Assignments or quizzes completed after April 14 will be graded as 0%.

Online quizzes and in class exams

There will be 7 online quizzes throughout the term. Each quiz corresponds to the theme for each week. The quizzes are study guides for the in class exams. There will be 3 in class exams throughout the semester. Exams will be based on weekly course content and the readings. 

Reflection papers

The Reflection Paper assignment is design to encourage reflection on critical issues affecting urban ecology and the way we develop and plan cities. There will be two reflection papers. The first will critically engage with a recent and important contribution from Schell et al. (2020) on the ways that systematic racism have shaped urban ecology, and the second will discuss a contribution from Spirn (2012) on ecological urbanism (see Additional Readings listed above). Each Reflection Paper should be approximately 500 to 1,000 words and should answer the following questions: 

1. What are the main points or arguments made by the author(s)?

2. Which of these points stand out to you? Are they new to you or did you know them before?

3. How does the paper challenge what you knew about urban ecology previously?

4. How can the ideas, concepts or methods be applied to improve the ways we plan and develop cities and urban environments?

Case study

The case study assignment involves working in a group to prepare an urban environmental baseline assessment and plan for a Canadian or international city. The assignment should focus on one of the key issues or themes raised during the class. Students choose the city that they want to focus on. The case study should describe each of the focal ecological components of the city and develop a plan to improve or enhance the sustainability of these components. More details will be provided in class.

The assignment is broken into three parts. 

1) Weekly in class activities. These will vary week to week, but are designed to prepare students to complete their case study assignment.

2) The Case Study Video should be 10 minutes in length. It should detail the main findings from the environmental baseline assessment and plan. 

2) The Case Study Report should be between 3,000 and 4,000 words in total (excluding tables, figures, and references). It should detail in written form the main findings from the environmental baseline assessment and plan.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as [fill this in with your discipline-specific skills]. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Research Ethics: The University of Waterloo requires all research conducted by its students, staff, and faculty which involves humans as participants to undergo prior ethics review and clearance through the Director, Office of Human Research and Animal Care (Office). The ethics review and clearance processes are intended to ensure that projects comply with the Office’s Guidelines for Research with Human Participants (Guidelines) as well as those of provincial and federal agencies, and that the safety, rights and welfare of participants are adequately protected. The Guidelines inform researchers about ethical issues and procedures which are of concern when conducting research with humans (e.g. confidentiality, risks and benefits, informed consent process, etc.).

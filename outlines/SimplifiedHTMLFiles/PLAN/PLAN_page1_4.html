Course Description

PLAN 110:

This course explores multiple forms of visual communication and design. Students will be introduced to two and three-dimensional drawings that are relevant for urban planners and designers. Students will gain practical design skills using both hand drawing/sketching and computer software. A key component of this course is student development of a critical understanding of the role of planners as producers and consumers of visual imagery. Students will also learn and experience design as an iterative process.

View requirements for PLAN 110

Course Introduction

Plan 110 introduces students to the technical and conceptual skills that professional planners use in the graphic communication of planning and design concepts. The course will build skills that are fundamental to planning and design practice through a variety of representation techniques that will equip students with the ability to communicate their ideas. Through a hands-on approach, students will learn to create clear, compelling visuals that enhance their ability to communicate planning ideas, policies, and proposals to diverse audiences, including community stakeholders, government officials, and the public. Key topics include the design of site plans, diagrams, infographics and presentation panels. Practical assignments will allow students to apply these skills in real-world planning scenarios, helping to bridge the gap between technical planning knowledge and effective visual communication.

In general, the course is intended to provide a foundation in graphic communication that all planning students will require for future course (i.e. PLAN 210) and professional work. In your future careers, whether you have a direct hand in the development of planning and design graphics, the visual communication of plan information is vital to all planning professionals. It is therefore essential that you become familiar with the content of plan graphics and the processes that contribute to their production.

Learning Objectives

Upon completion of the course, students will have acquired knowledge and skills in:

  • Develop foundational knowledge about the visual communication process and best practices in urban planning
  • Analyze elements of a site plan, understand uses of site elements to communicate ideas
  • Use appropriate tools to accurately portray visuals
  • Express ideas in a clear, organized and effective manners using visuals;
  • Utilize methods and practices to find relevant planning information; 
  • Apply current technology to complete work and make informed decisions;
  • Understand types of information and knowledge required to support planning-related decision making.

Student Evaluation

There are three main course assignments for Plan 110:

The grade breakdown and due dates for each Assignment (and their components) are as follows:

Assignment 1: Sketching as Observation: You will choose any site in the city of Kitchener to visit, photograph, observe and sketch. You will use visual (sketch) and written communication to prepare a four-page submission in Adobe Indesign summarizing your observations. Please complete the mandatory field trip form before travelling to your chosen site. 

Assignment 2: Infographic & Urban Design Guideline Review: Chose one section of Kitchener’s Urban Design Manual that corresponds to the site/building you have chosen in Assignment 1. Analyze how your site/buildings meet the objectives outlined in the Urban Design Manual by preparing an infographic(s) with accompanied written descriptions to visually tell the story of your site. You will use visual (infographics) and written communication to prepare a four-page submission in Adobe Indesign summarizing your urban design review. 

Assignment 3: Site Massing & Presentation Panel for Public Meeting: Clear and effective visual communication of your planning and design work, particularly to members of the public, is an essential aspect of planning communication. Students will use SketchUp to prepare a basic massing model of their site/building. Once this is complete, students should use CAD mapper/photoshop/procreate to add basic context to the massing model (ie: landscaping, people, transportation). Using your site sketch, infographic and 3D model, students will prepare a public meeting presentation panel. Students will be introduced to layout and graphic communication principles for design information panels that could be used in a Public Meeting setting.

Academic Honesty
Originality is the cornerstone of academic as well as professional planning and design practice. To protect and uphold academic integrity in the class, it is the responsibility of the student to demonstrate the originality of his or her work if called upon to do so. When there is any doubt regarding the originality of student work, a student may be asked at any time to explain his or her work as part of the normal assignment evaluation process. Graphic assignments that are duplications or reproductions of existing work produced by another source will result in an academic penalty.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

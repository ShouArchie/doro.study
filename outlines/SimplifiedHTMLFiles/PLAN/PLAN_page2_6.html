Course Description

PLAN 639:

This course examines the relationship between the environment (built/physical, economic, social, political and natural aspects) and population health. It focuses on conceptual and empirical links among current environment-health issues such as air quality, active transportation, injury prevention, climate change, and mental well-being. Emphasis is placed on the role of urban planners in collaboration with allied professionals (e.g., public health, engineering, law enforcement, architecture) in creating and maintaining healthy built environments to improve population health with a focus on key health issues. The importance of multi-sectoral collaboration, healthy public policy and effective tools for assessing healthy communities will be illustrated using case studies.
*eligible for MES.

View requirements for PLAN 639

Recommended: This senior level course assumes basic knowledge of the link between the built environment and health. Students who are unfamiliar with this relationship are strongly encouraged to complete the recommended readings prior to the start of the course.

Introduction

Planning and public health have a shared history: both professions emerged out of problems that grew out of the industrial revolution. This course will trace the history and evolution of both professions, academic disciplines, and will compare both theory and methods used. We will discuss particular health and planning issues (e.g., air pollution, physical activity, mental health and the built environment) as well as future cities. Increasingly, planning and public health organizations are realizing the importance of working together to improve urban health and equity. This course will provide an overview of the current issues in health and planning. This is a one term course with a credit weight of 0.5.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Compare planning and public health perspectives on: history and the evolution of the disciplines; theory; methods; power and jurisdiction
Identify and analyze interactions between natural and built environment characteristics and human health and disease
Demonstrate interdisciplinary collaboration skills and articulate the benefits of interdisciplinary group work
Articulate equity considerations of planning decisions and explain the extent to which equity may or may not be important in planning
Gather and synthesize evidence and develop data-driven predictions of future urban trends. Frame results of the synthesis to develop recommendations for healthy city building for different audiences.

Learning Modes 

This class will take a flipped classroom approach. This type of approach is designed to increase student engagement during class. Classes will be comprised of lectures that briefly summarize main points of each module. Online lecture material covers conceptual and empirical examples/case studies of environment and health relationships. Online materials, media sources (videos, news stories), discussions, and activities will be used to highlight the application of course concepts to practice. Students are responsible for completing the required readings each week, and for keeping informed about course information through LEARN. There is no required text, and all readings are available through the course reserves. Readings have been selected to provide a balance of breadth and depth into important course concepts. This course will use LEARN to disseminate course material, as a communication tool, and post grades. There are three components you will be graded on in this course (see break: 

  • Reading Engagement (10 weeks x 2% each) = 20% of overall mark
  • Reflection Activities (11 weeks x 1.5% each (lowest mark dropped)) = 15% of overall mark

Reading Engagement (worth 20% of your overall mark): Perusall will be used to collate and assess your engagement with course readings. A key part of your course experience involves learning through reading and purposeful engagement with materials assigned. Rather than reading on your own, we will be using Perusall – a software designed for collaborative reading where you will be annotating, questioning, and commenting on the assigned readings in groups of students. The completion of readings and your level and quality of engagement establish your grade.

Activity Reflections (worth 15% of your overall mark): Activities in our seminars will happen every class from Wednesday January 15th to Wednesday April 2nd. Of the 11 activities (no activity first or last week) we will complete, each will be connected with an activity reflection worth 1.5% towards your final grade, with your lowest mark being dropped. The activities will be based on the module readings and lecture materials, and will take a variety of different forms, including but not limited to: readiness assurance tests, participating in discussions with your peers, creating concept maps, and think-pair-share activities. After each seminar activity, there will be a required activity reflection (200-300 word commentary on your experience in the seminar activity) that you will need to submit on LEARN (via the SubmitàQuizzes tab) by 11:59pm that Wednesday. The point of the activities is to 1) ensure that you are familiar with each week’s materials, and 2) encourage you to deepen your understanding of the topic in real time. To ensure that students who are feeling ill or have emergency situations are not penalized for missing class, you may instead request an alternate reflection question that the course instructor will develop based on the week’s topic.

*Scaffolded Group Assignment (Annotated Bibliography [25%], Fact Sheet/Infographic and Issue Summary [15%], Final Assignment Report, Presentation [25%]): There is one larger group assignment in this course that consists of individual milestones which you will complete on your own in support of the group assignment. The final group assignment is due Sunday, April 6th, 2025, at 11:59pm (see Assignment Instructions in LEARN). As the focus of this course is the development of your ‘environment and health’ analytical and communication skills, the assignment is presented in a ‘scaffolded’ structure. This means that the assignment and its component parts will progressively build on each other as the course moves along. Smaller assignments will be administered earlier in the course and be completed to support your work in the next step of the assignment, and then larger and more heavily weighted assessments will be assigned later. This year the topics for your final group assignment, as well as the individual milestone components, will be connected to UW’s new Future Cities Institute. Your group will select a specific topic within one of the broad focus areas of the Future Cities Institute: Housing, AI/Machine Learning in City Planning/Building, Sustainable Infrastructure, or Mobility. The final topic should be mutually agreed upon by all members of the group. You may connect your selected theme with any relevant health issue that you would like (e.g., healthcare access, mental health, active living, food systems, social cohesion/fragmentation). To contextualize these topics and provide additional information for your work, representatives from the Future Cities Institute will be making appearances in the course throughout the term. A summary of each part of the course assignment is as follows:

Part 1: Annotated Bibliography - Annotated bibliographies are a method through which to summarize and critically appraise the quality of an evidence source with respect to the nature of the source, its reliability and validity (i.e., quality, limitations), and main findings. You will be responsible for conducting a review of 10 potential* papers (please make use of UW Libraries if you need help finding papers, resources) that relate to your group’s topic (*i.e., all of these do not need to be used in your group’s final report, these articles may be considered in your group’s final report). Again, you will be completing your own bibliography, you are not submitting one bibliography on behalf of your group. This is an individual assignment that you will be completing to aid your contribution to the final group report, and which will be marked based on your analytical and communication abilities. As such, each group member’s bibliography should be unique (i.e., you should not submit and appraise the same articles) and based on their own efforts and potential role within the group project. Each annotated article should be cited APA (6th or 7th edition) at the start of the appraisal and be around 150-200 words for the analysis. The final annotated bibliography should use APA referencing, be single spaced, and present unique appraisals of each article (you are not to do appraisals with your group members). You will be submitting your annotated bibliography on LEARN.

Part 2: **Fact Sheet/Inforgraphic and Issue Summary - Based on your own literature search and annotated bibliography (critical assessment of peer-reviewed academic journal articles, agency or governments reports) that you completed in the first part of this assignment, you will put together a fact sheet/infographic about this information. The fact sheet/infographic (Due March 26th submitted on LEARN) will be a one-page document on your topic that will use information from your annotated bibliography to: i) describe the scope of the issue your group is addressing and any relevant background details the general public should know to understand this issue, ii) present an outline of the epidemiology (incidence, distribution) of the relevant health issue(s), iii) provide context about the availability (or lack thereof) of community resources and/or policies to address your group’s issue with respect to the local area, iv) and very briefly offer a point or two about the future of your issue. The design is completely up to you! Remember you are communicating a complex topic to a general audience, so you will want to find a balance between the depth and accessibility of information presented. Use bullets, graphs, and images to effectively communicate your message. Be creative! 

Second, you will be providing a summary of your group project issue, as you have learned about it on your own through the annotated bibliography and fact sheet/infographic. The summary should be an extension of the fact sheet/infographic where you provide additional context for the document and cite your sources. The summary should be structured according to the four points above (i.e., follows the same outline of material) and should be about 2 pages single spaced (not including references). Please use APA reference (6th or 7th edition) style. The purpose of this exercise is to familiarize students with having to analyze and communicate complex environment and health topics to general (i.e., non-expert) audiences that in contemporary society often do not read policy reports but rather engage fact sheets, infographics, executive summaries, and other forms of high-level communications.

*Dimensions/specifics: We will be using examples from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). The fact sheet/infographic should be 8.5” x 11” (i.e., the regular or default sheet size) and have a resolution of at least 300 dpi. Examples of fact sheets/infographics can be found on the CDC website (Spotlight Infographic section) or via these links to infographics about heart disease, adolescent health, and health status and determinants. Please be sure to include your name and student ID number on the fact sheet/infographic. You may use whichever software you would like, just make sure your final output follows the above instructions. You will be submitting your fact sheet/infographic and summary documents on LEARN.

Part 3: Final Assignment Report - The final assignment will be completed in groups of about 4-5 students from different programs, and that you will be assigned to in mid-January (closer to the term’s add/drop deadline). As a group, you will work together on your assignment throughout the term (there will be time made available across some of the Wednesday seminars to meet, but you will have to work together outside class time) and create a group contract that will outline the expected duties and contributions of all members. Given the individual milestones, you will want to identify a group topic by early February to give yourself time to work on the annotated bibliography and fact sheet assignments. Should a member of your group fail to meet their required duties (as per the group contract), they may be removed by the group. In the event of a removal, this student will be required to complete the same assignment on their own, as well as give an individual presentation to the course instructor and TA in April (please see Assignment Instructions for more information). Ultimately, your group will put together a 10–13-page (double spaced, APA 6th or 7th edition) report that connects your group topic with one of the municipalities or townships in the Region of Waterloo (you may may choose Cambridge, Kitchener, Waterloo, North Dumfries, Wellesley, Wilmot, Woolwich, or The Region of Waterloo). Additional assignment instructions can be found on LEARN. Final reports will be submitted on LEARN. 

Part 4: Final Assignment Presentation – After completing the written portion of the assignment, members of your group will also complete a presentation of your final assignment that will account for 5% of final grade. The final assignment presentation will be 3 minutes long, contain 6 slides (details are on LEARN), and will be presented by as few as two members or all group members (this is up to the group). All presentations will take place on the final Wednesday of the term (April 2nd). Slides will need to be submitted to LEARN on April 1 by 3:00pm. You will be presenting the findings of your final reports to a guest panel of Future Cities Representatives.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as analyzing environment-health relationships while considering relevant theory, methods, and practical approaches. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

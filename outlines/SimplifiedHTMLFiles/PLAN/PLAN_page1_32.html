Course Description

PLAN 405:

This capstone course provides students with an opportunity to integrate their planning knowledge and skill in an applied context. Students will apply and integrate methodological, theoretical, analytic, and substantive planning knowledge from previous years in the program to complete a planning project for a professional client. Working in groups, students will develop, implement, and present their applied projects to clients.

View requirements for PLAN 405

To be effective practitioners, planners must master the fundamentals of land use planning, planning law, urban design, and many other domain-relevant skills and knowledge areas.  It is also important to be able to integrate and apply these diverse skills and knowledge bases, in concert with team members, to project-based planning initiatives.  

This course meets this need to help you develop capacity to design and manage a project for a client.  Plan 405 features real-world clients that span the private, public and non-governmental sectors who have identified projects for which they need advice, analysis, and/or applied research.  Your focus in this course is to act as professional planning consultants to your assigned client.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Identify, explain, and address the main challenges and opportunities for planning consultants when competing for and completing planning projects for clients.
Prepare a project proposal in response to a Request for Proposals (RFP) from a prospective public, private or non-governmental sector client.
Successfully apply project management, integrated problem solving, technical, and communication skills to deliver professional quality project deliverables and associated presentations to a client.

As a whole, this course addresses the OPPI competencies of Professional and Ethical Behaviour, Communication, Interpersonal Skills, and Critical Thinking.  Individual projects vary by student group and will address a range of additional OPPI competencies (https://ontarioplanners.ca/become-an-rpp/competency-tree).

Tentative Course Schedule

  1. During Week 1 of the course (Thursday, January 9), all students and the course instructor will meet together in our classroom (RCH 112).  Students will be introduced to the course structure, roles, and expectations.
     
  2. During Week 2 of the course (Thursday, January 16), student teams will receive a Request for Proposal (RFP) for a planning project provided by a real-world client. Two student consulting firm teams will work independently on each RFP. 
     
  3. By no later than 4:00 pm EST on Thursday, January 23, consulting teams will submit Project Proposals to their client for evaluation and feedback.  The Proposal must be in standard industry format.  Sample proposals will be provided on Learn.

    Note:  Do not reference Faculty Mentors in your Proposals as they are not part of your consulting teams.
     
  4. Faculty Mentors will be introduced to consulting teams on or before February 3.  Normally, a Faculty Mentor can assist a maximum of two teams.  Teams are not to contact Faculty members themselves to recruit them as a Faculty Mentor.  
     
  5. During the week of January 27 to 31 (subject to client availability), each consulting team can expect to receive feedback on their Proposal from their client.  The feedback may include revisions the client wants to made to the Proposal before the consultant team can begin work on the project.

    Note: Feedback may be received during a meeting that may take place via phone, Skype, Zoom or Teams.  Feedback can also be provided by email.  
     
  6. Each consulting team must revise their Proposal according to the feedback from the client. The revised Proposal must be submitted to their client (copy to Instructor) within one week of receiving the feedback (week of February 3 to 7).  No consulting team can begin work before revising their Proposal.  Once revisions are completed, consulting teams will work independently on their projects.

    Note: the client may share required background information with both teams.
     
  7. Consultant teams will need to arrange a Mid-Project meeting (online) with the client.  The meeting should occur during the week of March 3 to 7, subject to their client's availability.  This meeting is typically used as a point to:
    1. receive client feedback on work submitted to date,
    2. consider work progress and team performance relative to expectations set out in the Project Workplan, and
    3. confirm or revise the balance of the Workplan including Final Deliverables and associated timing, as necessary.

      Note: Please advise your TA and instructor of the date and time of the Mid-Project meeting and invite them to the online meeting.
       
  8. Mid-Project Presentations will be held in the week of March 3 to 7 via Skype, Zoom or Teams.  Each team will select one or several members of their team to provided a 10-minute narrated PowerPoint presentation that outlines the status of their team's project work.  A rubric detailing expected content will be posted on Learn.  The presentation must be deposited in the Mid-Project dropbox on Learn before it is presented to your client.
     
  9. Consultant teams must invite their clients to attend the Final Project Presentations and confirm client attendance with the Instructor and your team's TA by March 13.
     
  10. Final Project Presentations will take place on April 2, 3, and 4 via Skype, Zoom or Teams.  Consultant teams must ensure their client is available, as well as the Instructor and your TA.

    Note: Please advise your TA and the Instructor of the date and time for the Final Project Presentation meeting and invite them, and your Faculty Mentor, to the online meeting.
     
  11. The Final Report and associated deliverables must be submitted to the client and deposited in the Final Report dropbox by 4:00 pm EST on Thursday, April 17.
     
  12. Peer Evaluations must be completed and submitted to the Peer Evaluation dropbox by Thursday, April 17 by 7:00 pm.

Expectations:

  • Students will work in assigned consulting teams to complete a project in response to a Request for Proposal (RFP) from a public or private sector client.
  • Projects will meet the School of Planning's academic standards and the University's protocols.
  • All written and oral communications with clients and all project deliverables, as defined in the RFP, will be of professional quality.
  • Written work must be succinct, clear, error free and organized in a logical manner.  Conclusions and/or recommendations must be specific and evidence-based.
  • During the second week of class, teams will submit signed agreements that will allow the School of Planning to use final deliverables on the School's web site.

Roles:

  1. The instructor and the TAs will monitor team progress and provide guidance, content and support as appropriate.
  2. Students will select a Project Manager and a Deputy Project Manager as collaboratively agree on how tasks are assigned within the team consistent with industry standards.
  3. Clients are typically open to answering questions from consultant teams about the RFP before Project Proposals are submitted.  Competing teams with the same client should work together to prepare a short list of questions and arrange one phone or video call to confer with the client.  The call should be a maximum of 30 minutes in length unless the client says otherwise.
  4. Clients will provide feedback to the consultant teams.  The nature and frequency of feedback will be specified in the RFP and may be refined in discussions between the client and the consultant team.
  5. The RFP will set out the client's expectations with regard to communication from the consultant teams. Aside from meetings where the whole team is involved and unless the client indicates otherwise, communication with the client will be through the team's Project Manager.

 

Peer evaluation:

Completing peer evaluations is a required element of the course. In recognition of this, you will receive 2% of your final grade for submitting a complete and thoughtful peer evaluation on time.  

Each student is expected to contribute equally to all phases of their team's work throughout the term.  The peer evaluations will be used to assess individuals' contributions to team productivity, collegiality, and professionalism.  When appropriate, individual student grades will be adjusted to reflect team members' peer evaluations.  Peer evaluations will be completed using a form that will be supplied on the course Learn site and submitted to the Peer Evaluation dropbox.  Completing the entire form is mandatory including providing a well-written section in the open text field.

Note:  Incomplete peer evaluations will result in a grade of INC for the course for any student who has not completed this requirement by the due date.

 

Grading:

  • Rubrics for each component will be provided on Learn.
  • All of the course components cited above, including PowerPoint files, reports and analytical outputs required from a team's RFP, must be submitted to the appropriate dropbox on Learn.

 

Submissions:

  • Clients may require teams to submit project deliverables in digital and/or in hard copy format.  Refer to the RFP and any communication you have with your client.
  • All work is expected to be of professional quality in terms of research, writing, content, format, and graphics.
  • All assignments are due by 4:00 pm EST on the specified due date.
  • Proposals and Final Project deliverables are to submitted to the designated Learn dropbox, in addition to providing them by email to clients and Faculty Mentors.

Note:  Late work will be subject to a 5% per day penalty.  Requests for extension must be sent to the Instructor at least 48 hours before the applicable deadline.

Intellectual Property: For further information on IP related to teaching, please see https://uwaterloo.ca/legal-and-immigration-services/sites/ca.legal-and-immigration-services/files/uploads/files/volume_1_issue_3_winter_2018.pdf and the Guidelines for Faculty, Staff and Students Entering Relationships with External Organizations Offering Access to Course Materials, https://uwaterloo.ca/secretariat/faculty-staff-and-students-entering-relationships-external. The following text is recommended:

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo.  Intellectual property includes items such as:

-Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
-Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
-Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
-Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years. In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online. The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as [fill this in with your discipline-specific skills]. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Co-op interviews and class attendance: Co-op students are encouraged to try and choose interview time slots that result in the least amount of disruption to class schedules. When this is challenging, or not possible, a student may miss a portion of a class meeting for an interview. Instructors are asked for leniency in these situations; but, a co-op interview does not relieve the student of any requirements associated with that class meeting.

When a co-op interview conflicts with an in-class evaluation mechanism (e.g., test, quiz, presentation, critique), class attendance takes precedence and the onus is on the student to reschedule the interview. CEE provides an interview conflict procedure to manage these situations.

Students will be required to provide copies of their interview schedules (they may be printed from WaterlooWorks) should there be a need to verify class absence due to co-op interviews. 

Research Ethics: The University of Waterloo requires all research conducted by its students, staff, and faculty which involves humans as participants to undergo prior ethics review and clearance through the Director, Office of Human Research and Animal Care (Office). The ethics review and clearance processes are intended to ensure that projects comply with the Office’s Guidelines for Research with Human Participants (Guidelines) as well as those of provincial and federal agencies, and that the safety, rights and welfare of participants are adequately protected. The Guidelines inform researchers about ethical issues and procedures which are of concern when conducting research with humans (e.g. confidentiality, risks and benefits, informed consent process, etc.). If the development of your research proposal consists of research that involves humans as participants, the please contact the course instructor for guidance and see: https://uwaterloo.ca/research/office-research-ethics

University Policy

Course Description

PLAN 403:

Professional practice responsibility and ethics, administrative methods and organization are considered in the context of the nature of organizations, politics, and economics that provide opportunities and limitations. Perspectives are drawn from organizational theory, public administration, land economics, political and planning theory. Public sector decision making and policy development are discussed. This course may have a field component.

View requirements for PLAN 403

Introduction

This course will focus on specific areas of professional planning practice through lectures, guest speakers and in-class discussions. There will be a strong emphasis on planning practice in today’s policy and political climate, drawing on real life contexts, challenges, situations and events. The Instructor has worked in the public, private and not-for-profit sectors over the last 25+ years and has led a municipal planning department and also been a partner and senior practitioner in private practice. The course will be focused on skills and knowledge needed in professional practice for a newly graduated planner.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Actively listen to peers, instructor, and guest lecturers;
Engage in respectful and thoughtful dialogue with peers, instructor, and guest lecturers;
Learn how to communicate in planning concepts effectively and present information (written and oral) in a professional and engaging manner to peers and superiors;
Understand the role of the professional planner at the municipal level, including their work to advance municipal policy, development approvals, decision-making processes, diversity, and ethics;
Understand the role of the public and how to advance their interests in the planning processes;
Understand the practical application of planning theory in a variety of situations;
Develop a facility to write, understand and interpret planning policy and critically assess planning regulation;
Develop skills and understanding necessary to collaborate with practitioners in other disciplines toward the achievement of successful outcomes; and
Understand the inter-relationships between planning policy and theory, and policies and practices of other relevant disciplines.

Learning Modes

The classroom will be the primary learning venue for the course with the written assignments building on the in-class lectures and discussions.

There are two written assignments required in the course. Each will be provided at minimum two weeks in advance of the due date. The first is an individual assignment and the second will be assigned to groups. There will be group presentations for the second assignment. There will also be a series of in-class assignments, both individual and group, to be completed in-class.

This course builds heavily from the class lectures and discussion with the instructor and guest lecturers. Lectures will be presented primarily from a practitioner’s perspective and may be unique from previous academic learnings. The rate at which new information, concepts and perspectives are presented will happen faster than previously experienced in other courses. Therefore, it is essential that students keep up-to-date and attend all classes and participate in the class discussion to clarify their understanding.

For the assignments to be true learning experiences where students build relevant and applicable planning skills, the assignments have been developed to parallel “real life” issues and scenarios and will be challenging in thought and time. It will not be possible to do the assignments adequately at the last minute, or draw heavily from work from previous courses.

Computer work is part of the assignments. Students are expected to have already developed appropriate skills in Microsoft Word. Instruction and consulting regarding the use of these computer skills is not provided in this course.

EXAMINATIONS AND MAJOR PROJECTS

Mid-term and Final Examinations:

There will be no mid-term or final examination in this course.

Assignment #1 (Individual) – Preparation of an Urban Design Brief:

Students will be provided with an Urban Design Brief Guideline for a hypothetical municipality, and will be asked to prepare an Urban Design Brief to support a hypothetical Site Plan Application for an existing building on the University’s campus. The exercise will presume that the building has not yet been built, and is still a development proposal, and the Urban Design Brief will be part of the development application package. The brief will be a minimum of 500 words and a maximum of 750 words and will draw upon lessons learned about Planning for Design Excellence in Week 3. The assignment is due February 3, 2025, at 4 pm, and is to be submitted digitally. An overview of the assignment and the Urban Design Brief Guidelines will be presented during the January 20 class.

Assignment #2 (Group) – Writing Planning Policy for Collaborative City-Building:

Students will work in twelve groups of eight to prepare a policy document for the downtown of a mid-sized Ontario city that addresses each of the five themes explored in the guest lectures:

  • Planning for Design Excellence
  • Planning for Joy
  • Planning for Urban Greenspace
  • Planning for Decarbonization
  • Planning for Sustainable Mobility

For the written assignment, students will prepare a chapter of a planning document for each of the five themes that includes:

  • Goal / Vision Statement (approx. 200 words)
  • Policy Objectives (5 to 10)
  • Planning Policies (5 to 10)

For each theme, students will also provide a 300 to 500 word explanation of their rationale, drawing upon the information and discussion from each of the guest lectures. The written assignment is due March 17, 2025, at 4 pm, and is to be submitted digitally.

For the group presentation, students will select one theme and provide a 15-minute presentation on their goals and vision statement, policy objectives and planning policies and how they believe their approach would address the theme. Presentations will take place March 17, 24, and 31 and will be 15 minutes followed by 10 minutes for Q+A. Students will be assigned a group presentation time and will be required to participate in the presentations by other students during that time slot. 

Part of the group marking will involve the evaluation of group members.

In-Class Assignments:

Small assignments will be completed in-class in Weeks 4, 6 and 8. Each assignment will be based on the day’s lecture and is expected to take approximately 30 minutes. Assignments must be submitted at the end of the class.

Generative AI

Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

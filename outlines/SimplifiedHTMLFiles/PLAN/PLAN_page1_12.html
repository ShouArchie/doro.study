Course Description

PLAN 233:

This course explores the origins and practices of social planning and its relationship to the field of community development. Students will learn how interventions in the built environment and urban policy-making can be used to advance community well-being and human flourishing. Students will focus on the relationship between planning practice and social outcomes, and the importance of considering social objectives alongside the economic and environmental priorities of sustainable development. Students will expand their skills in demographic analysis to assess community needs through mapping community assets, resources, amenities, and services that support the advancement of complete communities.

View requirements for PLAN 233

Planners are increasingly acknowledging imperatives towards improving social outcomes through planning (e.g., planning for social justice, equity, diversity, inclusion). Accordingly, this course introduces concepts of social planning and community development in the field and practice of planning. The first half of the course (Week 1-6) focuses on foundational aspects of social planning and community development, that is, the constructs and concepts that bridge the intersection between the purpose and implications of planning to people and place. The second half of the course (Week 7-12) delves in depth on selected topics that are applicable to social planning and community development by critically alluding to the prospective planner as an actor in this work.

This course incorporates theoretical and applied learning opportunities that involve critical thinking about the context of planning and planners in social planning and community development. Course assignments will involve the development of technical skills such as the assessment and interpretation of community and demographic needs, to interpersonal skill development as related to the attentiveness of communication and language in planning deliverables. Comprehension of course material (i.e., course concepts and required readings) will be assessed in the Midterm Exam. Students are required to attend weekly tutorials, as a portion of the overall grade, where tutorials will provide an opportunity for further application of course material to learning and assignments. Weekly required readings must be read prior to lecture and tutorial. Additionally, guest speakers may attend lectures throughout the term. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Explain how social planning and community development are applicable to the practice of planners;
Connect social outcomes in communities to planning and the urban environment;
Assess the needs of communities through the application of social constructs and concepts in planning;
Demonstrate critical thinking abilities and effective communication skills

Student Assessment

Component Value
Midterm Exam 40%
Assignment 1 25%
Assignment 2 25%
Tutorial Participation 10%

Midterm Exam: Students will be writing the Midterm Exam in-person, at the University of Waterloo campus. Accordingly, students will be required to be present on the Midterm Exam date and location.

Assignment 1: Students will work in small groups, as determined in Tutorial 1, to assess community and demographic needs as the broad purpose of the assignment. The deliverable of this assignment will be a written report; details are provided in the Assignment 1 Guidelines posted on LEARN. Students will upload their submission to the LEARN dropbox. Late penalties will apply (see below).

Assignment 2: Students will work independently to produce a deliverable that applies course learnings with individual reflection to planning. The details of this assignment and its deliverables are provided in the Assignment 2 Guidelines posted on LEARN. Students will upload their submission to the LEARN dropbox. Late penalties will apply (see below).

Tutorial Participation: Students will be required to attend and actively participate in weekly tutorials, and will be assessed accordingly.

Course Policies for Late Submissions: Late submissions will have percentage deductions applied to the original grade for the assignment. Each late day (defined as a Calendar Day-including weekends) will receive a 5% grade deduction. No submissions will be accepted after 4 late Calendar Days, where a grade of zero will apply after the fourth (4th) late day. Students are responsible to seek accommodation and/or discuss concerns with submission, with the Course Instructor at least one week in advance before the deadline.

Generative AI
Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

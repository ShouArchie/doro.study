Course Description

PLAN 203:

This course explores transportation planning in the broader context of human mobility, including the history of transportation systems (passenger and freight), transportation planning tools and techniques, transportation demand management, and transportation system sustainability. Students will learn about the factors shaping transportation systems over time, the connections between transportation systems and broader societal trends, and the specific techniques used in transportation planning. Students will develop skills in how to evaluate the utility of different transportation modes, examine system performance from different perspectives, and conduct demand forecasting and analysis. The course helps students deepen their understanding of the complex interplay among land use, transport systems, and equity explored in prior courses.

View requirements for PLAN 203

Hello and welcome to transportation planning and mobility! Over the term, we will dive into understanding the planning that’s behind how we get around. The fact is that everyone relies on our transportation services and infrastructure. From day-to-day travel (like getting to school and work) to the often international journeys of our food, clothes, and online purchases to get to us- what we can do and when all comes down to the availability and quality of the transportation system. 

So, is it too bold to say that transportation systems define cities? I hope by the end of the course you will agree with me that it is not. The transportation system determines our access to the world around us and the form and function of our communities. It is a critical facet of urban planning that is intertwined with what makes sustainable, resilient, equitable cities. Although we will discuss the broad impact of all transportation, we will predominantly focus our attention on regional and municipal transport, with a fundamentally multi-modal approach that focuses on walking, cycling, transiting, and driving. 

We’ve got a lot to cover over the next twelve weeks. To help you with the flow of this information, I’ve organized the course into four blocks, as follows: 

  • Block 1: why talk about transportation planning/mobility?
  • Block 2: what is transportation planning?
  • Block 3: how we plan for transportation today
  • Block 4: futures of transportation planning

Through these four blocks, we will go over the history of transportation and how our current autocentric transportation systems came to be, followed by defining the scope and goals of transportation planning, including the fundamentals of network and demand planning. We will then get into the practicalities of ‘doing’ transportation planning, with a focus on the current political framework we plan within and the tools and techniques deployed by practicing transportation planners. Finally, we will end with an eye to the future- looking at where we can go from here. 

At the end of the course, it is my goal that you walk away “seeing the system”, and the ways transportation planning impacts your daily lives and is intertwined to every other subset of urban planning. After all, transportation planning sits at an interesting intersection, sharing its field with engineers and urban designers alike! We will explore the ‘hard’ sides of transportation planning along with the ‘soft’ and not shy away from calling out where transportation planning has historically fallen short and the opportunities for the discipline to continuously improve. By the end of the course, I hope you can trace the many socio-political outcomes of how we plan for transportation and know the importance of understanding mobility and accessibility- for all urban planners + designers. 

I’m so excited to dive into this course with all of you, so let’s buckle up (pun intended) and get into it! 

-SL

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Explain the historical and current context for transportation planning practices
Critically reflect on the intersections between land-use and transportation planning
Define, examine, and engage with the nuances of accessibility
Apply network planning fundamentals
Understand transportation planning policy structures and effectively utilize different policies and guidelines

Assignments (40% of total grade) 

There will be three assignments worth a total of 40%, each assignment is meant to have you apply key concepts and skills we learned in the lecture. More details on each assignment are included below, and specific assignment instructions will be circulated during the term. Assignments should be uploaded to a LEARN dropbox by the due date indicated on the course schedule.

  • Assignment 1: Multi-Modal Accessibility Analysis (15%): In this assignment students will apply skills on how to define access across different modes, calculating accessibility for different modes based on distance, availability of infrastructure, quality of infrastructure, and personal experiences and perceptions.
  • Assignment 2: Transportation Policy Review (5%): In this assignment, students will apply learning on the various hierarchies and types of policy/planning tools that define and inform how transportation infrastructure comes to be. In this assessment, students should learn the differences between different scales + types of planning policy, recommendations, and guidelines.
  • Assignment 3: Mini-Active Transportation Master Plan, Group Assessment (20%) In groups of 4-5, students will conduct their own mini-active transportation master plan, building off the policy review conducted in Assignment 2. In this assessment, students will plan + justify a new route, option and choose appropriate infrastructure, cost/phase the project, and identify applicable stakeholders and potential concerns and conflicts with the development of their new infrastructure.

Quizzes/Exams (40% of total grade)

At the end of each block, we will hold a ‘20-minute’ quiz (comprised of multiple-choice, true/false, and fill-in-the-blank questions). These quizzes are practice for the types of questions you may see on the final exam, and are meant as ‘check-ins’ for your overall understanding of course material. Each quiz is worth 5%, and there will be a quiz for Blocks 1, 2, and 3 (totalling 15%). The quizzes will be completed in the first 20-minutes of class, as indicated on the course schedule.  

At the end of the course, we will hold a final exam (worth 25%). This exam will include multiple choice, true/false, and fill-in-the-blank questions along with short and long answer questions. The exam will be two hours long and run during the exam period. Once the exam is scheduled it will be posted on LEARN. 

Participation & reflection activities (20% of total grade)

As part of this course, my goal is for you to not only understand the material but also apply it to your own life and reflect on what transportation & mobility planning means to you. To encourage this, 20% of your overall grade can be achieved in participation & reflection activities- of which there are two types:

  • Tutorial attendance and reflection statements (10%): Attendance will be taken in tutorials and some weeks you will be required to write a short (approx. 200-400 words) statement on the week's subject matter. These reflection statements will be submitted to a LEARN dropbox by 11:59pm on the day of the tutorial.  You do not need to include formal citations in your reflection statements, however they are welcome. This statement should be reflective, meaning you write your perspective and opinion. We will cover some basics of reflective writing practice in the lecture/tutorial, but some helpful links are included below.
    • https://www.rhulisc.com/blog/how-to-write-a-reflection-paper
    • https://libguides.cam.ac.uk/reflectivepracticetoolkit/reflectivewriting
  • Travel journal (10%): Starting on the second week of class, I’d like students to start keeping a travel journal documenting their experiences getting to campus for PLAN 203. You can discuss details like timing, weather, traffic, interesting things you saw during your commute, and your overall feeling for your commute that week. During tutorials on March 26, we will have an opportunity to reflect on your travel journal entries from the past 10 weeks and your experiences commuting from home to campus. I’m adopting an open format to these travel journals- meaning you can be as creative as you like in how the entries look! They can be a combination of writing, drawing, mapping, or more. Simple bullet points in a Word document will also be accepted, of course. We will chat more about travel journal expectations in the first week of class. Just as with the other assignments, the travel journals should be submitted to a LEARN dropbox by the due date indicated in the course schedule.

Late assignments will be penalized by 5% points per 24-hour period following the due date and time, including each weekend day up to one week after the original submission deadline. After that time, the assignment will receive a zero unless alternative accommodations are in place.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

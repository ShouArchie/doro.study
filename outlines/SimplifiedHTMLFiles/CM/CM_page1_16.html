Course Description

CM 763 / STAT 441 / STAT 841:

Classification is the problem of predicting a discrete outcome from a set of explanatory variables. Main topics include logistic regression, neural networks, tree-based methods, support vector machines, and nearest neighbour methods. Other topics include model assessment, training, and tuning.

STAT 441: View requirements for STAT 441

STAT 841: View requirements for STAT 841

CM 763: View requirements for CM 763

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe the basic principles and methods of statistical classification.
Know when to use each of these methods on a given dataset.
Use software to perform statistical classification.
Evaluate and compare the output of various classifiers.
Apply programming practices aiming for reproducibility, efficiency and sharability of computer code.
Communicate the methodology, results and limitations of a classification analysis.

Student Assessment

Component Value
Assignments 20%
Midterms 30%
Final Exam 50%

Assignments (20%)

There will be three assignments worth 6.66% each to be turned in via Crowdmark (and LEARN; see Software section below).

The lateness penalty will be 25% per day until the solutions are posted.  Once the solutions are posted, the assignment will not be graded.

If you submit your assignment to Crowdmark on time, but you do so incorrectly in any way (e.g., you upload your Question 3 solution in the Question 1 box), you will receive a 5% deduction (i.e., 5% of the assignment’s point total will be deducted from your point total).

You may work on assignments in groups, and you may use any external resources. However, you must include the names of all collaborators and cite all external sources. Moreover, each individual’s assignment must differ substantively from others’ work. Failure to follow these instructions will be treated as an Academic Integrity violation (see below).

If you fail to submit an assignment by the due date and have a valid reason with supporting documentation (see Missed Assessments section below), the weight from that assignment will be shifted to the final exam. Note that a valid reason requires for you to have been incapacitated for a majority of the timeframe between the assignment's release date and due date.  In the case of a 2-day self-declared absence (see below), a 2-day extension will be provided.

Midterms (30%)

There will be two closed-book midterms worth 15% each on the following dates:

  • Midterm 1: Thursday February 6
  • Midterm 2: Tuesday March 18

The midterms will be during class time (10-11:30am) in STC 1012.

If a test is missed due to a valid reason with supporting documentation, your test score will be imputed from your score on the final exam as follows.  Whatever quantile of the final grade distribution you receive, your score for the test will be the same quantile on the test score distribution.  So for example, if your score is the 75% quantile on the final exam, then your test score will be the 75% quantile of the test score distribution.  Relative to simply shifting a missed test's weight to the final exam, this ensures that students who miss a test are not unfairly advantaged or disadvantaged if the final exam is much easier or harder than the missed test.

There are no make-up midterms in this course.

Final Exam (50%)

There will be a 2.5-hour, closed book, cumulative final exam during the Winter 2025 Final Examination Period: April 9 – April 25. Please refrain from booking end-of-term travel before the actual exam date is scheduled.

If the final exam is missed for a valid reason with supporting documentation, an Incomplete (INC) grade will be granted if:

  1. Either both midterms or one midterm and two assignments have been completed.
  2. The student's average grade on the completed assessments is at least 50%.

Otherwise, a missed final exam will result in an grade of "Did Not Write" (DNW), which counts as a final grade of 32 in transcript averages.

There is no make-up final exam in this course.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assessment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights.  For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.  In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online. The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

Course Description

CM 762 / STAT 442 / STAT 842:

Visualization methods applied to data. Both human perception and statistical properties inform the methods used to display and visually explore categorical, continuous, time-ordered, map, and high dimensional data. Order and layout effects on tables and graphics. Statistical concepts visually presented include variability, densities, quantiles, conditioning, and hypothesis testing. Interactive graphics include linking, brushing, motion, and the navigation of high dimensional spaces guided via projection indices. Glyphs (e.g., cartoon, statistical, or heatmap) and radial and parallel coordinates.

STAT 442: View requirements for STAT 442

CM 762: View requirements for CM 762

STAT 842: View requirements for STAT 842

Aside from the one midterm, this class is entirely hybrid. The lectures and the tutorials will be recorded over Zoom and posted on the course Learn page.

Manim, a Python library that makes math animations, will be taught over the tutorials starting in Week 3. Starting in Assignment 2, there will be Manim-specific questions in the assignments and midterm that grad students (STAT 842, CM 762) are expected to answer. STAT 442 students are welcome to come to tutorials, but cannot receive marks for Manim-specific questions.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Interpret and critique the most commonly used kinds of visualizations today.
Create a variety of univariate, bivariate, and multivariate graphs using base R and the tidyverse.
Look up the necessary code to complete additional graphs not covered here.
Compose attractive and easy to understand data tables.
Create rudimentary animations and interactive data visualizations.
Compose effective data presentations, posters, and infographics.
(Optional for 442s) Create basic animations using the Manim library
(Deprecated) Conduct exploratory analyses on geographic data.

Tentative Course Schedule

  • Week 1 Jan 6 - 10: Introduction, perception. (Ch. 6 Cool Infographics, UN Design)
  • Week 2 Jan 13 - 17: Graphics fundamentals - raster vs. vector, colour, encoding.
  • Week 3 Jan 20 - 24: Plot fundamentals - axes, scales. Painter vs grammar, tidyverse, ggplot
  • Week 4 Jan 27 - 31: GGplot generals, aesthetics, layers, facets, geometries
  • Week 5 Feb 3 - 7: Networks and Sankey plots 
  • Week 6 Feb 10 - 14: Tables, kable, grammar of tables
  • Reading week: watch some Jon Bois and 3Blue1Brown on youtube
  • Week 7 Feb 24 - 28: Univariate visuals, histograms, KDEs, Boxplots, QQplots
  • Week 8 Mar 3 - 7: Time series, 2D graphs
  • Week 9 Mar 10 - 14: Fractals and other generative art, and geographic maps if time permits.
  • Week 10 Mar 17 - 21: Posters, infographics, and composition
  • Week 11 Mar 24 - 28: 3D and higher dimensional data.
  • Week 12 Mar 31 - Apr 4: Interactivity with Shiny

Administrative Policy

  1. Remark Requests: You may appeal the way an assignment/midterm was marked by submitting it to me mj2davis@uwaterloo.ca within 1 week of it being returned. Your email must include a completed Remark Request Form. Please find the form and full details of the process posted on LEARN.
  2. Late Penalties: A late penalty of 20% per day late, up to a maximum of three days will be applied to assignments for late submissions to Crowdmark. Please do not email us if you have technical issues that cause your submission to be a few minutes late. No waiving of this penalty will occur for technical issues. Instead, we encourage you to plan in advance and submit your assignments earlier than the moment of the deadline.
  3. Illness: If you are ill, you must submit a VIF indicating severe illness. (If you are self-isolating due to flu-like or COVID-19 symptoms, you can instead complete the self-declaration form in Quest). Details can be found here: https://uwaterloo.ca/math/accommodations
    • If you are ill for a midterm, you must provide satisfactory documentation within 48 hours by emailing an electronic copy of the VIF to the Instructional Support Coordinator (ISC) as well as submitting to the MUO (or email the ISC in addition to completing the self-declaration). For satisfactory documentation, the weight of the test will be shifted to the final exam.
    • If you are ill for the final exam, you will receive an INC grade and be required to complete the exam during the next course offering, if you are achieving a passing grade going into the exam. Further, you must have written at least one of the two midterms to receive an INC. If not, a failing grade of DNW will be assigned.
  4. If at any time you are feeling sick, or have been directed by public health to self-isolate, you should not be attending class. We encourage you to get the lecture notes from a classmate or from a recording on Learn, but if needed please email your instructor directly and we will determine an appropriate accommodation for the situation.

Personal Mental Health Support Message

You're not alone. Lots of people look like they have it together in public and online when they're a mess inside too. You're not the only student facing what you're facing, and it really is okay to seek out help from the above sources if you need.

Getting help is not an admission of failure. For the same reason that you cannot walk off a broken leg or a heart attack. It's just a thing that some people need to do.

It probably gets better. Obviously, nobody can guarantee that, but it is extremely likely to get better. More specifically, you get stronger and more capable at dealing with the problems in your life.

You are not your grades. Grades are not a reflection of your worth as a human being. They got you into university, so they've already done most of the work, but at this point, the most they can really do for you decide if you can get into graduate school before doing industry experience. (And if you're in the graduate version of this class, then congrats! You barely need grades at all now.)

This too will pass. Some people will say that being a student was the best time of their life. Don't let that be your reason for thinking this is as good as it gets. Everyone's life path is different.

Every day's 'best' is a little different. If you could do your theoretical best every day, then it wouldn't be your best it would be your average.

Others are not yardsticks. The takeaway message is NOT 'if they can become a prof with a mental illness, then I should be able to as well.', please don't use others as a point of comparison like that.

Take care of yourself when you can, don't blame yourself when you can't.

The world is better with you in it.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

SOC 710:

Applied multiple regression/correlation, with emphasis on data processing/computing, model construction and interpretation and underlying statistical assumptions.

View requirements for SOC 710

This course builds on the statistics background that students are expected to have from their undergraduate degree. It will begin with a basic review of data handling skills, univariate analysis, and bivariate analysis. From there, standard multivariate analysis techniques such as linear regression, logistic regression, and factor analysis will be covered. The course will finish with a focus on more advanced techniques such as Poisson-family regression models, methods for handling cluster sampling (e.g., multilevel models), and interaction effects. Principles of open science and causality will be discussed. This will allow students to address most quantitative research questions. However, each one of these techniques could have a whole course or more devoted strictly to that method. The goal here is to give students some exposure to all of these methods so that they will know the basics and can do the necessary reading and practice required to become proficient with any of the techniques presented in this course should their particular research interests necessitate it.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Recognise the conditions under which a particular statistical test is appropriate.
Correctly run a variety of univariate, bivariate, and multivariate statistical tests to answer a given research question.
Assess the trustworthiness of analysis results.
Correctly interpret and describe the results that are produced.
Evaluate the validity of the quantitative approaches taken in published research.
Research in greater detail the intricacies of statistical methods to acquire the depth of knowledge needed to be able to conduct defensible analyses using various statistical techniques given the fundamentals taught in this course.

Recommended readings

  • Tabachnick, B. G., & Fidell, L. S. (2013). Using multivariate statistics. (6th ed.). Boston: Pearson.
    -this is one of the most highly cited stats texts there is and fairly understandable as stats books go. At last check, it had been cited over 145,000 times (google scholar).
  • Long, J. S. (2009). The workflow of data analysis using Stata. College Station, TX: Stata Press.
    -exceptionally good resource on organizing your analysis to make it easily replicable.
  • Hamilton, L.C. (2013). Statistics with Stata: Updated for Version 12. Boston: Brooks/Cole, Cengage Learning.
    -not heavy on the underlying concepts, but it is an excellent resource for practical applications using Stata.
  • Field, A. (2018). Discovering Statistics Using IBM SPSS Statistics, (5th ed.). London: SAGE Publications. 
    -highly understandable general stats text. Focuses on SPSS, but principles apply more broadly.
  • Cameron, A. C., & Trivedi, P. K. (2021). Microeconometrics using Stata. (2nd ed.). College Station, Texas: Stata Press.
    -good general stats reference focusing on applications with Stata.
  • Mitchell, M. N. (2021). Interpreting and Visualizing Regression Models Using Stata (2nd ed.). College Station, Texas: Stata Press.
    -useful for plotting interaction effects.
  • Kaufman, R. L. (2019). Interaction Effects in Linear and Generalized Linear Models. Advanced Quantitative Techniques in the Social Sciences. Thousand Oaks, CA: SAGE Publications Inc. 
    -useful for plotting interaction effects in non-linear models.
  • Nagin, D. S. (2005). Group-based modeling of development. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press.
    -key reference for group-based trajectory modeling.
  • Cleves, M., Gould, W., & Marchenko, Y. V. (2016). An introduction to survival analysis using Stata. (3rd ed.). College Station, Texas: Stata Press.
    -excellent resource for survival analysis.
  • Rabe-Hesketh, S., & Skrondal, A. (2021). Multilevel and longitudinal modeling using Stata. (4th ed.). College Station, Texas: Stata Press.
    -not an easy read, but useful for clustered and longitudinal data.
  • Acock, A. C. (2013). Discovering structural equation modeling using Stata. (Revised ed.) College Station, Texas: Stata Press.
    -excellent introduction to SEM using Stata.
  • Atkins, D. C., & Gallop, R. J. (2007). Rethinking how family researchers model infrequent outcomes: A tutorial on count regression and zero-inflated models. Journal of Family Psychology, 21(4), 726-735.
    -useful tutorial on Poisson and negative binomial models.
  • Cunningham, S. (2021). Causal Inference: The Mixtape. Yale University Press.
    -excellent resource for issues around causal inference (and how to generate causal estimates).

Assignments

Students must complete 3 assignments in this course, each worth 15% of their final grade. Each assignment is focused mostly on the material that comes in the weeks immediately preceding it, but since the skills build on each other, the assignments will include material from throughout the term.

Students are to work in pairs for the assignment. They will be randomly assigned to work with a different partner for each assignment. An odd number of students may result in a group of 3.

Instructions for the assignments will be posted to Learn on the day that the assignment period begins. Students will generally have five days to complete the assignment (see specific dates above). Late assignments will be penalized 5% per day. Assignments will not be accepted more than 7 days late. Submissions must be made via the Learn dropbox.

During assignment weeks, in-person class time can be used to work on the material though attendance is not mandatory during those times.

Research proposal

A short research proposal is due at a sufficiently early point in the term to ensure that students are working on their research paper well in advance of the due date. This is because it takes time and effort to familiarize yourself with a data set, to clean the data, and to conduct and refine a theoretically driven, statistically defensible quantative analysis.

The proposal should be a maximum of 2 pages (double spaced). It should include:
-a brief overview of the theoretical backdrop of the paper;
-the research questions/hypotheses driving the analysis;
-a description of the data you plan to analyse;
-a basic (preliminary) overview of the variables/scales that you will include in your analysis;
-an analysis plan (outline the expected bivariate and multivariate analyses).

I understand that things can change between the time you submit your proposal and the time you submit your final paper. That’s fine. As long as you’re working on it early enough, the proposal will have served its purpose of trying to get students to work out details and develop a plan as early as possible.

The proposal will be marked holistically. As in, I will not be providing a specific mark breakdown. Instead, I will be looking for evidence that an effort has been made to think through how the analysis will flow, what it will test, and how it will be executed. A well thought through plan to test a theoretically driven and clearly articulated research question/hypothesis will warrant a high grade.

** You must submit this via the Learn drop box by 11:59 pm on the due date. There is a 2-day grace period on this deadline. It is considered an extension that anyone can use without having to provide notification or documentation. However, you should do your best to submit by the posted deadline as no extensions will be granted beyond the 2-day grace period. At that point, a late penalty of 5% per day will be applied.

Research paper

Students are expected to produce an 12-15 page research paper on a topic of their choice (text only; title page, abstract, tables, references, and appendices are not counted towards this limit). See expanded instructions posted on Learn for more details.

Students are expected to find an appropriate data set to use to address their particular research question. Many can be found on the website for the Inter-university Consortium for Political and Social Research – ICPSR - https://www.icpsr.umich.edu.

The research papers are to take the form of a publishable journal article.

Useful resources for finding data:

-https://abacus.library.ubc.ca/dataverse/open – Publicly available versions of Statistics Canada data can be found here (e.g., General Social Survey; Canadian Tobacco and Nicotine Survey; Canadian Labour Force Survey)

-www.openicpsr.org – This is an open access data repository for researchers to post their data.

-https://toolbox.google.com/datasetsearch – this is a Google search engine for data.

Generally speaking, data that you can access publicly is acceptable (and does not require ethics clearance). Do not use data if you suspect that it has been collected or posted illegally or unethically or if you think that it does not meet scientific standards of rigour. Consult with me if you have any questions.

It is considered an extension that anyone can use without having to provide notification or documentation. However, you should do your best to submit by the posted deadline as no extensions will be granted beyond the 2-day grace period. At that point, a late penalty of 5% per day will be applied. Software

The statistics package Stata is used heavily in this course. There is a very good reason that I am choosing to teach using Stata despite the fact that many students have previous exposure to SPSS. It is quite simply the best stats package available (in my opinion). It can do more than SPSS and is easier to use than SAS, R, or Python. I suggest you embrace Stata. The learning curve is about the same as SPSS but it will allow you much greater sophistication in your analyses.

It is available in the PAS computer labs (PAS 1237, 1080, 1098). The Arts Computing Office (ACO) has also enabled it to be remotely accessed from any location for free. A separate document is posted on Learn to help you get it set up. If you run into any difficulties accessing Stata, please first go through the material on Learn and consult with the online guidance posted by the ACO (https://uwaterloo.ca/arts-computing/). If that doesn’t solve the problem, they can be contacted at acohelp@uwaterloo.ca.

Alternatively, if you want a copy of Stata on your own computer, you can get a six month rental for $48 US (Stata/BE – generally a good choice, but limited to ~2000 variables; if you think you will be working with data sets with more variables than that, consider Stata/SE). If you foresee the need to conduct quantitative analyses in your future, a perpetual license might be worth it. To purchase and download Stata, go to this address and follow the links: https://www.stata.com/order/dl/

Self-declared absences due to COVID-related illness

Students can self-declare an absence on Quest due to COVID-related illness or a requirement to self-isolate. A self-declared COVID absence covers up to 10 days and no documentation is required. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in on-line components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence.  It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Self-declared short-term absences for any reason

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason. A short-term absence covers two calendar days, and no documentation is required. The student has only one self-declared short-term absence per term and this absence can only be used during the formal lecture period. Self-declared absences cannot be applied to Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), or Studio (STU) course components.  Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

Discipline: A student is expected to know what constitutes academic integrity to avoid committing an academic offence, and to take responsibility for their actions. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.] A student who is unsure whether an action constitutes an offence, or who needs help in learning how to avoid offences (e.g., plagiarism, cheating) or about “rules” for group work/collaboration should seek guidance from the course instructor, academic advisor, or the undergraduate associate dean. For information on categories of offences and types of penalties, students should refer to Policy 71, Student Discipline. For typical penalties, check Guidelines for the Assessment of Penalties.

Appeals: A decision made or penalty imposed under Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances (other than a petition) or Policy 71, Student Discipline may be appealed if there is a ground. A student who believes they have a ground for an appeal should refer to Policy 72, Student Appeals.

Note for students with disabilities: AccessAbility Services, located in Needles Hall, Room 1401, collaborates with all academic departments to arrange appropriate accommodations for students with disabilities without compromising the academic integrity of the curriculum. If you require academic accommodations to lessen the impact of your disability, please register with AccessAbility Services at the beginning of each academic term.

Turnitin.com: Text matching software (Turnitin®) may be used to screen assignments in this course. Turnitin® is used to verify that all materials and sources in assignments are documented. Students' submissions are stored on a U.S. server, therefore students must be given an alternative (e.g., scaffolded assignment or annotated bibliography), if they are concerned about their privacy and/or security. Students will be given due notice, in the first week of the term and/or at the time assignment details are provided, about arrangements and alternatives for the use of Turnitin in this course.

It is the responsibility of the student to notify the instructor if they, in the first week of term or at the time assignment details are provided, wish to submit alternate assignment.

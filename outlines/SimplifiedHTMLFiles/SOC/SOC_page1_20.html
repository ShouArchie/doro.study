Course Description

GERON 352 / HLTH 352 / KIN 352 / REC 362 / SOC 352:

An introduction to individual and population aging. Topics discussed include: aging from a historical and comparative perspective; aging in subcultures; aging and the social structure; aging and social processes; aging and the environment; work and retirement; and aging and leisure patterns.

HLTH 352: View requirements for HLTH 352

GERON 352: View requirements for GERON 352

KIN 352: View requirements for KIN 352

REC 362: View requirements for REC 362

SOC 352: View requirements for SOC 352

In this course, we will examine aging as a social process. In the first part of the course, we will learn to understand aging as a socially constructed phenomenon. We will discover how our views on old age have changed throughout history and how our culture shapes social attitudes towards aging. During the second part of the course, we will examine the influence of social structures on the experiences of aging. We will take a close look at inequities that are produced by gender and ethnicity and learn about differential access to healthy and successful aging. In the final part of the course, we will critically assess the social and financial supports available to older adults in our society. We will examine what can we do to ensure that older adults in Canada have better access to adequate housing, social and financial stability, health care, and “good death”. Research activities embedded in the course will allow us to apply what we have learned to practice.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Explain why aging can be considered a socially constructed phenomenon
Explain why Canadian population is aging and what contributes to this trend
Identify and summarize some of the theories in social gerontology
Describe major sources of social inequalities that impact individuals in old age
Describe how major social institutions shape the aging process
Define and explain healthy and successful aging
Explain the role of family and social support in the lives of older adults
Summarize and critically assess work and retirement policies in Canada
Identify major challenges in the end-of-life issues experienced by older adults
Enhance research, writing, and critical appraisal skills

Midterm (20%) and Final Exam (25%)

Why do we need to use midterm and final exams as assessments? 

Exams are an effective way to evaluate your knowledge in a somewhat objective manner. I can ask you questions that test your understanding of the readings, lectures, and class discussions, and assess your level of comprehension in relation to that of your peers. Moreover, exams also help me to see if I did my job right: If I see that some of the questions were not answered correctly by most students, it means I need to better explain this topic. This is why exams are an effective way for us all to understand how we absorb the learned material. 

How exams will be structured? 

The Midterm Exam (see the schedule) will be administered during the class time. It is worth 20% of the final grade and will be composed of 40 multiple choice questions. You will have 70 minutes to complete the exam. The Final Exam will be 25% of the final grade and will have 50 multiple-choice questions. The Final Exam will be administered during the Final Examination period.  

 
What if you miss a Midterm?  

Life happens and sometimes our performance might be negatively impacted by what is happening in our personal lives. If you miss your Midterm Exam or do poorly on it, you will have an option to transfer your Midterm mark to the Final Exam (so, your final exam will be worth 45% instead of 25%).   

Class Engagement (20%):

Why do we have class engagement as an assessment? 

I believe that the best way to learn is to think about the content and apply it to your own experiences. In this class, we will have regular workshops and other activities that will help you to apply the learned material. Engaging with the content we learn in this way will help you to retain the information, to do better on your research project and feel more prepared for the Exams. For us, your instructional team, engagement activities can help to “check-in” with the class and identify any topics or concepts that may need to be explained again.  

How engagement activities will be structured? 

Each week, we will have an engagement activity in class - you can work on it as a group or individually. Each engagement activity would count as 1.5% of your grade for a total of 15% (10 activities per term).  

What if you missed an engagement activity and/or missed a class?  

I appreciate that students have many different roles and responsibilities and that sometimes you may need to miss classes due to illness or personal circumstances. I built in the option to miss two engagement activities, which means you will have 12 opportunities throughout the course to complete 10 required activities. 

 

Research Project    

You will be expected to complete a mini-research project. You will conduct a semi-structured interview with an older adult. The goal of this assignment is to explore how the aging process is shaped by social and individual factors and to try yourself as a researcher in the field of social gerontology. Over the course of working on this assignment, you will develop (or improve) your skills and knowledge in qualitative research design and methodology, critical analysis and appraisal, and writing. We will have weekly workshops during class time to prepare you for research-related activities. You will be required to submit a number of assignments:

 

Part 1: Research Paperwork (3%)

The goal of this assignment is to prepare you for your role as a researcher. Conducting research with human participants is a privilege. As researchers, we should ensure that we do not abuse this privilege and that we treat our research participants with respect.  

n order to participate in research-related activities, you must familiarize yourself with Tri-Council Policy Statement on Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans available at https://www.pre.ethics.gc.ca/eng/policy-politique_tcps2-eptc2_2022.html  Each student also has to complete an online Course on Research Ethics (CORE) tutorial available at https://tcps2core.ca/welcome 

Before beginning your research activities, you are required to review and/or modify four documents that you will need for conducting your research project:

  1. Recruitment flyer
  2. Letter of Information and Consent Form
  3. Interview Guide, and
  4. Feedback letter.

To complete Part 1 of the research activities, you would need to submit via Dropbox:

  • Copy of CORE certificate (provided to you once you complete the tutorial)
  • Recruitment flyer (with your contact information on it)
  • Letter of information and consent form (with your contact information)
  • Interview guide
  • Feedback letter (with your contact information)

Please note that you are NOT allowed to engage in ANY research-related activities (including recruitment) until you received an explicit email from your course instructor or your TA that your documents have been approved and the permission to conduct research has been granted. 

 

Part 2: Interview Transcript (7%) 

The goal of this assignment is to develop (or hone) your qualitative interviewing skills and to provide you with an opportunity to learn about the life experiences of older adults. 

Upon receiving permission to begin your research, you will be required to recruit ONE older adult to participate in semi-structured interview of approximately 30 minutes. We will practice the technique of semi-structured qualitative interviewing during the workshop in class. You will be required to provide an older adult with the letter of information and consent form, to explain the research process to them, and to have them sign a consent form if they agree to take part in your research project.

With the permission of the participant, you will audio-record the interview. Upon completion of the interview, you will provide the participant with the feedback letter. You will then have to transcribe the interview, remove all identifiable information (e.g. their name or reference to someone’s name), and submit the transcript and the consent form via LEARN Dropbox. 

Please note that this part of the assignment can be done in a group of two students interviewing ONE older adult. If this is the case, students must clearly identify each interviewer, as each student will receive an individual grade for the assignment 

Part 3: Research Paper (20%)

The goal of this assignment is to evaluate your critical analysis and writing skills. This assignment will also demonstrate that you can think critically and apply what you have learned in this class to everyday life. You will submit a short research paper that will be based on the interview that you conducted with an older adult. Your paper will be 5 pages long. 

You will have to submit two copies of your paper – one (regular copy) will be submitted to LEARN Dropbox. The other copy will be anonymized (e.g. you will remove your name and student number from the title page) and submitted for blind peer review to peerScholar. 

Part 4: Peer Review Assessment 5% x 2 (10%)

The goal of this review is to provide you with an opportunity to develop critical appraisal skills and to give students constructive feedback to improve their papers. You will receive two anonymous papers for review and evaluation. You will be required to mark the two assignments and to provide detailed comments on the grading rubric which will be provided to you. 

Part 5: (Optional) Revised Paper (20%) 

After receiving the anonymous reviews from your peers and the feedback from your TA, you will have an option to revise your paper and resubmit it for a better mark. This submission can improve your mark by a maximum of 10 points (e.g. if you received 80 on the first submission, you will not be able to receive more than 90, even if you address all the comments and concerns raised by the TA and the reviewers). This is an optional assignment – you do not have to submit it. If you decide to submit it, you will have to (1) submit a short summary of the changes you have made to the paper in response to the TA’s and reviewers’ comments, and (2) use ‘track changes’ option to show where and how you have made the changes to your paper.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as [fill this in with your discipline-specific skills]. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Accommodating religious and spiritual observances 

[Applies only to students taking undergraduate courses]

The University of Waterloo has a duty to accommodate religious, spiritual, and other creed-based beliefs and practices under the Ontario Human Rights Commission (2015) Policy on preventing discrimination based on creed. Students may seek accommodations for missed course components on religious, spiritual, or other creed grounds. In such cases the students should please consult the instructor within two weeks of the announcement of the due date for which the accommodation is being sought.  Students also may request accommodations for temporary absences from classes or other course-related activities to engage in prayer or other daily spiritual practices. Instructors are expected to make reasonable arrangements to respectfully accommodate such requests. The University of Waterloo has a number of multi-faith spaces that students can use for faith-based practices.

Declaring absences

[Applies only to students taking undergraduate courses]

Please see the Academic Regulations section of the Undergraduate Calendar for more details. Regardless of the process used to declare an absence, it is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructor so that the instructor can determine how to accommodate the missed work. 

Absences due to religious, creed, and spiritual observances

Beginning Fall 2024, students can submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration Form in Quest. This form allows the student to register dates of potential conflicts with coursework. Students are expected to record their absence within two weeks of announcement of the due date or scheduled examination date for which academic accommodation is being sought. Submission through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. 

Absences due to verified illness 

For absences due to illness that are not pandemic-related, students need to obtain a Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances Process. Submission through the Absence Verification System, once registered, will notify your instructors of your absence. Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation to your instructors, teaching assistants, or other course personnel.  To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able to given their health condition.

Absences due to extenuating circumstances (e.g., bereavement)

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (e.g., bereavement, serious family illness), students submit a request for absence due to extenuating circumstances to the Absence Verification System for processing. Students use the form to submit supporting documentation, which will be considered in a comparable manner to a VIF. Official documentation is necessary before any action can be taken. Examples are plane/train/bus tickets, court documents, police reports, death certificates/obituaries/letters from funeral directors. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able to given their circumstances.

Self-declared absences due to COVID-related illness

Students can self-declare an absence on Quest due to COVID-related illness or a requirement to self-isolate. A self-declared COVID absence covers up to 10 days and no documentation is required. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in on-line components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence.  It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Self-declared short-term absences for any reason

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason. A short-term absence covers two calendar days, and no documentation is required. The student has only one self-declared short-term absence per term and this absence can only be used during the formal lecture period. Self-declared absences cannot be applied to Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), or Studio (STU) course components.  Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

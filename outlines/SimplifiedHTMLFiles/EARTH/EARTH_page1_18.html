Course Description

EARTH 436B:

An optional second term of a two-term supervised research project in which students learn to gather and interpret data. Students will present their findings in a formal presentation and written thesis.

View requirements for EARTH 436B

Earth 436B in the Winter term is devoted to the completion of the research activities related to the project, analysis and interpretation of the results, presentation of the results and preparation of a final Research Report. As was the case for the Earth436A course, a progress report must be submitted to your advisor midway through the term to ensure that the research is on track for completion. A detailed class schedule, time frame for all course deliverables and assessment structure are presented below. This schedule and the information in this document may be revised from time to time as the term progresses.

 

As an initial task in the Earth 436B course, you are required to select a format for the final written report on your BSc research project (Option A - Journal Paper Manuscript or Option B - Conventional Thesis format). This can be decided through discussions with your advisor. All students will be encouraged to consider formatting their research report as a journal paper with the potential to be submitted for publication consideration to a journal at the end of the course.

 

OPTION A - Journal Paper Manuscript

Select an example published paper that is similar in scope to the research topic and has been published in a journal that would be appropriate for the current work. The example paper will act as a detailed guide to follow in preparation of the final report for the Earth 436B course. The example paper will be discussed with and ultimately approved by the advisor.

Determine the details on what is expected in a journal article format at the selected journal website: (e.g., Canadian Journal of Earth Sciences. https://cdnsciencepub.com/journal/cjes)

This will include, among other things, information on how to correctly include references, preparation of figures and the inclusion of Supplementary Information if required. This Supplementary Information section is analogous to an Appendix in a conventional thesis format.

Review in detail the publication expectations with all co-authors who would be included if the work is submitted to a journal (advisors and potentially laboratory technical personnel).

 

OPTION B - Conventional Thesis

Obtain a past BSc thesis similar in scope to the research topic to be used as a guide document. The advisor can assist in selecting and providing an appropriate example.

Consider and discuss the possibility of preparing a journal article based on the research project at a later date.

Students selecting Option B should refer to and follow the thesis guidelines provided by the University Graduate Office to ensure that the final submitted document follows the standard guidelines for a UW thesis, related to the front pages and document format. These are available at: https://uwaterloo.ca/graduate-studies-postdoctoral-affairs/current-students/thesis

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Plan and conduct independent and original research under the guidance of a research manager
Learn how to explore, organize, execute, share and disseminate the results of your own scientific questions and thought
Acquire new practical research and analytical skills using data either collected by yourself in the field or assembled in the laboratory or information/data collected by someone else (often the faculty supervisor) in a research environment that you would not have had a chance to do elsewhere in your program
Learn discipline-specific protocols and procedures for addressing scientific questions and procedures and formats for reporting technical information according to discipline or sub-discipline standards
Acquire an appreciation for and basic skill level to organize, interpret, and identify relevance of new information and results
Understand how to organize, communicate effectively and disseminate clearly new information to peers and other professionals, both written and orally
Know how to undertake self-assessment, and learn how to give and receive constructive review and critique by your peers
Learn how to ethically and productively make use of AI tools including Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI)

Student Assessment

Component Value
Research Report Format and Outline (Required, no grade weighting)

Research Report Format and Outline (Required, no grade weighting): (Manuscript Option) Submit the selected example journal article and journal website along with a brief outline for your journal paper including each section and the information that will be included in each section in bullet form to the advisor for review and also upload this to the Learn Dropbox on the assigned date. (Thesis Option) Submit a brief outline for the thesis document including each section and the information that will be included in each section in bullet form to the advisor for grading and also upload this to the Learn Dropbox on the assigned date (January 29).

 

Progress Report (15%): Progress reports should contain an updated clear statement of goal(s) and objectives of the research, a brief description of methods, an analysis of progress and problems, and a realistic timetable for completion of the remaining stages of the research project. The reports will be no longer than 3 pages in length, including the clear expected timetable and will be submitted to the advisor for grading and uploaded to the LEARN Dropbox (February 26).

 

Presentation (35%): The posters will be presented in one session that will run for an entire day, Wednesday, April 2nd, 2025. Each student will select a 15 min time period when a group of Judges will visit the poster and the student will provide a 3 min explanation/presentation of the poster and research. This will be followed by a question-and-answer period. The students are requested to be at their posters to discuss their research and answer questions as much as possible during the day as people will be visiting randomly between 9:00 am and 4:30 pm.  A digital rubric will be made available to everyone visiting the posters as has been done in the past for the final oral presentations. Student participation and assessment with the rubric at the poster presentation session will be required and instructions will be provided later in the course. All assessments will be complied as has been done in the past.

 

Poster Printing and Setting up for the Presentation: With such a large number of posters to print, the poster will have to be uploaded to the Learn Dropbox in advance of the presentation day (Tuesday, March 25th). The posters will all be sent to the UW printing service for printing. The posters will all be mounted for you on assigned presentation boards in the EIT Foyer area (lower floor in the Museum area) by 9:00 am on Wednesday, April 2nd. A PDF of the posters must also be uploaded to the Learn Dropbox the night before the presentations (April 1st).  

Policies outlined in the Undergraduate Calendar for Academic Regulations Related to Assignments, Tests, and Final Exam Accommodations will be followed (https://ugradcalendar.uwaterloo.ca/page/Regulations-Accommodations). The poster presentation is considered the same as a final examination, and as such policies for a missed final examination apply.

 

Final Research Document (50%): A complete draft of your document must be emailed to your advisor at least 1 week before the posted submission due date on LEARN. Your advisor will review the document to ensure proper formatting and completion and may provide edits and comments for implementation prior to final submission.

Before or on the due date (Wednesday, April 16), you must upload your report to Dropbox for marking by the Advisor and a designated Second Reader. Selection of the second reader is the responsibility of the advisor. A PDF and Word version should be uploaded. If you are unable to complete your document, a draft of all your work completed to date and a realistic schedule for final submission should be shared and discussed with your advisor and uploaded to the Learn page. This will allow your advisor to evaluate how best to help you in this next phase.

To be fair to all students, extensions will only be allowed in rare and exceptional circumstances by written appeal to the Earth 436B course instructor. If your work is not complete, you must hand in what you have on the due date to your advisor accompanied by a letter of explanation and a revised timetable for completion. The letter of explanation and a revised timetable for completion must also be sent to the course instructor (drudolph@uwaterloo.ca). Late submissions may be subject to a 10% penalty, and even delay your degree.

The advisor is responsible for providing comments in sufficient time so the student can revise the thesis and submit it in final form by the date for mark submission to the Registrar (late April). If you are planning to graduate at the end of term that you are completing your thesis, then you must follow the specified deadlines in order to have everything completed by the dates when the marks for graduating students are due to the registrar.

Administrative Policy

• Office of Academic Integrity provides relevant information for students, faculty and staff. 

  • Academic Integrity: In order to maintain a culture of academic integrity, members of the University of Waterloo community are expected to promote honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility

 

• Plagiarism: Plagiarism is the act of directly copying material from another source word-for-word without acknowledging (citing) the source. Copying material from another student word-for-word (collusion) also constitutes plagiarism. It is unacceptable to claim another person's words as your own. Acts of plagiarism are not tolerated at the University of Waterloo and are subject to disciplinary action according to Policy 71, Student Discipline (see below). If you have not already done so, it is strongly recommended that you take the Academic Integrity Tutorial at http://www.lib.uwaterloo.ca/ait/. An answer to a question that consists solely of a quote to a properly cited source is also not acceptable. Although this does not constitute plagiarism, professors are unable to assess whether any actual learning, specifically the synthesis/application of knowledge, has been achieved by the student. Although finding sources is part of learning, this type of work by itself does not merit a passing grade. Students are expected to cite the source of information (e.g., Wikipedia, website, textbook, journal article, class slides) but ALSO to paraphrase the material (rewrite the information in your own words) when answering questions on tasks that require an internet and/or literature search. When writing scientific or engineering reports it is standard professional practice to cite the appropriate work of other research scientists when discussing ideas or information that are not our own. Plagiarism is a very serious charge and can ruin promising careers.

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as independent research, original technical writing and live oral presentations. Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and access to additional resources will be provided. Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library:

 https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai.

Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. Additionally, all information Gathered using generative technologies must also cite the correct primary research articles in which that information was originally published. Be aware that AI technologies are typically unreliable at providing correct primary sources. 

While generative AI technologies can provide general background information, you are responsible for demonstrating your application of knowledge by reporting, interpreting, and explaining your experimental findings in the context of the primary literature. TurnItIn® text-matching software, including the AI detection function, may be used for the final reports.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Overall, you are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

COURSE RULES AND CONSIDERATIONS:

Verification of Illness Forms:  Science students should be aware that the only Verification of Illness forms (VIFs) accepted for accommodation for missed assessments will be those issued by the University of Waterloo´s Health Services, when this service is open (https://uwaterloo.ca/health-services/). VIFs issued by walk-in clinics will not be accepted, except when obtaining a VIF from Health Services is not possible. If a student is sick on a weekend, during off-hours, while out of town or receiving ongoing care from a family physician or specialist, it is acceptable to provide documentation from other health service providers.

Information should include (1) date of the physician assessment, (2) dates of illness, (3) level of incapacitation and (4) whether the diagnosis was made by the physician or based on description by the student. Keeping the playing field level for all of our students is a priority. Students are reminded that obtaining a VIF under false pretences is an academic offense. For tests and exams, a student found guilty of misrepresentation will receive a failing grade

in the course and be suspended. Any questions concerning this policy can be directed to an undergraduate advisor in the Science Undergraduate Office. Student travel plans not considered acceptable grounds for granting an alternative examination time. Only illness and extenuating circumstances (such as a death in the family) will be considered.

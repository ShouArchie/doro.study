Course Description

EARTH 355:

This course explores approaches for interpreting environmental data to support water decisions using theoretical and computational techniques. These approaches focus on statistical methods, including descriptive statistics, probability, hypothesis testing, frequency and time series analyses, point patterns, and correlation and regression, and also include discussion of analytical and numerical approaches.

View requirements for EARTH 355

Blended Framework

Lectures: Lecture videos are provided online and asynchronous. Students are expected to have watched the videos prior to class.

In-Class Time (Mondays): Instructor will briefly review the lecture contents for the week and will lead example questions and in-class activities, working towards the weekly assignment and poster development goal. It is anticipated that there will be time during class to work on these activities with the instructor present.

In-Class Time (Wednesday): Any additional review for the week’s lecture contents will be completed. In addition, approximately one case study will be provided each week as reading material with an instructor led discussion during this time. The instructor will lead a discussion on the case study, and the remainder of the time will be to answer questions on this and other course content (assignments, coding labs).

Assignments/Labs: Assignments from weeks 1 – 10 (no assignment during week 9) will include calculations by hand, and a coding lab component (coding component is included in week 9).

EXPECTED *AVERAGE* WEEKLY TIME COMMITMENT: 8 – 10 hours

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, you should be able to design a study to test a scientific hypothesis by:
Assembling the required information
Identifying relevant statistical techniques
Assessing results of statistical techniques to form a conclusion (using hand calculations & coding language)
Acknowledging data uncertainty in statistical and mechanistic water models

ASSIGNMENTS and LABS:

This course will teach you some fundamentals of statistics and the use of R or Python – your choice! These are coding programs that work in Windows, Mac, and Linux. We will use them through Jupyter Notebook, accessible online through UW’s Jupyter server. Full instructions for use provided in course. If you prefer to use MATLAB or similar, we will happily mark your assignments but may not be able to provide help or scripts for any other program.

There are no late penalties for assignments or labs that are submitted prior to marks being posted and assignments returned, which is expected to be ONE WEEK after due date. Assignments will not be graded once marks are posted and they are returned, but can be submitted to check for correctness if the instructor/TA has time available.

IN-CLASS ACTIVITIES:

For the in-class activities, you will be given a worksheet to solve a problem related to the topic of discussion. The worksheets will be peer-marked, following solutions provided in class. Only the top 6 in-class activities will count towards your final grade, and no make-up activities will be provided.

POSTER DRAFT REVIEW:

For the assignments/labs you will have ‘adopted’ a dataset and performed several statistical analyses on this dataset. You will work with a partner on the final project, but must develop a unique, initial poster draft for your data. The poster draft will include the introduction, hypothesis/objective, and presentation and description of your data. The draft will be peer-marked using a rubric provided on LEARN.

FINAL PROJECT POSTER:

For the assignments/labs you will have ‘adopted’ a dataset and performed several statistical analyses on this dataset. You will work with one other student on the final poster, selecting one of your two datasets and decisions to present. The final project is a poster presenting a decision to be made based on this dataset (determined by you, in consultation with the instructor), the methods and analyses used to support this decision, and a recommendation to the stakeholder (the person for whom the decision is made for). These should be one page.

The datasets and the analyses are unique to each student. Please see Academic Integrity below for further details.

More information, including a grading rubric, is available on the class LEARN site.

There are no late penalties for a final poster that is submitted on or before the last day of class. Starting on the day after the last day of classes, a late penalty will be applied of 10% (1 day late), 25% (2 days late), 50% (3 days late), 75% (4 days late), and 100% (5 days late).

FINAL EXAM:

There is a final exam for this course. It will cover the entire contents of the course but will not include a coding component. There will be one open question where you will be asked to discuss a perspective of one of the case studies we covered in class. More information will be provided throughout the term.

Course Description

EARTH 438:

Review of basic concepts in engineering geology as applied to rock and soil, including material properties, variability in properties, external factors such as stress, and evaluation of design adequacy. Site investigation and characterization techniques used to define and characterize the properties of geological materials and their use in selected engineering geologic design and construction problems. Laboratory assignments will focus on the determination of physical properties and site assessment problems.

View requirements for EARTH 438

EARTH 638:

Time dependent behaviour of rocks, Griffiths crack theory, failure criteria for rocks, in situ stresses and their origins, behaviour of discontinuous rock masses, introductions to analytic and numerical methods for rock mechanics. Case studies, term project.

View requirements for EARTH 638

Project themes selected for EARTH 438, which provides the subject for the Team Project and Presentation, since 2010 has been The Engineering Geology of Open Pit Mining which has proved popular with both Civil and Geological Engineers as well as Geoscientists, since many students taking the course target careers in the resource extraction industry or its peripheral connections. The course is global in scope.

*Graduate students enrolling in EARTH 638 will be required to complete 1 additional project on top of the EARTH 438 coursework. This project can be parallel or supporting their graduate research themes.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe the relationship between geology and the design and performance of engineered structures (buildings, dams, slopes, tunnels, excavations, and infrastructure) as well as the implications of damaging geological processes for the siting and security of engineering facilities.
Understand basic concepts in engineering geology as applied to rock and soil such as material properties, variability in properties, the role of external factors such as stress history, evaluation of design quality in the context of geology and geological processes, site investigation and field techniques used to characterize the mass properties of geological materials, and their use in engineering geology inputs into the design and construction process
Discuss geological hazards as they impact on project feasibility and integrity, including an examination of engineering geology and risk

Student Assessment

Component Value
Lab Assignments (5% each, 5 Labs) 25%
Midterm (Stereonet Applications) 25%
Project (20%) and Presentation (5%) 25%
Final Term Test 25%

Labs will consist of five (5) assignments including i) Site evaluation using RMR/DMR for a hydroelectric facility, ii) 2 Stereonet analysis assignments, iii) Time-to-Failure analysis using the inverse-velocity method, iv) Hazard and Risk assessment of a debris flow and seismic event.

Midterm will be entirely based on Stereonet analysis

Project will be a group project of a selected mine site. The objectives are to summarize geological conditions and structural setting of the mine, to outline geohazard exposure for slope design (e.g., seismicity), identify key engineering geological problems in pit slope stability (including failure types, failure mechanisms, and failure mitigation), and to discuss issues related to the stability and characteristics of associated waste dumps and/or tailings. Students must also discuss engineering geology aspects of mine-access infrastructure construction and maintenance and outline a risk assessment for site hazards (e.g., seismicity). Finally, lessons learned for site characterization, design, and operations should be outlined.

Term Test will be held on Friday, April 4th, 2025, during the final lab session of the term and will cover course content, but will not require stereonet backing boards or tracing paper. It will be entirely written. A 1-letter-sized-page, single-sided, handwritten cheat sheet will be allowed during the term test. There is no Final Exam held during the Final Exam period.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

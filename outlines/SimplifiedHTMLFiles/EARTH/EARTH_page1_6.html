Course Description

EARTH 221:

Origin, abundance, and geochemistry of elements. Introduction to environmental isotope geochemistry and radiometric dating. Basic aqueous geochemistry: pH, carbonate equilibrium, and common ions in natural waters.

View requirements for EARTH 221

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Use geochemical terminology correctly
Calculate basic geochemical parameters and create associated diagrams
Identify applications of geochemistry for environmental problems/studies
Present and discuss geochemical scientific literature

Participation

This grade includes:

- Three surveys (one about your interests, a stop/start/continue one partway through the term, and one about the field day and blended learning activity) (0.5% each for 1.5%)

- Three study quizzes (a review of prereqs and the syllabus, a midterm and exam study quiz) done on Learn. You can try these quizzes up to 3 times and Learn will take your highest mark (0.5% each for 1.5%)

- Attendance at the field day and at the presentations (2% total)

Assignments

These must be completed using a graphing/spreadsheet program (e.g. Excel) where stated. Please hand in either as a Word Doc (.docx) or PDF (.pdf) to the appropriate dropbox on Learn. Assignments are individual. You are welcome to work with your friend(s) but you must hand in a unique assignment, i.e answers must be in your own words.

Group Oral Presentations

In groups of 4, you will present the findings of a recent scientific paper that includes some geochemistry. The idea here is to connect what you've learned in class with any other geochemistry you need to learn to make the paper understandable and interesting to your peers. More detail can be found on Learn under Content --> Group Oral Presentation. Presentations are graded by the instructor or TA (50% of your grade) and the class (averaged class score, 50% of your grade) using a rubric. You will be amazed how similar the two grades will be!

Midterm

The midterm covers the first half of the class and is written during class or tutorial time. It is a closed book test. Typical question types may be multiple choice, interpreting diagrams, explaining concepts, and doing calculations (equations provided). A periodic table is included. The students will vote on the midterm day as there are many programs represented and I don't know all your schedules.

Final Exam

The final exam is scheduled by the Registrar's office. Please do not book any travel plans until you see your exam schedule. It is a cumulative, closed-book test with similar question types to the midterm. Equations are provided and you should definitely bring a calculator.

Extensions and Accommodations

Extensions and other accommodations will only be considered on a case-by-case basis.

Students have the following three options for declaring an absence. Students are expected to notify their instructor(s) before or within 24 hours of any deadline for marks. • VIF Declaration: for absences that do not meet the short-term absence criteria, for self-declared absences that exceed the maximum number permitted per term, or for (retroactive) reports of illness or other extenuating circumstances, students must refer to their home Faculty's process for VIF submissions (instructions for Science students are found at: https://uwaterloo.ca/science-undergraduate-office/accommodation-illness-or-extenuating-circumstances#submitting).

o The VIF is the University of Waterloo’s official document to be submitted by students when they are seeking medical accommodation from their instructors. o Science maintains a page with up-to-date guidelines for Accommodations for Illness or Extenuating Circumstances and can be reviewed as necessary.

Using Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) In This Class

It is possible (and basically, untraceable) to use AIs such at ChatGPT for some work in this course. We will actually examine how good ChatGTP is at geochemistry in one assignment. The rules for Generative AI for are:

  1. Learning objectives: As far as we are able, we have set up the course so AI-generated answers would not receive a passing grade. This means your role is to be specific (AI answers are often vague), accurate, and use your critical thinking in a way AIs cannot.
  2. Referencing and validating. You are taking full responsibility for AI-generated materials as if you had produced them yourself: ideas must be attributed and facts must be true.
  3. Documentation. If you choose to use AI, you must document its use. We will cite ChatGPT (or other AIs) as a personal communication, using APA format: In-text citation:

In-text citation: (ChatGPT, 2025)

In reference list: 

ChatGPT. (2025, April 1). How do you cite an answer in ChatGPT. [Response to user question]. Retrieved from [URL of the platform where the conversation took place].

PLEASE BE AWARE that you should NOT put other people's work into most GenAI programs as they will be used as training datasets. For example, please do NOT put text of a paper into a GenAI program and ask it to summarize. This is not fair to the authors of the paper. Additionally, do not share anything with a GenAI that you would not be willing the world to know, i.e. your social insurance number, your address, your mental health status, etc.

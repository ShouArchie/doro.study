Course Description

CS 445 / CS 645 / ECE 451:

Introduces students to the requirements definition phase of software development. Models, notations, and processes for software requirements identification, representation, analysis, and validation. Cost estimation from early documents and specifications.

CS 445: View requirements for CS 445

CS 645: View requirements for CS 645

ECE 451: View requirements for ECE 451

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Ability to separate requirements from specifications from domain assumptions and argue that a specification + domain assumptions satisfy requirements
Ability to elicit requirements using different strategies
Ability to model requirements in a variety of modelling paradigms
Ability to elicit and document non-functional requirements such that the to-be-developed software can be objectively tested for their satisfaction
Ability to prioritize requirements using AHP
Ability to explain and compare different techniques for validating and verifying a requirements document
Ability to use tools to manage a Software Development Team.

Tentative Course Schedule

1. Introduction (1 hour)
Course Philosophy and Logistics. Why is requirements analysis complex? 

2: Requirements Engineering Reference Model (1 hour)
Context diagram. Identifying or determining a system's interfaces.  Monitorable inputs and controllable outputs.  Requirements vs. specifications.

3: Domain Modelling (4 hours)
Modelling of the system's environment.  Entity-relationship diagrams.  UML Class and object diagrams.  Constraints on the domain model.  OCL

4: Functional Modelling (2 hours)
Functions as a modelling notation.  Pre- and post-conditions. Use-case diagrams.  Functions over a class diagram.

5: Behavioural Modelling (7 hours)
Modelling dynamic behaviour of a software system.  Use cases, scenarios, and sequence diagrams. Extended state machine models, including state hierarchy, concurrent regions, communication, activities, and history.  UML State Machines.

6: Constraint Modelling (2 hours)
Temporal Logic.  Specification patterns. 

7: Model Integration (1 hour)
Model integration.  Composition of models.  Consistency among overlapping models.  Feature interactions.

8: Requirements Elicitation (4 hours)
Stakeholders, sources of requirements, strategies for identifying requirements.

9: Requirements Analysis (4 hours)
Requirements triage and prioritization.  Cost-benefit analysis.  Analytic Hierarchy Process.  Conflicts and negotiation.  Cost Estimation.  Risk Analysis.

10:  Quality Requirements (2 hours)
Nonfunctional requirements (e.g., performance, security, usability, maintainability).  Fitness criteria.  User-interface requirements.

11: Validation and Verification (2 hours)
Walkthroughs, inspections, and technical reviews of requirements documents.  Executable specifications.  Automated analysis. Verifying that specifications satisfy requirements.

12: Documenting Requirements (2 hours)
The expected contents and organization of a Software Requirements Specification (SRS).  Non-traditional means documenting requirements (e.g., user manuals, test cases).

13: Software Project Management (3 hours)
Managing Goals, Time, and Costs.

14: RE for AI Systems (1 hour)
AI vs non-AI Systems based on what we covered in this course.

Student Assessment

Component Value

Assignments:

D0 - 4%  D1-D6 each is 6 %. Total = 40%

Late assignment submission: A penalty of 2% will be applied for each hour (or fraction thereof) past the deadline.

The weight of any missed assignment will be shifted to the Final.

You must not miss more than two assignments to receive a final grade in this course.

You must pass the assignments component to pass the course.

Exams:

You must pass the weighted exams to pass the course.

 

CS645 adjusted marking Scheme:

The final exam and the assignments are scaled to 80%, and 20% of your grade is the evaluation of your paper.

Graduate students in this course (i.e., students enrolled in CS645) are expected to do a small library-research project on top of the regular coursework.  The deliverable of this project is a 15-20 page written paper, complete with references.

The project can be related to requirements engineering or a more general software engineering topic.  Example topics include requirements notations not covered in class (e.g., Alloy, SCR, SDT), requirement-phase activities (e.g., conformance to privacy laws, security requirements, requirements for product lines, and goal-oriented requirements modelling), and other research areas of software engineering (SEI's CMM, concept analysis). 

Topics and paper structure must have the instructor's approval.

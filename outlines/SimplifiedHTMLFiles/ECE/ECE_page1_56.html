Course Description

ECE 495:

Autonomous driving system overview; computer vision basics, deep learning for perception, motion modelling and state estimation, localization and mapping, object tracking, behavioral planning and reinforcement learning, path planning and vehicle control, safety and verification and validation, adoption and impact.

View requirements for ECE 495

Required Background

  1. Linear algebra
  2. Differential calculus
  3. Probability
  4. Basic mechanics
  5. Feedback control
  6. Basic programming skills in Python and C++

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand the functions required to control an autonomous vehicle
Design and implement perception functions using classical and deep learning computer vision techniques
Formulate and implement Extended Kalman Filter for dynamic state estimation
Understand and implement basic vehicle control and trajectory methods
Understand the limitations and risks of current methods and be aware of future research and industry trends

Additional Reading and Online Resources

Additional Reading

There is no single textbook that adequately covers the course materials. The lecture slides cover all the concepts required for this course. However, some students may prefer to read text explaining these concepts.

A recent book discusses the major functions of autonomous vehicles, touching on many of the topics covered in the course and beyond

S. Liu, L. Li, J. Tang, S. Wu, J.-L. Gaudiot. Creating Autonomous Vehicle Systems, Second Edition, Morgan and Claypool Publishers, 2020

Sadly, the editing is substandard and scholarly attention to detail is lacking; however, the text is reasonably easy to follow and gives a good introduction to many topics covered in the course, especially planning. The PDF of the book is available from UW library.

The following selection of book chapters (including from the book by Liu et al.) and papers cover the topics presented in class. The advanced materials section includes two papers that cover the motion planning approach covered in the class; however, keep in mind that there is a vast variety of methods for planning, some of which are discussed in the book by Liu et al.

Online courses

There are also freely accessible online courses that cover a lot of the material from this course, and you may find it helpful to watch some of the online lectures.

Coursera has a Self-Driving Cars Specialization, which you can access at

https://www.coursera.org/specializations/self-driving-cars

The specialization has four courses:

  1. Introduction to Self-Driving Cars
  2. State Estimation and Localization for Self-Driving Cars
  3. Visual Perception for Self-Driving Cars
  4. Motion Planning for Self-Driving Cars

The specialization covers more material than this course since it assumes roughly two terms. You may find some of the lecture videos to be useful supplements.

Object detection can be a tricky concept to grasp. The object detection videos by Andrew Ng in the Coursera course on Convolutional Neural Networks provide an excellent introduction, at

https://www.coursera.org/learn/convolutional-neural-networks

Finally, the Stanford course on Convolutional Neural Networks for Visual Recognition (cs231n) gives a great introduction to neural networks in general and convolutional neural networks in particular. The online materials include lecture slides, videos, and notes. You can access them at http://cs231n.stanford.edu/

Administrative Policy

Course Policies

Collaboration & Plagiarism: Plagiarism, taking credit for work that others did, is not permitted, and this applies to source code as well as exams. The course staff will be checking for it through a variety of different methods. Any cases of plagiarism I detect will be reported, according to university policy (see the University Policy section below).

For the assignments, you may discuss ideas and design alternatives; however, each student must submit their own, independently developed reports and code. You also need to properly cite any external sources you use in preparing your deliverables. Copying solutions developed by others is unacceptable under any circumstances. You are expected to develop solutions on your own.

No collaboration of any kind is permitted during exams.

I want to emphasize that I take the issue of plagiarism very seriously, and so does the University of Waterloo. If you are uncertain about this subject, please seek some guidance. There are many resources available to you. You can check the university policies, talk to the course instructor or teaching assistant, et cetera.

Use of generative AI: Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) using large language models (LLM) or other methods trained to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. However, you are recommended to work through the assignment problems yourself first before eliciting help from GenAI and to ensure you understand and critically analyze the suggestions produced by GenAI.

Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library:

 https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai.

Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee:

 https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Late Submissions: Late deliverable submissions will not be accepted.

Re-marking: If you believe that your grade on a written, submitted deliverable (e.g., a midterm exam question) is incorrect or unfair, you may ask that it be re-marked. Presentations or demos cannot be re-marked.

To request that a question be re-marked, you will need to submit your request as a private post to the Instructor team (including the TAs) on Piazza, clearly indicating which question or part of the deliverable is to be re-marked and including an explanation of why you believe the grade assigned was incorrect.

I will accept items for re-marking any time before the final exam. Be forewarned, when a deliverable is being re-marked, your grade could go up, it could stay the same, or it could go down. I will notify you of the outcome.

Extra Credit: In this class, there will be no opportunities to earn extra credit. Make-up projects or examinations will not be offered under any circumstances.

Attendance: Scheduled in-person class activities give an opportunity to engage with the material in a group setting with your peers and also to interact directly with the instructor. That said, this is university and you are capable of deciding for yourself if you are going to the lecture or not. Attendance is not taken and not graded, except for when the in-class exercises or quizzes are administered or bonus points offered for guest lectures.

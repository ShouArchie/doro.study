Course Description

ECE 416:

This course introduces advanced topics in networking with a focus on applications and wireless technologies. Topics include: cellular networks (2G, 3G, 4G and beyond), applications (Domain Name System [DNS], Simple Mail Transfer Protocol [SMTP], Post Office Protocol [POP], Internet Message Access Protocol [IMAP], Hypertext Transfer Protocol [HTTP]) and socket programming, Content-centric networks (content delivery networks, peer-to-peer protocols, data centers, etc.), protocols for multimedia applications (Session Initiation Protocol [SIP], Real-time Transport Protocol [RTP], RTP Control Protocol [RTCP]), emerging technologies (Internet of Things, sensors, software defined networks), policy issues (network neutrality, who controls the Internet?).

View requirements for ECE 416

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Gain a comprehensive understanding of SDN principles, architectures, and applications, with a focus on improving network programmability, scalability, and control.
Understand the structure, addressing, and operational aspects of IPv6, and evaluate its advantages over IPv4 in addressing scalability and security challenges
Analyze the architecture and protocols of Wi-Fi networks and Virtual LANs (VLANs), understanding their role in modern networking infrastructure.
Understand the principles of Quality of Service (QoS) in networking, including techniques and protocols used to manage bandwidth, reduce latency, prioritize traffic, and ensure reliable data transmission for diverse applications.
Examine the concepts of mobility in 4G and 5G networks, including tunnel routing mechanisms, and assess their impact on network performance and application delivery.
Investigate IoT architectures, protocols, and security challenges, and understand their applications in smart environments and industrial systems.
Conduct research-driven projects on advanced networking topics, present findings through technical papers and presentations, and engage in constructive peer discussions

Tentative Course Schedule

Topics Covered in the Course:

  • Introduction and review of ECE 358
  • Introduction to Security in Computer Networks
  • Applications (DNS, SMTP, HTTP) and Content-centric networks (content delivery networks, peer-to-peer protocols, data centers, etc.)
  • Software defined networks
  • Network layer: IPv6, Software Defined Network, ICMP.
  • Link Layer: Virtual LANs, Multiprotocol Label Switching.
  • Wireless Networks
  • Performance and QoS
  • Security in Computer Networks (Will be split across different layers)
  • Presentations from students (Time permits)

Deliverables

 

 

 

 

 

 

Project

  • Each group of two students will select a topic in advanced networking, obtain consent from the instructor, prepare a short white paper, and make a 20 minute presentation video. A list of proposed topics is posted on LEARN.
  • You and your project partner need to join the same group on LEARN by going to “connect -> groups -> project -> join” on LEARN.
  • A project white paper template has been posted on LEARN.
  • Multiple dropboxes has been created for your submissions as follows:
    • "Project White Paper" drop box: Submit your white paper in PDF format in this dropbox.
    • "Project Presentation Video" dropbox: Submit your presentation video in "mp4" format with "720p" resolution in this dropbox. This is a private dropbox for evaluating your presentation.
    • "Public Project Presentation Video" dropbox: Submit the same presentation video again in this dropbox. This is a public dropbox that will allow other students to watch your video and ask you questions on Piazza.
  • Rubrics for marking the white paper and the presentation video are created and published on LEARN. Have a look at these rubrics to inform yourself on the evaluation criteria.
  • Both project partners must contribute equally in the presentation video and the white paper.
  • The students will deliver in-class presentations in addition to their recorded videos if time permits.
  • Start a thread under the title of your project on Piazza to collect at least 20 different questions from different students about your project and answer these questions. You cannot start this thread before the due date for submitting your project video to the public "Public Project Presentation Video" dropbox. 
  • Each student (it is individual, not a group activity) must watch the presentation videos for all the projects and ask at least one question in 10 different projects in Piazza. Do not ask questions anonymously as you will be getting marks for asking these questions. Questions should be at the level of a 4th year engineering student.
  • The due date for asking these 10 questions on different projects is March 21st.
  • The due date for answering all the questions on your project is April 4th.
  • Once a project has 25 questions on Piazza, no more questions should be asked in that project. Any more questions will not be considered as valid questions and the group responsible for that project are not obligated to answer more questions beyond that number.
  • The white paper should be based on multiple references that have to be cited. It should describe the topic in today’s technology context. It should clearly identify some questions in the introduction and give (balanced and unbiased) responses to those questions in the conclusion. It should be no more (and no less) than 5 pages of text (from the “Introduction” section till the end of the “Conclusion” section) in 12 point (single space).

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as writing a white paper. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

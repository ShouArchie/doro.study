Course Description

REC 151:

This course is designed to examine models, theories, tools, and techniques required in the design, facilitation, and evaluation of therapeutic recreation services for individuals and groups.

View requirements for REC 151

As the title implies, this course provides an overview of the foundations of therapeutic recreation. In this course, you will learn about the importance of having a philosophy, values/virtues and ethics that guide therapeutic recreation practice. The course examines therapeutic recreation in the context of diverse settings where therapeutic recreation professionals work, from clinical settings to more community-based settings, not-for-profit organizations, and social justice initiatives. It explores the different philosophical foundations and social theories that guide practice in these different settings and relevant to the critical exploration and practice of therapeutic recreation. Finally, it explores issues important to the practice of therapeutic recreation such as the therapeutic recreation process and practice models, specific therapeutic recreation facilitation techniques and modalities, program design, and approaches to assessment, documentation, and program evaluation.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Identify values/virtues and ethics important to their own therapeutic recreation practice
2. Apply components of the therapeutic recreation process relevant to a specific client/participant, including different approaches to assessment, planning, implementation, documentation, and evaluation
3. Analyze different theories, approaches, and models of practice for therapeutic recreation/recreation therapy and their relevance in different settings
4. Examine a range of therapeutic recreation facilitation techniques, modalities, and interventions commonly used in therapeutic recreation/recreation therapy
5. Work as part of a therapeutic recreation team to develop a comprehensive therapeutic recreation plan for a specific client/participant

Department Policy Regarding Late Assignments

Please familiarize yourself with the departmental policies regarding late assignments, missed quizzes and examinations, and reweighing of assignments. 

  1. Assignments are due in accordance with the dates indicated in the course outline. 10% will be immediately deducted if they are not submitted on time. An additional 10% will be deducted each day (including weekends) until the assignment is submitted.
  2. Exceptions will only be made under unforeseeable and extraordinary circumstances. Evidence of such circumstances must be provided to the instructor in the form of supporting documentation from an appropriate source e.g. doctor’s certificate, copy of obituary, letter from Counselling Services, etc. Students with acceptable documentation must contact the instructor no later than 48 hours after a due date to make alternative arrangements. In special unforeseeable circumstances where due dates cannot be met, it is the student’s responsibility to provide acceptable written documentation substantiating the reasons for not meeting the deadline (the “Request for Variance from a Course Outline” form may be used for this purpose). When there are exceptional circumstances, there will be no penalty assigned; if no contact is made, a mark of “zero” will be given.

Student Absences

It is your responsibility to be aware of the procedures related to absences due to illness or other reasons. The expectations outlined below are being implemented University-wide and apply to all undergraduate courses in Winter 2025. Please review these guidelines and be prepared to follow them should it become necessary for you to do so this term.

Absences in Quest

 1. Religious or creed-related absence

Undergraduate students can now submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration in Quest. This allows students to register the dates of potential conflict with coursework. As the dates of important religious observances are generally known well in advance, students must consult with their instructors within two weeks of the announcement of the due date or scheduled examination date for which academic accommodation is being sought.

 2. Short-term absence (once/term) – Undergraduate students only

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason (no documentation required). A short-term absence covers up to two calendar days during the formal lecture period. Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), and Studio (STU) courses/class components do not qualify for accommodation as a result of a self-declared short-term absence. There is no expectation that a self-declared short-term absence be total; that is, students may elect to participate in any course and complete any course element(s) despite not being required to. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

 3. COVID-19-related absence (once/term)

Students can self-declare an absence of up to 10 days due to COVID-19-related illness. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in online components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Absences Requiring Documentation - Absence Database – Undergraduate students only

 1. Absence due to illness  

When students are unable to meet academic obligations due to illness, they are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their Verification of Illness Form (VIF) to the site. The University of Waterloo Verification of Illness Form (VIF) is normally the only medical documentation accepted to support requests for academic consideration. Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation to their instructors, teaching assistants, or advisors. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their health condition. Once approved, the student and their instructors will receive a confirmation email of the student’s absence. Course instructors will determine how missed course components are accommodated.

 2. Absence due to extenuating circumstances

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (bereavement, family illness, varsity sports, etc.), students are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their official documentation (e.g., plane tickets, court documents, police reports, etc.) to the site. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact instructors within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their circumstances.

Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI)

GenAI that is trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code (e.g., Chat GPT, DALL-E, GitHub CoPilot) is permitted for some uses in this course (i.e., not all uses are permitted, and these guidelines apply to this course only.). 

Proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement of GenAI use is required as it is for any other source you may consult and reference (e.g., journal articles, textbooks).  Unauthorized use of GenAI in this course, such as submitting content generated by Artificial Intelligence (AI) without proper attribution, or representing content generated by AI as your own idea or work will be considered a violation of Policy 71 and treated as such. 

  • Permitted uses of GenAI:
    • Brainstorming or generating prompts/examples that encourage reflection
    • Refining your original idea
    • Supporting your editing process
  • Prohibited uses of GenAI:
    • Representing content generated by GenAI as your own without proper attribution.
    • Using GenAI without documenting your process or proper attribution.
    • Sharing copyright-protected materials with open access GenAI (e.g., inputting any course materials, such as lecture content, into a GenAI tool).

If you choose to use GenAI, you must ensure you: 

  • Maintain primary authorship and your own voice throughout your work.
  • Critically assess and evaluate whether output from GenAI is factual, verifiable, and relevant.
  • Do not rely on GenAI as your primary or sole source of information.
  • Attribute GenAI use properly using APA for all submissions.
  • Maintain documentation of where/how you’ve used GenAI in case proof is requested by the course instructor or Associate Dean.
  • Remember that you remain responsible for ensuring that your use of AI tools complies with copyright and intellectual property laws.

Resources for using GenAI:

  • To learn more about documenting and citing Generative Artificial Intelligence, visit the Writing and Communication Centre's AI and the Writing Process – Documenting and Citing page or the Library's Research Guide on Generative Artificial Intelligence.
  • The Writing and Communication Centre has a great resource on Using ChatGPT and other Text-Generating Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) and how to be productive and ethical in your use of GenAI as well as cautions and risks to consider.

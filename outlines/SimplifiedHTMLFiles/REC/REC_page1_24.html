Course Description

REC 455:

This course is designed to facilitate an in-depth exploration and analysis of philosophical issues and interdisciplinary theories to discuss how they relate to therapeutic recreation practice and research.

View requirements for REC 455

This course includes student-led seminars (see course schedule for dates).

Seminar 1 - BMH 1016

Seminar 2 - BMH 1703

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Reflect inwards and integrate course learnings to articulate a personal philosophy for deep therapeutic practice in therapeutic recreation
2. Examine (TR) philosophies and theories/frameworks in this course to engage in complex and critical thinking on TR practices.
3. Reflect outward to the TR profession and contemporary social, cultural, and political issues influencing TR practices.
4. Identify ways to contribute to innovation, advocacy, and action within the TR profession.
Vision for the Course - Our vision for REC 455 is to create a dialogical space for reflective practice in which learners:
1. Inform their own philosophical foundation to grapple with the complexity surrounding professional practice in TR.
2. Engage in deep reflective practice around core topics in therapeutic recreation.
3. Engage in creating their own learning environment through dialogue and shared leadership.
4. Become a "community" of practitioners in which dialogue (collective reflection) around core issues in which TR occurs (strengthening both individual and collective understanding).

Q: Sounds great. So, what exactly are we learning?

A: The ILOs and course vision, described above, create a framework for deepening our understanding of the following:

(TR) Philosophy - (TR) Practice & Application - (TR) Theory/Frameworks - (TR) Research/Knowledge Building & Sharing - Personal (TR) Practitioner Philosophy 

Practice, frameworks, theory, and research are not so simply teased apart. In each of our meetings and in your individual reflection, if you look closely, you will see how many of these aspects of knowing, feeling, and doing TR occur simultaneously. My hope is that you have the opportunity to experience this complexity and see how each comes together to shape your unique understanding and practice of TR. In short, we will 1) think critically about what you’ve learned about TR so far, 2) (re)learn TR through philosophy, and 3) (re-)construct your landscape of TR by developing a personal TR philosophy statement.

Principles that Guide our Teaching Philosophy for REC 455

Teaching and learning is an art and a science and it requires deep listening, dialogue, and the active engagement of teacher-learners in relationship. Education and learning is a lifelong process, and the conversations we begin in REC 455 are a continuation of conversations you started in other courses in your undergraduate degree and the beginning of some new ones as you launch into your careers.

Excerpt from Arai, Berbary, and Dupuis (2015):

We have to be ready to engage in all of these conversations and engage practice to critically explore the intertwined implications and possibilities of recreation and leisure in all of its complexities. The field must encourage fluidity and multiplicity in our approaches and practices so that TR remains historically and culturally relevant for those who engage its transformative powers. This requires that we break out of the silos we have created dividing us into groups of practitioners, educators, and researchers – we must each be all three and this calls all of us to:

  1. Embrace diversity and difference –understanding there are multiple versions of the world and different perspectives open up our view to things we may not have seen before.
  2. Challenge education – rather than accepting a banking approach to education we must deeply explore and understand multiple theories and ways of learning/knowing and how each works when put into practice, including what becomes possible and what gets denied.
  3. Embrace ambiguity – letting go of rigid thinking that moves us on a quest for the one right answer, the best practice that will work in every situation. This also calls for us to give up a quest for control and the sorting of things into categories in which ideas and people are seen as bounded and separate.
  4. Engage in compassionate critique– as we reflect on our own ideas and practices and those of others there is a need to stay open to contextual and historical understanding, holding in mind the intention to serve that has fueled other theories and paradigms.
  5. Approach practice with humility– connected to the idea of there being no right answer, no one is all knowing or able to predict, protect others, or pronounce outcomes.
  6. Embrace vulnerability – being experience-near in practice requires that we come closer to the experiences of others and also our own. This also calls for us to become more comfortable with, and able to hold, emotion and not-knowing and to see these as strengths and opportunities for something new and unpredicted to emerge.
  7. Look into the paradox– when reflecting on theory, language, practices, and actions in therapeutic recreation consider not only what it does, but also what it protects us from seeing or knowing. It is in this practice that we are more likely to see the unintended harms of TR. When we engage in reflective practice it is important for us to recognize not only where we are already situated and what we see, think, feel, and do but also aspects of the same that go unrecognized.

In short, we need to engage multiple theories in practice and therapeutic recreation should now, and forever, be up for grabs and critically troubled (pp. 317-318).

1. Person Profile (2%) I The Personal Profile form can be found on LEARN and asks you questions about yourself and your experiences. Completion of your Personal Profile will help us get to know each other and provide useful information on how to make this course the most beneficial for you. There are no right or wrong answers on this inventory, they are simply used to help maximize your learning experience. All responses will be kept confidential and will only be read by the teaching staff on this course. The Personal Profile is due to the corresponding LEARN Dropbox by Monday, January 13, 2025, by 11:59pm.

2. Shared Leadership in Learning (20%) | Creating a learning environment involves the active engagement of each person in REC 455. We can come to understand diversity and difference and collectively create new knowledge by hearing different voices and perspectives in the classroom. Consequently, a portion of your grade will be assigned to your weekly participation in our weekly seminars. Each member of REC 455 is responsible for attending lecture, their weekly seminar and to contribute to the discussions and activities being facilitated. The reflection papers will be based on your engagement in the seminar unless otherwise stated. See LEARN for more details.

3. Seminar Facilitation (25%) | As a group of two (or three) you will lead a seminar discussion about the assigned readings for your assigned week. Your goal is to promote dialogue of the readings among your fellow students. The purpose of your seminar facilitation is to guide an exploration of the readings; it is not to summarize the articles, but rather to encourage a better understanding of the core ideas presented in the readings through group discussion. How participants will be engaged is up to the facilitators to decide. Examples of participation include guided discussions, games, role playing, case studies, etc. The time presenters have to facilitate discussion/participation is limited to 40 minutes each.

You are responsible for the following:

  • A 2-3-page written summary of main ideas and concepts that underlie your seminar. Please submit a copy of this summary to LEARN on the day of your seminar facilitation (7%).
  • A 40-minute seminar facilitation based on your submitted summary sheet (15%).
  • A one-page self/peer evaluation of your seminar (due the Friday after your facilitation) (3%).

See Seminar Facilitation Package on LEARN for more details.

Some aspects your facilitation may be evaluated on include:

  • Creativity (originality of ideas, activities, and group involvement)
  • Relatedness to reading (is the reading being related to material covered in lecture to encourage deeper understanding of the topics)
  • Ability to include all seminar members (all people are included, and presenter is able to facilitate participation/discussion)
  • Flow (are the presenters prepared? Is the presentation/facilitation flowing smoothly?)
  • Depth of discussion (is time used well to make important connections?)

4. Participant Studies (2 x 12% = 24%) | In this course we seek to grow knowledge from individual and collective experiences of our therapeutic recreation practices. There is a growing belief that if we are to learn from our experiences, reflection is a key in that process. In her text on reflective practice, Wang (2012) notes, “experience teaches nothing to the non-reflective practitioner” (p. 59). To this end, throughout the term you will complete two participant study assignments related to the weekly topics and required readings that have you reflect on your learnings and readings and apply them to a participant (see Preparatory Work document).

Format of the assignment | You will be asked a series of questions about an assigned participant. Your papers will incorporate your reflections on the readings assigned from the weeks previous and content from seminars and ask you to apply your learning to these participants. Since you are speaking in your own voice in these reflection papers, it is completely appropriate for you to use “I” in your writing. Please use APA formatting for font, margins, style of headings, citations, and your list of references. This is an opportunity for you to practice writing with purpose and stating your ideas clearly. A strong participant study reflection will demonstrate strong reading comprehension and synthesis of the topic of the weeks included, connections drawn between readings and issues that face folx in TR, and deep reflection on self and society in relation to relevant discussions related to the course.

Due Dates | Participant Study 1 due Tuesday, February 11th at 11:59pm. Participant Study 2 due Tuesday, March 11th at 11:59pm. See LEARN for full assignment details.

5. TR Practitioner Panel (4%) I We will invite individuals who work in various TR contexts into the classroom to share their experiences. This is a great opportunity for all of you to ask any questions you may have and listen to the experiences of individuals working in various contexts and/or with different populations in the field.

Our TR practitioner panel will occur during lecture time on Tuesday March 25th from 11:30 - 12:45pm.

For this component you are responsible for:

  1. Submitting two questions to be asked during the TR practitioner panel (due to the corresponding dropbox by 11:59pm on Friday, March 14th (1%)
  2. Completion of the TR practitioner panel reflection document due Monday, March 31 at 11:59pm (3%)

A document with the bios of each of our guest speakers will be shared on Learn. You can use these bios and information to form your provided questions.

6. Final ePortfolio Reflection Assignment (25%) | This is your chance to return to everything you have learned through REC 455 and (re)construct and articulate your personal (TR) practitioner philosophy. The ePortfolio will provide you with a brave space to share what you have learned and provide evidence to demonstrate this. Using PebblePad or Padlet (link in LEARN), you will create a Reflection Portfolio that will articulate your personal philosophy for deep practice in TR. You will be asked to reflect upon concepts such as: My TR Philosophy, My Definition of TR, My Personal Strengths and Resources, My View of the TR Process, Myself as a Strengths-Based Advocate, and My Vision for the future of TR. A sample portfolio will be provided, which will outline the specific tabs and indicate the questions to reflect on and answer (see LEARN for details). Submit link to portfolio to Dropbox by Wednesday, April 9, 2025, at 11:59pm.

Student Absences

It is your responsibility to be aware of the procedures related to absences due to illness or other reasons. The expectations outlined below are being implemented University-wide and apply to all undergraduate courses in Winter 2025. Please review these guidelines and be prepared to follow them should it become necessary for you to do so this term.

Absences in Quest

 1. Religious or creed-related absence

Undergraduate students can now submit a Religious Observance Self-Declaration in Quest. This allows students to register the dates of potential conflict with coursework. As the dates of important religious observances are generally known well in advance, students must consult with their instructors within two weeks of the announcement of the due date or scheduled examination date for which academic accommodation is being sought.

 2. Short-term absence (once/term) – Undergraduate students only

Students can self-declare one short-term absence per term on Quest for any reason (no documentation required). A short-term absence covers up to two calendar days during the formal lecture period. Laboratory (LAB), Clinic (CLN), and Studio (STU) courses/class components do not qualify for accommodation as a result of a self-declared short-term absence. There is no expectation that a self-declared short-term absence be total; that is, students may elect to participate in any course and complete any course element(s) despite not being required to. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 24 hours after submitting their self-declaration to discuss accommodations for missed course components.

 3. COVID-19-related absence (once/term)

Students can self-declare an absence of up to 10 days due to COVID-19-related illness. No in-person course activity is permitted during the period of a self-declared COVID absence. Participation in online components is permitted but should not be required if the student notifies the instructor that they are too ill to participate in the online work. Submitting the self-declaration through Quest will notify your instructors of your absence. It is the student’s responsibility to contact their instructors within the first 48 hours after submitting their COVID-related absence, or as soon as they are well enough, to discuss arrangements for missed course components.

Absences Requiring Documentation - Absence Database – Undergraduate students only

 1. Absence due to illness  

When students are unable to meet academic obligations due to illness, they are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their Verification of Illness Form (VIF) to the site. The University of Waterloo Verification of Illness Form (VIF) is normally the only medical documentation accepted to support requests for academic consideration. Students should not submit their VIF or any other medical documentation to their instructors, teaching assistants, or advisors. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact the instructor within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their health condition. Once approved, the student and their instructors will receive a confirmation email of the student’s absence. Course instructors will determine how missed course components are accommodated.

 2. Absence due to extenuating circumstances

For absences due to extenuating circumstances (bereavement, family illness, varsity sports, etc.), students are required to complete the Verification of Illness or Extenuating Circumstances web form (log in required) and upload their official documentation (e.g., plane tickets, court documents, police reports, etc.) to the site. To request and arrange accommodations, it is the student’s responsibility to contact instructors within 48 hours of the date of any missed course component, or as soon as the student is able given their circumstances.

Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI)

GenAI that is trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code (e.g., Chat GPT, DALL-E, GitHub CoPilot) is permitted for some uses in this course (i.e., not all uses are permitted, and these guidelines apply to this course only.). 

Proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement of GenAI use is required as it is for any other source you may consult and reference (e.g., journal articles, textbooks).  Unauthorized use of GenAI in this course, such as submitting content generated by Artificial Intelligence (AI) without proper attribution, or representing content generated by AI as your own idea or work will be considered a violation of Policy 71 and treated as such. 

  • Permitted uses of GenAI:
    • Brainstorming or generating prompts/examples that encourage reflection
    • Refining your original idea
    • Supporting your editing process
  • Prohibited uses of GenAI:
    • Representing content generated by GenAI as your own without proper attribution.
    • Using GenAI without documenting your process or proper attribution.
    • Sharing copyright-protected materials with open access GenAI (e.g., inputting any course materials, such as lecture content, into a GenAI tool).

If you choose to use GenAI, you must ensure you: 

  • Maintain primary authorship and your own voice throughout your work.
  • Critically assess and evaluate whether output from GenAI is factual, verifiable, and relevant.
  • Do not rely on GenAI as your primary or sole source of information.
  • Attribute GenAI use properly using APA for all submissions.
  • Maintain documentation of where/how you’ve used GenAI in case proof is requested by the course instructor or Associate Dean.
  • Remember that you remain responsible for ensuring that your use of AI tools complies with copyright and intellectual property laws.

Resources for using GenAI:

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of the instructor, Alyssa Vanwyck, and any guest lecturers. Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or guest lecturer, or used by the instructor, guest lecturer, or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. I provide you with robust lecture notes and assignment outlines to support your success in the classroom. However, sharing this intellectual property without my permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, guest lecturer, and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

You may not distribute or reproduce the materials, whether for commercial purposes or not, without the express written consent of the faculty or instructor. Neither the faculty nor the instructors waive their intellectual property rights when granting limited use of their course materials to students.

There are institutional guidelines pertaining to organizations external to the University of Waterloo that make available teaching resources from the University of Waterloo.

Uploading course materials, including lectures, quizzes, and exams, onto online sharing platforms such as Course Hero, OneClass, and similar platforms is considered a copyright violation, regardless of whether an individual did so intentionally or unintentionally. Ignorance of the law is not an excuse. 

 

University Policy

Academic integrity: In order to maintain a culture of academic integrity, members of the University of Waterloo community are expected to promote honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.]

Grievance: A student who believes that a decision affecting some aspect of their university life has been unfair or unreasonable may have grounds for initiating a grievance. Read Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances, Section 4. When in doubt, please be certain to contact the department’s administrative assistant who will provide further assistance.

Discipline: A student is expected to know what constitutes academic integrity to avoid committing an academic offence, and to take responsibility for their actions. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.] A student who is unsure whether an action constitutes an offence, or who needs help in learning how to avoid offences (e.g., plagiarism, cheating) or about “rules” for group work/collaboration should seek guidance from the course instructor, academic advisor, or the undergraduate associate dean. For information on categories of offences and types of penalties, students should refer to Policy 71, Student Discipline. For typical penalties, check Guidelines for the Assessment of Penalties.

Appeals: A decision made or penalty imposed under Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances (other than a petition) or Policy 71, Student Discipline may be appealed if there is a ground. A student who believes they have a ground for an appeal should refer to Policy 72, Student Appeals.

Note for students with disabilities: AccessAbility Services, located in Needles Hall, Room 1401, collaborates with all academic departments to arrange appropriate accommodations for students with disabilities without compromising the academic integrity of the curriculum. If you require academic accommodations to lessen the impact of your disability, please register with AccessAbility Services at the beginning of each academic term.

Turnitin.com: Text matching software (Turnitin®) may be used to screen assignments in this course. Turnitin® is used to verify that all materials and sources in assignments are documented. Students' submissions are stored on a U.S. server, therefore students must be given an alternative (e.g., scaffolded assignment or annotated bibliography), if they are concerned about their privacy and/or security. Students will be given due notice, in the first week of the term and/or at the time assignment details are provided, about arrangements and alternatives for the use of Turnitin in this course.

It is the responsibility of the student to notify the instructor if they, in the first week of term or at the time assignment details are provided, wish to submit alternate assignment.

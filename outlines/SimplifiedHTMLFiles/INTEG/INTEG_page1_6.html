Course Description

INTEG 230:

Introduction to the issues and intent of the Museum Course. Planning and research for the destination. Field trip to study museums, exhibits, audiences, and curatorial practices on-site in a major museum city. For additional information on itinerary and travel costs, contact the Department.

View requirements for INTEG 230

INTEG 230 builds on the Observation and Design Skills introduced in INTEG 121, and adds domain-specific knowledge about museums and learning in order to prepare you for your Field Trip – whatever form it takes. You’re building relationships with the people you’ll travel with, learn with, and eventually create an exhibit with in INTEG 320 and 321. We’ll lay the groundwork, but expect you to shoulder the responsibility for your – and others’ – success.

Information regarding the Field Trip that follows this course can be found on the LEARN Field Trip community site.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Analyze, classify and summarize Museums, Exhibits and other Public Sites: using a variety of theoretical frameworks; from a variety of design viewpoints
Demonstrate and appreciate knowledge of the Field Trip City: familiarity with its layout and range of museums, sights and activities; confidence to safely and quickly navigate its streets and transit system; prioritized, personal plan for what to see while traveling
Contribute and partake as a member of a Cohesive Travel Cohort: cooperative and collaborative behaviour; document and share individual discoveries; collaborate with every member of the travel cohort; enjoyably and productively explore the Field Trip city

Late Submissions

Each student will be allocated a total of 4 grace days, which allow deliverables to be handed in late without penalty. Grace days used for group deliverables will be subtracted from EACH member of the group. Late days, including weekends, will be tracked individually. At the end of the term, I will total the number of late days, subtract 4, and each student will receive a 1% penalty on their overall grade for each late day beyond the 4 grace days. 

Grace days cannot be used for the PK slides (D3).  Readings (D1) have their own late mechanism, detailed in the D1 description.

Alternate arrangements

Students with valid justification may make alternate arrangements with the instructor prior to the due date.

Use of Generative AI

Note that this is my position, in my course.  Other instructors and courses may vary.

  • Generative AI tools such as ChatGPT or DALL-E can provide a great starting point for thoughts.
  • These tools are imperfect and should not be blindly trusted.
  • These tools can be ethically problematic.
  • Like any other external resource you might use in producing your work, your use of them must be properly acknowledged otherwise you could be guilty of a Policy 71 violation.
  • Like any work you submit based in part on external resources, you should expect your grade to reflect the amount of original content and thought in your submission.  If you submit work that is entirely assembled from external sources without original thought (even if sources are properly cited), then you are not guilty of plagiarism but you might appropriately expect to receive a low grade.
  • In this course, any work you submit that is based in part on generative AI must include a cover paragraph explaining when you used GenAI as a tool, what it contributed to your work, and what you did with the information it provided.  Use must also be appropriately cited.

More resources, from University of Waterloo guidelines, can be found in the Administrative Policy section below.

In-class discussion & activities

Students are expected to be prepared for thoughtful, relevant, and respectful contributions to class discussions and small-group activities. Absorb the readings; do the exercises; offer input / feedback; engage in the class.

Individual vs. Group Work

For individual work, discussing assignments can improve your understanding and learning.  However, you must still produce original work from individual effort, maintaining your academic integrity. A simple trick to avoid copying and other academic offenses (in this class and others!): take a one-hour break after your discussions and before writing up the idea. The ideas will come out in your own words, ensuring that you have integrated them – acquired new knowledge – rather than just reproducing the discussion.

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images.

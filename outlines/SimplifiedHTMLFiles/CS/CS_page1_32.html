Course Description

CS 330:

An introduction to information systems and their strategic role in business. Topics include types of information systems, organizational requirements, systems development strategies, decision support systems, data and information management, and information systems management, control, and implementation.

View requirements for CS 330

This course is designed for non-CS students who are interested in continuing their study of computer science beyond first year

Topics include types of information systems, organizational requirements, systems development strategies, decision support systems, data and information management, and information systems manage- ment, control and implementation.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
assess trade-offs in technological solutions, such as build, rent or buy decisions;
make informed decisions about using technology in a business environment;
define and explain the characteristics of a variety of business information systems;
describe strategic roles and management usage for information systems in business; and
understand the relationships among various information systems.

The course has the following learning objectives:

  1. To gain an understanding of basic concepts related to the design and of information systems in business and other settings.
  2. To understand of the impact (detrimental and beneficial) that information systems play in supporting (possibly even determining) the strategic direction of modern organizations.
  3. Be familiar with emerging issues facing organizations as they seek to exploit the power of information technology.

Thematic Topics

Module content is delivered using a collection of video lectures with a set of accompanying printable slides. Both can be found in LEARN, organised by lecture.

These are the some of the topics we will explore this term:

  • IT Infrastructure
    • All the parts that make IT work
  • Trends and Technology Drivers
    • What’s driving change?
  • Databases
    • Organising data to mine information
  • Networks
    • Connecting stuff together
  • Management Information Systems
    • How businesses use computers
  • Business Processes
    • The business of running a business
  • Organisations and IS
    • The human side of MIS
  • Social, Ethical and Legal Issues
    • Why privacy, security, IP, all matter
  • Managing Knowledge
    • How knowledge gets shared

The recorded lectures were created from a previous live class offering and thus the videos are based on how on-campus lectures work where we can stop at any point and then pick up again the next day.

For online courses, we would normally create videos that cover a complete topic rather than a block of time. So we’ve got a bit of a challenge ahead of us as I try to adapt “campus lectures” (what we have) for “online” (what we are) use. But working together, we’ll figure it out.

It is complex enough though that I’m not going to try to present a schedule of days and topics here in the outline. For each module you will find a START HERE page in LEARN. The START HERE page helps you focus on concepts that are important to grasp, readings you should complete, slides you should pay attention to (and maybe some slides you can skip over), tasks to complete, etc.

Watch the video called “So Many Videos, So Many Slides” to better understand where to place your focus when following long with the slides and videos.

There are some rules that are common to many CS courses with respect to final grades.

Note these carefully:

  1. In order to pass the course you must earn an overall course grade of at least 50% using the allocation algorithm above AND
  2. You must have earned a grade of at least 50% on the combined score of your midterm + final.

In situations where either of these conditions are not met, the grade awarded will be 46% or less – irrespective of the mathematical result obtained using the grade allocation algorithm above.

Your final grade is determined using the breakdown above but can be affected (upwards ordownwards) by the Effort Adjustment Factor. The Effort Adjustment Factor is used in lieu of having a participation grade.

Watch the video on how the Effort Adjustment Factor works. The video can be found in the IMPORTANT COURSE INFORMATION folder in LEARN.

I find it helpful to remind everyone that online learning does not mean independent study. This is not a course where you can decide what to do, how much to do, and when to do it. Everyone in the class, including me, is counting on your involvement.

Details on the deliverables that contribute to your evaluation are described in the detailed course outline. Below are mainly due dates:

Self-tests (Quizzes) (12 quizzes = 8% total)

The self-tests are short and are intended to be easy if you have completed the related readings and tasks. Self-tests are completed inside LEARN and can be found under SUBMIT >> QUIZZES.

Self-tests are due Monday at 23h59 in the week that follows the related online lecture. This means the quiz for lectures during week 1 is due on the Monday of week 2, etc.

Course Outline Quiz (2%)

There is a course outline quiz that is designed to ensure you are familiar with the course structure a nd is due on the Monday of Week 2 at 23h59. (13 Jan 2025)

Individual Thinking Tasks (20% - 2 at 10% each)

There are 2 individual thinking tasks. These usually involve solving a problem of some type. Each is to be completed individually. The due dates are:

  • Task1 – Wed 05 Feb 2025 at 23h59 EST
  • Task2 – Mon 24 Feb 2025 at 23h59 EST

We will use Crowdmark to manage submissions for the individual thinking tasks.

Group Activity Task (15%)

Your group will develop a business plan for a strategic information system. I will post more information about this activity after you have watched the lecture on Strategic Information Systems. (Occurs in Week 2.) 2/3rds of your grade for this task will be awarded by me. The other 1/3 will be awarded by others in the class. (So each group will not only create a business plan, each of you will have the chance to evaluate a business plan created by another group.)

The due dates are:

  • Group Submission – Fri Mar 14 2025 at 23h59
  • Evaluation Due – Fri Mar 28 2025 at 23h59

Midterm + Final (20% midterm/35% final)

The midterm is scheduled for Thursday March 6, 2025 from 19h00 – 20h50 EST and can be completed online.

If you are unable to write the midterm for acceptable reasons, your final exam score will be used as your midterm score. (There is no make-up midterm exam.)

The final exam will be held in person. The examination date and room location will be announced by the Registrar when the Registrar posts the exam period details for the term.

The final exam for this course will be held on the Waterloo campus – NOT in Stratford. You must attend in person to write the final exam in Waterloo.

Remember, never to make personal plans (like buying an airplane ticket) until after the examination schedule is released by the Registrar. Regrettably, the University does not accept a Caribbean holiday (or even your cousin’s friend’s sister’s wedding) as an acceptable reason to miss a final examination.

Notice of Recording

This course includes the opportunity to join a live video session held during a scheduled tutorial slot in your timetable.

Whereas in a classroom you have a bit of anonymity, the technology needed to allow streaming video strips some of that away. As a professor who teaches our security and privacy courses, I’m sensitive to the potential impact.

To respect the privacy rights of those attending, I will not be recording our live sessions. Nor should you. (Just as you shouldn’t be recording a live lecture without the instructor’s permission.)

The university has adopted a code of practice regarding live video which you can read here. The code of practice describes the options available to you designed to respect any particular need for privacy that you may have. Pay particular attention to the Notice of Recording that appears as an Appendix in the code of practice.

Please speak with me directly so that I can help accommodate any specific needs that you may have with respect to synchronous classes.

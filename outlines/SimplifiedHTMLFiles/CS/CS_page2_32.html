Course Description

CS 479 / CS 679:

An introduction to neural network methods, with some discussion of their relevance to neuroscience. Simple neuron models and networks of neurons. Training feedforward networks for classification or regression. Learning using the backpropagation of errors. Unsupervised learning methods. Optimal linear decoding. Recurrent neural networks. Convolutional neural networks. Advanced topics, including adversarial inputs and biologically plausible learning methods.

CS 479: View requirements for CS 479

CS 679: View requirements for CS 679

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Write a program to simulate the activity of a network of neurons
Formulate a neural learning method as gradient-based optimization
Derive a neural learning method based on energy minimization
Design neural-network solutions for different learning scenarios
Identify some commonalities between artificial neural networks and the brain

Passing Average on Exam Components

In order to pass CS 479, you must have a passing average (at least 50%) on the written exam component of the course. That is, if you get M out of 20 on your midterm, and F out of 50 on your final exam, then you need (M+F)≥35(M+F)\ge35(M+F)≥35to pass the course, regardless of what your course average is.

Assignments

There will be 5 assignments, approximately one every 2 weeks. All submissions will be electronic. Some questions will be mathematical derivations or proofs, and some questions will involve programming in Python.

For each programming question, you will submit your solutions through Github Classroom, of as a PDF to Crowdmark. Instructions will be given for each programming assignment. We will use Python (version 3.9 or later).

For each non-programming question, you will submit a PDF or JPEG to Crowdmark, either of handwritten solutions (scanned or photographed), or typeset (eg. using Microsoft Word, LaTeX, etc.). If a photo of handwritten work is submitted, it is your responsibility to ensure that it is legible. Solutions that are not legible may be assessed a penalty.

Due Dates

Late Policy

Your assignment solutions should be submitted online before the deadline. When multiple files are submitted, your official assignment submission time will be determined by your last submission. Also, if you submit solutions more than once, only your most recent submission will be graded.

If your assignment is late, your assignment grade will be multiplied by a lateness scaling factor, s. This factor decreases by 10% per hour (on the hour), and then drops to zero after 10 hours. For example, if your assignment is 4 hours late, then the scaling factor is 0.6.

Midterm Exam

There will be a midterm exam from 7:00-9:00pm on Wednesday February 12, 2025. You will be allowed to bring a crib sheet; you can put anything you want on one side of an 8.5x11" piece of paper.

If you miss the midterm due to an absence, the weight of the midterm will be shifted to your final assessment (final exam for students taking CS 479, and final project for students taking CS 679).

Final Assessment

Final Exam

If you are registered in CS 479, you will write a 2.5-hour final exam, meant to test your knowledge about all parts of the course. You will be allowed to bring a crib sheet; you can put anything you want on both sides of an 8.5x11" piece of paper.

Project

Only students registered in CS 679 can do a project. The projects will be done in groups of 2 students, and will involve implementing a method from a paper, and reproducing some of its results. If you desire some inspiration, a list of example papers will be posted.

Your group must submit a project proposal by March 7. Your group must also submit a final report and github repository by April 18. More information about the project will be given at a later date.

Administrative Policy

Re-Grading Requests

Did we give you too few marks (or too many)? You can request to have part of your assessment reviewed. Here's how.

Submit a private message to the instructors on piazza describing specifically what you think the error was with your grading. Make sure to label the post with the "regrade" folder, as well as the appropriate assessment folder (eg. "a2"). All re-grade requests must be made within 7 days of when the assessment was returned to you.

Two-Day Absence

If you declare a 2-day absence and there is an assignment deadline during that absence, you have two choices:

  1. The weight from the missed assignment can be put on your final assessment, or
  2. The assignment deadline is given a 48-hour extension.

For example, suppose assignment 1 (A1) is due at 11:59pm on Jan 22, and is worth 9% of your total grade, and you declare a 2-day absence that overlaps that deadline. If you choose option 1, then the 9% for A1 will shift onto your final assessment (exam, or project, as appropriate), which would then be worth 59% of your total grade (instead of 50%). If you choose option 2, your deadline for A1 would be 11:59pm on Jan 24, and the late policy would extend for 10 hours after that.

You must inform the professor of your absence in writing via email (not on Piazza), and state which option you would like. Note that neither option will be applied by default. You have to explicitly choose one of the options, and indicate your choice in an email to Prof. Orchard/Prof. Hibat-Allah before the original deadline.

If you miss the midterm due to an absence, the weight of the midterm will be shifted to your final assessment (final exam for students taking CS 479, and final project for students taking CS 679).

Continuity of In-Person Course Components

If there is an interruption in our ability to meet in-person for lectures, the course will continue in a modified form. Videos of the lectures will be posted in the Resources page on Piazza. Students will be expected to watch these lecture videos in lieu of attending class. Regularly scheduled Zoom sessions will be held to answer questions about the lecture material or assignments.

The assignment procedures will not be affected by a cancellation of in-person classes since they are distributed and submitted online.

If the in-person final exam is cancelled, I will instead issue a take-home final exam. Students will be asked to sign a confidentiality and academic integrity agreement before writing the exam. Students will be given a 24-hour period during which the exam must be written. The exam will likely be a combination of two parts: (1) questions that should be answered by hand-written or typeset solutions and submitted to Crowdmark, and (2) an online quiz. Once a student starts the quiz, they will have a fixed window of time to complete it.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgment. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class, and are outlined as follows: 

  • What you can do: GenAI can be used as a smart substitute for Google search, e.g., code debugging, finding plotting commands. Cite GenAI when it is used and where it helped. Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai
  • What you can't do: GenAI must not be used to code up the logic of a solution, or part of a solution. It cannot be used to generate significant portions of code. Students are encouraged to read more about the ethical use of GenAI here: https://uwaterloo.ca/academic-integrity/artificial-intelligence-and-chatgpt#tipsforstudents . If you are not sure when it is ethical/permitted to use GenAI, please consult with your instructors.

Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

CS 492:

This course is designed to consider the problems encountered by individuals, organizations and society as computer technology is adopted, with a view towards assessing possible courses of action.

View requirements for CS 492

CS 492, as a whole, considers the impact of computing on individuals, organizations, and society. Students learn how to make informed judgements about the social and ethical consequences of deploying computer technologies and how to foster critical thought and professional responsibility.

Section 003 of CS 492, in particular, will explore in depth the many ways in which modern computing systems—including the data they ingest, the decisions made by the people who develop them, and their myriad and nearly ubiquitous applications—may enable, encourage, or prevent societal discrimination or privacy violations of various types. Students will examine how algorithms and artificial intelligence (“AI”) systems may provide differential treatment and/or outcomes for different populations, and how they may invade privacy and cause other harms to individuals and society. Students will also consider the potential technical, legal/regulatory, and social/policy interventions that could ameliorate the harms caused by such algorithms and systems, and will weigh the advantages and disadvantaged of each. 

By the end of the seminar, students should be able to (i) discuss with colleagues and others the positive and negative discriminatory and privacy consequences of various current AI applications and other technological innovations, and (ii) suggest different approaches to address systemic bias or privacy violations—including technical solutions and legal/regulatory or social/policy changes—and explain why certain of these approaches might or might not work in specific circumstances.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Identify important social implications of computerization;
Describe how to behave ethically as a computer scientist;
Propose technological and social/policy solutions to current challenges presented by computerization (i.e., recommended actions);
Combine their opinions with information from existing research in both written and oral formats;
Collaborate with peers on open-ended topics where there is often no clear, definitive response;
Formulate clear, constructive feedback on arguments constructed by their peers;
Identify where their knowledge of computer science provides a valuable contribution to the overall dialogue about the social and ethical implications of computing.

for CS 492, as a whole.

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Proactively identify potential ethical issues related to data and new technologies and applications;
Evaluate the potential risks and benefits of different courses of action that could be taken with respect to data and new technologies and applications;
Succinctly and confidently articulate and justify your views on ethical issues related to data and new technologies and applications;
Take ownership of and accept responsibility for the impact of your work as a computer or data scientist, on various constituents, in particular, on marginalized populations.

for CS 492 Section 003 in particular

Student Assessment

Component Value
Weekly Commentaries or Position Papers 30%
Class Participation 30%
Small-Group Project 40%

This course has neither a midterm nor a final exam. Evaluation in this course will be comprised of the following three components:

a. Weekly Commentaries or Position Papers: 30%

  • There will be weekly writing assignments, which will consist of either commentaries on the readings or position papers (i.e., thought pieces). There will be around eleven (11) such papers assigned; only the top six (6) will count towards a student’s grade. Students may submit as many of the assignments as they wish. Students will receive regular, written feedback on their papers from one of the three TAs. The prompt for the weekly commentary or position paper will be provided at the end of class each week and will also be posted on the course Slack channel. Unless otherwise specified, the weekly paper will be due the following Monday night at 11:59 PM to the Dropbox for this course on LEARN. Please submit the paper in either .docx or .pdf format.
  • The six (6) best commentaries or position papers submitted by a student will each count for 5% of the student’s final grade. Therefore, the written component of the course will account for a total of 30% of a student’s final grade. Papers will be graded on a scale of zero to five. Should a student wish, they may submit any commentary or position paper to the instructor by email in an editable format (i.e., docx) for additional feedback beyond that received from the TA, particularly with respect to improving their writing style.
  • The weekly commentaries or position papers should be approximately two pages in length (i.e., 1000 words). We will provide some examples of strong papers from previous offerings of this course and similar courses on Slack, as well as the rubric the TAs will use for grading the papers, which offers some suggestions on what constitutes a strong paper for this course.
  • The University of Waterloo’s Writing and Communications Centre (“WCC”) works across all faculties to help students clarify their ideas, develop their voices, and write in the style(s) appropriate to their discipline(s). Centre staff offer one-on-one support in planning assignments and presentations, using and documenting research, organizing and structuring papers, and revising for clarity and coherence. We particularly encourage using the WCC for students whose English or writing skills are not at the level where they wish them to be. Staff from the WCC will also offer a “lab” on how to create a thesis statement and develop arguments around it as one of the third- hour sessions of CS 492 Section 003.
  • A note on the use of large language models (“LLMs”) and generative AI systems: While it is my strong preference that students not use these systems and prepare their journal entries entirely on their own, students are permitted to use such systems to improve the technical quality of their writing, but they may not use them to do their writing for them. For example, you may not ask an LLM to generate your weekly commentary or position paper, but you may draft your commentary or position paper and ask an LLM to assist you in improving it (i.e., correcting your spelling or grammar, idiom, punctuation, or sentence mechanics, clarity, or brevity, but nothing beyond this). You may certainly use generative AI to include images or artwork with your commentary or position paper. If you do use one of these systems, you must inform us that you have done so and how you used it. A good rule of thumb is the following. If you cannot fully explain your entry and how you came up with the ideas expressed in it, you have likely misused the LLM or generative AI system and you should redo your paper. In other words, the ideas should be your own; you may obtain assistance in expressing your ideas better, particularly if communicating in written English is challenging for you. You may certainly supplement your ideas using AI-generated imagery; just say so.

b. Class Participation: 30%

  • Students’ class participation will be assessed based on overall thoughtfulness and quality, rather than overall quantity. What I am looking for is for students to demonstrate meaningful engagement with the material, each other, and me, and for students to be able justify their arguments and respond respectfully to contrary positions presented by others. During the course of the term, students are likely to be asked to advance or defend positions that may differ from their own personal views.
  • As mentioned above, suggesting articles, videos, or podcasts for the “From-the-News” component of the class will count towards a student’s class participation grade, as will contributing meaningfully to any discussions in the course Slack channel.
  • Students for whom communicating orally or in English is a challenge or who are introverted may need to work especially hard for this component of the course. Please let me know how I can help you participate if English is not your first language or you are introverted (for example, should I call on you?).

c. Small-Group Project: 40%

  • There will be a small-group project due at the end of the term that will be completed by a team comprised of between two and four students. Each team will submit a joint work product that, depending on the group’s preference, will consist of some kind of creative project, such as the development of an online program or application, website, chatbot, game, video presentation, conducting a series of interviews or questionnaires, or conducting other research related to a topic covered in the course.
  • I urge you not to do a podcast for this small-group project unless you have something novel to say or a particularly unique style for presenting the material; it is too easy to just make a chatty conversation with not much content, which will not garner a good grade on the project. If you insist on doing a podcast, please focus on a small number of topics, do research to find articles, videos, or other materials to discuss that are not on the course syllabus, keep a tight thematic focus, and find a very creative way to present the topic.
  • Unlike the weekly commentaries or position papers, students are encouraged to experiment with LLMs and generative AI systems and to incorporate them as part of their small-group projects!
  • The small-group project should involve a technical, legal/regulatory, ethical, or social/policy issue of the sort addressed in the course, involving a computing topic that connects to discrimination, surveillance, and/or privacy. Teams will identify their topic, confirm the appropriateness of the topic with the instructor and TAs, divide the labour among their team members, and accommodate different perspectives, viewpoints, and expertise of the team members.
  • If students encounter any challenges in the small-group project work, they should contact the instructor and/or TAs promptly; in particular, please do not wait until the week before the project is due to indicate that group members have been unresponsive to their assignments. At that point there is nothing we can do to address the problem.
  • Teams may, but are not required, to prepare a short presentation to give at the final class session of the term. For students who choose to present, this will contribute to their class participation grade.
  • The small-group project will have two interim milestones due during the course of the term:
    • Milestone #1: By email to the instructor and TAs by 11:59 PM on Friday, February 14, 2025, each team will submit a one-page project proposal in editable format (i.e., .docx) identifying the group membership, providing the topic under consideration, and the form the project will take, with as much detail and specificity as possible. Tell us why the topic is interesting, how or why it is relevant to the seminar, and how you will explore the topic in your project. We will review and respond in writing to the proposal largely for the purpose of confirming the suitability of the topic and the form of the project, and to advise you whether what is described is consistent with the goals and objectives of the small-group project.
    • Milestone #2: By email to the instructor and TAs by 11:59 PM on Friday, March 14, 2025, each team will submit a two- to-three page progress report in editable format (i.e., .docx) describing the goal(s) of the project, what question(s) the team is seeking to answer, why the form of the project is appropriate to answer those questions, what research the team has undertaken thus far, and what the team has learned thus far. If any problems or challenges have arisen, they should be raised in the progress report. A key component of Milestone #2 is to describe the current research the group has done, and what challenges have been encountered (if any) in finding good resources or content for the project. We will again provide written feedback on the scope and level of seriousness of the research and identify any gaps or deficiencies in the project proposal, including around the research itself or its suitability for the small-group project.
  • The two Milestone submissions above will be considered as part of the overall grade for the small-group project.
  • The submission of the small-group project itself will consist of three or four components:
    1. A one-page description of the project, including the group members’ names, what question or topic was being addressed, what form the project took, and a summary of the findings. This may also include a link to any particular items you want to have considered in your grade on the project (e.g., a bibliography of your research, your source code, etc.).
    2. A copy of the PowerPoint or other slide deck used by the team for its presentation at the final class session of the term (if any);
    3. A five-minute video tour of the project and its findings. This should focus on results and demonstrate any tool or application, website, game, or chatbot you have built. For a video or podcast project, identify the research you did, the articles you discussed, how you found them, and the technology you used to prepare the video or podcast.
    4. The actual project: a usable tool, application, or website, a finished video, a functional chatbot, a playable game, or whatever form the final project takes.
  • All of the above should be submitted to the course’s LEARN Dropbox by 11:59 PM on Friday, April 4, 2025. No extensions will be granted.

Rules for Small-Group and Written Work

The small-group project should consist of work performed by the group members: in particular, nothing you submit for marking should be work performed by anyone else. You may, of course, cite to other people’s work that has been inspirational, that you have relied on for ideas, or that your project seeks to critique. That said, the main intellectual contribution of your small-group project must be your own.

For the weekly commentaries or position papers, they must consist entirely of your own writing; you may discuss your thoughts and ideas with your classmates through the class sessions and in your own conversations outside of class, whether on Slack or otherwise. You may use LLMs or generative AI only as described above. A good rule of thumb is that if I were to pull you aside and ask you to explain the ideas or arguments underlying your writing, you should be able to explain to me what you meant by every sentence; if not, it suggests that it is likely not sufficiently your own ideation.

Marks, Discussions and Appeals

Should any student wish to discuss the written feedback or mark received on an individual weekly commentary or position paper, that student should first reach out to the TA who provided the feedback or mark by email to schedule a time to discuss the feedback or mark. Should the student continue to be dissatisfied with the feedback or mark, they may reach out to the instructor by email to schedule a time to discuss their concerns. This should be rare since overall grades can easily be improved by submitting an additional written assignment. Formal appeals may result in assignment of the same grade, a higher grade or a lower grade than the previous grade awarded by the TA. Should any student wish to discuss the written feedback on either of their small-group project Milestones, their mark on any component of the course, or their grade in the course as a whole, they should email the instructor to schedule an appointment.

Intellectual Property

You should be aware that this Workshop contains the intellectual property (“IP”) of the instructor, TAs, and/or the University of Waterloo. IP includes items such as:

  • Workshop content, both spoken and written (including any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Workshop handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the Workshop (e.g., this syllabus, PowerPoint slides, poll questions, etc.);
  • Questions or solutions sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, prompts, quizzes, tests, final exams or small-group projects, etc.); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor, TAs, or another student, or used by the instructor, TAs, or another student, with the permission of the copyright owner).

Workshop materials and the IP contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this IP without the IP owner’s permission is a violation of their IP rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, the TAs, another student, or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading or otherwise sharing their IP online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from the instructor, the TAs, another student, or the University of Waterloo is also necessary before sharing any IP from prior Ethics Workshops with students taking the same or similar workshops in subsequent terms of years. In many cases, the instructor may be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without express permission is considered a violation of the IP owner’s IP rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of IP belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online. The owner of the IP rights deserves to know (and may already have given their consent).

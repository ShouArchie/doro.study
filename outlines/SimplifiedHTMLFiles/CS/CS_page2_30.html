Course Description

CS 476:

The interaction of financial models, numerical methods, and computing environments. Basic computational aspects of option pricing and hedging. Numerical methods for stochastic differential equations, strong and weak convergence. Generating correlated random numbers. Time-stepping methods. Finite difference methods for the Black-Scholes equation. Discretization, stability, convergence. Methods for portfolio optimization, effect of data errors on portfolio weights.

View requirements for CS 476

CS 676:

The interaction of financial modes, numerical methods, and computing environments. Basic computational aspects of option pricing and hedging. Numerical methods for stochastic differential equations, strong and weak convergence. Generating correlated random numbers. Time-stepping methods. Finite difference methods for Black-Scholes equation. Discretization, stability, convergence. Methods for portfolio optimization, effect of data errors on portfolio weights. (Heldwith CS 476).

Tentative Course Schedule

The following are the tentative topics in chronological order:

1. Introduction to financial markets: 

  • Introduction to stocks, bonds, options
  • no arbitrage pricing
  • random walks on lattices
  • option pricing on lattices
  • Black Scholes model and Ito's Lemma
  • Risk Neutral Valuation

2. MC Methods

  • Random variate generation
  • Option pricing
  • Hedging
  • VaR and CVaR estimation
  • Correlate assets

3. PDE Methods

  • Black Scholes PDE
  • PDEs for European and American Options
  • Explicit and Implicit Finite Difference Methods

4. Calibration and Optimization

  • Option model calibration
  • CVaR portfolio optimization
  • non-linear optimzation

Regrading policy

Requests for regrading a document must be made within 7 days after the document has been returned to you. To have your document considered for regrading you must formulate a request in writing by email to the instructor, providing the problem and a reason for why you believe it should be regraded. Your entire document may then be regraded and, in case necessary, your original mark raised or lowered. Keep in mind that if a problem has been graded consistently for all students, it is unlikely that changes for a particular student can be made as this would make grading unfair towards the other students.

The importance of practice: Being a mathematician (in the wider sense; you could be a probabilist, statistician, financial analyst, actuarial scientist etc.) is, to a large degree, not different from being a mechanic. A mechanic encounters a problem, analyzes it, and fixes it with the tools from her/his toolbox. Mathematics is the same, just our ‘tools’ are results we have previously derived or learned about: Our hammer is the Central Limit Theorem, our screwdriver the Strong Law of Large Numbers, our drilling machine is Monte Carlo simulation etc. . . . you get the idea. The computer we use to simulate a result is also one of our tools. Now the whole goal is to fill up your toolbox. If you then encounter a new problem, you analyze it, open your toolbox, see what tools you have and apply what works best – just as a mechanic. But if you open your toolbox and there are only few tools in there, you don’t have many options. . . that’s why we work on filling that toolbox in the course, and the practice questions are simply the ‘problems’ you may encounter. And to become a good mechanic, you need a good toolbox plus practice in using it (doesn’t help if you have a thousand tools with you but you don’t know how to use any of them. . . ).

Thus, work on the practice problems! If you don’t try them and wait for the solution to be presented in class, you look at it and say ‘okay, that sounds reasonable, I would have probably come up with the same idea’. But then you forget about that idea because it didn’t take you any effort to understand. On the other hand, if you tried to find a solution by yourself, you will always remember that point where you got stuck and which was challenging for you. And once you see the solution, you have this aha-effect saying ‘aha, I see, this is what I was missing’. Because of this effect, you will remember that idea/trick in the future, in particular also in an exam. So do try the practice problems alone (if it helps you, set a time limit before starting to ask others). Also, obviously, do look at the solutions after being made available to make sure you learn the underlying ideas and tricks how to approach the problems.

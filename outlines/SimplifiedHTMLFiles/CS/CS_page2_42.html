Course Description

CM 730 / CS 487 / CS 687:

An introduction to the use of computers for symbolic mathematical computation, involving traditional mathematical computations such as solving linear equations (exactly), analytic differentiation and integration of functions, and analytic solution of differential equations.

CS 487: View requirements for CS 487

CS 687: View requirements for CS 687

CM 730: View requirements for CM 730

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Objectives: To introduce students to how symbolic computation is done and broaden their knowledge of algorithmic techniques used to efficiently solve problems in many different algebraic and geometric settings, their limitations, and to gain a deeper understanding about symbolic algorithms and how to analyze them.

Tentative Course Schedule

Topics (tentative):

  • Basic arithmetic operations with polynomials
  • Polynomial Factoring (univariate, multivariate, and over finite fields as well as over the rationals)
  • Multivariate Polynomial Division
  • Groebner Bases
  • Basic Algorithms in Group Theory
  • Applications

Administrative Policy

If you fail entirely to submit an assignment, a grade of zero will be recorded. Late assignments will have a penalty applied as discussed in the Assignments section below. The only exceptions are the following:

If you take a Short-Term Absence whose 2-day period covers an assignment deadline, your assignment deadline will be

correspondingly shifted back exactly two days. Please inform the instructor to make the deadline adjustment.

If you take a Self-Declared (COVID) Absence, which may cover up to 10 days, contact your instructor to arrange for appropriate accommodation. Depending on timing, circumstances, and preferences, we may either shift the deadline for you, or completely cancel that specific assignment for you as discussed in the next paragraph.

If you have another valid justification (e.g., illness with Verification of Illness Form [VIF], etc.) for missing an assignment deadline, the grade component of that assignment will be removed from your grade and the weight of the other assignments adjusted accordingly.

(e.g., if one assignment is missed, the remaining 3 assignments will each become worth 1/3 of the total assignment grade).

Assignments:

Assignments will be made available through Crowdmark and LEARN. Check to ensure you are using the most recent version (if necessary, corrections will be made to the posted assignment and a corresponding announcement made on Piazza).

The assignments will consist of programming problems and analytical work. Your code is an important part of the assignment, and as such it should be readable and well-commented. Assignment figures and graphs should be carefully thought out and labelled to present the data and your conclusions in an effective and clear manner. Poor presentation of your work will result in a poor mark. Any code and algorithms you write should be sufficiently well documented through code comments or descriptions as to be readily understood. 

There will be four assignments. Assignment submissions generally consist of both analytical/written work and programming problems. Late assignments received in the 24 hours following the due deadline will be marked, and receive (at most) 75% credit. Assignments submitted after 24 hours will not be marked. You cannot submit some questions on time and some questions late -- the whole assignment will be considered late in such a case.

Assignments and midterms will be returned electronically via Crowdmark. Complete written solutions will not be posted. You are instead encouraged to attend office hours to further discuss and review solutions as needed (or ask questions on Piazza).

Assignment/midterm marks will be posted on Crowdmark and LEARN. Notify the instructor or a TA immediately if you believe a mark was recorded incorrectly. The marks recorded on Crowdmark/LEARN are considered final two weeks after the grades are released for that specific course component.

Assignment Submission Procedures

You must submit your work in two parts:

1. Upload one or more PDF(s) to Crowdmark containing both your written/analytical work and your Maple implementation with the results/output included (including tables, figures, numerical data, etc). (i.e., the TA should be able to look at your PDF to immediately see your results and code, without needing to rerun the code themselves.)

2. Any and all Maple files you wrote/used to solve the programming problems should also be submitted electronically as a single ZIP file to the appropriate assignment Dropbox on LEARN. This code submission should allow the TAs to run your code if and when necessary to verify the results that your code produces.

If you submit one part before the deadline and one part after, by default the later of the two submission times will be used. Contact the instructor immediately if you have failed to submit [only] one part on time.

Graduate students are required to complete a course project. Administrative Policy:

Collaboration and Plagiarism:

You are encouraged to discuss general assignment concepts with other individuals in the class. However, the submitted assignment must be your own work.

Plagiarism is representing the work of others as your own. Plagiarism on exams includes using unauthorized aids or communicating in any way with others (aside from instructors or TAs) during an examination. Plagiarism on assignments includes copying another student's solution and submitting it as your own, allowing another student to copy/adapt your solution, collaborating closely/excessively with another student, or obtaining solutions from any other source. Penalties for academic violations vary, but, for example, may include a zero on the assignment and a -5% to the course grade. Refer to Waterloo's Guidelines for the Assessment of Penalties at: https://uwaterloo.ca/secretariat/guidelines/guidelines-assessment-penalties. All academic offenses are reported to the

Associate Dean for Undergraduate Studies and are recorded in the student's file. Subsequent academic offenses in the same course or in other courses will lead to more severe penalties, up to and including suspension and expulsion.

We encourage you to discuss general concepts and problems with classmates, TAs, and instructors. However, as noted above, the solution that you submit must be worked through by yourself and written in your own words. It is not acceptable to work on an assignment with someone else and then write it up individually. The only exceptions are assignments or projects which the instructor specifically designates as group activities. When discussing course matters with fellow students, do not take notes, and do not look at another person's partial solutions, or show them yours.

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes,

brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved

drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your

instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have

not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean.

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructors, TAs, and/or the University of Waterloo.

Intellectual property includes items such as:

Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);

Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);

Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams);

Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student's educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner's permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository). Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years. In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through

the student body or online. The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

Health and Illness

While Ontario and the University of Waterloo have long since suspended mandatory mask requirements in most settings, COVID-19

remains widespread and continues to evolve. The most severe COVID-related risks have lessened for many people, such as individuals

with fully up-to-date vaccinations. However, some risks of (re)infection and the potential for negative longer term health effects remain. are made available online partly to facilitate catching up in such situations, and to reduce the need to attend class when you are sick.

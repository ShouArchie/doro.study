Course Description

STAT 847:

This course introduces Data Science students to exploratory data analysis with a focus on the concepts and tools necessary for understanding and successfully undertaking an end to end exploratory analysis of large and complex data. All stages of data collection, preparation and cleaning, importation into a statistical programming environment, data manipulation, as well as analysis and visualization will be treated. Material will be presented via case studies which will involve hands on programming and analysis. Methods and models (both statistical and computational) will be introduced as needed according to the nature of the data encountered in each case. The case studies will be chosen to give a breadth of experience to the student. A modern statistical programming language will be used throughout.

This is a survey course

The main goal of this class is to give you a go-to library of possible approaches to try. We will focus on the WHAT and the WHY, but for the sake of breadth we will be light on the HOW. That is, the mathematical details will only be covered as far as it serves the use cases presented. A more theoretically rigorous treatment of some of these methods is warranted before using them in a major project.

You’ll want to know more about a method to justify it to a client, boss, or supervisor. Similarly, you’ll want to have more depth in case something goes wrong or unexpected with the data. But for now, knowing what is possible and how to try it, and where to look for more details is going to get you very far, very fast.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Handle a variety of formats and system of multiple files.
Clean, arrange , and organize data. (Including text data)
Examine the summary stats (mean, median, standard deviation, skewness) of many variables and relationships between them quickly
Handle missing data.
Model continuous response data. (e.g., with regression)
Select an important subset of variables to use in a model among many available.
Plot 2 dimensions against a categorical or continuous 3rd dimension.
Plot publication-worthy graphs and tables.
Manage confidential data responsibly.
Write about your findings in a clear, precise style.

Analysis assignments will be code-heavy applications of the methods learned in class. In previous years, these were based around getting basic summary information from a record of thousands of chess matches from https://lichess.org/ ,  around getting basic information from hundreds of horse races from Woodbine Racetrack (Toronto) and Hastings Racecourse (Vancouver). 

 

Reading assignments will be short form interpretation questions from assigned reading. Last year, the first one was based on Chapter 2 of the book Dark Data, which is about missing data.

Midterm and final projects will be holistic projects. You are expected to run a complete EDA and write a report. The expectations of the midterm project will be scaled back to reflect that writing and visualization won’t have been covered. 

In the final, you are also given a choice of datasets.

Personal Mental Health Support Message

You're not alone. Lots of people look like they have it together in public and online when they're a mess inside too. You're not the only student facing what you're facing, and it really is okay to seek out help from the above sources if you need.

Getting help is not an admission of failure. For the same reason that you cannot walk off a broken leg or a heart attack. It's just a thing that some people need to do.

It probably gets better. Obviously, nobody can guarantee that, but it is extremely likely to get better. More specifically, you get stronger and more capable at dealing with the problems in your life.

You are not your grades. Grades are not a reflection of your worth as a human being. They got you into university, so they've already done most of the work, but at this point, the most they can really do for you decide if you can get into graduate school before doing industry experience. (And if you're in the graduate version of this class, then congrats! You barely need grades at all now.)

This too will pass. Some people will say that being a student was the best time of their life. Don't let that be your reason for thinking this is as good as it gets. Everyone's life path is different.

Every day's 'best' is a little different. If you could do your theoretical best every day, then it wouldn't be your best it would be your average.

Others are not yardsticks. The takeaway message is NOT 'if they can become a prof with a mental illness, then I should be able to as well.', please don't use others as a point of comparison like that.

Take care of yourself when you can, don't blame yourself when you can't.

The world is better with you in it.

Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

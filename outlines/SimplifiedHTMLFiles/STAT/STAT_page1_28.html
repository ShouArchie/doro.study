Course Description

STAT 340:

Random variate generation in the univariate and multivariate case, Monte Carlo integration, advanced computer implementation, variance reduction, statistical analysis of simulated data, extensions to challenging simulation problems. Mathematical treatment of the underlying stochastic concepts and proofs.

View requirements for STAT 340

Random variate generation in the univariate and multivariate case, Monte Carlo integration, advanced com- puter implementation, variance reduction, statistical analysis of simulated data, extensions to challenging simulation problems. Mathematical treatment of the underlying stochastic concepts and proofs.

Topics include:

1)  Introduction to simulation and Rprogramming;

2)  Review of binary and double representation;

3)  Pseudo-random number generators, LCGs, modular arithmetic;

4)  Inversion method, acceptance-rejection method, table-look up method to sample from non-uniform distributions;

5)  Sampling from multivariate distributions and copulas;

6)  Sampling from (simple) stochastic processes;

7)  Monte-Carlo estimators and its properties;

8)  Variance reduction techniques (antithetic variates, stratification, importance sampling, control variates, ...)

9)  Applications of Monte-Carlo estimation (finance, queuing, . . . )

10)  Further topics time-permitting

Midterm Rules

Please check Odyssey for your room and seat prior to each exam. The focus of each midterm is the material discussed since the previous midterm; e.g., the focus of Midterm 3 is the material discussed after Midterm 2. 

Assignment Rules

The four assignments can be submitted in groups of up to and including two students. Moreover, all assignments are handled by Crowdmark. Make sure your name only appears on precisely one assignment (otherwise the minimum is applied). Justify all your answers; a correct answer without justification or derivation will only give a minor fraction of marks. Solutions to R exercises have to be reproducible, show all source code, with comments, and relevant output (such as numbers or plots). Unless stated otherwise, using R markdown or LaTeX is required for all assignments and hand-written solutions and/or imported screenshots may lead to a penalty of up to 30% per question. A RMarkdown and LaTeX template is available on Learn. 

Tutorial Quiz Rules

The tutorial quizzes will be run in two stages. First, in Stage 1, each student gets a paper with questions that they work on individually and submit after 20 minutes. These papers are then collected. Next, in Stage 2, a new set of papers with questions is distributed (with very similar or even identical questions). In the next 20 minutes, you can work on that second set of questions, and this time, you are allowed to collaborate with anybody in the classroom! The overall grade of each quiz is then a weighted average of the Stage 1 and Stage 2 components. 

Diary Entry Rules

In order for you to track your progress throughout the course, there will be a form of an online diary on Learn, where you are asked to reflect on certain questions (for example, ”What were my major mistakes in Quiz 1?”). This also helps me gathering feedback from you. The diary entries are handled via Crowdmark and are graded by completeness. 

iClicker Rules

Through the use of iClicker, you can earn up to 2% on top of your grade. Each lecture will have a couple of iClicker questions, each worth 2 marks (1 mark for participation; 1 mark for correctness). At the end, everyone's clicker mark will be divided by 0.75 to allow for absences. No further accommodation will be provided for any reason.

Regrading policy

Requests for regrading a document must be made within 7 days after the document has been returned to you. To have your document considered for regrading you must formulate a request in writing by email to the instructor, providing the problem and a reason for why you believe it should be regraded. Administrative Policy

The importance of practice: Being a mathematician (in the wider sense; you could be a probabilist, statistician, financial analyst, actuarial scientist etc.) is, to a large degree, not different from being a mechanic. A mechanic encounters a problem, analyzes it, and fixes it with the tools from her/his toolbox. Mathematics is the same, just our ‘tools’ are results we have previously derived or learned about: Our hammer is the Central Limit Theorem, our screwdriver the Strong Law of Large Numbers, our drilling machine is Monte Carlo simulation etc. . . . you get the idea. The computer we use to simulate a result is also one of our tools. Now the whole goal is to fill up your toolbox. If you then encounter a new problem, you analyze it, open your toolbox, see what tools you have and apply what works best – just as a mechanic. But if you open your toolbox and there are only few tools in there, you don’t have many options. . . that’s why we work on filling that toolbox in the course, and the practice questions are simply the ‘problems’ you may encounter. And to become a good mechanic, you need a good toolbox plus practice in using it (doesn’t help if you have a thousand tools with you but you don’t know how to use any of them. . . ).

Thus, work on the practice problems! If you don’t try them and wait for the solution to be presented in class, you look at it and say ‘okay, that sounds reasonable, I would have probably come up with the same idea’. But then you forget about that idea because it didn’t take you any effort to understand. On the other hand, if you tried to find a solution by yourself, you will always remember that point where you got stuck and which was challenging for you. And once you see the solution, you have this aha-effect saying ‘aha, I see, this is what I was missing’. Because of this effect, you will remember that idea/trick in the future, in particular also in an exam. So do try the practice problems alone (if it helps you, set a time limit before starting to ask others). Also, obviously, do look at the solutions after being made available to make sure you learn the underlying ideas and tricks how to approach the problems.

Course Description

STAT 333:

This course provides an introduction to stochastic processes, with an emphasis on regenerative phenomena. Topics cover generating functions, conditional probability distributions and conditional expectation, discrete-time Markov chains with a countable state space, limit distributions for ergodic and absorbing chains, applications including the random walk, the gambler's ruin problem, and the Galton-Watson branching process, an introduction to counting processes, connections between the exponential distribution and Poisson process, and non-homogeneous and compound Poisson processes.

View requirements for STAT 333

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
use moment generating functions as a tool to determine expected values and identify probability distributions of random variables;
use joint probability mass/density functions and conditional probability mass/density functions to characterize probability distributions associated with two or more random variables;
use the law of total expectation to calculate expected values and probabilities through the principle of conditioning;
specify a discrete-time Markov chain (DTMC), in terms of its transition probability matrix and initial conditions, and calculate various transition probabilities;
classify the states of a DTMC;
define fundamental DTMC concepts including irreducibility, periodicity, and recurrence/transience;
find limiting/stationary probability distributions for certain DTMCs through the use of the Basic Limit Theorem;
understand the mechanics associated with well-known DTMCs such as the random walk, the Galton-Watson branching process, and the gambler’s ruin problem;
calculate absorption probabilities and expected absorption times for DTMCs using the principle of conditioning with respect to the first transition;
use special properties associated with the exponential distribution (such as the memoryless property) and understand its important connection to a Poisson process;
calculate various distributional quantities associated with the Poisson process, the non-homogeneous Poisson process, and the compound Poisson process.

Administrative Policy

Missed Assessments: If you miss any tutorial test, or tutorial assignment with a valid reason and documentation (such as self-declared absence, VIF, etc), the weight of the missed assessment will be shifted to the final exam. 

If you miss one midterm with a valid reason and documentation (such as self-declared absence, VIF, etc), 15% of the weight will be shifted to the final exam while the other midterm will count for 15% of the overall grade.

If you miss both midterms with a valid reason, the entire weight of the midterm component (30%) will be shifted to the final exam.

If you miss the final exam with a valid reason and documentation, the Math Faculty's INC policy applies (https://uwaterloo.ca/math/incomplete-process). 

If you miss any number of lectures and cannot participate in iClicker, no accommodation is given, with or without valid reason and documentation. The iClicker grade is entirely bonus, and all iClicker grades are divided by 0.75 to allow for missed lectures.

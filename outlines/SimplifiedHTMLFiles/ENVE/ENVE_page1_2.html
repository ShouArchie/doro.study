Course Description

ENVE 223 / GEOE 223:

An introduction to ordinary differential equations with applications to mass and energy balance problems in engineering. Standard methods of solution of first and second order linear equations with constant coefficients. Numerical methods for solving ordinary differential equations. Partial differential equations.

ENVE 223: View requirements for ENVE 223

GEOE 223: View requirements for GEOE 223

Many practical physical and engineering problems can be analyzed using mathematical models formulated in terms of ordinary differential equations; these often arise from the application of mass, momentum, or energy balance laws. The objective of this course is to equip engineering students with a working knowledge of the methods which are used to solve these equations. Differential equations are introduced, along with standard methods of solution for first and second order linear equations with constant coefficients.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
identify and differentiate between the various types of differential equations; with this knowledge, be able to identify the most appropriate method of solution
pose appropriate boundary or initial conditions and solve that equation using standard methods for given a mathematical problem statement expressed in terms of a linear differential equation
check whether a solution to a differential equation is correct
think critically about a mathematical problem and evaluate the validity of results from mathematical methods
appreciate the utility and value of having an education in mathematics as part of their training as an engineer

Canadian Engineering Accreditation Board

This course has 42 Accreditation Units (AUs) which is a measure of contact hours.  It has been judged to be 75% Mathematics (M) and 25% Engineering Science (ES).

This course has also been judged to provide the following graduate attributes (GAs) at the developed or intermediate level: Knowledge base (KB) for engineering, problem analysis (PA), investigation and use of engineering tools (Tools).

Assignments

This course has three individual assignments that will be posted on LEARN and submitted to Crowdmark.  The assignment deadlines are Tuesdays at 10 pm and are listed on the topic schedule below.

Assignment late penalties are 10% for up to 24 hours late, and 30% for 24 up to 48 hours late.  Assignments over 48 hours late will not be accepted.  Exceptions to this late policy are (1) an approved accommodation provided by the course instructor, or (2) students claiming their grace days.  

Each student has 4 grace days to claim on assignment deadlines throughout the term.  Each claimed grace day removes the late penalty for that day, but each assignment is still required to be submitted by 48 hours after the deadline.  It is important to note that grace days are not extensions; they allow you to hand in an assignment up to two days late without penalty. Students claim their grace days by emailing the TA; otherwise, late penalties will be imposed.

Assignment solutions must be laid out properly, with all steps shown, answers clearly identified, and diagrams properly labeled.  Uploads to Crowdmark must be of good quality, legible and properly organized.  Students are expected to use only allowable aids; otherwise, use of disallowed aids must be acknowledged on assignment submissions (marking to be adjusted accordingly).

Solutions using MATLAB or a spreadsheet may require submission of the MATLAB code or spreadsheet (this will be specified on the assignment).  Teaching team assignment solutions will be posted on LEARN by the Friday after the due date.  

Quizzes & Final Exam

This course has three 1-hour quizzes scheduled in the Tuesday tutorial slot during the term (see schedule below) and a 2.5-hour final exam (to be scheduled in the final exam period).  

Each quiz will focus on the topics covered in the preceding assignment and associated lectures.  The final exam will be cumulative, including the later topics covered in the course.

The quizzes and exam are individual assessments.  More details on the topics, structure and allowable aids will be provided before the assessments.  The location of the quizzes will be announced (to provide double-spaced seating).

Assignment Screening

Submitted assignments must be completed as an individual. Any exceptions to individual work must be acknowledged, including the use of generative AI. If you are in doubt, contact the teaching team.

For assignments you are encouraged to seek/give help from/to peers, but this help must stop short of using another peer’s or group’s work as your own. The best practice is to receive/give help without exchanging copies of the work. Then redoing the work on your own.

Submitted assignments are not formally screened using software, but the teaching team may compare submitted assignments to other assignments submitted this term and/or from previous terms.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of engineering specific skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Any exceptions to this policy will be explicitly communicated by the teaching team for specific deliverables.

Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

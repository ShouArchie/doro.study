Course Description

CIVE 583 / ENVE 583:

Design of water supply and distribution systems. Design of waste and storm water collection systems. Storm water management. The course consists of 24 hours of lectures and a subdivision design project. The emphasis is on computer aided design and sustainability, using commonly used software packages.

ENVE 583: View requirements for ENVE 583

CIVE 583: View requirements for CIVE 583

CIVE 583 is intended for students who are interested in water resource engineering.  The goal of the course is for you to apply knowledge from previous courses on fluid mechanics, hydrology and open channel flow to the particular problem of city building. This area of work is needed to designing resilient water supply and drainage systems that address the challenge of ongoing climate and biodiversity crises.

The course has several theme areas including storm water management, drinking water supply systems, and waste water drainage systems. We will consider pressure and open channel flow processes, pumping and storage, analysis and design of water distribution systems, design of collection systems (storm and sanitary), approaches to storm water management (BMP’s, LID, etc.) and urban hydrologic modelling/design. For each theme area, assignments will involve review of theory, hand calculations, EXCEL solutions, MATLAB solutions, software tutorials, and design problems using site data, problem statements, and municipal or provincial design criteria/constraints.  The two major units on stormwater management and drinking water distribution will be tested with design challenge problems.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
1. Design synthetic rainfall events (storms)
2. Calculate storm runoff
3. Design stormwater drainage (sewer) networks
4. Design stormwater management systems including lot-level, conveyance, and end-of-pipe controls
5. Model drinking water demand (steady-state, extended-period, fire, emegency)
6. Calculate flow and pressure in water distribution systems
7. Design water distribution systems including pipes, pumps, storage, and other network components
8. Model sanitary sewer system loading
9. Design sanitary sewer drainage systems

Student Assessment

Component Value
Assignments 40
Sustainable stormwater management on UW campus 35
New water distribution system for Elmira 25

Weekly assignments will consist of an in-class and a homework portion.  In-class assignments should be completed and submitted during the lab session. Homework will be submitted before the lab session of the following week. Assignments which are less than 2 days late will be accepted with a 15% late penalty per day. Assignments more than 2 days late without what is deemed by the instructor to be a legitimate excuse will receive no marks.  

Assignments must follow a standard memo format that I will provide.  Your assignments must be submitted to Crowdmark in a pdf format.  For best results you should first arrange your answers in the memo (Word format) using a combination of text and high quality figures and then print to pdf to produce the final document for submission. Results should be printed and uploaded separately for each question to ensure that we can mark your assignment easily and provide feedback in Crowdmark.

Two projects will be assigned through the term. These projects will be completed in groups of 3-4 students.  Project 1 will focus on improving an aspect of stormwater management on the University of Waterloo Campus. Project 2 will focus on designing a pumping system and sewer mains for the City of Elmira. 

Projects must follow the standard memo format. They should be of engineering report quality and organized such that someone can check your work and understand your solution. Messy, incomprehensible work may not be graded.  Learning to produce high quality reports for submission is part of the desired course outcomes 

Outside sources, including from the textbook, reputable online sources, or other appropriate sources must be cited.  The quality of your source will also be graded (i.e. textbooks, academic journals, and government documents are considered good sources.  Unverified posts in social media or in unaffiliated websites are poor sources).  If you are in doubt, contact your instructor.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as [fill this in with your discipline-specific skills]. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Intellectual Property: Be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo. Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof).
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides).
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein are used to enhance a student’s educational experience. However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights. For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years. In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials. However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights and academic integrity.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online.

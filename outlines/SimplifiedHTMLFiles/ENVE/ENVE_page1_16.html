Instructor & TA (Teaching Assistant) Information

 

Course Description

ENVE 573:

Importance and complications associated with environmental modelling, the model building process, limitations, and measures of success. Types of contaminants; transport phenomena with a focus on advection-dispersive transport; development of governing equations; types and utility of boundary and initial conditions; and mass balance considerations. Review of completely mixed systems including lakes, streams, source functions, feedback systems, and toxic substance models. Model calibration, sensitivity, and uncertainty; methods and approaches. Solute transport models and solution techniques including random walk, method of characteristics, finite difference method and finite volume method. Aspects of multiphase flow (gas/water and NAPL/water systems) with an emphasis on groundwater problems. Introduction to mass removal technologies for remediation of soil and groundwater systems.

View requirements for ENVE 573

One of the key responsibilities of the environmental engineering profession is to monitor, assess, and clean up environmental contamination in the soil, water, and air.  Engineers are tasked to develop, implement, and design technology and best management practices to mitigate contamination, interpret uncertain environmental data to determine the source(s) and or fate of environmental contaminants, and make predictions about how past and future pollution may pose a health threat to humans and ecosystems. The objective of this course is to introduce you to theoretical and practical aspects of mass transport in natural environmental systems, and to learn how to model mass transport processes.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
describe various types of contaminants and their behaviour in a variety of media
formulate and apply a mathematical mass transport model in response to an environmental problem statement (i.e., select appropriate ICs & BCs, recognize appropriate processes to include, and solve the problem using appropriate mathematical methods)
explain the physics and mathematical treatment of key transport processes and be able to distinguish and prioritize their role in the transport and remediation of contaminants
justify the use of assumptions used in contaminant transport models
interpret observation data in conjunction with model results and critically evaluate model results subject to (often significant) uncertainty

To satisfy the above outcomes, you will hone your programming skills, a necessary tool for solving the types of problems addressed in this course.

Students taking this course should be comfortable with solving differential equations analytically and numerically by hand and using code (MATLAB).

Canadian Engineering Accreditation Board

This course has 42 Accreditation or Academic Units (AUs) which is a measure of contact hours.  It has been judged to be 100% Engineering Science (ES).

Assignments

This course has 5 assignments that will be posted on LEARN and submitted to Crowdmark.  The assignments are due at 10 pm on the following Wednesdays:

Assignment late penalties are 10% for up to 24 hours late, and 30% for 24 to 48 hours late.  Assignments over 48 hours late will not be accepted.  Exceptions to this late policy are (1) an approved accommodation provided by the course instructor, or (2) students claiming their grace days.  

Each student has 5 grace days to claim on assignment deadlines throughout the term.  Each claimed grace day removes the late penalty for that day.  Students claim their grace days by contacting the TA; otherwise, penalties will be imposed.

As a result of the content and learning objectives for this course, some of the assignments are lengthy and therefore can be completed and submitted by groups of two (maximum).  It is expected that both group members fully understand the assumptions, limitations, and detailed solution steps and calculations for all questions.  Groups of two can be different for each assignment.

Assignment solutions must be technically well written, properly laid out with all steps shown, answers clearly identified, and good quality tables and figures.  Uploads to Crowdmark must be of good quality, legible and properly organized.  

Solutions using MATLAB (or other code) or a spreadsheet will require submission of the code or spreadsheet (example portion of large spreadsheets with formulas exposed).  Other requirements may be specified on each assignment.

Assignment Screening

Submitted assignments must be completed as an individual or a group of two. Any exceptions to individual or group of two work must be acknowledged, including the use of generative AI. If you are in doubt, contact the teaching team.

For assignments you are encouraged to seek/give help from/to peers, but this help must stop short of using another peer’s or group’s work as your own. The best practice is to receive/give help without exchanging copies of the work. Then redoing the work on your own.

Submitted assignments are not formally screened using software, but the teaching team may compare submitted assignments to other assignments submitted this term and/or from previous terms.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of engineering specific skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Any exceptions to this policy will be explicitly communicated by the teaching team for specific deliverables.

Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

ERS 669:

This course covers study design, and data management, visualization, and statistical analysis. The primary focus will be the development and understanding of generalized linear models as they are foundational to most analyses in Environment and Ecology. Students will learn to fit models to data and assess model fit. Students will also be introduced to the use of the statistical software R and provided the opportunity to use that software to manage and analyze their own data. Special topics will be developed based on the needs and interests of the students and may include hierarchical modelling, multivariate statistics, survey analysis, non-linear modelling, or other topics. Students are expected to bring their own computer and data. For cases in which data have not been collected, students will work with the professor to source similar data or generate simulated data to support their progress through the course.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Formulate research questions and hypotheses suitable for statistical analysis in the context of environmental and ecological studies
Develop study designs that consider the unique challenges and characteristics of environmental data, including spatial and temporal dependencies.
Demonstrate proficiency in data cleaning, transformation, and organization for environmental and ecological datasets.
Utilize appropriate visualization techniques to effectively communicate patterns, trends, and outliers in environmental data.
Apply foundational statistical techniques to analyze environmental and ecological data, including descriptive statistics and inferential methods.
Critically assess the assumptions underlying statistical models and select appropriate techniques based on the nature of the data.
Develop a deep understanding of the principles and applications of Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) in the context of environmental and ecological studies.
Apply GLMs to model relationships between variables, interpret model parameters, and assess the goodness of model fit.
Demonstrate the ability to interpret and communicate the results of model assessments in the context of environmental and ecological research
Effectively use the statistical software R for data management, analysis, and visualization in the field of environment and ecology.
Write and customize R scripts to implement advanced statistical techniques, with a focus on GLMs.
Provide a foundation for future advanced or more specific learning in applied statistics.

Tentative Course Schedule

One of my main goals in developing this course was to produce a somewhat bespoke course that meets the particular needs of the graduate students enrolled in the class. Therefore, you will be provided many opportunities to work with your research data (or similar). The goal is for you to make progress on your analysis and develop content directly relevant to your graduate research project. Given the diverse needs of students in the class, this means we will have to be somewhat flexible with the course outline and adapt to where students are in their data management, analysis, and programming journey. The core of the course provides the knowledge and skills that are fundamental to almost all quantitative research in Environment and Ecology. Additionally, this is only the second offering of the course, so we will learn together how long it takes students to understand concepts, develop assignment deliverables, and learn the hard skills of R programming to manage data, fit models, visualize inputs and outputs, and communicate findings. Therefore, it is best to consider the timelines presented in this course outline as my best guess. I have been teaching graduate-level mixed-methods and R programming at the undergraduate level for 10 years (so the "best guess" should be close) but we will likely need to be somewhat flexible and adapt throughout the course.

We meet for one three hour session per week. I envision each session broken into three 50 minute sections (providing two 10 minute breaks throughout). The focus of each of the three sections will vary each week depending on our progress through the course material. However, the general structure will include: 1) lecture content, 2) lab content, and 3) student research. The lecture content will provide the knowledge and theoretical background for each topic. The lab content will be based on the book developed by Dr. Fedy that presents hands-on applications of the week's topic and implementation in R statistical software. The student research section will be opportunities for the students to focus on assignments, data and to provide time for programming, discussion, and problem-solving.

Assignment due dates are subject to change based on class progress through the course. Any revisions in due dates will be discussed as a group during class time.

Administrative Policy

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

ERS 320:

This course offers an examination of relationship between the economy, the environment and sustainability. The course aims to provide students with an understanding of the differing viewpoints on the economy-environment relationship and their associated methods, including their strengths, weaknesses and applicability in a policy context.

View requirements for ERS 320

This course examines the relationship between economics and sustainability in the context of global challenges, spanning from water and food security to climate change, biodiversity, and urbanization. We cover multiple perspectives in economics and trace the evolution of key drivers, debates, and policy approaches before turning to new frontiers of research and practice. The course format combines interactive lectures with exercises to build skills in economic and policy analysis, technical writing (policy briefings) for specialized audiences, and data analysis and visualization. The term will culminate in a poster session on sustainable economic futures with a focus on water, climate, and biodiversity - three areas where economists have prepared major reviews in the last 15 years. In 2025, we will carry out a class-wide project to build an atlas of the world's water markets, and we will critically engage with the 2024 findings and report of the Global Commission on the Economics of Water. Weekly tutorials support interactive learning and exercises. These exercises form an integral part of the course and typically comprise debates, practicals (e.g. developing figures, working with datasets), and occasional discussions with practitioners. Students are encouraged to check for updates (and the revision history) for the outline at outline.uwaterloo.ca. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Describe and compare different economic perspectives on sustainability and development
Identify trends and debates on the economics of sustainability from a systems perspective;
Evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of economic methodologies for policy analysis;
Apply knowledge on the economics of sustainability by developing analytical, writing and communication skills tailored for specialized audiences
Integrate economic ideas and methods with other disciplinary perspectives in the humanities, natural sciences, engineering sciences and social sciences

Student Assessment

Component Value
Participation 25%
Policy briefing 20%
Mid-term 15%
Poster Session 15%
Final exam 25%

Participation (25%)

Participation and class contributions are the foundation for the course. Students are expected to attend each class and participate in three ways: critical reading; active engagement in class discussions and debates; and completion of practical exercises during tutorials. Occasional pop-up quizzes will focus on key readings. Practical exercise #1 is due Feb 11th, and practical exercise #2 is due March 4th.

Mid-term (15%)

The mid-term exam, held Feb 26th, will be an in-class exam assessing understanding of core concepts and debates. The format will include multiple choice and short essays. A review session will occur on Feb 24th. Anyone missing the mid-term will have the weight of the missed mid-term added to their final exam.

Policy briefing (20%)

Each student will be tasked with completing a policy briefing that applies economic concepts and methods to identify the causes of a water sustainability challenge and outline potential solutions. This year's theme will focus on water markets, and a list of case studies will be presented for students to choose. The policy briefing will total no more than 2000 words, and will include a summary of 100 words and no more than four (4) headline messages. Students are encouraged to include one (1) table and (1) figure to support their argument. A detailed protocol and guidelines will be available in Learn and discussed during class on 29th January. An outline (required) is due by Feb 13th and formative feedback will be provided by Friday, Feb 28th . The final submission is DUE March 21st.

Final exam (25%)

The final exam, held during the exam period, will include multiple choice questions and essays addressing a series of focused on the economics of sustainability challenges and solutions. Essays will be assessed based on the clarity of the argument, the strength of the supporting evidence, and the quality of the writing. HELD during final exam slot.

Poster Session: Atlas of Water Markets (15%)

Students will prepare and present posters examining water markets around the world. The objective will be to characterize the supply and demand for water, the characteristics of water markets, including their market failures, and potential and proposed policies for addressing them. The poster presentation will occur in class on March 31st.

Administrative Policy

 

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

ERS 101:

This course is one of 'solutions-based education' designed to use environmental case studies from local, provincial, national and international communities. We will analyze historical and contemporary cases and consider the interdisciplinary lessons that can be learned from them within the context of climate and technological change, political and economic constraints, community engagement and communication. The specific cases may change year by year depending on current events. Students will be encouraged to critically engage the scholarly, non-governmental, public media literatures and personalities.

View requirements for ERS 101

ERS 101 is designed for students registered in one of the SERS undergraduate plans (Honours Environment, Resources and Sustainability; Joint Honours; and Minor). It is not a general-purpose environmental studies course open to the wider university community.

In addition to its goal of solutions-based education, ERS 101 serves a second function in the SERS curriculum: communication skills training. Students in the SERS BES degree program must complete the English Language Proficiency Examination (ELPE) milestone by the end of their 2B term. ERS 101 allows students who receive a grade of 65% or higher in the course to meet this milestone. In addition to providing an opportunity to meet the ELPE requirements, the course helps you develop the professional writing and oral communication skills you will need during your undergraduate education, and in your working life. Class sessions, tutorials and assignments fit together to help you develop essential research, writing and presentation skills.

Case studies and real-world examples are an important to learning in ERS 101. We will be using two kinds of cases this year:

  • Water quality in the transboundary Great Lakes Basin shared by Canada and the United States provides a large frame within which we will explore challenges and solutions. I will use it during initial classes to explore key course themes such as sustainability, equity, systems thinking, interdisciplinarity, and collaboration.
  • Smaller, focused cases relating to environmental problems and solutions around the world at a host of scales appear throughout the course and add richness and diversity to our thinking. I will introduce some of these during the foundational class sessions before Reading Week; guest speakers will bring other cases to class in theme sessions that take place after Reading Week.

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students will have gained the following:
Strengthened understanding of social-ecological systems thinking, and basic tools and concepts needed to understand the social-ecological contexts within which environmental and sustainability problems and solutions exist.
Basic ability to identify and describe environmental governance systems.
Clearer understanding of the core skills and areas of knowledge developed through the undergraduate program.
Improved ability to think critically, and to use logic, reason and evidence in support of positions on key environmental and sustainability challenges and opportunities.
Basic understanding of the major strengths, limitations and challenges of disciplinary approaches, and of working across disciplines.
Strengthened ability to constructively engage with people who possess different backgrounds, knowledge, values, and positionality.
Increased ability to present ideas orally for audiences with varying levels of expertise, to actively seek and hear feedback to gauge reception, and to modify messages accordingly.
Improved ability to write clearly and concisely for various audiences, using proper grammar, and appropriate professional styles.
Basic understanding of effective knowledge mobilization strategies.

This section provides a brief overview of the assignments and examination in this course. Detailed information is provided on LEARN. 

Engagement with Class Readings via Perusall Before Class

Most class sessions have assigned readings that must be completed in advance of the class. These readings are essential to your learning. You will use an online tool called Perusall to read documents, to leave questions and comments, and to respond to questions and comments from your peers. I will evaluate the quality of your engagement with the readings and your peers using Perusall. Your grade for engagement with the readings will be based on the total score across all the reading assignments, with each reading assignment equally weighted. During the first week of class, I will provide training and resources for using Perusall. Note that tutorials have their own reading requirements from the Ewald textbook; these readings are not part of the Perusall reading assignment. 

Professional Email and Letter

Knowing how to write professional emails and letters is a basic and important skill that all SERS students need to have. In this assignment, you will write one of each, for two different scenarios. This assignment is an early opportunity for your TA to evaluate the quality of your writing. Submit your Professional Letter to the LEARN drop box, and email your Professional Email to your TA, before the start of your Week 3 tutorial. 

Challenge Presentation Slide Deck

In this assignment, you will prepare a professional presentation using Microsoft PowerPoint. Your presentation slide deck will present insights into one aspect of the Great Lakes water quality challenge that your team is investigating during the course. This is an individual assignment, but you will coordinate with your team members to ensure that you are addressing different aspect of your team's challenge. You will not deliver this presentation; however, with approval from team members, it is acceptable to re-use material from individual Challenge Presentation slides in the team's Solutions Showcase presentation. You will submit your Challenge Presentation PowerPoint file through LEARN on February 14, 2025 by 11:59 PM.

Solutions Report and Presentation

Teams of students will analyze a Great Lakes water quality challenge and identify and assess relevant solutions. Teams will be created, and topic areas assigned, during the Week 3 tutorial sessions. The topic areas assigned to teams are broad. Therefore, each team will have a lot of scope to identify its own specific Great Lakes water quality challenge within the assigned topic area. A group self-evaluation process will be used to evaluate the performance of each team member; grades assigned to individual team members will be weighted based on this evaluation.

Teams will develop a linked Solutions Report and Solutions Showcase Presentation.

Solutions Showcase Presentation

Using short oral presentations with slides, your team will present a Great Lakes water quality challenge, and potential solutions, to the class during one of two sessions (weeks 10 and 11). A draft of your team's Solutions Showcase presentation is due in your tutorial session during Week 9; to gain experience and confidence, your team will practice delivering your draft presentation during the tutorial session in Week 9. The final Power Point slide deck for your team's presentation is due on LEARN on March 17, 2025 by 8:00 AM (note early morning time!). 

Solutions Report

You and your team will prepare a formal written report that identifies and assesses potential solutions for a Great Lakes water quality challenge within your assigned topic area. The work that you do to prepare your Solutions Report will support development of your Solutions Showcase Presentation. At the same time, feedback you receive from the audience, your TA and me following your presentation will allow you to fine tune and strengthen your Solutions Report. The report is due through LEARN on April 3, 2025 by 11:59 PM.

Tutorials: Assignments, Engagement and Professionalism

The tutorial sessions are central to learning in the course. They provide the small group setting needed to help you develop and strengthen your teamwork, professional writing, and oral communication skills. You will learn to give and receive feedback to your peers through tutorial sessions as you develop your individual and group assignments. 

All students have been assigned to one of the scheduled tutorial sessions. You must attend only your assigned tutorial session. Your grade for the tutorials is based on several small assignments, the quality of your engagement during the tutorials, and your professionalism. Small assignments that are part of the tutorial grade are described in detail on LEARN; they are linked to specific chapters in the Ewald text, and to stages related to completion of course assignments. The quality of your engagement and professionalism are evaluated by your TA at each tutorial session; this means that you have to attend your assigned tutorial session to be evaluated. 

Final Examination

The final examination takes place during the examination period and will be scheduled by the Registrar's Office. It is your responsibility to know the dates of examinations, and to plan accordingly. All course material is covered on the exam. This includes assigned readings and videos, and content from lectures and tutorials. You will not be specifically tested on the material in Ewald, but you will be expected to know it and apply it during your examination.

Assignment Submission and Late Penalties

Assignments must be submitted by the time and date specified in the instructions for that assignment. Follow the submission instructions for each assignment.  

If you have declared an absence or illness, and your absence has been accepted, you can normally submit assignments without penalty. However, in every case where your request has been accepted, I or your TA (as appropriate) will set a new deadline or grade allocation that matches the nature of the absence or illness. Submissions beyond the agreed extension date will incur the late penalty. 

Late assignments normally will be penalized at the rate of 5 percent of the value of the assignment per day, including Saturday and Sunday. Assignments submitted more than 5 days after the due date will receive a grade of 0 unless a longer extension was approved. 

Importantly, some assignments cannot be accepted late under any circumstances; in these cases, another grading scheme will have to be used (to be determined by the instructor or TA).

Manage your time carefully. Pressure of work, computer glitches, etc. are not an acceptable reason for seeking an extension without penalty.

Co-op interviews and class attendance

Co-op students are encouraged to try and choose interview time slots that result in the least amount of disruption to class schedules. When this is challenging, or not possible, a student may miss a portion of a class meeting for an interview. Instructors are asked for leniency in these situations; but, a co-op interview does not relieve the student of any requirements associated with that class meeting.

When a co-op interview conflicts with an in-class evaluation mechanism (e.g., test, quiz, presentation, critique), class attendance takes precedence and the onus is on the student to reschedule the interview. CEE provides an interview conflict procedure to manage these situations.

Students will be required to provide copies of their interview schedules (they may be printed from WaterlooWorks) should there be a need to verify class absence due to co-op interviews.

Generative AI

You may use generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce images for your presentation slide decks, but you must properly document each image. You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all images you use, including any created or supported by generative AI. Ideally, you should use real photographs with proper documentation instead of generated images, but I am making the option to use GenAI images available to you for the specific purpose of creating imagery.

You may not use GenAI tools such as Chat GPT to generate text for your assignments, or to paraphrase other peoples' text for your assignments. Learning how to write professionally is a core activity in ERS 101. Using GenAI tools to do your writing undermines that goal, and is treated as academic misconduct. 

You should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

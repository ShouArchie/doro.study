Course Description

ERS 674:

These courses allow for additions to the program on a short-term basis, and for development of future permanent courses. (Note: Field trip fee may apply)

View requirements for ERS 674

This course is designed for graduate students from across faculties seeking to apply their academic expertise to solve pressing real-world issues in biodiversity conservation. Course participants will work directly with Parks Canada and the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) to design strategies essential for effective, bi-national conservation in the rapidly changing Great Lakes region. Key student deliverables in the course may include: defining metrics to evaluate the status and extent of Great Lakes protected and conserved areas; identifying priority areas for conservation using ecological and social criteria; and analyzing spatial and other data (e.g., governance, ecological design, management effectiveness) to assess the potential for protected and conserved area networks. Throughout the course, participants will receive mentorship to develop essential professional skills aimed at enhancing their employability and career readiness, including project management, science communication, critical thinking, and more. This unique interdisciplinary experience allows students to apply their academic knowledge in a practical setting, cultivating a meaningful contribution to environmental conservation. 

The course will be structured around three potential projects (to be finalized), including:

  • assessing the current state of protected and conserved areas (PCAs) and identify critical geospatial data layers needed for PCA establishment and management. 
  • exploring key biodiversity areas and examine the process for identifying and prioritizing species and ecosystems areas for conservation. 
  • investigating the benefits and designs of protected area networks with the aim of improving connectivity and promoting collaboration. 

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Applied academic concepts and skills to real-world work environments, demonstrating a practical understanding of how knowledge translates into professional practice by collaborating with Canadian and US conservation practitioners.
Gained a comprehensive understanding of global and national commitments to place-based conservation, with a specific focus on achieving the 30X30 protected and conserved areas target.
Collaborated effectively in teams, demonstrating skills in communication, problem-solving, time management, decision-making, critical thinking, and analytical skills.
Planned and managed projects by setting objectives, allocating time resources, and meeting deadlines, showcasing your ability to work effectively with individuals from diverse backgrounds.
Participated in critical career reflection exercises to inform your career decision-making, skills articulation, and networking strategies.
Strengthened your CV and network profile with skills and experiences that support your ongoing career development.

Administrative Policy

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as developing and practicing a variety of oral and written communication skills. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.).  

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean.  

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

CHE 220:

Introduction to statistical methods for analyzing and interpreting process data. Introduction to statistical ideas, probability theory, distribution theory, sampling theory, confidence intervals, and significance tests. Introduction to regression analysis. Introduction to design of experiments and statistical quality control.

View requirements for CHE 220

This course introduces statistical methods for analyzing and interpreting process data. Basic statistical

topics discussed include probability theory, distribution theory, sampling theory, confidence intervals,

significance tests, and regression analysis. These topics are applied within the context of experimental

design and statistical quality control.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Define the role of statistics in engineering problem-solving and decision-making. [1a, 1c, 12a]
Apply statistically rigorous methods of data collection and treatment. [1a]
Calculate the probability of an event occurring using an appropriate discrete or continuous probability distribution. [1a, 2a, 2b, 2c]
Assess for significance, independence, and other common statistical measures using appropriate testing methodologies. [1a, 1c, 2a, 2b, 2c]
Perform industry-standard quality and control assessments of process data. [3b, 8b]
Construct and assess the adequacy of linear regression models for process variables. [2b]

The numbers in brackets refer to program indicators defined by the Faculty of Engineering to assess the twelve CEAB defined graduate attributes ( https://uwaterloo.ca/engineering/graduate-attributes).

Quizzes:

There will be four quizzes conducted during tutorial sessions, held every other week throughout the term. The specific topics for each quiz will be announced one week in advance, allowing students sufficient time to prepare.

Assignments:

Assignments will be given every other week and must be submitted via Crowdmark within one week. The schedule alternates between assignments and quizzes, with assignments due one week and a quiz conducted during the following week's tutorial session. Assignments are due on Friday at 5:00 PM.

Tests and Examinations:

The course includes one midterm test and one final examination, both serving as summative assessments. These assessments may cover all material taught up to the test date. Both the midterm and final will be closed-book; however, all necessary data, equations, and reference materials will be provided during the exams.

Discussion Participation:

To enhance understanding and foster deeper engagement with the course content, students are encouraged to actively participate in the Piazza discussion forum. Your TA will monitor Piazza and respond to questions within 24 hours on working days. 

Assignment Screening

Text matching software (Turnitin®) may be used to screen assignments in this course. Turnitin® is used to verify that all materials and sources in assignments are documented. Students' submissions are stored on a U.S. server, therefore students must be given an alternative (e.g., scaffolded assignment or annotated bibliography), if they are concerned about their privacy and/or security. Students will be given due notice, in the first week of the term and/or at the time assignment details are provided, about arrangements and alternatives for the use of Turnitin in this course.

Administrative Policy

Missed or late submissions of assessments will not be accepted and will be assigned a grade of 0. Exceptions may only be made to this policy if a student can provide suitable documentation of a relevant AccessAbility accommodation at least one week before the due date, or of a personal or medical incident that prevented them from submitting the assessment on time to the course instructor within one week of the original due date. The instructor may then decide to exempt the student from the assessment or offer an extension to the due date. If exempted, the grade associated with the assessment will be redistributed amongst the remaining assessments. This policy does not apply to a missed Final Examination.

 

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as [fill this in with your discipline-specific skills]. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

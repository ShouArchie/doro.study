Course Description

CHE 516:

Energy systems in society. Review of fossil fuel reserves, production and consumption trends, including unconventional sources such as shale gas, and oil sands. Transportation fuels and alternative fuels; the design of hybrid power trains, fuel cells and batteries. Design considerations of carbon-free energy generation and carbon-neutral technologies, including nuclear, wind, solar and bio-energy. Design of energy storage on a 'utility scale' and portable power sources. Applications of thermodynamics in the design of energy systems, including the Rankin and Brayton cycles. Other energy system topics as appropriate.

View requirements for CHE 516

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Define the common thermodynamic cycles behind energy generation methods, comparing ideal conditions to practical conditions. [1b, 1d, 5a]
Describe the extraction, processing, and delivery processes for common fuels and their associated energy sources. [1d, 3b, 4a, 7b]
Differentiate between energy sources by their comparative practical and economic advantages and limitations. [3a, 7a, 7b, 8a, 8b, 9a, 9b, 11b]
Lay out process descriptions for fuel extraction/processing, energy generation, and energy distribution facilities. [2a, 2b, 4a, 4b, 7a, 7b]
Identify sociopolitical and socioeconomic concerns associated with existing and emerging energy generation methods. [6a, 6b, 7a, 7b, 8a, 8b, 9a, 9b]

The numbers in brackets refer to program indicators defined by the Faculty of Engineering to assess the twelve CEAB defined graduate attributes ( https://uwaterloo.ca/engineering/graduate-attributes).

Biweekly Problems


During each Module, a short problem based on the Module's content will be assigned. This problem will be briefly discussed in class and will be due one week after it is assigned.

Design Projects


Design Projects comprise a summary report of a process, product, or facility that students will design based on the previous Module. Design Project 2 will also incorporate an in-class presentation (Pitch Meeting) in the form of a business pitch meeting for the designed facility, with an opportunity for instructors and colleagues to ask questions to the group. Each project will require submission of a report containing sections specified by an appropriate rubric. In the case of Design Project 2, each group member will be responsible for specific sections of the report and presentation as specified in its rubric, and will be graded individually on those sections.

Detailed calculations, simulation, modeling, etc. may be submitted as un-graded appendices to each project.
 

Case Study


The Case Study involves the examination of an operating process and its contextualization within the broader socioeconomic environment. A specific series of questions regarding this process will be provided for students to answer. A summary document of these answers will be submitted.

Detailed calculations, simulation, modeling, etc. may be submitted as un-graded appendices to the Case Study.
 

Debate


During Week 9, the lecture periods will be used to host a debate on a module topic. In groups of 3, students will present their case for or against a particular resolution assigned to their group, with 4 groups participating in debate on each day. Performance in the debate will be assessed via a detailed rubric distributed prior to the debate, and graded both by the instructor and by student peers.

Administrative Policy

Delivery Mode
 

Lectures will be delivered in-person, using "guided slides". Slides will be posted as .pdf files in advance of lectures; students are encouraged to bring hard copies or digital copies of the slides to lecture. During lecture, segments of slides, diagrams, examples, definitions, etc. will be discussed and filled in by the instructor. It is recommended that students follow along and complete their own copies of the slides for the week.

Any other materials needed for a lecture, such as reference materials, solved examples, videos, etc. will be posted or linked within the course content associated with the lecture's Module on LEARN. 
 

Progression Policy


Both of the following criteria must be met to receive a passing grade in CHE516:

  1. Students must receive a passing total grade (50%/100%), as per Department policy.
  2. Students must receive a passing grade (30%/60%) on the total of the Design Projects/Pitch Meeting. Students who do not meet this criterion will receive a final grade equal to their total grade on the Design Projects.

    Example: A student receives 80/100 on the Biweekly Problems, 20/60 on Design Project 1, 25/45 on the Case Study, 32/45 on the Debate, 36/75 on Design Project 2, and 27/45 on the Pitch Meeting. This represents a grade of 54.7% per the Subject Outline, and meets Criterion 1 above.

    However, the total of the Design Projects/Pitch Meeting is 83/180 or 27.7%/60%. Since this total is not greater than or equal to 30%, Criterion 2 is not met. The student will be assigned a grade of 83/180 or 46.1% in the course.
     

Missed or Late Assessments


Missed or late submissions of any assessment will be assigned a grade of 0. Missed presentations or debates will also result in a grade of 0 for the group member who missed the presentation or debate; other group members will not be penalized. Missing sections from the Design Project 2 report will only affect the grade of the group member who was responsible for them.

Exceptions may only be made to this policy if:

  • A student has an appropriate accommodation through the AccessAbility office allowing for an extension on assessments; usually this extension is 72 hours from the original due date. Extensions must be requested formally through the AccessAbility office, after which the instructor will contact the student directly with a revised due date and time.
  • A student has a verified illness and has submitted the appropriate Verified Illness Form (VIF). An extension, exemption, or make-up assessment details  will be made at the instructor's discretion and communicated directly to the student.
  • A student declares a Short-Term Absence. An extension, exemption, or make-up assessment details will be made at the instructor's discretion and communicated directly to the student. Short-Term Absences cannot be declared during the Final Examination period. 

    A missed presentation or debate will not have a make-up or extension option and may only be exempted.

If an exemption is made, the assessment will have no grade assigned. The instructor will communicate how the grade of the missed assessment will be distributed amongst the remaining assessments in the course.

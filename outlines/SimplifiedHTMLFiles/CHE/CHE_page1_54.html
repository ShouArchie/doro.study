Course Description

CHE 543:

Overview of polymer production technology and polymer reaction engineering analysis tools. Examples from chain growth polymerization processes (free radical, ionic, living/controlled radical variants, metal co-ordination catalysis, etc.), different modes of polymer reactor operation (batch, semi-batch, continuous flow stirred-tank reactor (CSTR), tubular, trains of CSTRs), homogeneous (bulk/solution/suspension) and heterogeneous (emulsion/dispersion/co-ordinated/etc.), linear and branched/cross-linked chains. Calculations and mathematical models for rate, copolymer composition, molecular weight and sequence length characteristics, for polymer reactor design/optimization of polymer productivity and quality variables.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand basic properties of polymer molecules (both micro-structural properties, formed in the reactor, and final application properties), such as molecular weight averages, molecular weight distribution, average chain composition, copolymer composition distribution, average sequence length and distribution, crystallinity, glass transition temperature, etc. Students will be able to appreciate what makes polymer molecules unique (and so different from low molecular weight molecules) and hence so popular in nowadays applications (both commodity and specialty applications) (1, 2)
Be fluent with Polymer Reaction Engineering (PRE) tools (mathematical modelling and reaction engineering tools for understanding/designing/troubleshooting/optimizing polymerization reactors, and for controlling polymer product properties); optimization and control strategies in order to deliver desirable properties for final applications (3, 4, 5)
Differentiate between design scenarios vs data analysis (safety/toxicity/effects on the environment/consumer) (6, 7, 8, 9)
Understand basic polymer characterization or sensing techniques to monitor polymer properties; bridge between Polymer Science/Chemistry and Polymer Processing/ Modification/Rheology) (3, 4, 5, 9)
Understand basics of Polymerization Production processes and how they relate to product design and properties (multicomponent polymer products, branched vs linear vs crosslinked molecules; emulsion/suspension vs bulk/solution; chain growth vs step growth vs ionic/coordinated systems with a catalyst vs controlled free radical or other specialty cases, etc.) (3, 4, 5, 9, 10, 11, 12)

The numbers in brackets refer to program indicators defined by the Faculty of Engineering to assess the twelve CEAB defined graduate attributes ( https://uwaterloo.ca/engineering/graduate-attributes).

Course Objectives

This is a quantitative course. The course will discuss the role of Polymer Reaction Engineering (PRE) as part of modern Polymer Production Technology. The emphasis is on PRE and production technology (process/product) troubleshooting aspects. Interspersed in the course are aspects of polymeric material properties (both micro-structural and final application properties) and some related material from Polymer Characterization techniques. Hence, PRE is the bridge between Polymer Science/Chemistry and Polymer Processing/ Modification/Rheology. The idea is to become fluent with polymer reactor aspects and the basic tools of PRE from mathematical modelling in aid of polymerization process (and polymer product) understanding, operation troubleshooting and, finally, optimization/control. 

Very few undergrad (or grad) curricula cover this (internationally) from a Chemical Engineering perspective!

Assignment and homework problems, along with examples in notes (and other practice exercises/questions given in class during lectures) are important to understand principles and techniques.

Interactions (via e-mail) between instructor/students are encouraged (for addressing quick/simple questions and for other clarifications); if a question is longer, then individual/ group meetings will be scheduled via e-mail; if you have questions, the time to ask them and clarify them is during the course, in advance, and not 5 minutes before the assignments (or other homework problems or project components) are due!

Course Outline (tentative) (6 main chapters in course notes (more extensions depending on time and/or class appetite), covering the general themes that follow):

CH 0:  Introduction (to polymerization and polymeric materials)

CH 1:  Basic Concepts/Definitions

CH 2:  General Introduction; Polymerization Process Information

CH 3:  Homogeneous Polymerizations-Chain Growth (contains 5 major parts)

CH 4:  Heterogeneous Polymerizations-Emulsion/Dispersion (contains 2 major parts)

CH 5:  Other Polymerization processes-Ionic

CH 6: Other Polymerization Processes-Coordination

Given the generalized development of the course material, applicable to different types of reactors and processes, CH 0 to 3 constitute the main course. CH 4-6 are more specialized topics. Some parts of posted notes may be reading material or special topics. The above chapters will be complemented by 11 Addenda files (with either additional practical hints and/or more information on polymer applications/uses); the topics above are the hard-core PRE topics; discussions on polymeric material properties (both micro-structural and final application properties) will be interspersed in all chapters.

CH 0 to 2 represent review/overview material, so they will be covered more quickly.

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor and/or the University of Waterloo.  Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint or pdf slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or used by the instructor with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student’s educational experience.  However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights.  For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.  In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials.  However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online.  The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

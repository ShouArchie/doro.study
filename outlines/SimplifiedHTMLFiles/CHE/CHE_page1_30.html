Course Description

CHE 341:

Laplace transform techniques. Proportional-integral-derivative control. Frequency response methods. Stability analysis. Controller tuning. Process control simulation and computer control systems. Process identification.

View requirements for CHE 341

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand the concepts of process control and its relation to industrial processes. [1d]
Develop basic process dynamic models from fundamental chemical engineering processing units. [2a, 2b]
Differentiate between the different process dynamics expected from chemical engineering processes. [1a, 1d]
Understand the concept of feedback in process control and the basic equipment/devices needed to perform basic feedback control for chemical engineering processes. [2a, 2c, 3c, 5a]
Design and tune basic feedback controllers (i.e. P, PI, PID) and understand the advantages and limitations of each controller. [1d, 2a, 2b, 3b, 4a, 4b, 5a, 5b, 5c]
Understand the basic concepts of advanced process control systems (e.g. cascade control, feed forward). [1d, 3b]
Develop the ability to use computer software to help describe and design control systems. [2b, 4c, 5a, 5b, 5c]
Comprehend the concept of process stability and predict the closed-loop behavior of simple control loops. [1a, 1d, 2c, 5c]
Understand (qualitatively) the role of valves and sensors in process control. [1d]
Model single-input-single-output (linear) dynamic systems in different representations (e.g., time-domain, Laplace domain, state-space) and describe the relative advantages/limitations of each. [1a, 1d, 2b]

The numbers in brackets refer to program indicators defined by the Faculty of Engineering to assess the twelve CEAB defined graduate attributes ( https://uwaterloo.ca/engineering/graduate-attributes).

Assignments

There will be assignments throughout the term designed to reinforce you understanding of course material. The assignments are designed to help you practice for your midterm and final exam. Problem sets will be graded based on the correctness and presentation of your solutions. You may work with your peers to complete the assignment; however, the final work you submit should be your own personal solution. Assignments submitted after the due date will receive a grade of zero. All assignments will be submitted and marked using CrowdMark and all Python files must be submitted in a Learn dropbox. 

Tentative due dates of assignments: Jan. 23, Feb. 6, Feb. 27, Mar. 13, Mar. 27, Apr. 4

 

Tutorials

You will receive a grade based on your attendance and participation in tutorial. Tutorial sessions will be used to complete activities that reinforce the material covered in lecture. The format of tutorials will vary from week to week.

 

Midterm and Final Exam

You will be notified of the format of each exam at least one week prior to the exam. The midterm is scheduled for Tuesday March 4th from 3:00 - 5:00 PM. The final exam will be scheduled by the Registrar.

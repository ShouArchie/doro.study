Course Description

Welcome to ChE640: Principles of Polymer Science. This course is designed to provide a practical foundation in polymer science. We will start with a general introduction to polymers and then learn about how to synthesize, characterize, design, and apply polymers. We will also emphasize the sustainability challenges associated with polymers and emerging strategies to deal with those challenges. Finally, we will discuss some of the emerging applications of polymer-based materials. This course will also emphasize the skills needed for a career in polymer science: critical analysis of the literature, research proposal development, and communication. Please see the learning outcomes below for more details.

 

Course Structure

The course consists of three lecture hours each week split into two 90-minute sessions. Lecture slides for each week will be posted on Learn on the Sunday evening before class at the latest. Assigned readings for the literature discussions will be provided at least one week in advance. Thirty minutes of lecture time each week will be dedicated to discussing a research article from the literature. The grade evaluations in the course include lecture participation, midterm and final exams, and a research proposal project.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Demonstrate a deep understanding of polymer science, polymer application, and the environmental impact of polymers.
Use fundamental knowledge to describe, synthesize, and characterize polymers and the materials that are made of polymers.
Understand what governs the physical properties of polymer materials.
Awareness of the sustainability concerns that surround polymers and some potential solutions.
Awareness of emerging applications of polymer materials.
Critically analyze the literature in polymer science.
Design and propose research projects in the area of polymer science.
Present soft skills, including technical communication, reading, presentation, and critical thinking.

Lecture Participation (10%)

The lecture participation grade is split into two components: 

  1. In class problem solving and concept check questions (5%) 
    • Vevox, a Q&A and polling platform, will be used multiple choice for in-class problem solving and concept check questions. The questions will be designed to prepare you for the midterm and final exams.
    • One mark will be given for answering each question. A bonus mark will be given for providing the correct answer. Bonus marks from correct answers can be used to make up for points missed when absent from lectures.
  2. Literature discussion reflections (5%) 
    • Critical analysis of the literature is a key learning outcome of this course.
    • One research article will be assigned for each unit (see lecture schedule, there are 7 total literature discussions throughout the course).
    • We will have a 30min in class discussion on each article at the end of lecture on Thursdays.
    • To receive participation marks, in groups of 2-3, students will write me at least one sentence response to each of the following questions by the end of class on Thursday: 1. What was a strength of the study? 2. What was a weaknesses of the study? 3. What would you do next as a follow up to this study?
    • Each response will be worth one mark.
    • In the last two weeks of the course you will earn participation grades by asking at least two questions during student presentations.
    • At the end of the term, I will divide the literature discussion marks by 0.7. In other words, you only need to receive 70% of the marks to get full marks for literature discussion reflections.

Midterm Exam (20%)

The midterm exam will be held in class on February 13th. The midterm exam will cover content taught in weeks 1-5. Questions will have a similar format to the in class example problems and concept check questions.

Final Exam (30%)

The final exam will be held during the final exam period. The final exam will be cumulative. Questions will have a similar format to the in-class example problems and concept check questions.

Proposal Project (40%)

Developing and proposing research ideas is one of the key skills you should gain as a graduate student. Through it this course you will learn how to critically analyze studies done by various research groups in polymer science. You will use those skills to propose your own research project. You will select a research group that works in polymer science and then develop proposal for a project you would work on during a two-year postdoctoral fellowship within that research group. The proposal will follow the format of an NSERC Postdoctoral Format (2 page written proposal, not including references). The project is divided into three parts:

  1. Outline (5%, due on February 10)
    • The outline will be maximum 1 page and will include:
      • The name of the research group you selected.
      • The title of the proposal.
      • A 3-4 bullet point description the the motivation for the proposal.
      • The overall all objective of the proposal.
      • Two to three sub-objectives with a brief description of methods used in each.
      • A 1-3 bullet point description of the project's impact.
  2. Proposal (25%, due on March 17)
    • The proposal will follow the format of an NSERC Postdoctoral Fellowship Proposal. (scroll down to the heading "Outline of Proposed Research")
    • The proposal must be 2 pages, and figures are optional. The page limit does not include references.
    • More details on specific exceptions will follow.
  3. Pitch Presentation (10%, presentations, presentations will take place from March 18th to April 3)
    • You will do a 10-minute presentation to pitch your proposed research project, followed by up to 4 min of discussion.
    • More details on specific exceptions will follow.

Late Submission: Late submissions for the outline and proposal will be accepted, but 10% will be deducted from the assignment's grade for each day late. Exceptions to this rule include AccessAbility accommodations, self-declared absences, and VIF.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as scientific writing, critical analysis of the literature, and proposal development. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

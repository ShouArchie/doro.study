Course Description

CHE 331:

Topics and applications of electrochemistry and electrochemical engineering. Industrial process examples. Environmental aspects. Ionic equilibria. Laws of electrolysis. Theory of electrolytes. Transport properties of electrolytes. Reversible cell potentials. Irreversible electrode processes. Thermodynamic and kinetic aspects of corrosion. Common examples of corrosion. Electrochemical energy conversion and storage.

View requirements for CHE 331

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Extend chemical thermodynamics to electrochemical systems. [1c, 1d]
Use thermodynamics to predict when a reaction can happen and to describe the activation overpotential of electrokinetic processes. [2b, 7c]
Apply fundamentals of transport phenomena to describe overall cell performance. [2b, 7c]
Design electrochemical cells and reactors for electrolysis and galvanic processes under given conditions. [3b, 3c, 4b, 7c]
Apply principle of galvanic cells to understand and predict environmental corrosion processes. [3b, 3c, 4b, 5a, 5c, 7c]
Design and assess corrosion mitigation strategy to extend the lifetime of materials/parts. [4a, 4b, 5a, 5c, 7c]

The numbers in brackets refer to program indicators defined by the Faculty of Engineering to assess the twelve CEAB defined graduate attributes ( https://uwaterloo.ca/engineering/graduate-attributes).

Suggested Textbook: 

Since this is a special topic, there is not one specific textbook for this course. Several textbooks, custom courseware and various journal articles have been used to develop the course content. The most useful textbook in the context of learning fundamental electrochemistry is Bard & Faulkner, Electrochemical Methods: Fundamentals and Applications and also, Electrochemical Science and Technology by Oldham, Myland and Bond was used as a reference for part of the lecture material. Excerpts from these texts will be uploaded to learn as background reading.

Student Assessment

Component Value
Assignments 10%
Quizzes (4) 65%
Final Quiz (during exam period) 25%

While some collaboration on assignments is expected, quizzes are to be completed independently and any evidence of cheating on quizzes will be subject to disciplinary action.

IMPORTANT:   You cannot fail quiz component and still be given a passing mark in the course. If you fail the quizzes, your overall mark in the course will be the average of your quiz grades.

Assignments will be administered through Crowdmark and Learn. Assignments and assignment solutions will be posted on Learn while clear, scanned (or photographed) digital copy of completed assignments will be submitted to Crowdmark for grading. Assignments will be graded coarsely based on level of completion and distributed back to students via Crowdmark. 

The first four quizzes will be carried out in class according to the schedule posted on Learn. The fifth and final quiz will be carried out during the final examination period and schedule by the registrar. Since each section of this course builds on the previous content, all quizzes are effectively cumulative and are normally in the form of one or two long answer problems. The final quiz, like any final exam, is a mandatory component and cannot be dropped.

Intellectual Property

Students should be aware that this course contains the intellectual property of their instructor, TA, and/or the University of Waterloo.  Intellectual property includes items such as:

  • Lecture content, spoken and written (and any audio/video recording thereof);
  • Lecture handouts, presentations, and other materials prepared for the course (e.g., PowerPoint slides);
  • Questions or solution sets from various types of assessments (e.g., assignments, quizzes, tests, final exams); and
  • Work protected by copyright (e.g., any work authored by the instructor or TA or used by the instructor or TA with permission of the copyright owner).

Course materials and the intellectual property contained therein, are used to enhance a student’s educational experience.  However, sharing this intellectual property without the intellectual property owner’s permission is a violation of intellectual property rights.  For this reason, it is necessary to ask the instructor, TA and/or the University of Waterloo for permission before uploading and sharing the intellectual property of others online (e.g., to an online repository).

Permission from an instructor, TA or the University is also necessary before sharing the intellectual property of others from completed courses with students taking the same/similar courses in subsequent terms/years.  In many cases, instructors might be happy to allow distribution of certain materials.  However, doing so without expressed permission is considered a violation of intellectual property rights.

Please alert the instructor if you become aware of intellectual property belonging to others (past or present) circulating, either through the student body or online.  The intellectual property rights owner deserves to know (and may have already given their consent).

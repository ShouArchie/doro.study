Course Description

CO 353:

Formulations of combinatorial optimization problems, greedy algorithms, dynamic programming, branch-and-bound, cutting plane algorithms, decomposition techniques in integer programming, approximation algorithms.

View requirements for CO 353

A discrete optimization problem, broadly speaking, involves a discrete set of feasible solutions and a real-valued objective function that assigns a value to each solution. The goal is to find a solution that minimizes  (or maximizes) the objective value. Enumerating all feasible solutions is typically impractical or results in algorithms with prohibitively large running times. This class of problems includes many NP-hard problems, which, unless P=NP, do not have efficient solution methods.

Discrete optimization problems are of immense practical importance, are solved every day (sometimes each hour or more frequently) by many Fortune 500 companies;  well-known problems in the class include

  • The Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP): Given n cities and pairwise distances between (some of) these cities, the goal is to find the shortest tour that visits all cities.
  • The Set Cover Problem: Given a universe U of elements and subsets S1,…,Sm​ of U, the objective is to find the smallest number of these subsets that collectively cover all of U.

This course provides an introduction to the broad field of discrete optimization and covers several classes of commonly used techniques for the design of algorithms, including:

  • Greedy algorithms,
  • Dynamic programming,
  • Local search, and
  • Solution approaches using mathematical programming.

We will discuss algorithmic efficiency and introduce the basics of complexity theory. For NP-hard problems, we will explore the field of approximation algorithms, which provides efficient methods for computing solutions with guaranteed quality. 

Students will complete two practical projects as part of the course, where they will develop, implement, and test algorithms for specific discrete optimization problems.

The class will rely on formal proofs to argue about properties of the presented algorithms. Students will be asked, on occasion, to produce proofs to argue in their assignment work. Handling proofs at the level of CO250 suffices to pass this course. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
formulate discrete optimization problems;
identify and apply algorithms that can be used to solve selected discrete optimization problems;
prove correctness of above algorithms and results related to the structures of discrete optimization problems.

Student Assessment

Component Value
Final exam 40 %
Midterm exam 30 %
Projects 20 %
Assignments 10 %

Assignments. Assignments are to be done individually. Students are allowed to discuss the assignments with classmates, but all submitted solutions should be obtained and written by the submitting student. For example, copying or paraphrasing of solutions for previous course offerings or online resources are to be treated as a violation of academic integrity. Each collaboration and resource used to prepare the submission should be appropriately acknowledged in the submission. ("I discussed the possible approaches for Q1 with Alice Sand.", "The idea for Q1 was taken from Chapter 8 in the book "Approximation Algorithms" by Vijay Vazirani.") A presence of such an acknowledgement is not going to have a negative impact on the grade, but an absence of an acknowledgement or its parts can be considered as a violation of academic integrity.

Projects. Projects are to be done in groups of two-three. Projects are to be implemented using C++ or Python or Java, it is your responsibility to make sure that you have a sufficient proficiency in C++ or Python or Java to succeed in the course. This is the responsibility of students to follow the format specified in the project description. Submissions not following the specified format might be assessed based only on their performance and correctness of their output without providing feedback. For the projects, the same policies hold as for assignments. The collaboration within the submitting group do not need to be mentioned, while all other collaborations and used resources need to be acknowledged. 

Midterm exam. The exam takes place during the regular class time slot.

INC. To be considered for INC a student has to pass at least one Midterm exam, i.e. obtain at least 50 % for one of the Midterm exams. Additionally, to be considered for INC a student has to obtain at least 70 % of the projects and assignments, i.e. obtain at least 21 % of the final grade through projects and assignments. 

In case you are not able to participate in some of the assessments please visit this webpage to submit the required information. After the instructor is notified about the successful submission, the weight of the missed assessment should be reallocated to the Final exam.

To pass the course one needs to get at least 50% on two of the three in-person assessments, i.e. one final exam and two midterm exams.

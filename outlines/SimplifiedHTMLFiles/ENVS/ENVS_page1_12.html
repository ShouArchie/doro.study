Course Description

ENVS 220:

Evaluation of various economic approaches to the environment. The links between economics, systems and the natural environment will be explored and future directions examined.

View requirements for ENVS 220

This course provides an engaging introduction to ecological economics, framed through the lens of Doughnut Economics by Kate Raworth. Designed for students from diverse academic backgrounds, no prior knowledge of economics is required. Together, we will explore how ecological economics challenges the assumptions of conventional economics and expands its scope to address the pressing challenges of sustainability, equity, and planetary boundaries.  The course begins by defining ecological economics, distinguishing it from traditional economic theories and environmental and resource economics. Using the Doughnut model as a central guide, we will examine the concept of a “safe and just space for humanity” and the interplay between social foundations and ecological ceilings.  

Key topics include:  

  • Rethinking Growth: Investigating the limits of economic growth, the role of natural resources, and critiquing growth-centric paradigms.
  • Redefining Progress: We will compare conventional measures like GDP with alternative metrics such as ecological footprints, planetary boundaries, and well-being and indices of human to assess their relevance in shaping policy and decision-making.
  • Equity and Justice: Exploring themes of inequality, just distribution, and global economic dynamics. Special attention will be given to the intersection of ecological economics with issues like climate justice, social equity, and intergenerational ethics.
  • Tools for Change: Evaluating policies and strategies, including taxes, subsidies, quotas, emissions trading, and innovative approaches like circular economies and regenerative practices. We will analyze their effectiveness through both economic and ecological perspectives.
  • Globalization and Local Action: Understanding the environmental implications of globalization and the role of local communities in driving sustainable change.

 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Understand and Differentiate: Explain the foundational principles of ecological economics and how they contrast with conventional economic theories and environmental/resource economics. Articulate the core concepts of the Doughnut Economics framework, including social foundations and ecological ceilings.
Critically Evaluate: Assess the strengths and limitations of traditional economic metrics (e.g., GDP) and alternative measures of progress, such as ecological footprints, well-being indices, and planetary boundaries. Critique growth-centric economic models from ecological, social, and ethical perspectives.
Apply Analytical Tools: Analyze policy options like taxes, subsidies, quotas, and emissions trading through both economic and ecological lenses, considering their potential impacts on sustainability and equity. Use systems-thinking approaches to apply Doughnut Economics principles to real-world challenges in areas such as energy, food systems, or urban planning.
Engage with Complex Issues: Explore the relationship between economic globalization, environmental degradation, and social inequality, identifying potential pathways toward a more sustainable and just global economy. Discuss the ethical dimensions of economic policies, including intergenerational equity and climate justice.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

Course Description

STV 100:

This course examines the interaction of the technologies developed by a culture with the values and social organization of that culture. The course exposes students to various definitions of society, technology and values, and it presents alternative views about how the three interact. These views are then applied to a number of spheres of influence, including patterns of employment and the role of work; medicine and health; polity and economy; sustainable development and the environment.

View requirements for STV 100

STV 100 is a course about how people (including you!) think about technology and society. It’s open to all students, and draws on many disciplines, including history, philosophy, sociology, anthropology, and more. In the end, the goal is to improve your critical thinking abilities, particularly when it comes to technology and how it interacts with society and values.

This is a “flipped” course. That means before most classes there are short video lectures, readings, and a quiz which are all required. This way, we can use the in-class lecture time for active learning, group activities, and discussions.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Identify concepts and perspectives related to the interaction between technology and society, and classify examples.
Compare such perspectives, and test their claims against factual evidence.
Identify embedded technological values and surrounding contexts.
Assess their own technological perspectives and values within the context of their academic discipline and personal experience.

A new laptop should:

  • Be light enough to carry regularly.
  • Have a hinge that is going to last for years - avoid plastic cases.

Pre-Knowledge Surveys

There are 11 pre-knowledge surveys. The purpose is to gather student perspectives, which helps spark in-class discussions and analysis. There are no right or wrong answers. You get credit for submitting almost anything, aside from jokes or something inappropriate. They are not anonymous.

Your grade is proportional to the number of complete surveys. So 11 surveys across 5% means each is worth approximately 0.45%.

The deadline is always 9am on Friday. No late submissions are permitted, and if you forget there are no extensions or alternatives.

Unfortunately, LEARN's “Survey” feature does not work well for an activity that corresponds to a grade credit, so look under Quizzes or follow the link in the module folder.

Module Quizzes

There are eight quizzes, based on pre-class study material. These are low-stakes and have two goals: so you can test your understanding of the material, and to encourage everyone to be ready for group activities in-class.

Quizzes are marked with * on the schedule, and the deadline is always 6pm.

Each quiz is multiple choice and graded automatically, with questions drawn randomly from a pool. You can choose when to complete the quiz, but there is a 25-minute time limit once you begin. No late quiz submissions are possible.

Your single lowest quiz grade will be automatically dropped. Seven quiz grades count across 10% so each quiz is worth around 1.43%.

The quizzes are open book. That is, during the quiz you may review your notes, the lectures, and any materials I posted to LEARN. You may not communicate directly or indirectly with anyone else, except the instructor or TAs, or share any quiz questions or answers. By starting a quiz, you agree to honour these terms.

These are not complicated or difficult quizzes, if you review the study material ahead time and take notes. Pay heed: many students have told me that taking notes while studying was key to doing well on the quizzes and the in-class exercises.

In-class Engagement

During the lecture slot, students will work in groups on multiple activities. Groups are small (3-4 students) and you can choose your own group. The activities allow you to practice with concepts and prepare you for assignments.

Each group submits a summary of their discussion before leaving for the night, and each student in the group will share the grade.

There are twelve engagement opportunities, one per week. All weigh the same, and the lowest single grade is automatically dropped. The top eleven count, so each is worth about 3.2%.

No remote participation is permitted. If you can’t attend, your grade is zero. If you provide Verification of Illness or self-declare an absence, the grade will be exempted from the final calculation.

Complete attendance is required. All group members must be present for the entire engagement activity for full credit. If you can only attend for some of the activity, speak to the instructor to arrange partial credit. Otherwise, leaving before it is complete, or arriving after it has begun, or adding the name of someone who is not present is misrepresentation and will lead to academic discipline for the entire group.

Assignments

There are two major assignments worth 17.5% each. See the schedule above. The deadline is always 11:59pm.

These are not essays, but structured reports, where you will analyze a topic using concepts taught in class and add your own research to reach an enlightened conclusion.

Further instructions and a dropbox will be available on LEARN.

Self-Assessments

There are three self-assessments, spread evenly across the schedule, worth 5% each. The deadline is always 11:59pm.

You will reflect on your efforts and assign yourself a grade. I will review the self-assessments, and your grade should correspond with the provided rubric. If not, I may lower or raise the grade.

Further instructions and a dropbox will be available on LEARN.

Course policies

Asking for help.

Please: Don’t be afraid to ask for help! There’s no shame!

Being a student is challenging and it is normal to need support from time-to-time. I know it can feel embarrassing to ask for help, but I seriously don’t mind helping everyone. That’s what I’m here for, and I won’t think anything less of you.

I also take mental health concerns seriously. Students who are experiencing anxiety, stress, or other mental health issues, whether related to this course or not, can get in touch with me at any time. I will always listen and do what I can to help.

There are mental health professionals available at UW's Counselling Services and Campus Wellness programs. If you need to speak with someone for emotional support, Good2Talk is a post-secondary student helpline based in Ontario, Canada that is available to all students.

Extensions

You can request three-day extensions for any and all Assignments or Self-Assessments, so long as you do so by noon on the day of the original deadline. Just email me and let me know how many extra days you need. You don’t need a reason or an excuse. I’m happy offering a small bit of flexibility.

There are no extensions for surveys, quizzes or in-class exercises.

Gender-inclusive language

Gender-inclusive and fair language should be used in the interests of precision and politeness. It’s not hard to familiarize yourself with this kind of writing and why it’s important.

Excessive disruptions

Arriving late to class, talking loudly with neighbours, or using phones or laptops inappropriately in class can be disruptive to those around you. Excessive disruptions that affect others will not be tolerated. The TAs and I will call you out.

Final grades

There are no re-submissions or bonus assignments at the end of term to increase your grade. If you want to maximize your grade, ask for help before each deadline during the term from the instructor or TA.

I do not curve or adjust final grades. There is no “golden boot” that bumps a 79 to an 80. What you see on LEARN is what you’ll see on your official transcript, rounded to a whole integer.

Feedback

I encourage students provide feedback at all times. Contact me directly or submit your thoughts to the Anonymous Feedback Survey on Learn. I will address all feedback as transparently and as soon as possible.

 

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used in this course with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Permitted uses of and expectations for using GenAI will discussed in class and outlined on assignment instructions. 

Recommendations for how to cite generative AI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

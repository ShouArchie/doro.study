Course Description

PHYS 370L:

Selected experiments in astronomy.

View requirements for PHYS 370L

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
create a detailed observing plan for an astronomical experiment
collect observational data using modern equipment, such as CCDs and robotic telescopes
scientifically analyze observational data using research-grade software packages
interpret the results of a scientific analysis astrophysically for the selected experiments
describe an astronomical experiment and communicate the results in a format suitable for a peer-reviewed publication

Tentative Course Schedule

 

*availability of experiments subject to change; repeating previously completed experiments is not normally permitted

The experiments require evening observation from the roof-top observatory and are dependent on the weather (hence the dates marked with double-asterisks** are subject to minor adjustment at the instructor's discretion). Since clear skies are uncommon this time of year, please collect your data at the earliest opportunity; we will attempt to make every clear night during the term (Monday – Friday, and likely including weekends and reading week) available for observation. If the evening cloud forecast is favourable, we will announce observing sessions daily in the early afternoon on LEARN. Students may be assigned to specific days and/or allowed to sign-up for timeslots via an online poll. If you are travelling, remember that all scheduling will be in the time zone for Waterloo (UTC-4 or UTC-5 depending on Daylight Saving Time).

CONTINGENCY PLANS

Student Absence: If a legitimate reason (including, for example, illness, family emergencies, COVID-19 related absence, international travel restrictions, etc.) prevents you from participating in your scheduled lab session, please contact the coordinator as soon as possible so alternative arrangements can be made (do not attend other lab sections without asking the coordinator). You may be asked to submit relevant documentation/forms. Please note that short-term self-declared absences are specifically not accepted for LAB courses.

If the completion of a report was affected, please go ahead and complete the missing work as soon you are able after submitting the relevant documentation/forms (do not wait for a response from the instructor, since this may be slow at times).

Note that midterm exams are not an acceptable reason since you will typically have many days to complete reports, however we do accept late reports (see policy in the appropriate section below). 

TA Absence: If a TA is unable to attend a observing session then an alternate TA or instructor will take their place. If one is not available at short notice, then the lab session may be rescheduled or offered in a different format. Please monitor your e-mail and LEARN announcements for this type of information.

Student Assessment

Component Value
Lab reports #1 and #2 (equally weighted) 60%
Lab report #3 40%

Note that your overall grade may be adjusted to account for variation between different markers.

General Expectations:

  • please read the “Course Overview” document posted on LEARN for general information on preparing for your observing sessions

Collaboration Rules:

  • you will normally be required to collect data in pairs or groups of three, and you are expected to equitably participate in all aspects of doing so
  • unless the instructor permits otherwise, "raw" or "unreduced" data taken as part of an experiment may only be shared with students that participated in collecting it (i.e. present during the observation)
  • you are encouraged to discuss and critically analyze ideas with your colleagues in order to better understand the principles and concepts in these labs, but you may not share partially or fully completed work or your electronic files with anyone
  • your lab report (see next section) must reflect your own understanding, and all aspects must be completed 100% independently (not collaboratively)
  • see more below under “Academic Integrity” or feel free to ask the coordinator if you have questions

Report Format:

  • please write lab reports following the format in the document posted on our LEARN course hub; the Writing and Communication Centre also has resources to help you with lab report writing (information in the section below)
  • lab reports must be submitted as a single PDF file to the correct LEARN dropbox folder by 11:59 PM on the due date

Late Policy:

  • late reports will be penalized at a rate of 2 marks per day out of 100 total (weekends and holidays/study days/reading week do not count)
  • late reports will be accepted for a maximum of 10 working days; please contact the instructor to make arrangements if your report might be later than this
  • if you earn a passing grade (minimum 50/100) on each report (before applying late penalties), then you will be awarded (at least) an overall course grade of 50
  • the final date to submit any content will be April 21st, 2025

Final Experiment:

  • you are expected to incorporate your learnings and feedback from prior experiments in the final report
  • there may be some additional requirements for this experiment; further details will be announced near the end of term
  • this report will be due on April 21st, 2025

Bonus:

A typo bounty is up for grabs in this course! If you are the first person to spot and report a typo in any of the course material, you could be awarded a bonus to your overall course grade, perhaps from 0.5% to 2%, depending on how egregious the typo is. Report of minor typos is appreciated but will be worth less than something that could result in a real misunderstanding of some part of an experiment (you are welcome to make your case for how important the typo is). Note that a very minor grammar mistake may not result in any bonus. The value of the bonus is entirely at the instructor's discretion.

The Writing and Communication Centre works with students in all Faculties to help you consider your audience, clarify your ideas, develop your voice, and write in the style appropriate to your discipline.

We offer one-on-one support for writing papers, delivering presentations, integrating research, and revising for clarity and coherence. Group appointments for team-based projects, presentations, and papers are also available.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as [fill this in with your discipline-specific skills]. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

University Policy

Read Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances, Section 4. When in doubt, please be certain to contact the department’s administrative assistant who will provide further assistance.

Discipline: A student is expected to know what constitutes academic integrity to avoid committing an academic offence, and to take responsibility for their actions. [Check the Office of Academic Integrity for more information.] A student who is unsure whether an action constitutes an offence, or who needs help in learning how to avoid offences (e.g., plagiarism, cheating) or about “rules” for group work/collaboration should seek guidance from the course instructor, academic advisor, or the undergraduate associate dean. For information on categories of offences and types of penalties, students should refer to Policy 71, Student Discipline. For typical penalties, check Guidelines for the Assessment of Penalties.

Appeals: A decision made or penalty imposed under Policy 70, Student Petitions and Grievances (other than a petition) or Policy 71, Student Discipline may be appealed if there is a ground. A student who believes they have a ground for an appeal should refer to Policy 72, Student Appeals.

Note for students with disabilities: AccessAbility Services, located in Needles Hall, Room 1401, collaborates with all academic departments to arrange appropriate accommodations for students with disabilities without compromising the academic integrity of the curriculum.

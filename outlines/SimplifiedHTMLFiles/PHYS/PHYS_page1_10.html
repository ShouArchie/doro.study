Course Description

PHYS 115:

Brief review of kinematics. Particle dynamics, work, energy, conservation of energy. Conservation of linear momentum, collisions, rotational kinematics and dynamics, conservation of angular momentum. Equilibrium of rigid bodies.

View requirements for PHYS 115

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Define and describe core concepts of classical mechanics (e.g., free fall, force, energy) and provide real-world examples to demonstrate their applications [KB, LL].
Break down complex physical scenarios into their underlying principles, identifying relationships between forces, motion, and energy; for instance, by drawing free body diagrams [KB, PA].
Identify known and unknown variables, organize given information, and formulate problem statements to solve physical problems effectively [KB, PA].
Evaluate physical problems to determine the governing mechanical principles, such as Newton's laws, conservation laws, and kinematic equations, and apply them systematically [KB, PA].
Solve for unknown quantities through algebra, calculus, and vector analysis, and validate results through unit consistency and order-of-magnitude estimates [KB, PA].
Assess the plausibility of results by considering physical constraints, assumptions, and approximations, and identify discrepancies when outcomes deviate from expectations [KB, PA, LL].
Present solutions and explanations in a structured, concise, and professional format to others (e.g., classmates, teammates, TAs, instructors), using appropriate mathematical notation, diagrams, and verbal descriptions [IaTW, LL].

Note: Abbreviations in brackets (i.e., Knowledge Base (KB), Problem Analysis (PA),  Individual and Team Work (IaTW),  Life-long Learning (LL)) are Graduate Attributes required for Accreditation by the Canadian Engineering Accreditation Board (CEAB). See https://uwaterloo.ca/engineering/graduate-attributes for more details.

Missed Assessment Policy

The lowest two quizzes, lowest two tutorial questions, and lowest two assignments will be dropped—regardless of the reason. If you miss a quiz, tutorial question, or assignment, it will be recorded as a zero for grade calculation purposes. However, if you miss the midterm for a university-approved reason, the weighting will be transferred to the final exam. Please also read Illnesses and Absences below.

As outlined below, your final mark incorporates both in-class and out-of-class activities, individual and teamwork, as well as test-based and non-test-based assessments.

  • Frequency: Every Lecture Session
  • Performance Metric: Individual Participation
  • Level of Collaboration Allowed: Limited – Collaboration permitted only for designated think-pair-answer questions (See the “think-pair-answer” document on LEARN).

Participation in these activities also serves as an indicator of attendance, as lecture attendance is mandatory. Students must bring a personal device with secure Internet access to participate.

More about the Course

Attendance and Active Participation

Attendance and active participation in lecture and tutorial sessions are essential. Active teaching and learning are implemented throughout the course, so your engagement in the course activities is of paramount importance for your learning outcomes.

Lecture and tutorial sessions begin promptly at the scheduled time slot, so it is important that you arrive on time. Your attendance is monitored through different assessment components integrated into the course.

Note: This section is adapted from a similar section in the PHYS 115 course outline, Winter 2020.

Problem Solving Skills

The goal of the exams is to test your knowledge of the material as well as your problem-solving skills. While getting help will often be necessary, you will need to maximize your problem-solving proficiency. You must build the ability to solve problems independently within a limited amount of time, without assistance. The assigned problems are the minimum of what you should master. There is a strong alignment between the quizzes, assignments, practice problems, and exams.

TAs will mark the exams; hence, showing all your work and thought processes will be necessary for partial marks. Your grade will depend on clarity, organization, and correct presentation of solutions. The weekly quizzes are designed to help you practice problem-solving and solution presentation skills. You are encouraged to think critically, creatively, and develop confidence in approaching challenging problems.

Note: This section is adapted from a similar section in the PHYS 115 course outline, Winter 2020.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as problem solving and teamwork, related to the general attributes outlined by the Canadian Engineering Accreditation Board (CEAB) and used by the University of Waterloo. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

  • Student Success Office for help with skills like notetaking and time management
  • Writing and Communication Centre for assignments with writing or presentations
  • AccessAbility Services for documented accommodations
  • Library for research-based assignments

Students' submissions are stored on a U.S. server, therefore students must be given an alternative (e.g., scaffolded assignment or annotated bibliography), if they are concerned about their privacy and/or security. Students will be given due notice, in the first week of the term and/or at the time assignment details are provided, about arrangements and alternatives for the use of Turnitin in this course.

It is the responsibility of the student to notify the instructor if they, in the first week of term or at the time assignment details are provided, wish to submit alternate assignment.

Course Description

PHYS 444:

This course introduces students to the standard model of particle physics. Topics covered include symmetries, particle classification, experimental methods and tools, scattering, Feynman diagrams, gauge theories, quantum electrodynamics, quarks, quantum chromodynamics, weak interactions, and the Higgs mechanism.

View requirements for PHYS 444

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
To become familiar with basic concepts of elementary particle physics, and be able to use them to do quantitative computations relevant to physical experiments

Assignments: 

Half of your grade will be based on 5 biweekly assignments that are due on Fridays at 11:59pm EST/EDT, two weeks after they are posted. 

  • All assignments must be submitted in hand-written form with complete derivations and details. No exceptions will be made. Scanned copies of your assignment may be submitted through the Dropbox on LEARN.
  • The lowest graded assignment will be dropped from the final mark, so you can miss one assignment without loss of grade. Other assignments contribute equally to your final grade.
  • In case of late submissions, a penalty of 10% per 24h past due will apply (with the late time rounded up to the nearest multiple of 24h).
  • Grading will be based on accuracy, quality, and clarity of solutions.
  • Be aware of various opportunities for bonus marks which may crop up throughout the term.

Exams:

  • The midterm exam will be scheduled as an in-person written exam covering all material presented in the lectures/assignments up until that point.
  • The end term exam will be scheduled as an in-person written exam covering all material presented in the lectures/assignments after the midterm only. It is not cumulative.

Work expected: 

Students are expected to attend lectures, complete assigned readings, and work through the biweekly assignments. No exceptions to these requirements will generally be made in cases where students miss classes or do not complete assignments. The general workload for the course should be (on average) 3.5 hours of lectures+tutorials, 2 hours of reading, and 2 hours of assignments. Students should also check the course website regularly for course announcements.

Group Work/Discussion of Results: 

Collaboration and/or discussion with other students are allowed on assignments. Assignments must, nonetheless be completed individually by each student, using his/her own account.

Plagiarism in completing assignments is a serious offence, that can significantly affect your academic standing, and even prevent you from graduation. This includes (but is not limited to) verbatim copies of answers off the web (or off other students), without following proper citation protocols.

Medical Exceptions: 

Any medical exceptions must be documented by a signed medical note from a doctor or clinic, indicating the date and severity of the illness, and will only be accepted at the instructor’s discretion. Exams will not be excused for minor complaints. Students with chronic health problems should consult with the Office of Disabilities for alternate exam arrangements.

Please consult the Accommodation for illness or other extenuating circumstances.

Drop-dates: 

Students concerned about the level of the course, course requirements, or their performance should keep in mind cut-off dates for dropping courses without penalty. Please check these with the undergraduate advisor or the Faculty of Science office: http://ugradcalendar.uwaterloo.ca/page/uWaterloo-Calendar-Events-and-Academic-Deadlines

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

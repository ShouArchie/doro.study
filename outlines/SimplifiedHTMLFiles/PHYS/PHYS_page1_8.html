WHAT TO INCLUDE IN YOUR EMAIL:

When emailing either the TA's or the Course Administrator you should include the following things to make it as easy as possible to answer your question or solve your problem:

  • Subject Line: When writing an email you should be including something in your subject line so its easy to understand what your problem is. You should not be leaving this blank at any point.
  • Course Code: Both the TA's and the Course Administrator can be working on or facilitating multiple courses so it is important that you make it clear what course you are enrolled in and asking about.
  • Section Number: Similar to the Course Code there are a number of sections in this course so to make it easier for a solution to be determined it is important to include your section. The section number will be the number following the course code, something like "001"'
  • Name: It is important that you include your full name in your email so it is easy to determine who you are and to find more information on LEARN based on your name.

The key thing to understand is that both the TA's and the Course Administrator get many emails a day so anything you can do to make it easier for us to prioritize and answer question is important, especially if it prevents us from having to go back and forth to get information from you. A final note, whenever you are emailing someone related to a course you should be using your @uwaterloo.ca email address, the reason being is it acts a validation of who you are. There are generally limits on who we can talk with about student information, so to make it easier to confirm who you are simply use your University of Waterloo email address.

Course Description

PHYS 112L:

Biweekly lab for students who have taken or are taking PHYS112.

View requirements for PHYS 112L

Physicists are in the business of measuring the energy levels of hydrogen to six significant figures, and the distance to far away stars with surprising accuracy. But how do we know when we have made a good measurement? How do we set ourselves up for success in these ventures? 

This course will focus on developing experimental skills and critical thinking in data collection and analysis. Our primary goal is to leave you with skills, strategies, and attitudes that will be of value no matter what your future aspirations may be. You will learn how to make observations and measurements, how to build models that fit those measurements, and to derive meaning from the success or failure of those models. You will also learn a variety of technical skills including data-analysis techniques, keeping thorough laboratory notes, computer skills, statistical methods, and the ability to communicate results and ideas.

In this lab the experiments will formatted and designed in a different way compared to other Faculty of Science labs (CHEM, BIOL, etc.) or other labs you may have taken. The goal of this course is to teach you how to be a Scientist, not just to follow instructions. There will be times where you will be asked to figure out the best way to investigate a certain phenomena and will be given minimal instructions, the goal being to get you used coming up with ways to experimentally determine what you are looking for like scientists do when performing research. In some cases this may make it more difficult then other labs as you will not be given in-depth instructions for how to do things and need to rely on your own understanding and knowledge, but you should come out of this course being more confident in the aspects outlined in the previous paragraph. 

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Clearly communicate their experimental processes and results and justify their decisions.
Critically evaluate data collection methods and choose optimal experimental methods.
Analyze their data and reflect on and react to their results.
Develop models of physical systems and methods to test and refine them based on experimental results.
Quantify and propagate different types of experimental uncertainty.

STUDENT ABSENCES:

Illness: If you are sick, please do not come to lab. If you are ill and do miss an experiment you must submit a Verification of Illness Form (VIF) to the Absence Database in order to receive an accommodation. This form can be filled out by a healthcare practitioner at Campus Wellness OR  your own family/private healthcare practitioner. Please only submit your forms to the Absence Database, do not email them to the Course Administrator. It is your responsibility to contact the Course Administrator to make them aware of your absence. You must make accommodations with your instructor within 1 week of the end date of your VIF or else no accommodation can be guaranteed as the term progresses.

Creed/Religion: If you will be unable to attend a lab or meet a deadline due to a creed or religious observance you must complete the Religious Observance Self-Declaration Form in Quest. As the dates of important religious observances are generally known well in advance, students must consult with the Course Administrator within two weeks of the announcement of the due date or lab for which academic accommodation is being sought. Since all the deadlines of this course are outlined in this Course Outline the two weeks will be counted from the first day of the term, if you reach out to the Course Administrator after two week there is no guarantee that an accommodation will be able to be made.

Extenuating Circumstances: Students can request academic considerations for extenuating circumstances (bereavement, emergencies, University of Waterloo sanctioned academic or athletic events, etc.), and requests will be reviewed on a case-by-case basis. You must provide and submit official documentation to the Absence Database, the supporting documentation will be considered similarly to a VIF. Documentation is required before any action can be taken. The supporting documentation should provide a reason for the absence and the impacted dates, some examples are: plane/train/bus tickets for emergency travel, court documents, police reports, death certificates/obituaries/letters from funeral directors, and counsellor letters. The previous lists is not exhaustive. It is your responsibility to contact the Course Administrator to make them aware of your absence.

Short-Term Absences: Students have the ability to declare a two-day absence to excuse themselves from most academic responsibilities for up to two consecutive calendar days. This does not apply to LAB courses, if a student declares a self-declared short-term absence and it overlaps with a component of this course they are still expected to complete/attend.

More information about absences can be found here: https://uwaterloo.ca/science-undergraduate-office/academic-policies-and-procedures/accommodation-illness-or-other-extenuating-circumstances

Other absences: Any other absence is considered an "unapproved absence", and there will be no guarantee that an accommodation will be provided and is up to the discretion of the Course Administrator. Absences related to the course work or expectations of other classes--such as studying for midterms or completing assignments in other courses--will not be accommodated

If you have a chronic or on going issue that impacts your academics I would highly recommend you reach out to AccessAbility services (more information at the end of this outline) to determine a more comprehensive accommodation plan.

LAB NOTES:

Labs will be performed in groups of 2-3. Groups will submit one set of lab notes which are due at the end of the lab session, submitted to a Dropbox on LEARN. There will be a 5 minute grace period at the end of your lab session, but after that the Dropbox will be closed and no late submissions will be accepted for any reason (including technical). If for whatever reason you are having issues submitting your notes to the Dropbox on LEARN then you must email your submission to the Course Administrator before the deadline. Generally, you should aim to have a completed set of notes around 5 - 10 minutes prior to the deadline, this will allow you to use the remaining time to finalize and polish your submission. The grace period should not be treated like a blanket extension, it is there to accommodate any last minute issues you have, if you find yourself and your group working right to the final minute of the grace period you may want to reconsider how you are approaching the experiments.

The reason for these strict deadlines is to ensure the deadlines are fair to the students who managed their time effectively. To meet a deadline choices are made, whether that is to spend less time on certain sections, or adjust the process to make a measurement, all of which may have an impact on the grade. So to give some groups extra time while others have possibly made sacrifices to get their submission on time is not fair.

The Pre-Lab Reading Quizzes will have a deadline of 1 hour prior to the associated experiment.

POST-LAB QUIZZES:

Post- Lab Quizzes will be completed online, asynchronously. You will only have three attempts at each of the Post-Lab Quizzes. They should be completed individually, and students may use any course materials to guide their answers (i.e., "open book"). The Post-Lab Quizzes will be made available at the end of the associated experiment, and you will have 1 week to complete the quiz. The Pre-Lab Reading Quizzes will be made available 1 week prior to the deadline.

Generative AI: This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as data analysis. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

Course Description

PHYS 111:

An introduction to physics for students intending to concentrate their further studies in biology, dentistry, medicine and paramedicine; includes particle kinematics and dynamics, energy and momentum conservation, and rotational mechanics.

View requirements for PHYS 111

Course Overview

The main physics principles and laws covered in PHYS 111 include: “Particle kinematics and dynamics, energy and momentum conservation, and rotational mechanics.” PHYS 111 focuses on basic physics principles and laws, on conceptual understanding and problem-solving skills, and an appreciation for the power of physics in explaining how nature works, in the contexts of both the physical and life sciences.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Physics Principles and Laws: By the end of the course, students will be able to construct scientifically sound explanations of a variety of basic physical phenomena by applying basic physics principles/laws. For example, regarding conservation of momentum, students will be able to:
define linear momentum, and calculate and compare momenta of various real-life objects;
define and calculate impulse, and establish the conditions for conservation of linear momentum; and
apply the laws of conservation of momentum and energy to collisions. Real-life examples will be drawn from both the physical and life sciences, in students’ own disciplines and beyond. For example, regarding conservation of momentum, students will be able to explain how a rocket works and why modern cars have crumple zones and air bags to help reduce injuries to the human body during collisions.
Problem-Solving Skills and Strategies: By the end of the course, students will be able to creatively apply a variety of structured problem-solving strategies:
interpret, visualize and convert the problem into a physical model (e.g., draw a free body diagram);
identify assumptions, variables, and physics principles/laws involved;
represent these in the language of algebraic mathematics (write down the appropriate equations, combine, and solve them); and
evaluate the result — does it make sense? These problem-solving skills are useful in the students’ own disciplines and beyond.

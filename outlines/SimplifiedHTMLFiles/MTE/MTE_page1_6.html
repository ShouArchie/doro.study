Course Description

MTE 120:

Basic electromagnetic theory; magnetic circuits; electric circuit elements; DC circuit analysis; first-order transient response; AC circuit analysis. [Offered: W,S]

View requirements for MTE 120

MTE 120 is the course designator for an introductory circuits course divided into two main topics: electromagnetism and electric circuits. The electromagnetism portion explores the physics behind electric charge, electricity and magnetism. The electric circuits portion builds on the ideas from electromagnetism to explore fundamental circuit ideas and build a toolbox for solving circuit problems. The course concludes with a brief introduction to some more advanced circuit elements.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Apply electrostatics concepts to find the force, field and potential associated with a system of charges
Apply fundamental circuit laws to solve linear circuits
Choose and apply appropriate circuit theorems to simplify circuit models
Solve circuits with negative feedback operational amplifiers
Identify the relationship between magnetism and current
Solve first order RL and RC circuits using a standard solution form
Use phasors to solve AC circuits with R, L and C components
Use the AC power triangle relationship to solve problems with complex power

Tentative Course Schedule

In this course, lectures, tutorials, labs and problem sets are used to instruct you in the fundamentals of electricity, magnetism and circuits. Lectures introduce course material. Assignments and problem sets review concepts through a broader range of problems. Labs and tutorials provide an opportunity to experiment with select concepts and attempt challenging problems with plenty of help available.

Overall Grade Calculation

Your overall grade is calculated differently depending on your midterm and final exam marks. 𝑀 is your midterm average grade out of 100 % and 𝐹 is your final examination grade out of 100 %:

If 20/68𝑀 + 48/68𝐹 ≥ 55, your grade will be calculated as follows:

  • Labs & tutorials: 25 %
  • Online graded assignments: 7 %
  • Midterm: 20 %
  • Final Examination: 48 %

If 20/68𝑀 + 48/68𝐹 ≤ 40, that is your final grade.

Otherwise, the weight of labs and online assignments will drop linearly from 32 % to 0 % as the calculated grade of your quizzes and final drops from 55 % to 40 %.

NOTES:

  1. A grade of INC (Incomplete) is to be assigned if you do not satisfactorily attempt 8 of 9 labs. If the labs are not completed within 4 months of the end of the course to the satisfaction of the course instructor, the INC (Incomplete) grade will be converted to FTC (Failure to Complete). If the lab studies are completed successfully within the required timeframe, the overall grade will be calculated according to the formula provided with a grade of 0 used for any deliverables that were incomplete at the time of overall grade calculation.
  2. A grade of DNW (Did Not Write) is to be assigned if you do not write the final examination for any reason other than an acceptable, verifiable illness.
  3. The instructor reserves the right to use alternative grading schemes in exceptional circumstances.

Assignments

zyBooks Circuits (ZC) is the online circuit textbook used in this course (subscription required). Many topics include interactive problems to help you practice applying the concepts. While only challenge problems are included in the graded assignment, you are strongly encouraged to use the participation problems to check your understanding of key concepts as they are taught. Each graded assignment is marked on completion of the corresponding practice problems before the assignment deadline. You have unlimited attempts of each problem. There are 8 assignments throughout the term, each of which is worth 1% per assignment, to a maximum of 7%. If you are struggling with an assignment, please seek help from a TA, first year tutor or the instructor. Assignments completed after the deadline will receive a grade of 0 unless an extension has been granted by the instructor due to extenuating circumstances.

Ungraded problem sets are posted on Learn each week, final answers are included with the problem set and full solutions are posted the following week. Problem sets are not handed in; however, completion of these problems will allow you to regularly test and reinforce your comprehension of the concepts and analysis techniques presented throughout the term. Weekly completion will also help with pacing of the course. If you are struggling with a problem set, please seek help from a TA, first year tutor or the instructor.

Tutorials and Labs

Tutorial problems are completed individually and are graded during the tutorial. Lab studies are to be completed in pairs. Your lab report will be submitted to Crowdmark. It must be submitted by 11:00 pm on the day you complete the lab. Submissions up to 1 hour late will have a 25% penalty applied. A submission more than 1 hour late will have a 50% penalty applied. A submission that is more than 1 day late will not be accepted. If you anticipate needing to submit late for an unavoidable reason, you must contact the lab instructor before the deadline to discuss alternative arrangements. In the case of an emergency where you are unable to contact the lab instructor before the deadline, you must contact the lab instructor as soon as possible after the emergency has been dealt with. You may be asked for documentation to obtain an extension. Extensions will not be granted for reasons such as having too many assignments due. You are required to attempt at least 8 of the 9 labs. If there are outstanding labs at the end of the course, a grade of INC will be assigned until the labs are completed.

TAs and lab staff are available to assist you. Discussion between students is encouraged, but copying is prohibited. You are expected to prepare, perform and document lab experiments as your own work. This means you must not copy the calculations, measured data, or write up from other students (including submissions from previous terms). You are welcome to ask your classmates questions, but complete lab reports should never be shared.

Repeating students often have questions related to the completion of deliverables in the course. Students repeating this course are expected to participate in the course fully. The following material provides further clarification:

Exams

Written exams are the primary form of assessment used in this course. There will be two exams. A midterm will be held during midterm week and a final exam will be held during the final exam period.

Midterm
The midterm is 90 minutes in length. This is a closed book exam to be written in person. During the exam, no questions will be answered by the teaching team to ensure fairness for all students. Further details on the midterm will be provided on Learn at least one week before the exam. You will be provided with a copy of the formula sheet and may use a non-graphing calculator. No other resources are permitted.

Missing the midterm due to illness or a declared absence will result in the weight of the midterm exam being shifted to the final exam, provided that illness has been self-declared on Quest or a VOI has been submitted in accordance with current First Year Office guidelines, and you have notified the First Year Engineering Office and the course instructor. If the illness is not declared or if the explanation is not deemed acceptable, a grade of 0 will be assigned as the midterm grade. The weight of the examination will not be changed under any other circumstances. Declaration of illness forms must be submitted within 48 hours in accordance with university policy.

Final Exam
The final exam is a 150 minute, closed-book exam. The exam is cumulative. During the exam, no questions will be answered by the proctors to ensure fairness for all students. Further details on the final exam will be provided on Learn at least one week in advance. You will be provided with a copy of the formula sheet and may use a non- graphing calculator. No other resources are permitted.

Missing the final exam due to illness will result in an assigned grade of INC (Incomplete) provided that illness is self- declared on Quest or a VOI is completed in accordance with current First Year Office guidelines, and you have notified the First Year Engineering Office and the course instructor. The grade of INC (Incomplete) will be resolved by a deferred exam within 4 months of the end of the course. If no deferred exam is written within that timeframe, the grade will be converted to FTC (Failure to Complete). If the illness cannot be verified or if the explanation is not deemed acceptable, a grade of DNW (Did Not Write) will be submitted for the student.

Every effort will be made to provide a makeup exam on the last day of exams for students that are verifiably ill during the scheduled final exam slot. If you are ill and unable to write the final exam as scheduled, you are expected to be available for the makeup exam. The timing of this makeup exam will be determined once it is known which other exams on that date are to be written by the students who were ill.

***In the event that the University releases updated guidelines on the declaration of illness for the Winter 2025 term, those guidelines will supersede the declaration instructions in this document.***

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as analyzing and designing circuits. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence

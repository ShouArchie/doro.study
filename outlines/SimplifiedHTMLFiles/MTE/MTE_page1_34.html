Course Description

MTE 380:

In this course, students study the design process, including needs analysis, problem definition; design criteria and critical parameter identification, generation of alternative solutions; conceptual design, detailed design, optimization; and implementation. Most of the term is devoted to a significant design project in which student groups work independently and competitively, applying the design process to a project goal set by the faculty coordinator. The design project typically includes construction of an electro-mechanical prototype, and part of the course grade may depend on the performance of the prototype in a competitive test. In exceptional circumstances, the requirement for a prototype may be replaced by a computer simulation, or may be waived.

Learning Outcomes

The goals of this course are to gain experience with the nature, demands, and ramifications of the engineering design process. The students will gain an understanding of the complexity, difficulty, and time span involved in solving real open-ended problems. By the end of the course the students will be able to apply the design process to identify a need, formulate a problem

statement along with design specifications, generate alternative solutions, and implement their chosen solution. Students will be able to communicate the application of the design process through report writing and presentations.

The primary learning outcomes that are covered in MTE 380 are:

  • Apply the design process
    • Needs analysis
    • Problem formulation
      • Problem statement
      • Objectives
      • Constraints
      • Criteria
    • Conceptual/preliminary design
      • Idea generation
      • Conceptual design selection
    • Detailed Design (applying the theory from your other courses: design calculations, CAD drawings)
    • Implementation (construction, commissioning, testing, demonstration)
  • Ability to communicate your design
    • Report writing
    • Presentations

 

The secondary goals are not covered in depth in the lecture content, but my observation shows there is a lot of skill development and learning that happens through the project such as:

  • Manage a multi-discipline project
    • Work plan, negotiation, execution, evaluation
    • Decisions and tradeoffs
    • Project control via schedule and budget
    • Vendor relations
    • Resource limitations
  • Develop leadership/membership in team efforts:
    • Team planning
    • Division of work, delegation
    • Authority/responsibility/accountability
    • Resolution of personal conflict
    • Negotiation
    • Utilization of a variety of talents and skills
    • Personnel evaluation and criticism
    • Error correction/risk mitigation
  • To increase students' ability to apply course material, from this course and others:
    • Recognize applications and limitations
    • Reality vs. model

Course Information

“Engineering (design) is like dancing; you don’t learn it in a darkened lecture hall watching slides: you learn it by getting out on the dance floor and having your toes stepped on.”

-Jack Alford, Professor of Engineering Emeritus, Harvey Mudd College, Claremont, CA, USA.

Science, math, and engineering problems that you see in academia are usually well posed. The solution is unique, you know when you have solved the problem, and the problems you are solving have a narrow solution area. Design problems, however, are usually poorly posed, they can be ambiguous, and they rarely have unique solutions and you may not be sure if you have actually solved the problem. Design problems are usually multi-disciplinary.

MTE 380 is intended to be a “hands-on” and practical course different from already existing design courses. Design, construction, testing, and competition are the main features of this course. Having an understanding of previous and current courses will be necessary. Students study the design process, including needs analysis, problem definition, design constraints and criteria, generation and development of alternative solutions; conceptual design, detail design, optimization, and implementation. A significant design project is the centerpiece of this course.

Milestone 0 (a and b): Team Formation and Team Contract

Students work in project teams of four or five. The teams work on the design and construction of a project. The client (faculty) expects a level of effort of greater than 500 hours per term, per project team. To meet this goal, each student should plan to work an average of 8 hours per week on the project. The objective is to produce useful results on an authentic project to the client's satisfaction within the constraints of time and budget. The students do the work; the faculty informs, advises, coaches, and evaluates the results.

The proper formation of teams is essential for success. Each team should be comprised of members with the following skill sets: 

  • Planning and management functions
  • Leadership
  • Procurement
  • Material and parts selection
  • Machine shop and/or wood shop experience
  • CAD drawing expertise
  • Design analysis
    • Mechanical
    • Electrical and electronics
    • Software
  • Computer simulation
  • Microcontroller (Arduino, etc.) and computer programming
  • Microcontroller/Computer-sensor-actuator integration

It is the responsibility of the students to make sure that their team has the proper skill sets to ensure success.  While students are free to pick their teammates, the teaching team reserves the right to alter team composition for a better distribution of skills. If you are not registered in a group of 4 or 5 by the date stated above, the teaching team will form a team for you (if you are registered in a group of 3 or less, you may be split up into other groups). We encourage you to contact the TAs as soon as possible if you are having issues forming a team. 

As a part of working in a group you will create a group contract, which will then be applied to resolve any conflicts within the group. This contract should outline all member’s roles and responsibilities, and any penalties if group members do not fulfill their duties. If you do not contribute to your group you will lose marks, though the group must provide evidence that a member(s) did not contribute.

Milestone 2: Presentation – Proposal and Conceptual Designs

After forming your group, each team must come to an agreement on a work plan, which covers the team's activities and expectations for the term. Writing and negotiating this document takes two to three weeks of consultation, background study, and revision at the beginning of the term. Included with this presentation are the details of the needs assessment, problem formulation, conceptual designs and the decision-making process that leads to the selected design. The following are among the considerations:

  • The Problem
    • What is understood from the problem?
    • Background information
    • Needs assessment
    • Problem formulation
      • Problem statement
      • Desired functions and goals
      • Design specifications (objectives, constraints)
      • Design selection criteria
    • Key design problems that need to be considered
  • Project Planning
    • Team resume
    • Work breakdown structure
    • Description of progress monitoring
    • Risk identification (technical, schedule, resources, cost, …) and risk mitigation plans
    • Responsibility assignment
    • Gantt chart
    • Budget
    • Required facilities and equipment
  • Conceptual Design
    • Morphological chart
    • Design concepts
      • At least three different viable designs
      • Include freehand sketches of each conceptual design
      • Mapping of objectives and constraints to the design
    • Evaluation of alternative designs
      • Elimination process (if you have more than four design concepts, can any be eliminated instead of dragging a weak design through the evaluation process?)
      • Assigning weighting factors to design goals
      • Design rating and decision matrix – Explain and justify your numbers
  • The high-level design of your device must be completed, including mechanical, electrical, electronics, microcontroller, actuators, sensors, power, and software and control algorithms.

Each group will meet one on one with the teaching team to review your design. You are expected to demonstrate all the above within 20 minutes (with at least 2 minutes to spare for questions) during this formal presentation. 

Please refer to the Milestone 2 rubric on LEARN for more details of what you are expected to demonstrate in this presentation.

 

Milestone 3: Memo – Design Calculations (individual), 4 - 5 pages maximum

After your final design has been selected from Milestone 2, each individual should complete the calculations assigned in your presentation. Milestone 5: Presentation - Design Specifications and Reflection

Each group will meet one on one with the teaching team for a formal presentation. The presentation should be cohesive and to receive marks everyone has to contribute in the discussion. Milestone 6: Check-Up - Design Specification Check

In Milestone 6, you will meet with the TAs for the final time for an informal check-up of your final design specifications. Final Report

The written reports are limited to 25 pages; figures, pictures, appendices, table of contents, and the executive summary are not included in the page limit. Refrain from using appendices; students tend to dump important material in the appendices that should be in the main body of the report, resulting in a lower mark. The type styles and formatting styles of the report are open; however, students are encouraged to use the same format used for the work term reports. The reports will be evaluated based on: report content (appropriateness, adequacy, comprehensiveness, originality, and significance), writing skills (clarity of communication, organization), and appearance and structure (quality of drawings and figures, layout, formatting). Important: Refer to the lecture on report writing and the rubrics (document: Final report marking scheme on LEARN) to gain a good understanding of what we look for when we mark technical reports.

Construction

Building and testing the project is a centerpiece of the course. During this phase of the project, teams will gain firsthand experience in manufacturing and assembling of a designed product. The following are among the considerations for this phase:

  • Time needed to manufacture a part
  • Check materials and parts availability (consult with Paul Groh and Eugene Li as needed)
  • Lead time for purchasing materials
  • Student Machine Shop accessibility, safety rules and hours of operation
  • Try not to use mill or lathe machines, or minimize such use
  • Simplify design for easier manufacturability

The Project

The centerpiece of this course is the project. The design process will be applied to the design, construction, and testing of a device that addresses the problem statement. The design process includes understanding the problem, identifying design objectives, constraints, and selection criteria, generating and developing design concepts, evaluation of design options, selection of the final design, detailed design analysis, technical drawings, construction, commissioning, and testing.

Problem Statement

The problem statement is: Design and build a device that execute a search and rescue (S&R) mission to save a Lego person. The navigation course will contain sand, gravel, and empty traps. Please refer to file: MTE380 project description on LEARN to learn more about the task your device/robot is expected to complete on demo day. You are free to build a story around this problem statement.

Project Objectives

The size of the rescue device/robot that your team builds is limited such that it can be lifted and carried by one person. Dimensions of the device are limited to 0.25 m x 0.25 m x 0.25 m (10" x 10" x 10"). The webcam can extend beyond the dimensions if needed to ensure an unobstructed view. Each panel shown in the navigation course is generally 0.3 m x 0.3 m or 0.3 m x 0.6 m. The overall dimensions are 1.8 m x 1.8 m (6 feet x 6 feet). All dimensions are approximate and subject to change.

MTE 380 serves as an introduction to the application of computer vision. In the Milestone 1 workshops, you will learn how to use the provided vision module, and in this project you are expected to apply vision to:

  • Implement line tracking for your device to traverse the obstacle course using the vision module
  • Detect safety zones for your device to drop your Lego-person
  • Optional: Object detection of a Lego-person

Project Constraints

The device has to run autonomously through the S&R course. The device is not permitted to leave the boundaries of the search and rescue site. The device is not permitted to climb over the walls. Flying devices are not permitted. LiDAR is not permitted. The device must adhere to the designated path; for instance, it is not allowed to take a shortcut by passing over the traps from the starting point to reach the bullseye target. Donations from any source must be cleared by the instructor or TAs. The objective is to prevent a team from obtaining an unfair advantage. What you purchase for your project is up to you subject to your budget.

Prohibited Designs

The following features are strictly prohibited in your designs:

  • Projectiles of any means, including using the Lego-Person as a projectile.
  • Aerial devices.
  • Anything that can modify or destroy the obstacle course.
  • Devices that separate into multiple pieces.

The teaching team reserves the rights to modify and append to this list of prohibited designs.

The Competition

The objective of the competition is for your device to navigate from the start of the S&R course to the end of the course. The S&R course may be reconfigured after each run. The S&R operation will be timed. Each group will be given THREE attempts. An attempt is considered to be a run that lasts for more than 10 seconds or your devices passes the designated “attempted tile” (TBA). All group members are expected to be present during their group’s run.

For the Obstacle Course layout, please refer to the course outline posted on Learn, as images cannot be inserted on this platform.

Successful Completion

A successful completion is a run where your device is able to traverse the course, rescue the Lego person (pick-up and drop off at a designated safe-zone), and returns back to the start tile.

Generative AI

Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, may be used for assignments in this class with proper documentation, citation, and acknowledgement. Recommendations for how to cite GenAI in student work at the University of Waterloo may be found through the Library: https://subjectguides.uwaterloo.ca/chatgpt_generative_ai. Please be aware that generative AI is known to falsify references to other work and may fabricate facts and inaccurately express ideas. GenAI generates content based on the input of other human authors and may therefore contain inaccuracies or reflect biases. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. Exercise caution when using large portions of content from AI sources, especially images. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence 

You are accountable for the content and accuracy of all work you submit in this class, including any supported by generative AI.

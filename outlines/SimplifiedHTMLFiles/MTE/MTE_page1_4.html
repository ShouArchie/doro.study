Course Description

MTE 119:

Basic concepts of mechanics, vectors. Statics of particles. Rigid bodies and force systems, equilibrium of rigid bodies. Analysis of trusses and frames. Distributed forces, centroids, and moments of inertia. Friction. Internal shear and bending moments in beams.

View requirements for MTE 119

MTE 119 provides a basic understanding of the part of mechanics which is concerned with the equilibrium of bodies under the action of forces. It lays the foundation and framework for subsequent courses, namely Dynamics (SYDE 182) and Mechanics of Deformable Solids (MTE 219). Topics covered include basic concepts of mechanics and vectors, Free-Body diagrams and equilibrium of particles, Free-Body diagrams and equilibrium of rigid bodies, force systems, analysis of trusses, beams and frames, distributed forces, centroids and moments of inertia, internal shear and bending moments in beams, friction and application of frictional forces.

 

Course Structure:

Lectures: Lectures will be held on Tuesday and Thursday from 10:00-11:20am EST. Lectures will take place in the STC0040 lecture hall. There are three make-up lecture dates noted above (note different room location). Lectures will introduce the textbook material being covered in the course along with corresponding solutions to example problems. Attendance and participation during lectures are expected for learning and skill development. Lecture notes will be made available through Learn, but lectures will not be recorded or streamed. 

Tutorials: Tutorials are used to help you apply the concepts learned in class to the solution of typical statics problems. Tutorials will be held on Wednesdays or Fridays from 10:30-11:20pm EST based on the different class section in which you are assigned. Problems solved are similar to assigned problem set questions and test problems. 

Weekly Problem Sets: A selection of problem set questions from the course textbook will be posted each week. The problem set assignments will not be marked; however, solutions will be posted on LEARN the following week. These problem sets are valuable study aids and are good examples of possible exam-type questions. Weekly completion will also help with pacing of the course. If you are struggling with a problem set, please seek help from a peer, TA, first year tutor or the instructor.

Weekly Checklist: A checklist will be posted in Learn each week that indicates the course material being covered and expected progress each week.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course students should be able to:
Draw complete free-body diagrams and write appropriate equilibrium equations from the free-body diagram, including the support reactions on a structure. (1,2)
Apply the concepts of equilibrium to various structures. (2)
Able to calculate moments, centers of mass, and forces for particular structures. (1)
Describe position, forces, and moments in terms of vector forms in two and three dimensions. (1)
Determine the resultant of a force system including distributed forces and frictional forces. (1,2)
Simplify systems of forces and moments to equivalent systems (1)
Determine the internal forces in a structure. (2)

The numbers in parentheses in the learning outcomes above refer to the CEAB Engineering Graduate Attributes defined by the Canadian Engineering Accreditation Board. These are listed below. 

1. A knowledge base for engineering

2. Problem analysis

Course Deliverables

1. Design Project (10%): 

There will be a design project worth 10% of your final grade in the course. This is a group projects (2-3 students). Groups are self-selected. Any material taken from other refereed sources such as text books, handbooks, or journal articles must be properly referenced. Submissions that contain unreferenced copied or plagiarized material from other student reports or other external sources is considered an academic offence. 

 

2. Tests (45% = 3 x 15%):

There will be three (3) in-class tests (75 minutes) spread throughout the term. Each test is worth 15% of your final grade in this course. Tests are considered closed book. Further details on the tests will be provided on Learn. 

Test 1: January 23, 10:00-11:15am EST in STC0040

Test 2: February 13, 10:00-11:15am EST in STC0040

Test 3: March 20, 10:00-11:15am EST in STC0040

Missing a test

Missing a test due to illness will result in the weight of the test being shifted to the final exam, provided that illness has been self-declared on Quest and you have notified the First Year Engineering Office and the course instructor. If the illness is not declared or if the explanation is not deemed acceptable, a grade of 0 will be assigned as the test grade. The weight of the examination will not be changed under any other circumstances. Self-declaration of illness forms must be submitted within 48 hours in accordance with university policy. 

Outside of a self-declared illness, it is your responsibility to attend and complete the test during the assigned test period. There will be no accommodations for conflicts with other activities such as co-op interviews.

 

3. Final Exam (45%):

The final exam will be held in person. It is a 2.5 hours, cumulative, closed-book and closed-notes exam. Allowed materials are pen, pencil, eraser, sharpener and scientific calculator. No assignments or any other papers are allowed (blank sheets will be provided). No telecommunication devices will be permitted to be used during the exam (e.g., cell phones, wireless devices, etc). 

Missing the Final Exam

Missing the final exam due to illness will result in an assigned grade of INC (Incomplete) provided that illness is self-declared on Quest and you have notified the First Year Engineering Office and the course instructor. The grade of INC (Incomplete) will be resolved by a deferred exam within 4 months of the end of the course.  If no deferred exam is written within that timeframe, the grade will be converted to FTC (Failure to Complete).  If the illness cannot be verified or if the explanation is not deemed acceptable, a grade of DNW (Did Not Write) will be submitted for the student. If you self-declare illness, you must not access the exam paper. If you do, you will receive a grade of 0 on the exam. If you become ill during the exam, notify the instructor immediately.

Every effort will be made to provide a makeup exam on the last day of exams for students that are verifiably ill during the scheduled final exam slot.  If you are ill and unable to write the final exam as scheduled, you are expected to be available for the makeup exam.  The timing of this makeup exam will be determined once it is known which other exams on that date are to be written by the students who were ill.

Generative AI

This course includes the independent development and practice of specific skills, such as the use of statics principles in a practical design project. Therefore, the use of Generative artificial intelligence (GenAI) trained using large language models (LLM) or other methods to produce text, images, music, or code, like Chat GPT, DALL-E, or GitHub CoPilot, is not permitted in this class. Unauthorized use in this course, such as running course materials through GenAI or using GenAI to complete a course assessment is considered a violation of Policy 71 (plagiarism or unauthorized aids or assistance). Work produced with the assistance of AI tools does not represent the author’s original work and is therefore in violation of the fundamental values of academic integrity including honesty, trust, respect, fairness, responsibility and courage (ICAI, n.d.). 

You should be prepared to show your work. To demonstrate your learning, you should keep your rough notes, including research notes, brainstorming, and drafting notes. You may be asked to submit these notes along with earlier drafts of their work, either through saved drafts or saved versions of a document. If the use of GenAI is suspected where not permitted, you may be asked to meet with your instructor or TA to provide explanations to support the submitted material as being your original work. Through this process, if you have not sufficiently supported your work, academic misconduct allegations may be brought to the Associate Dean. 

In addition, you should be aware that the legal/copyright status of generative AI inputs and outputs is unclear. More information is available from the Copyright Advisory Committee: https://uwaterloo.ca/copyright-at-waterloo/teaching/generative-artificial-intelligence
